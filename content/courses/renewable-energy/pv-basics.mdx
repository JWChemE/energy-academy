Solar photovoltaics (PV) convert sunlight directly into electricity with no moving parts, no combustion, and no noise. They are now the cheapest form of new generation in much of the world and the most common on-site renewable for buildings, which is why this course opens with them. This lesson covers the photovoltaic effect, how cells build into arrays, the inverter, the cell technologies, and why PV has come to dominate.

## The photovoltaic effect

A PV cell is made of semiconductor material, usually silicon. When photons of sunlight strike the cell, they knock electrons free, and the cell's internal structure drives those electrons around an external circuit as direct current (DC). Light in, electricity out, with nothing moving and nothing consumed.

## From cell to array

- **Cell**: the basic unit, producing around 0.5 V
- **Module (panel)**: many cells wired in series, typically producing 350 to 450 W in standard sizes
- **String**: modules wired in series to build up voltage
- **Array**: strings combined to deliver the required power

## DC to AC: the inverter

PV produces DC, but buildings and the grid use AC, so the **inverter** converts DC to grid-synchronised AC. The types trade cost against shade tolerance: **string inverters** (one per string, simple and cost-effective), **microinverters** (one per panel, better with [shading](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-sizing) and panel-level monitoring), and **optimisers** (per-panel DC devices feeding a string inverter, a middle path). The inverter also performs maximum power point tracking (MPPT), continuously adjusting to extract the most power as the light changes.

## Cell technologies

- **Monocrystalline silicon**: highest efficiency (20 to 23%), uniform black appearance, now the mainstream choice.
- **Polycrystalline silicon**: slightly cheaper and slightly lower efficiency, less common now.
- **Thin film**: lower efficiency but flexible, lightweight, and better in diffuse light or high temperatures, used in niches.
- **Emerging (perovskite, tandem cells)**: pushing efficiencies higher and beginning to reach market.

Module efficiency, the fraction of incident solar energy turned into electricity, is typically 18 to 23% for modern silicon. The theoretical limit for a single silicon junction is about 33% (the Shockley-Queisser limit); tandem cells stack materials to exceed it. Higher efficiency means more power from a given roof area, which matters where space is limited.

<Callout variant="key" title="PV won because it is modular, silent and cheap">
PV has become the default on-site renewable not because it is the most efficient way to use a roof but because it is the most *practical*: it scales from a few panels to vast farms, has no moving parts to maintain, runs silently, lasts 25 to 30 years under warranty, and has fallen dramatically in cost. That combination is why it appears in almost every [net-zero pathway](/courses/renewable-energy/net-zero-pathway) for buildings, and why the following lessons focus on how to size it, connect it, store its output, and judge whether it pays, rather than on whether to use it at all.
</Callout>
