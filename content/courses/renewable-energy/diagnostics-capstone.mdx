You've learned how PV works and is sized, why self-consumption drives the value, how shading and orientation hurt yield, what batteries add, and why on-site wind so often disappoints. This capstone sends you round as the renewables engineer, sizing systems, building business cases, and spotting the designs that quietly throw value away.

The thread through every case: **self-consumed power is worth two-to-four times exported power**, so size and operate renewables to the building's own demand. And know when the technology fits: PV beats wind on almost every building.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate**: read the brief and the panel: capacity, yield, self-consumption, wind speed, orientation.
2. **Calculate**: work out the figure that decides it: annual yield (kWp × specific yield), the self-consumption value, the shading or orientation loss, the wind cube law. The **reference data is on hand**: PV yield, self-consumption value, shading, battery and the cube law, but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose**: pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe**: build an ordered work plan (free operational wins first, then capital).
5. **Verify**: score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="Size and run to your own demand">
On-site renewables earn most from the power you use yourself, not the power you sell. So size the array to the daytime load, keep it unshaded and well oriented, shift flexible loads into the sunny hours, and add storage only where the self-consumption uplift justifies it. Don't flood the grid with cheap midday export, don't roof-mount a turbine into turbulent air, and don't assume a "maintenance-free" system is actually working, monitor it.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **PV yield & economics**: capacity × specific yield, and the all-important self-consumption rate.
- **Sizing**: why an oversized array exports its best energy at a loss.
- **Shading**: the series-string penalty, where two shaded panels cost far more than two panels.
- **Storage & load-shifting**: banking surplus, and the free uplift from scheduling loads into generation.
- **Wind**: the cube law, and why roof-mounted turbines almost never pay.
- **Operation**: orientation penalties, and catching the invisible faults that monitoring reveals.

## Differential thinking

"It's renewable, it must be good" misses most of these. A north roof gives up a third of its yield; an oversized array exports its best power for pennies; a roof turbine makes an eighth of what a mast would; a dead string can sit invisible for months. The decisive evidence is the numbers: the self-consumption rate, the wind speed cubed, the orientation factor, the monitoring (or its absence).

## After you finish

- Which **number** decided each case: the self-consumption rate, the cube law, the orientation factor, the shaded-string loss?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, is it a free operational change (shift loads, monitor), a design fix (optimisers, orientation, sizing), or a capital project (array, battery) that has to earn its self-consumption value?
