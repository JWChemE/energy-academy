Wind turbines convert moving air into electricity. At large scale wind is a cornerstone of the grid; at small scale for individual buildings it is far more situational, and often disappointing. The reason is one unforgiving physical law that rewards genuinely windy sites and punishes poor ones severely. This lesson covers how a turbine works, the cube law and siting, the reality of small-scale wind, where it does work, and the practical considerations.

## How a turbine works, and the cube law

Wind turns aerodynamic blades, driving a generator. The power available is governed by one crucial relationship: **power is proportional to the cube of wind speed**. Double the wind speed and you get *eight* times the power. This cubic law dominates everything about wind, and it means a turbine's output depends overwhelmingly on having a genuinely windy site: a modest difference in average wind speed transforms the economics.

Because of the cube law, average wind speed matters far more than peak gusts, wind speed rises with height (away from ground friction and turbulence, so taller masts capture much more energy), and smooth, open exposure (hilltops, coasts, open countryside) vastly outperforms turbulent, sheltered sites. A site's viability hinges on the measured average wind speed, typically 5 to 6 m/s or more at hub height for a turbine to make sense.

## Small-scale wind: the reality

Small turbines on or near buildings usually disappoint, because they sit low, in turbulent air disturbed by buildings and trees; urban and suburban wind speeds are low and gusty; the cube law punishes these poor conditions severely; and turbulence increases wear and noise. Many building-mounted micro-turbines generate a fraction of their rated output and rarely repay their cost, and roof-mounting in particular is generally a poor investment.

## Where small and medium wind works

Wind can pay where conditions genuinely suit it: exposed rural sites (farms, remote facilities) with high measured wind speed, a free-standing well-sited mast (not roof-mounted) on open land, and sites where a proper wind assessment confirms strong, steady wind. For such sites, a medium turbine on a tall mast can be very productive and complement [solar](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-basics), since wind often blows hardest in winter and at night when solar is weakest, a useful pairing in [hybrid systems](/courses/renewable-energy/hybrid-systems).

<Callout variant="key" title="Measure the wind, avoid the roof, and be honest">
The cube law makes wind a technology where honest assessment is everything, because the difference between a good site and a poor one is not marginal but eightfold. The practical guidance follows directly: measure the wind resource on site before committing, never rely on maps alone, avoid roof-mounted micro-turbines entirely, and invest only where a tall mast on an exposed site has *measured* wind speeds to justify it (plus the [grid connection](/courses/renewable-energy/grid-codes), planning, noise and maintenance that any turbine brings). For most buildings, PV is the more reliable on-site renewable; a genuinely windy rural site with a proper mast is a different and often excellent matter.
</Callout>
