Wind turbines convert moving air into electricity. At large scale wind is a cornerstone of the grid; at small scale for individual buildings it is far more situational, and often disappointing.

## How a turbine works

Wind turns aerodynamic blades, driving a generator. The power available is governed by a crucial relationship:

Power is proportional to the cube of wind speed (v3)

This cubic law dominates everything about wind. Double the wind speed and you get *eight* times the power. It means a turbine's output depends overwhelmingly on having a genuinely windy site - a modest difference in average wind speed transforms the economics.

## The cube law and siting

Because of the cube law:
- Average wind speed matters far more than peak gusts
- Wind speed rises with height (away from ground friction and turbulence), so taller masts capture much more energy
- Smooth, open exposure (hilltops, coasts, open countryside) vastly outperforms turbulent, sheltered sites

A site's viability hinges on measured average wind speed - typically you want 5-6 m/s or more at hub height for a turbine to make sense.

## Small-scale wind: the reality

Small turbines on or near buildings usually disappoint because:
- They sit low, in turbulent air disturbed by buildings and trees
- Urban and suburban wind speeds are low and gusty
- The cube law punishes these poor conditions severely
- Turbulence increases wear and noise

Many building-mounted micro-turbines generate a fraction of their rated output and rarely repay their cost. Roof-mounting in particular is generally a poor investment.

## Where small/medium wind works

Wind can pay where conditions genuinely suit it:
- Exposed rural sites - farms, remote facilities - with high measured wind speed
- A free-standing, well-sited mast (not roof-mounted) on open land
- Sites where a proper wind assessment confirms strong, steady wind

For such sites, a medium turbine on a tall mast can be very productive and complement solar (wind often blows hardest in winter and at night, when solar is weakest).

## Practical considerations

- **Wind assessment:** measure on site before committing - do not rely on maps alone
- **Planning and noise:** turbines need planning permission and can raise noise and visual objections
- **Grid connection:** as with any generator, parallel operation needs network approval
- **Maintenance:** moving parts mean more maintenance than PV

## The verdict

Wind is a superb large-scale renewable but a niche on-site one. The honest guidance: assess the wind resource rigorously, avoid roof-mounted micro-turbines, and only invest where a tall mast on an exposed site has measured wind speeds to justify it. For most buildings, PV is the more reliable on-site renewable - though a genuinely windy rural site is a different matter.
