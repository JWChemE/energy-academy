As on-site generation, storage, and smart control spread, buildings and sites are evolving from passive consumers into active participants in the energy system. Microgrids and local energy networks are where this heads.

## What a microgrid is

A **microgrid** is a local network of generation, storage, and loads that can manage itself - balancing its own supply and demand, and able to operate connected to the main grid or, when needed, islanded from it. A campus, hospital, industrial site, or community can run as a microgrid.

Its building blocks:
- Local generation (PV, wind, CHP)
- Storage (batteries, thermal stores)
- Controllable loads
- A control system that orchestrates them
- A connection to the main grid (usually)

## Local generation and self-balancing

Within a microgrid, generation and demand are matched locally as far as possible - solar feeding daytime loads, batteries shifting surplus to the evening, CHP and storage covering the rest. Drawing less from and exporting less to the main grid reduces both cost and network strain, and a microgrid that can island keeps critical loads running through grid outages - valuable for hospitals, data centres, and essential services.

## Peer-to-peer and community energy

Local energy networks enable sharing between participants:
- A building with surplus PV supplies a neighbour who needs power
- **Peer-to-peer trading** lets prosumers buy and sell directly, often via smart metering and (sometimes) blockchain-based settlement
- **Community energy** schemes let groups jointly own and benefit from local renewables

This keeps energy - and its value - local, and can make renewable projects viable that no single participant could justify alone.

## Virtual power plants

Individually small assets become significant when aggregated. A **virtual power plant (VPP)** pools many distributed batteries, generators, and flexible loads, controlling them as one to provide services to the grid - balancing supply and demand, responding to price signals, and earning revenue for participants. Your building's battery or heat pump, combined with thousands of others, can help balance the national grid and be paid for it.

## Demand flexibility and grid services

A key role for local energy assets is **flexibility** - shifting demand in time to help the grid:
- Reducing consumption at system peaks (demand response)
- Soaking up surplus renewable generation when it is abundant
- Providing fast response to grid frequency events

Flexibility is increasingly rewarded financially, turning a building's controllable loads and storage into revenue-earning grid assets.

## The bigger picture

The energy system is shifting from large central plants pushing power one way to a distributed, two-way network of millions of small generators, stores, and flexible loads. Microgrids, local networks, peer-to-peer trading, and virtual power plants are the architecture of that transition. For the energy manager, it reframes the building - from a cost centre that merely consumes, into an active, potentially revenue-generating node in a smart, decentralised, renewable energy system.
