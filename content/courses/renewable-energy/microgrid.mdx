As on-site generation, storage and smart control spread, buildings and sites are evolving from passive consumers into active participants in the energy system. Microgrids and local energy networks are where this heads, and they reframe what a building can be: not just a cost centre that consumes, but a node that generates, stores, trades and earns. This lesson covers what a microgrid is, local self-balancing, peer-to-peer and community energy, virtual power plants, and demand flexibility.

## What a microgrid is

A **microgrid** is a local network of generation, storage and loads that can manage itself, balancing its own supply and demand, and able to operate connected to the main grid or, when needed, islanded from it. A campus, hospital, industrial site or community can run as a microgrid. Its building blocks are local generation ([PV](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-basics), wind, [CHP](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/how-chp-works)), storage ([batteries](/courses/renewable-energy/battery-storage), [thermal stores](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/load-shifting)), controllable loads, a control system that orchestrates them, and (usually) a connection to the main grid.

## Local self-balancing and community energy

Within a microgrid, generation and demand are matched locally as far as possible: solar feeding daytime loads, batteries shifting surplus to the evening, CHP and storage covering the rest. Drawing less from and exporting less to the main grid reduces both cost and network strain, and a microgrid that can island keeps critical loads running through grid outages, valuable for hospitals, data centres and essential services. Local energy networks also enable sharing: a building with surplus PV can supply a neighbour, **peer-to-peer trading** lets prosumers buy and sell directly (via smart metering and sometimes blockchain-based settlement), and **community energy** schemes let groups jointly own and benefit from local renewables, keeping energy and its value local and making projects viable that no single participant could justify alone.

## Virtual power plants and flexibility

Individually small assets become significant when aggregated. A **virtual power plant (VPP)** pools many distributed batteries, generators and flexible loads, controlling them as one to provide services to the grid, balancing supply and demand, responding to price signals, and earning revenue for participants. Your building's battery or heat pump, combined with thousands of others, can help balance the national grid and be paid for it. The key enabler is **flexibility**, shifting demand in time to help the grid: reducing consumption at system peaks (demand response), soaking up surplus renewable generation when it is abundant, and providing fast response to grid frequency events. Flexibility is increasingly rewarded financially, turning a building's controllable loads and storage into revenue-earning grid assets.

<Callout variant="key" title="The building becomes an active node, not a passive load">
The energy system is shifting from large central plants pushing power one way to a distributed, two-way network of millions of small generators, stores and flexible loads. Microgrids, local networks, peer-to-peer trading and virtual power plants are the architecture of that transition, and for the energy manager they reframe the whole job. The building is no longer just a meter to minimise; it is a controllable node that can generate, store, shift and trade, potentially earning revenue from the flexibility it provides. The [controls and storage](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/economics-payback) that this course and the storage course describe are what unlock that role, and the [net-zero pathway](/courses/renewable-energy/net-zero-pathway) is what sequences them into a coherent strategy.
</Callout>
