PV is now a mature, low-risk investment, but whether a specific system pays depends on its cost, how much of its output is used on site, and the value of the electricity it displaces.

## The cost side

PV capital cost has fallen dramatically. Installed costs (2020s) are roughly:
- Domestic (under 10 kWp): 1,200-1,800 GBP/kWp
- Commercial (10s-100s kWp): 700-1,100 GBP/kWp
- Large ground-mount: below 700 GBP/kWp

Bigger systems are cheaper per kWp through economies of scale. Running costs are low - occasional inverter replacement (every 10-15 years), cleaning, and monitoring.

## The value side

The annual benefit combines:
- **Self-consumed energy** valued at the retail electricity price (the big one - perhaps 0.25 GBP/kWh)
- **Exported energy** valued at the export rate (perhaps 0.05-0.15 GBP/kWh)
- Any incentive payments

So the return hinges on the **self-consumption rate** - the fraction of generation used on site. A building consuming 70% of its PV earns far more than one exporting 70% of it.

## A worked example

A 50 kWp commercial system:
- Cost: around 45,000 GBP
- Generates: roughly 45,000 kWh/year
- If 60% is self-consumed at 0.25 GBP/kWh = 6,750 GBP
- And 40% exported at 0.10 GBP/kWh = 1,800 GBP
- Annual benefit: around 8,550 GBP
- Simple payback: roughly 5-6 years

With a 25-30 year panel life, the system generates value for two decades after payback - a strong long-term return.

## The 25-year view

PV's appeal is its longevity. Panels carry 25-30 year performance warranties, degrading only around 0.4-0.5% per year. Judge it with lifetime methods - NPV and IRR over 25 years - not just simple payback. Over that horizon, well-sited PV typically returns an IRR well into double figures, and the lifetime cost per kWh (LCOE) often undercuts grid power.

## What strengthens the case

- High self-consumption (size to demand, shift loads, add storage)
- High and rising grid electricity prices
- Good orientation and minimal shading
- Low-cost installation (larger systems, simple roofs)
- A carbon price or value placed on emissions avoided

## Risks and sensitivities

The main uncertainties are future electricity prices, the export rate, and the achieved self-consumption. Test the case against these. PV is low-risk technically - no moving parts, long warranties - so the financial risk lies mostly in these price and usage assumptions rather than in the technology itself.
