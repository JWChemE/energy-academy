PV is now a mature, low-risk investment, but whether a specific system pays depends on its cost, how much of its output is used on site, and the value of the electricity it displaces. Because the technology is so reliable, the financial risk lies almost entirely in these price and usage assumptions rather than in the panels. This lesson covers the cost side, the value side, a worked example, the 25-year view, and what strengthens the case.

## The cost side

PV capital cost has fallen dramatically. Installed costs in the 2020s are roughly £1,200 to £1,800/kWp for domestic (under 10 kWp), £700 to £1,100/kWp for commercial (tens to hundreds of kWp), and below £700/kWp for large ground-mount, since bigger systems are cheaper per kWp through economies of scale. Running costs are low: occasional inverter replacement (every 10 to 15 years), cleaning, and monitoring.

## The value side

The annual benefit combines self-consumed energy valued at the retail price (the big one, perhaps £0.25/kWh), exported energy at the export rate (perhaps £0.05 to £0.15/kWh), and any incentive payments. So the return hinges on the [self-consumption rate](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-integration): a building consuming 70% of its PV earns far more than one exporting 70% of it.

<WorkedExample title="a 50 kWp commercial system">
  <Given>
    - 50 kWp system costing ≈ £45,000, generating ≈ 45,000 kWh/year
    - 60% self-consumed at £0.25/kWh; 40% exported at £0.10/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual benefit and simple payback.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Self-consumed: 45,000 × 0.60 × £0.25 = **£6,750**. Exported: 45,000 × 0.40 × £0.10 =
    **£1,800**. Annual benefit ≈ **£8,550**, giving a simple payback of 45,000 ÷ 8,550 ≈
    **5 to 6 years**. With a 25 to 30 year panel life, the system then generates value for
    two decades after payback. Note how the split drives it: if the same system exported
    60% instead of self-consuming it, the benefit would fall by roughly a third, which is
    why [sizing to demand](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-sizing) matters more than squeezing
    on more panels.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## The 25-year view

PV's appeal is its longevity. Panels carry 25 to 30 year performance warranties, degrading only around 0.4 to 0.5% a year, so judge PV with lifetime methods, [NPV and IRR](/courses/economic-analysis/npv-and-irr) over 25 years, not just simple payback. Over that horizon, well-sited PV typically returns an IRR well into double figures, and its lifetime cost per kWh (LCOE) often undercuts grid power.

<Callout variant="key" title="The risk is in the prices, not the panels">
PV is about as low-risk as generation technology gets, no moving parts, long warranties, predictable output, so the uncertainty in its business case lives almost entirely in three assumptions: future electricity prices, the export rate, and the achieved self-consumption. Test the case against all three, because a system that looks marginal at a 40% self-consumption rate can look excellent at 70%, and rising grid prices steadily improve every PV investment already in the ground. The case is strengthened by high self-consumption (size to demand, shift loads, add storage), high and rising grid prices, good orientation and minimal shading, low-cost installation, and any value placed on avoided carbon.
</Callout>
