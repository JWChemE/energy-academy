Biomass burns organic material, wood chips, pellets, agricultural residues, to produce heat. As a renewable it occupies a contested middle ground: low-carbon in principle, but only under the right conditions, and its "renewable" label has to be examined rather than assumed. This lesson covers how biomass heating works, the conditional carbon argument, the air-quality drawback, where it fits, and how it compares with heat pumps.

## How biomass heating works

A biomass boiler burns wood fuel to heat water for space heating and hot water, much like a gas boiler but with solid fuel. The key fuels are **wood pellets** (compressed sawdust, uniform and dry, allowing automated feed and clean combustion, the premium fuel), **wood chips** (cheaper, less uniform, needing more storage and handling, common at larger scale), and **logs** (manual, suited to small or domestic use). Systems need fuel storage, automated feed (auger or vacuum), and ash removal, more handling than gas and a key practical consideration.

## The carbon argument, and its conditions

Biomass is considered low-carbon on the principle that the CO₂ released by burning was recently absorbed by the growing plant, a short, closing carbon cycle, unlike fossil fuels that release carbon locked away for millions of years. But this is only true under conditions: the fuel must come from **sustainably managed sources** where harvested trees are replanted, **transport emissions** must be low (shipping pellets across the world undermines the benefit), burning must be efficient and clean, and ideally the fuel is **waste or residue** that would otherwise decompose. Where these hold, biomass genuinely cuts carbon; where they do not (long-haul fuel, unsustainable forestry, the carbon "payback time" of regrowing trees), the benefit shrinks or disappears.

## Air quality

Biomass combustion emits particulates and NOx. In urban and air-quality-sensitive areas this is a serious drawback, and tight emissions standards apply. Biomass is generally better suited to rural settings with local fuel supply than to cities, where [heat pumps](/courses/renewable-energy/heat-pump-renewable) are usually cleaner at the point of use.

## Where biomass fits

Biomass works best where there is a local, sustainable fuel supply (woodland, sawmill residue, agricultural waste), the heat demand is large and constant (district heating, industrial process, rural estates), high-temperature heat is needed that heat pumps struggle to provide, or gas is unavailable (off-grid rural sites). It is less suited to small urban buildings.

<Callout variant="key" title="One tool among several, justified case by case, not by label">
The renewable-heat debate increasingly favours heat pumps for most buildings, because they are cleaner at the point of use, get greener automatically as the grid decarbonises, and avoid fuel logistics. Biomass retains a genuine role for large, constant, or high-temperature rural heat demands with real local sustainable fuel, and for biogas and biomethane in [CHP](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/chp-technologies). But it is the one "renewable" in this course whose carbon credentials genuinely depend on how it is sourced and used, so treat it as one tool among several, justified case by case on a real sustainability and air-quality assessment rather than a blanket "renewable" label. Assume nothing; check the fuel's origin and the air-quality context before counting the carbon saving.
</Callout>
