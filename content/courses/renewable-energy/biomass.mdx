Biomass burns organic material - wood chips, pellets, agricultural residues - to produce heat. As a renewable it occupies a contested middle ground: low-carbon in principle, but only under the right conditions.

## How biomass heating works

A biomass boiler burns wood fuel to heat water for space heating and hot water, much like a gas boiler but with solid fuel. Key fuels:
- **Wood pellets:** compressed sawdust, uniform and dry, allowing automated feed and clean combustion - the premium fuel
- **Wood chips:** cheaper, less uniform, needing more storage and handling - common at larger scale
- **Logs:** manual, suited to small or domestic use

Systems need fuel storage, automated feed (auger or vacuum), and ash removal - more handling than gas, and a key practical consideration.

## The carbon argument

Biomass is considered low-carbon on the principle that the CO2 released by burning was recently absorbed by the growing plant - a short, closing carbon cycle, unlike fossil fuels that release carbon locked away for millions of years.

But this is only true under conditions:
- The fuel must come from **sustainably managed sources** where harvested trees are replanted
- **Transport emissions** must be low - shipping pellets across the world undermines the benefit
- Burning must be efficient and clean
- Ideally the fuel is **waste or residue** that would otherwise decompose

Where these hold, biomass genuinely cuts carbon. Where they do not - long-haul fuel, unsustainable forestry, the carbon "payback time" of regrowing trees - the benefit shrinks or disappears. The carbon case must be examined, not assumed.

## Air quality

Biomass combustion emits particulates and NOx. In urban and air-quality-sensitive areas this is a serious drawback, and tight emissions standards apply. Biomass is generally better suited to rural settings with local fuel supply than to cities.

## Where biomass fits

Biomass works best where:
- There is a local, sustainable fuel supply (woodland, sawmill residue, agricultural waste)
- The heat demand is large and constant (district heating, industrial process, rural estates)
- High-temperature heat is needed that heat pumps struggle to provide
- Gas is unavailable (off-grid rural sites)

It is less suited to small urban buildings, where heat pumps are usually cleaner and simpler.

## Biomass versus heat pumps

The renewable-heat debate increasingly favours heat pumps for most buildings - they are cleaner at point of use, get greener as the grid decarbonises, and avoid fuel logistics. Biomass retains a role for large, constant, or high-temperature rural heat demands with genuine local sustainable fuel, and for biogas/biomethane in CHP. Treat it as one tool among several, justified case by case on a genuine sustainability and air-quality assessment rather than a blanket "renewable" label.
