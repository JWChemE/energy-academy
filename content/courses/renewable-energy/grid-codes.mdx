Any generator connected to the grid must follow strict rules to keep the network safe and stable. Understanding grid codes and the connection process is essential before installing renewables, because the connection terms, and sometimes the cost of network reinforcement, can make or break a project. This lesson covers why grid codes exist, the critical anti-islanding function, the UK connection process, protection requirements, and export limitation.

## Why grid codes exist

The grid is a tightly balanced system shared by everyone, and a generator that misbehaves (feeding power when it should not, or destabilising voltage and frequency) endangers people and equipment across the network. **Grid codes** set the technical requirements every connected generator must meet to protect the system and those working on it.

## Anti-islanding: the critical safety function

The single most important requirement is **anti-islanding protection**. If the grid loses power, for maintenance or a fault, a connected generator must *not* keep energising the local network, because doing so could electrocute engineers working on lines they believe are dead, and damage equipment when the grid reconnects out of sync. Grid-tied inverters continuously monitor the grid and disconnect within milliseconds if it fails or drifts out of limits. This is why a standard [grid-tied PV system](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-integration) gives no power during an outage: the safety function shuts it down. Battery-hybrid systems with islanding capability handle this with a deliberate, controlled disconnection.

## The connection process (UK)

Connecting generation involves the Distribution Network Operator (DNO). **Smaller installations** (for example domestic PV under a threshold) can often connect under a "connect and notify" (**G98**) process: install, then inform the DNO. **Larger installations** need prior application and approval (**G99**), where the DNO assesses the network's capacity to accept the generation. The DNO sets a **maximum export capacity**, and large connections may require costly network reinforcement. Engaging the DNO early is essential, because connection terms, timescales and any reinforcement cost can make or break a project.

## Protection and export limitation

Connected generators must include protection that disconnects them if the grid goes out of safe limits for voltage (too high or too low), frequency (too high or too low), and loss of mains (the anti-islanding function). These are built into compliant inverters certified to the relevant standard, which is why specifying certified, grid-code-compliant equipment matters. Where a site should not export (or export above a set limit), **export limitation** controls throttle generation or divert surplus (to storage or loads) to stay within the agreed cap, which can ease or speed up a connection where network capacity is constrained, allowing a larger array than the export limit alone would permit as long as the surplus is used or stored on site.

<Callout variant="key" title="Engage the network operator early; the connection can decide the project">
The recurring lesson of renewable connection is that the grid side, not the technology, is where projects stall. The practical sequence: assess the renewable resource and size the system, check DNO capacity and the connection route (G98/G99), apply for connection where required and agree the export capacity, install certified grid-code-compliant equipment with proper protection, then commission and register. The step that catches people out is the DNO application, because a constrained network can impose a low export limit or an expensive reinforcement that changes the whole economics. Getting the grid connection right, and early, keeps a renewable project legal, safe and on schedule.
