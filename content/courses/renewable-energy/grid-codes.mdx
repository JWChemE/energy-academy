Any generator connected to the grid must follow strict rules to keep the network safe and stable. Understanding grid codes and the connection process is essential before installing renewables.

## Why grid codes exist

The grid is a tightly balanced system shared by everyone. A generator that misbehaves - feeding power when it should not, or destabilising voltage and frequency - endangers people and equipment across the network. **Grid codes** set the technical requirements every connected generator must meet to protect the system and those working on it.

## Anti-islanding: the critical safety function

The single most important requirement is **anti-islanding protection**. If the grid loses power - say for maintenance or a fault - a connected generator must *not* keep energising the local network. If it did, it could:
- Electrocute engineers working on lines they believe are dead
- Damage equipment when the grid reconnects out of sync

Grid-tied inverters continuously monitor the grid and disconnect within milliseconds if it fails or drifts out of limits. This is why a standard grid-tied PV system gives no power during an outage - the safety function shuts it down. (Battery-hybrid systems with islanding capability handle this with deliberate, controlled disconnection.)

## The connection process (UK)

Connecting generation involves the Distribution Network Operator (DNO):
- **Smaller installations** (e.g. domestic PV under a threshold) can often connect under a "connect and notify" (G98) process - install, then inform the DNO
- **Larger installations** need prior application and approval (G99), where the DNO assesses the network's capacity to accept the generation
- The DNO sets a **maximum export capacity**, and large connections may require costly network reinforcement

Engaging the DNO early is essential - connection terms, timescales, and any reinforcement cost can make or break a project.

## Protection requirements

Connected generators must include protection that disconnects them if the grid goes out of safe limits for:
- **Voltage** (too high or too low)
- **Frequency** (too high or too low)
- **Loss of mains** (the anti-islanding function)

These are built into compliant inverters certified to the relevant standard, which is why specifying certified, grid-code-compliant equipment matters.

## Export limitation

Where a site should not export (or export above a set limit), **export limitation** controls throttle generation or divert surplus (to storage or loads) to stay within the agreed cap. This can ease or speed up a connection where network capacity is constrained, allowing a larger array than the export limit alone would permit, as long as the surplus is used or stored on site.

## The practical sequence

1. Assess the renewable resource and size the system
2. Check DNO capacity and the connection route (G98/G99)
3. Apply for connection where required, agreeing export capacity
4. Install certified, grid-code-compliant equipment with proper protection
5. Commission and register the installation

Getting the grid connection right - early - keeps a renewable project legal, safe, and on schedule.
