Solar thermal collectors capture the sun's heat directly to warm water - a different technology from PV, and a very efficient one for hot-water demand.

## How it works

A **solar thermal collector** absorbs sunlight and transfers the heat to a fluid, which is pumped to a hot-water store. Unlike PV (which makes electricity at 18-23% efficiency), solar thermal converts 50-70% of incident solar energy into useful heat - because turning sunlight into heat is far more direct than turning it into electricity.

## Collector types

- **Flat-plate collectors:** an absorber plate in an insulated, glazed box. Robust, cost-effective, good for domestic hot water.
- **Evacuated-tube collectors:** rows of vacuum-insulated glass tubes. More efficient, especially in cold weather and low sun, because the vacuum cuts heat loss. More expensive.

## The system

A typical solar thermal system comprises:
- Collectors on the roof
- A pumped primary circuit (with antifreeze in cold climates)
- A heat exchanger into a hot-water cylinder (often a twin-coil tank, solar feeding the lower coil)
- Controls that run the pump only when the collector is hotter than the store

The store is essential - it banks solar heat collected during the day for use whenever hot water is drawn.

## Seasonal performance and matching

Output follows the sun: strong in summer, weak in winter. A well-sized domestic system can provide 50-60% of annual hot-water demand, typically meeting most of the summer need and supplementing a boiler or heat pump in winter. Sizing aims to meet summer demand without large unused surplus - oversizing leads to stagnation (the system overheating when there is no demand).

## Best applications

Solar thermal shines where there is a steady, year-round hot-water demand:
- Housing, especially with consistent occupancy
- Hotels, care homes, leisure centres, swimming pools
- Any site with high domestic hot-water use through the summer

Swimming pools are an ideal match - a large, constant low-temperature heat demand that coincides with the sunny season.

## Solar thermal versus PV

With PV costs having fallen so far, many now install PV (sometimes driving a hot-water diverter or heat pump) rather than solar thermal, because PV is versatile - its electricity can serve any load, not just heat. Solar thermal still wins on raw efficiency per square metre for heat and remains strong for high, constant hot-water demands. The choice depends on the building's heat-to-power needs, available roof area, and whether a single technology can serve the wider energy strategy.
