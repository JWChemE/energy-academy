Solar thermal collectors capture the sun's heat directly to warm water, a different technology from [PV](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-basics) and a very efficient one for hot-water demand. Where PV makes electricity at 18 to 23% efficiency, solar thermal converts 50 to 70% of incident solar energy into useful heat, because turning sunlight into heat is far more direct than turning it into electricity. This lesson covers how it works, the collector types, the system, seasonal performance, and the solar-thermal-versus-PV question.

## How it works

A **solar thermal collector** absorbs sunlight and transfers the heat to a fluid, which is pumped to a hot-water store. The [store](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/dhw-heating) is essential: it banks solar heat collected during the day for use whenever hot water is drawn, since collection and demand rarely coincide.

## Collector types and the system

**Flat-plate collectors** use an absorber plate in an insulated, glazed box: robust, cost-effective, and good for domestic hot water. **Evacuated-tube collectors** use rows of vacuum-insulated glass tubes: more efficient, especially in cold weather and low sun (the vacuum cuts heat loss), but more expensive. A typical system comprises collectors on the roof, a pumped primary circuit (with antifreeze in cold climates), a heat exchanger into a hot-water cylinder (often a twin-coil tank with solar feeding the lower coil), and controls that run the pump only when the collector is hotter than the store.

## Seasonal performance and matching

Output follows the sun: strong in summer, weak in winter. A well-sized domestic system can provide 50 to 60% of annual hot-water demand, meeting most of the summer need and supplementing a boiler or [heat pump](/courses/renewable-energy/heat-pump-renewable) in winter. Sizing aims to meet summer demand without a large unused surplus, because oversizing leads to **stagnation**, the system overheating when there is no demand to absorb the heat. Solar thermal shines where there is a steady, year-round hot-water demand: housing with consistent occupancy, hotels, care homes, leisure centres, and especially swimming pools, an ideal match of a large, constant, low-temperature heat demand with the sunny season.

<Callout variant="key" title="Solar thermal wins per square metre for heat; PV wins on versatility">
Solar thermal is more efficient than PV per square metre at making heat, and it remains a strong choice for buildings with high, constant hot-water demand. But PV's costs have fallen so far that many now install PV instead, sometimes driving a hot-water diverter or a heat pump, because PV is versatile: its electricity can serve *any* load, not just heat, and it fits the wider [net-zero](/courses/renewable-energy/net-zero-pathway) strategy of electrifying everything. The choice comes down to the building's heat-to-power balance, available roof area, and whether a single technology can serve the whole energy strategy. For a pool or a care home with year-round hot water, solar thermal still makes strong sense; for a general building, PV plus a heat pump increasingly wins.
</Callout>
