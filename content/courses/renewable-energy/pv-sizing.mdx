Sizing a PV system means matching the array to the available space, the site conditions, and - crucially - the building's own electricity demand. A well-sized system maximises value; a poorly sized one exports cheap power it could have used.

## Capacity and yield

PV capacity is rated in **kilowatts-peak (kWp)** - the output under standard test conditions. Actual annual energy depends on location, orientation, and losses:

Annual energy (kWh) = kWp x specific yield (kWh/kWp/year)

In the UK, specific yield is around 800-1,000 kWh/kWp/year for a well-oriented system; sunnier climates reach 1,400-1,800. So a 10 kWp array in the UK generates roughly 8,500-9,500 kWh/year.

## Orientation and tilt

Output depends strongly on which way the panels face and their angle:
- **Orientation:** south is optimal in the northern hemisphere; east or west typically lose 10-20% but spread generation across morning or afternoon
- **Tilt:** around 30-40 degrees is optimal in the UK; flatter or steeper reduces annual yield modestly
- **East-west split:** flat-roof systems often face panels east and west to flatten the generation curve and fit more capacity

## The enemy: shading

Shading hurts PV disproportionately. Because cells are wired in series, shading even one cell can throttle a whole string - like a kink in a hosepipe. Survey for:
- Chimneys, parapets, plant, and adjacent buildings
- Trees (and their growth over time)
- Self-shading between rows on flat roofs (set row spacing to avoid winter shading)

Microinverters or optimisers mitigate shading by managing panels individually, recovering much of the lost output where shading is unavoidable.

## Sizing to demand: the key decision

The most important sizing choice is matching generation to on-site consumption, because **self-consumed power is worth far more than exported power**:
- Power used on site displaces grid electricity at the retail price (perhaps 0.25 GBP/kWh)
- Power exported earns only the export rate (perhaps 0.05-0.15 GBP/kWh)

Overlay the building's daytime load profile on the expected generation. A system sized so most output is consumed on site (or stored) returns far more than one that floods the grid with cheap exports at midday. For a building with low daytime demand, a smaller array - or added battery storage - often makes better financial sense than filling every square metre of roof.

## Practical constraints

Final sizing reconciles the ideal with reality:
- Available unshaded roof area and its structural load capacity
- The size of the grid connection and any export limit
- Budget and the point of diminishing returns on self-consumption
- Inverter sizing (often slightly undersized relative to array peak, since peak output is rare)

The result is a system tuned to the building - large enough to capture the value, not so large that it exports its best energy at a loss.
