Real buildings rarely run on a single energy source. Hybrid systems combine renewables with conventional supply, or several renewables together, so that each technology works where it is strongest, delivering lower carbon and cost than any one alone while keeping the supply reliable. This lesson covers why to hybridise, the two most common hybrids (heat pump plus boiler, and PV plus battery plus grid), renewables plus storage, multi-source systems, and how to design a good hybrid.

## Why hybridise

No single source is perfect: renewables are variable, conventional plant is carbon-intensive, and each technology has a sweet spot. Hybrids combine them so renewables provide low-cost, low-carbon energy when available while conventional plant guarantees supply when they are not, giving lower carbon and cost than either alone with reliability maintained.

## Heat pump plus boiler (bivalent heating)

A common and powerful hybrid is a [heat pump](/courses/renewable-energy/heat-pump-renewable) with a backup boiler. The heat pump handles the bulk of the year (mild weather, where its COP is high and running cost low), while the boiler (gas or biomass) tops up on the coldest days, when heat-pump output and efficiency fall and demand peaks. The control system chooses the cheaper or cleaner source for the conditions (the "bivalent point"). This lets a smaller, cheaper heat pump cover most of the annual heat (often 80%+) while the boiler covers the rare peaks, avoiding the cost of a heat pump sized for the worst day and easing retrofit in buildings not yet ready for full electrification.

## PV plus battery plus grid, and renewables plus storage

On the electricity side, a typical hybrid is **PV + battery + grid**: PV generates clean power by day, the [battery](/courses/renewable-energy/battery-storage) stores surplus for evening use and provides backup, and the grid fills any remaining gap and absorbs export, maximising self-consumption and resilience while keeping the grid as a safety net. More generally, hybridising any renewable with [thermal or electrical storage](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/load-shifting) smooths its variability: solar thermal plus a hot-water store banks daytime heat for evening, PV plus battery shifts midday generation to the evening peak, wind plus storage captures gusty output for steady use. Storage turns intermittent renewable supply into something closer to dispatchable, greatly increasing its useful value.

<Callout variant="key" title="The art of a hybrid is in the sizing and the controls">
A good hybrid is not just several technologies bolted together; it is each component sized to its strength and a control system that dispatches the cheapest, cleanest option moment to moment. The design rules: size each component to its strength (renewable to the base load, backup to the peak), set sensible switchover logic (the bivalent point for heat, the battery dispatch strategy for power), use storage to bridge the gaps, and let the controls optimise for cost or carbon as priorities dictate. Done well, a hybrid delivers most of the carbon benefit of full renewables with the reliability of conventional supply, which is often the most practical and economic path, especially in retrofit, on the road to net zero. Larger sites extend this into full [multi-source systems](/courses/renewable-energy/microgrid), blending PV, heat pumps, CHP, solar thermal and storage under one controller.
</Callout>

Where multiple sources and storage are managed together intelligently, the building becomes a microgrid, the next lesson.
