Real buildings rarely run on a single energy source. Hybrid systems combine renewables with conventional supply - or several renewables together - to balance cost, carbon, and reliability.

## Why hybridise

No single source is perfect: renewables are variable, conventional plant is carbon-intensive, and each technology has a sweet spot. Hybrids combine them so each works where it is strongest:
- Renewables provide low-cost, low-carbon energy when available
- Conventional plant guarantees supply when they are not
- The result is lower carbon and cost than either alone, with reliability maintained

## Heat pump plus boiler (bivalent heating)

A common and powerful hybrid is a **heat pump with a backup boiler**:
- The heat pump handles the bulk of the year - mild weather, where its COP is high and running cost low
- The boiler (gas or biomass) tops up on the coldest days, when heat pump output and efficiency fall and demand peaks
- The control system chooses the cheaper or cleaner source for the conditions (the "bivalent point")

This lets a smaller, cheaper heat pump cover most of the annual heat (often 80%+) while the boiler covers the rare peaks - avoiding the cost of a heat pump sized for the worst day, and easing retrofit in buildings not ready for full electrification.

## PV plus battery plus grid

On the electricity side, a typical hybrid is **PV + battery + grid**:
- PV generates clean power by day
- The battery stores surplus for evening use and provides backup
- The grid fills any remaining gap and absorbs export

This maximises self-consumption and resilience while keeping the grid as a safety net.

## Renewables plus storage

Hybridising a renewable with thermal or electrical storage smooths its variability:
- Solar thermal + a hot-water store banks daytime heat for evening
- PV + battery shifts midday generation to the evening peak
- Wind + storage captures gusty output for steady use

Storage turns intermittent renewable supply into something closer to dispatchable, greatly increasing its useful value.

## Multi-source systems

Larger sites combine several sources into an integrated system - PV, heat pumps, CHP, solar thermal, and storage - managed by a controller that dispatches the cheapest, cleanest option moment to moment. District energy schemes often blend waste heat, heat pumps, solar, and thermal storage in exactly this way.

## Designing a good hybrid

The art is in the controls and sizing:
- Size each component to its strength (renewable to the base, backup to the peak)
- Set sensible switchover logic (the bivalent point, the battery dispatch strategy)
- Use storage to bridge the gaps
- Let the control system optimise for cost or carbon as priorities dictate

Done well, a hybrid delivers most of the carbon benefit of full renewables with the reliability of conventional supply - often the most practical and economic path, especially in retrofit, on the road to net zero.
