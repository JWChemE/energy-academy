Heat pumps are often listed among renewable technologies, which can puzzle people - they run on electricity, after all. The key is that they *move* far more heat than the energy they consume, harvesting renewable heat from the environment.

## Why a heat pump counts as renewable

A heat pump extracts low-grade heat from the air, ground, or water outside and upgrades it to a useful temperature using a compressor. For each unit of electricity it consumes, it delivers three or four units of heat - the rest comes from the renewable environmental heat it harvests.

This is captured by the **coefficient of performance (COP)**:

COP = heat delivered / electricity consumed

A COP of 3.5 means 1 kWh of electricity yields 3.5 kWh of heat - 2.5 kWh of which is free, renewable environmental heat. That harvested portion is genuinely renewable, which is why regulations classify the renewable share of heat-pump output as renewable energy.

## COP as leverage

The heat pump's power is leverage. Compare ways to heat with electricity:
- **Direct electric heating:** COP of 1 - 1 kWh of power gives 1 kWh of heat
- **Heat pump:** COP of 3-4 - the same 1 kWh of power gives 3-4 kWh of heat

A heat pump delivers three to four times more heat per unit of electricity than a resistance heater. This multiplier is what makes electrified heat affordable and low-carbon.

## The renewable-electricity synergy

Heat pumps become powerfully renewable when paired with clean electricity:
- Run a COP-4 heat pump on grid electricity, and you use a quarter of the energy of direct heating
- Run it on renewable electricity (on-site PV or a clean grid), and the heat is almost entirely low-carbon
- Combine PV with a heat pump and the building can heat itself largely from its own roof

As the grid decarbonises, every heat pump automatically gets cleaner - unlike a gas boiler, which is locked to fossil fuel for its whole life.

## The decarbonisation keystone

Heat pumps are the keystone of heat decarbonisation precisely because of this leverage. They convert each unit of (increasingly clean) electricity into several units of heat, harvesting renewable environmental energy in the process. Electrifying heat with heat pumps - rather than burning fuel - is the central move in almost every credible net-zero pathway for buildings, which is why this "renewable" technology recurs throughout the curriculum.

## Maximising the renewable benefit

To get the most renewable value from a heat pump:
- Design for a high COP - low flow temperatures, good emitters (underfloor, large radiators), well-insulated buildings
- Pair with on-site renewables and time-of-use tariffs
- Use thermal storage to run the heat pump when electricity is cleanest and cheapest

Done well, a heat pump turns a modest amount of clean electricity into the bulk of a building's heat - the essence of why it earns its place among renewables.
