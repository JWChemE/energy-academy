Heat pumps are often listed among renewable technologies, which puzzles people, since they run on electricity. The key is that they *move* far more heat than the energy they consume, harvesting renewable heat from the environment. Understanding why a heat pump counts as renewable, and why it is the keystone of heat decarbonisation, ties this course to the [refrigeration and heat pumps course](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/reverse-cycle) that covers the machines themselves. This lesson covers the renewable logic, COP as leverage, the renewable-electricity synergy, and how to maximise the benefit.

## Why a heat pump counts as renewable

A heat pump extracts low-grade heat from the air, ground or water outside and upgrades it to a useful temperature using a compressor. For each unit of electricity it consumes, it delivers three or four units of heat, and the rest comes from the renewable environmental heat it harvests. This is captured by the **coefficient of performance (COP)** = heat delivered ÷ electricity consumed.

<WorkedExample title="the renewable share of heat-pump output">
  <Given>
    - A heat pump running at a COP of 3.5
  </Given>
  <Find>How much of its delivered heat is renewable.</Find>
  <Solution>
    1 kWh of electricity yields 3.5 kWh of heat, so **2.5 kWh is free, renewable
    environmental heat** harvested from the surroundings, and only 1 kWh came from
    electricity. That harvested portion is genuinely renewable, which is why regulations
    classify the environmental share of heat-pump output as renewable energy. Compare the
    alternatives: **direct electric heating** has a COP of 1 (1 kWh of power gives 1 kWh of
    heat, no harvest), while the heat pump delivers three to four times more heat per unit
    of electricity. That multiplier is what makes electrified heat both affordable and
    low-carbon.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## The renewable-electricity synergy

Heat pumps become powerfully renewable when paired with clean electricity. Run a COP-4 heat pump on grid electricity and you use a quarter of the energy of direct heating; run it on renewable electricity (on-site [PV](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-basics) or a clean grid) and the heat is almost entirely low-carbon; combine PV with a heat pump and the building can heat itself largely from its own roof. And as the grid decarbonises, every heat pump automatically gets cleaner, unlike a gas boiler, which is locked to fossil fuel for its whole life.

<Callout variant="key" title="The keystone of heat decarbonisation">
Heat pumps are the keystone of heat decarbonisation precisely because of this leverage: they convert each unit of increasingly clean electricity into several units of heat, harvesting renewable environmental energy in the process. Electrifying heat with heat pumps, rather than burning fuel, is the central move in almost every credible [net-zero pathway](/courses/renewable-energy/net-zero-pathway) for buildings, which is why this "renewable" technology recurs throughout the curriculum. To get the most renewable value from one: design for a high COP (low flow temperatures, good emitters, a [well-insulated building](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/fabric-performance)), pair it with on-site renewables and time-of-use tariffs, and use [thermal storage](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/dhw-heating) to run it when electricity is cleanest and cheapest.
</Callout>

Done well, a heat pump turns a modest amount of clean electricity into the bulk of a building's heat, the essence of why it earns its place among renewables. The next lessons move from individual technologies to combining them: biomass, hybrids and microgrids.
