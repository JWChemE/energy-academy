A PV array has to connect into the building's electrical system and, usually, the grid. How it integrates - and whether it includes battery storage - determines how much of its value the building actually captures.

## Grid-tied systems

The vast majority of building PV is **grid-tied**: it works in parallel with the mains supply.
- PV supplies the building's load first
- Any surplus exports to the grid
- Any shortfall is drawn from the grid
- When the grid fails, a grid-tied inverter shuts down for safety (anti-islanding) - so without batteries, a grid-tied system gives no power in an outage

This is the simplest, cheapest configuration and suits most buildings where grid power is reliable.

## Self-consumption versus export

The central integration goal is to maximise **self-consumption**, because on-site use is worth two to four times the export rate. Strategies:
- Size the array to the daytime base load
- Shift flexible loads (water heating, EV charging, pumping, cooling) into the sunny midday hours
- Add storage to bank surplus for evening use

A building that uses most of its own generation has a far better return than one exporting heavily at low midday prices.

## Battery storage

A **battery** stores surplus daytime PV for use in the evening peak, sharply increasing self-consumption:
- Typical home/building batteries: 5-30 kWh of usable capacity
- Round-trip efficiency: 85-95% for lithium-ion
- Benefits: more self-consumption, evening peak avoidance, and (with the right inverter) backup power during outages

Batteries add significant cost, so they pay best where the export rate is low, the self-consumption uplift is large, the tariff has a wide peak/off-peak spread, or resilience is valued. They also enable charging from cheap off-peak grid power for arbitrage - covered in the storage module.

## Off-grid and hybrid systems

- **Off-grid:** PV plus substantial battery (and often a backup generator) with no grid connection - for remote sites where connection is impractical
- **Hybrid:** a grid-tied system with battery and the ability to island during outages - increasingly popular for combining cost savings with resilience

## Monitoring and control

Good integration includes monitoring - tracking generation, consumption, self-consumption, and export. This verifies performance, catches faults (a failed string can go unnoticed for months otherwise), and informs load-shifting to lift self-consumption. Smart energy management systems can automatically divert surplus PV to water heating, EV charging, or batteries, squeezing maximum value from every generated unit.
