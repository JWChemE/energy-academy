Batteries store electrical energy for later use, transforming what renewables and smart tariffs can deliver. They are central to maximising self-consumption, shifting demand, and providing resilience, and their falling cost is steadily widening the range of buildings where they pay. This lesson covers what batteries do, the dominant lithium-ion chemistry, the key metrics, the uses for buildings, the economics, and sizing.

## What batteries do

A battery charges when energy is cheap or abundant and discharges when it is expensive or scarce. For a building this means storing surplus [PV](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-integration) generated at midday for use in the evening, charging from the grid on a cheap off-peak tariff and discharging at peak, providing backup power during outages (with suitable inverter capability), and avoiding peak [demand charges](/courses/motors-and-drives/demand-management) by discharging at the daily peak.

## The dominant chemistry: lithium-ion

Most building and home batteries use **lithium-ion**, for its high round-trip efficiency (85 to 95%, so little energy is lost per cycle), high energy density (compact), long cycle life (thousands of cycles), and falling cost. Older **lead-acid** batteries are cheaper up front but have lower efficiency (70 to 80%), shorter life, and limited depth of discharge, now largely confined to niche or off-grid uses. Emerging chemistries (sodium-ion, flow batteries) target longer-duration and grid-scale roles.

## Key metrics and uses

The metrics that matter are **usable capacity** (kWh actually available, less than nameplate because of depth-of-discharge limits), **depth of discharge** (how much can be used without harming life; lithium tolerates 80 to 90%+), **round-trip efficiency** (energy out ÷ energy in; 90% means 10% is lost each cycle), **cycle life** (often 6,000 to 10,000 for lithium), and **power rating** (kW, how fast it charges or discharges, distinct from how much it stores). The building uses follow: **self-consumption** (the main one, banking daytime PV for the evening and lifting the fraction used on site from perhaps 40% to 70 to 80%), **time-of-use arbitrage** (charge off-peak, discharge on-peak), **peak shaving** (cut demand charges), **backup** (ride through outages), and **grid services** (aggregated into [virtual power plants](/courses/renewable-energy/microgrid) for revenue).

<Callout variant="key" title="Batteries pay where the value stacks; size to the real opportunity">
Batteries remain capital-intensive, so they pay best where several values stack: a large self-consumption uplift (lots of PV surplus otherwise exported cheaply), a wide peak/off-peak tariff spread, high demand charges, and real value in resilience. Account for round-trip losses and capacity degradation over life, and appraise with [lifetime NPV/IRR](/courses/economic-analysis/npv-and-irr) rather than simple payback. Then size to the genuine opportunity: enough capacity to capture the typical daily PV surplus or ride the peak-tariff window, with a power rating suited to the loads. Oversizing wastes capital on capacity rarely used; undersizing leaves value on the table. As costs fall and tariffs grow more dynamic, the battery case strengthens year on year, so a system marginal today may be clearly worthwhile within its own lifetime.
</Callout>

Batteries are the electrical companion to the [thermal storage](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/sensible-latent) covered in its own course; together they are what turn variable renewables into a controllable supply. The final lesson sets all of this into a sequenced net-zero pathway.
