Compressed air is the most expensive utility on most sites — typically only about 10% of the electricity that goes into a compressor ends up as useful work at the tool. That makes the compressor house one of the richest places to find savings, if you know how to look. This capstone puts you there as the auditor.

Some of these call-outs you'll crack with a quick sum — a leak load, the cost of a compressor idling, a pressure saving, recoverable heat. Others you reason out from the symptoms. Read the raw panel, work out what the waste is worth, then diagnose the root cause and prescribe the fix.

## How the cases work

Each is a five-step loop:

1. **Investigate** — read the brief and the raw panel readings.
2. **Calculate** — work out the figure that matters (leak flow and cost, unloaded energy, pressure saving, recoverable heat). The **reference data is on hand** — specific power, the pressure rule, heat-recovery and prices — but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose** — pick the root cause(s) from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe** — build an ordered work plan (no-cost first, then capital).
5. **Verify** — score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

## What you'll practise

- **Leaks** — the shutdown audit that turns "the compressor keeps running" into a hard number and a repair programme.
- **Loaded vs unloaded running** — why a fixed-speed compressor on load/unload burns money idling, and what a variable-speed machine saves.
- **Pressure** — the ~7%-per-bar rule, artificial demand, and the cost of a clogged filter forcing the pressure up.
- **Inappropriate use** — spotting the jobs that should never have been on compressed air at all.
- **Heat recovery and sequencing** — reclaiming the ~90% of input that becomes heat, and co-ordinating multiple compressors so one isn't idling.

<Callout variant="key" title="Generate less, at lower pressure, and use it for less">
The compressed-air hierarchy is simple: **fix the demand before you touch the supply.** Repair leaks, stop inappropriate uses, fit zero-loss drains and turn the pressure down to what the tools actually need — then size and control the compressors (VSD, sequencing) to the smaller, steadier demand that's left, and recover the heat. Reaching for a bigger compressor or more pressure to mask a leaky, wasteful system just pays for the waste forever.
</Callout>

## Differential thinking still applies

A compressor that never stops might be leaks — or a machine far too big for the demand, idling on load/unload. Tools starving at the far end might mean the pressure's too low — or a clogged filter dropping a bar before the air even gets there. A steady hiss might be a pipe leak — or a timer drain venting air with every discharge. The decisive reading (the shutdown loaded-time, the unloaded power, the pressure either side of the filters, the hiss at the drains) tells you which.

## After you finish

- Which **single reading** decided each case?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, what goes in the report — and is it a no-cost change, a repair programme, or a capital project that has to earn its payback?
