Compressor choice affects both efficiency and capital cost. The three main types have different energy profiles.

## Reciprocating (piston) compressors

Pistons cycle up/down, drawing air in and compressing it. Simple, robust, effective.

Pros: Cheap upfront, highest pressure (100+ bar possible), reliable.
Cons: Lower efficiency at part-load, more noise, higher maintenance (valve wear).

Efficiency: 70-80% typical.

## Screw compressors

Rotating screws mesh together, progressively compressing air. Smooth, efficient.

Pros: Better part-load efficiency (70-85% across wide range), quieter, less maintenance.
Cons: More expensive upfront, lower max pressure (typically under 13 bar), risk of oil carryover.

Efficiency: 75-85% typical.

## Rotary vane compressors

Sliding vanes in a rotor compress air. Niche use.

Pros: Compact, medium efficiency.
Cons: Wear on vanes, lower efficiency than screw, not as versatile.

Efficiency: 60-75% typical.

## Selection rule

For most facilities: **screw compressor** is best (good part-load efficiency, low maintenance, moderate cost). Reciprocating for extreme pressure or budget constraint. Vane rarely chosen today.

Right-sizing is critical: an oversized compressor running light (50% load) is inefficient. A properly sized screw at 50-80% load is better than an oversized reciprocating at 30% load.
