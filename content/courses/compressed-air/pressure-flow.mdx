One number governs a compressed air system's running cost more than any other, and it is set by a screwdriver: the generation pressure. Every extra bar the compressor must reach costs energy across every cubic metre the site consumes, feeds every leak a little faster, and pushes more air through every unregulated use. Understanding the pressure-energy relationship, and the flow units the trade quotes, is the foundation for the cheapest saving in the whole course.

## The units, quickly

Pressure is quoted in **bar gauge** (bar g): pressure above atmospheric. UK industrial systems conventionally generate at around 7 bar g. Flow is quoted as **free air delivered (FAD)** in litres per second or m³ per minute: the volume the air would occupy at atmospheric conditions, which is the honest way to compare machines and demands. (Older kit and American references use CFM; 1 m³/min ≈ 35.3 CFM.) A compressor's efficiency combines the two as **specific energy**: kWh per m³ of free air delivered, typically around 0.10 to 0.13 at 7 bar for a decent system. Tracking specific energy month by month, exactly as the [M&T course](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/why-mt) tracks any performance indicator, is the single best health metric a system has.

## The 1 bar rule

Compression work rises with the pressure ratio, and near normal operating points a reliable rule of thumb emerges, quoted in Carbon Trust and BCAS guidance alike: **each 1 bar of generation pressure costs roughly 7% of compressor energy**. The rule works in both directions, which is what makes it valuable: most systems run higher than any tool actually requires, usually because pressure was nudged up years ago to mask a problem nobody then diagnosed.

<WorkedExample title="turning the setpoint down">
  <Given>
    - A site generates at 7.5 bar g; a survey finds the highest genuine requirement is 6 bar g, and 6.5 bar g gives a safe margin
    - Compressed air plant: 90 kW average draw, 6,000 h/yr
    - Rule of thumb: ~7% energy per bar; electricity £0.20/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The saving from reducing generation pressure by 1 bar.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Saving ≈ 0.07 × 90 kW × 6,000 h = 37,800 kWh a year, worth about **£7,560**.

    The intervention is a setpoint change: no capital, no downtime, reversible in seconds
    if anything complains. Done properly it follows a survey of what each use actually
    needs, a staged reduction (0.2 bar at a time, a week at each step), and a note in the
    log of which use sets the floor. Few savings in industry are this cheap.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

The rule's corollary: pressure raised to compensate for a problem (a blocked filter, an undersized pipe, a failing regulator) converts that problem into a permanent 7%-per-bar tax. The [pipework lesson](/courses/compressed-air/pipe-sizing) returns to this.

## Pressure also drives demand, not just supply cost

Higher pressure makes every unregulated consumer use more air. Leaks flow faster at higher pressure; open blowing and venting scale similarly; and any tool fed above its rated pressure simply wastes the excess. This effect, called **artificial demand**, means a pressure reduction saves twice: once in compression energy per m³, and again in m³ that were never needed. Systems with large leak loads see the double benefit most strongly, which is why pressure reduction and the [leak programme](/courses/compressed-air/leaks-detection) belong together.

<Callout variant="key" title="Generate at the minimum the neediest genuine use requires">
The design rule for the whole system in one sentence: find the highest pressure any legitimate use actually needs, add the distribution losses between compressor and that use, add a small control margin, and set generation there. Anything above that figure is a recurring bill with no customer. And if one use needs 2 bar more than everything else, the question is not "raise the site" but "give that use its own booster", which is almost always cheaper.
</Callout>
