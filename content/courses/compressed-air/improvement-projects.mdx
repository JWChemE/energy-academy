Compressed air rewards a programme more than any single project. The measures in this course interact: fixing leaks lets the pressure come down, lower pressure shrinks the remaining leaks, better control converts both into electricity not drawn, and metering proves the lot. This closing lesson assembles the course into a sequenced improvement plan, with the numbers from earlier lessons gathered in one place for a reference 90 kW system spending about £108,000 a year.

## Sequence the work so each step funds the next

**First, measure.** Sub-meter the compressor house, establish [specific energy](/courses/compressed-air/pressure-flow) (kWh/m³) as the KPI, and run the shutdown test to size the leak load. This week of work makes every later saving visible and defensible, and it is what the [audit course](/courses/energy-audits/measurement-and-logging) would prescribe anyway.

**Then stop the bleeding (paybacks in weeks to months).** The [leak survey and repair](/courses/compressed-air/leaks-detection) programme; the [demand-side walk](/courses/compressed-air/pressure-regulation) (regulators down, nozzles on blow-offs, air-line misuse eliminated); switching compressors off outside production hours rather than letting them idle against the leak load overnight.

**Then lower the pressure.** With leaks and misuse reduced, the [staged setpoint reduction](/courses/compressed-air/pressure-flow) captures its ~7% per bar across everything that remains, and usually reveals which single use was holding the whole site high.

**Then fix the control (paybacks of one to three years).** A [VSD trim machine or sequencer](/courses/compressed-air/compressor-types) to eliminate unloaded running; [distribution corrections](/courses/compressed-air/pipe-sizing) where drops force compensation; [heat recovery](/courses/compressed-air/heat-recovery) once running hours and a heat demand line up.

<WorkedExample title="the programme, totalled">
  <Given>
    The reference 90 kW system (6,000 h/yr, ≈£108,000/yr), applying this course's worked figures:
    - Leak programme: several finds of the 3 mm class, net ≈ £8,000/yr after repairs
    - Demand side: one open blow-off engineered out, ≈ £15,000/yr of its £20,400 retained after adding a proper nozzle
    - Pressure: 1 bar off the setpoint ≈ £7,560/yr
    - Control: VSD trim vs load/unload ≈ £9,900/yr
    - Heat recovery to the adjacent workshop ≈ £13,300/yr
  </Given>
  <Find>The programme's annual value and scale.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Total ≈ **£53,800 a year**, roughly half the system's bill, though the honest figure
    is lower because the measures overlap (lower pressure shrinks what the leaks and the
    VSD each save). Even discounting interactions heavily, a 30 to 40% reduction is the
    repeatedly observed outcome of a full programme on an unmanaged system, and most of
    it comes from the early, near-free steps.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Keep it saved

The [maintenance lesson](/courses/compressed-air/maintenance) is the guarantee on everything above: leak surveys on the calendar, ΔP-driven filter changes, drains checked, and specific energy trended monthly with an owner, per the [M&T discipline](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/setting-targets). Savings on compressed air do not stay captured by themselves; systems drift back toward waste at a few percent a year unless a number is watching.

<Callout variant="key" title="Demand first, supply second, always">
The strategic order matters more than any individual measure: shrink the demand (leaks, misuse, pressure) before optimising the supply (machines, controls, recovery). Every m³ of demand eliminated saves its full generation cost and shrinks the machine the site needs next time it buys one. Sites that start with a shiny new VSD compressor and skip the demand work buy an efficient machine to feed their leaks.
</Callout>
