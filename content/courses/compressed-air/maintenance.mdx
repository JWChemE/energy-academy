A compressed air system's efficiency is not a fixed property; it is a perishable one. Filters load, separators saturate, coolers foul, drains stick and leaks regrow, and each adds a little to the energy bill without tripping a single alarm. A system left alone drifts upward by percent after percent until, within a couple of years, it costs 20% more than it should for the same air. Maintenance is where that drift is caught, and on compressed air it pays better than almost any other plant-room activity.

## The components that tax the system as they age

**Inlet filters** protect the airend from dust and pay a penalty as they load: restriction at the intake makes the machine work harder for the same output, costing roughly **2% of energy for every 25 mbar of pressure drop** across the element. A differential-pressure indicator, and replacement on ΔP rather than on the calendar, keeps the tax capped. On a 90 kW plant running 6,000 hours, each neglected 25 mbar is about £2,160 a year.

**Oil separators** in screw compressors add back-pressure as they saturate, with the same per-millibar arithmetic, plus oil carryover that fouls everything downstream when they fail. Change at the manufacturer's ΔP limit.

**Coolers** (oil and after-) foul with dust and raise operating temperatures. A hot compressor is less efficient, trips more, ages faster, and delivers warmer, wetter air that the [dryer](/courses/compressed-air/pipe-sizing) must then work harder on. Cleaning cooler cores is cheap and chronically postponed.

**Condensate drains** fail in the expensive direction: stuck open, a drain is simply a leak with a job title. A timer drain jammed open passes air like a 2 mm hole, roughly 1.9 kW of compressor power, about **£2,300 a year** at reference prices. Zero-loss drains eliminate the standing loss and repay their premium quickly on larger systems.

<WorkedExample title="the cost of a year's neglect">
  <Given>
    - A 90 kW system, 6,000 h/yr, £0.20/kWh (annual bill ≈ £108,000)
    - After a year without attention: inlet filters at +50 mbar (≈4%), one drain stuck open (≈1.9 kW), leaks regrown by 5% of capacity (≈4.5 kW)
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual cost of the accumulated drift.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Filters: 0.04 × 108,000 ≈ £4,320. Drain: 1.9 × 6,000 × 0.20 ≈ £2,280.
    Leak regrowth: 4.5 × 6,000 × 0.20 ≈ £5,400.

    Total ≈ **£12,000 a year**, roughly 11% of the bill, from three quiet failures that a
    routine would have caught for a few hundred pounds in parts and hours. Multiply by the
    years since the last proper service to estimate what an unmanaged system is losing.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## A routine that holds the line

The schedule that keeps a system honest is modest. **Weekly**: log discharge pressure, temperature and (if metered) power; glance at drains; listen. **Monthly**: check filter and separator ΔPs, oil level and condition, and walk the highest-risk leak areas. **Annually**: full service to the manufacturer's schedule, cooler cleaning, and the formal [leak survey](/courses/compressed-air/leaks-detection). The single most valuable habit is trending **specific energy** (kWh per m³ delivered): it is the system's vital sign, and a rising trend flags trouble months before anything fails, exactly as an [M&T exception](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/exception-reporting) should.

<Callout variant="key" title="Maintain to a number, not a feeling">
The difference between maintenance that saves energy and maintenance that merely happens is instrumentation: ΔP gauges on filters and separators, hours-run and power logging at the panel, and a specific-energy figure reviewed monthly. Each says precisely when attention pays and when it can wait, converting the schedule from ritual to economics. Ask of any compressed air maintenance contract: which numbers does it read, and who reviews them?
</Callout>
