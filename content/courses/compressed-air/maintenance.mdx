Compressed air systems lose efficiency steadily without maintenance. A neglected system can drift 20-30% above its baseline energy use within a couple of years.

## Where efficiency leaks away

**Air intake filters** clog with dust. A blocked filter forces the compressor to work against extra suction resistance - every 25 mbar of pressure drop across the inlet costs roughly 2% in energy. Replace or clean filters on the manufacturer's schedule (often every 2,000-4,000 running hours).

**Oil separators** in lubricated screw compressors load up over time. A saturated separator adds back-pressure and lets oil carry over into the air, fouling downstream filters. Differential-pressure gauges tell you when to change them - typically at 0.8-1.0 bar drop.

**Coolers and heat exchangers** foul with dust and scale, raising discharge temperature. Hot air holds more moisture and stresses the compressor. Keep aftercoolers clean so approach temperature stays within spec.

## A practical maintenance schedule

| Interval | Task |
|---|---|
| Weekly | Drain condensate traps, listen for leaks, log discharge pressure and temperature |
| Monthly | Inspect intake filter, check oil level and colour, verify drain operation |
| Quarterly | Check belt tension, inspect couplings, review pressure-drop trends |
| Annual | Change oil and separators, service motor bearings, calibrate pressure sensors, full leak survey |

## Why it pays

A compressor running 6,000 hours/year at 50 kW costs around 30,000 GBP/year in electricity at 0.10 GBP/kWh. Restoring lost efficiency through maintenance - even 10% - saves 3,000 GBP/year against a maintenance cost of a few hundred pounds. Maintenance is one of the highest-return activities in the whole plant room.

Log key parameters (pressure, temperature, power) so you can spot gradual drift before it becomes expensive. A rising specific energy (kWh per m3 of air) is the clearest early warning.
