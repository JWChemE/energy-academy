Between the compressor house and the tools sits the demand side of the system: regulators, hoses, nozzles and habits. This is where well-made 7-bar air gets wasted at the point of use, through unregulated drops feeding tools that need far less, open pipes used as brooms, and applications that should never have used compressed air at all. Demand-side discipline costs almost nothing and typically returns several percent of the whole system's energy, and this lesson covers its three fronts.

## Regulate every use to its real requirement

Most pneumatic tools and machines are designed for around 6 bar or less, and many uses (blow-offs, air knives, instrument air) work well below that. Any point of use taking unregulated line pressure consumes more than it needs on every cycle, because flow through a tool or orifice rises with the pressure feeding it. This is the point-of-use face of **artificial demand**, introduced in the [pressure lesson](/courses/compressed-air/pressure-flow): air consumed only because it was offered.

The fix is the humble **filter-regulator** set at each drop, dialled to the use's actual requirement and locked. A walk of the drops with a notebook, recording each regulator's setting against the tool's rating, is a standard audit task; finding half of them wide open at line pressure is a standard result.

## Open blowing: the expensive broom

The costliest habit on most sites is using an open pipe or gun for cleaning, drying and moving swarf. An open orifice flows enormously compared with a working tool, and it tends to be used generously.

<WorkedExample title="the open blow-off, priced">
  <Given>
    - A fixed blow-off is a 6 mm open pipe at 7 bar g, passing ≈ 43 L/s of free air
    - It blows whenever the line runs: 6,000 h/yr
    - Specific energy 0.11 kWh/m³; electricity £0.20/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The compressor power and annual cost behind it.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Power ≈ 0.043 × 0.11 × 3,600 ≈ **17 kW** of compressor output, continuously.

    Annual cost ≈ 17 × 6,000 × £0.20 ≈ **£20,400 for one open pipe**. The engineered
    alternatives are dramatically cheaper: a venturi amplifier nozzle does the same job on
    a fraction of the air, an electric blower does it for perhaps a tenth of the energy,
    and a solenoid interlock makes any remaining blow-off run only while product is
    actually present. Ten-pound nozzles returning four figures a year are the norm here.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## The uses that should not be air at all

Some jobs use compressed air only because a hose was nearby: cabinet cooling that a fan would do at a tenth of the cost, agitation and aeration that a blower serves better, vacuum generation that a dedicated pump beats easily, personnel "dusting down" that is both wasteful and a genuine safety hazard (never permitted; HSE guidance is emphatic). The audit habit is to ask of every air use: *what work is this doing, and what is the cheapest way to do that work?* Air should win only where its portability, safety or power density genuinely matters.

<Callout variant="key" title="Survey the demand side like an asset register">
The demand-side review is a half-day walk that lists every point of use, its purpose, its regulator setting against its real requirement, and whether air is the right utility for the job at all. Its output is a short table of reductions: settings turned down, nozzles fitted, interlocks added, uses converted to electric alternatives. Repeated annually alongside the [leak survey](/courses/compressed-air/leaks-detection), it keeps the demand side honest, and together the two typically hold total system waste to half what an unmanaged site tolerates.
</Callout>
