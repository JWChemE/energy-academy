Compressed air has a fair claim to being the most expensive utility on any industrial site, and the least respected. It is generated from electricity at strikingly poor efficiency, leaks away silently through every worn fitting, and is treated by most of its users as free. An energy manager who understands why air costs what it does holds one of the most reliable saving opportunities in industry, because on a never-examined system, 30% reductions are routine. This lesson establishes the economics everything else in the course builds on.

## Where the electricity goes

Compressing air is thermodynamically expensive. Squeezing air to the standard 7 bar gauge heats it enormously, and nearly all of that heat is removed by the compressor's coolers and thrown away. By the time the air has been compressed, cooled, dried and delivered, only around **10 to 15% of the input electricity remains as useful energy at the tool**. The rest has left as heat.

Put another way: a pneumatic tool doing 1 kW of useful work is drawing roughly 8 to 10 kW at the compressor. Any job that can be done with a direct electric drive instead of an air tool usually should be; air earns its place only where its real virtues (simplicity, safety in wet or explosive atmospheres, power density in small tools) are actually needed.

    are consuming a manufactured product with a real unit price, and every open blow-gun
    and leaking coupling is buying it around the clock.

## Why the waste survives

Three features make compressed air uniquely prone to neglect. It is **invisible**: a leaking pipe drips, but a leaking air line just hisses, and in a noisy plant not even that. It is **unmetered** on most sites: the compressor's consumption hides inside the site's total electricity, so nobody owns the number. And it is **culturally free**: operators who would report a water leak will work next to an air leak for years, because "it's just air". Sector experience, collected in Carbon Trust and British Compressed Air Society guidance, consistently finds 20 to 30% of generated air lost to leaks alone on unmanaged systems, before counting over-pressure, inappropriate uses and idling compressors.

<Callout variant="key" title="Think of the compressor house as a factory making a product">
The mental model that unlocks compressed air savings: the site manufactures a product called "7-bar air" at about £22 per thousand cubic metres, and that product deserves the same management as any other. It should be metered, its unit cost known, its scrap rate (leaks) surveyed, its specification (pressure) no higher than customers need, and its waste heat recovered. Every lesson in this course is one of those disciplines applied to air.
</Callout>
