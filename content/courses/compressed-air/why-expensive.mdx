Compressed air is often called the "most expensive utility" on-site because compressing air to 7 bar requires significant electrical work, yet most facilities leak 20-30% of output undetected.

## The thermodynamics

Compressing air from atmospheric (1 bar) to 7 bar (typical workshop pressure) requires work proportional to pressure ratio. A 50 kW compressor uses ~50 kW electricity to produce ~50 kW of compressed air output (theoretical). In reality, efficiency is 60-80%, so actual input is 60-85 kW.

Compare to other utilities: a boiler at 85% efficiency is excellent. A compressor at 80% efficiency is average.

## Where the waste happens

A small 1 mm pinhole leak at 7 bar loses ~10 CFM continuously. Running 24/7 = 14,400 CFM/day × 365 days = 5.3 million CFM/year = **massive waste**.

Cost: If electricity is £0.10/kWh, that single leak costs ~£5,000/year.

A typical facility with 100+ connection points might have 20-30 leaks totalling 30-50% of compressor output escaping unused.

## Efficiency paradox

While boiler, chiller, and motor efficiency improvements are well-monitored, compressed air efficiency is often ignored:
- No submeter (total consumption is known, but waste is invisible)
- Leaks are silent (unlike HVAC or boiler noise)
- "It's just air" (perceived as low-cost, so waste is tolerated)

Reality: Compressed air can be the largest electrical load after HVAC in a building.
