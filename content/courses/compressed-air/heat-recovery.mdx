Around 90% of the electrical energy a compressor consumes turns into heat. In most plants that heat is simply thrown away through the cooler. Recovering it is one of the best paybacks in compressed air.

## Where the heat goes

For every 100 units of electrical energy into a compressor:
- About 72 units end up in the cooling oil
- About 13 units in the compressed air itself (removed by the aftercooler)
- About 9 units radiate from the compressor body
- Only a few units leave usefully in the air stream

That means roughly 80-94% of the input energy is theoretically recoverable as low-grade heat at 50-90 C.

## Recovery methods

**Air heating (simplest):** Duct the warm cooling air from an air-cooled compressor into an adjacent workshop or warehouse during winter. A damper diverts it outside in summer. Near-zero capital cost, immediate payback.

**Water heating:** A heat exchanger on the oil circuit of a screw compressor produces hot water at 70-90 C for washrooms, process pre-heating, or boiler feed. This is the highest-value option where a hot-water demand exists.

## The economics

A 90 kW compressor running 5,000 hours/year rejects around 360 MWh of heat annually. Recovering even half of that displaces gas heating worth roughly 9,000 GBP/year (at 0.05 GBP/kWh, 85% boiler efficiency). Capital for a water-recovery package is often 5,000-10,000 GBP, giving a payback under 18 months.

## Matching supply to demand

Heat recovery only pays when there is a genuine, time-coincident demand for low-grade heat. A compressor running through summer produces heat nobody needs. The best candidates run year-round and sit near a constant hot-water or space-heating load.
