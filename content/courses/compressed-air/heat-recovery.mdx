Almost every kilowatt a compressor draws becomes heat. Around 90% or more of the input electricity leaves through the oil cooler, the aftercooler and the machine's casing, at temperatures genuinely useful for space heating and hot water. On most sites this heat blows out of a louvre into the sky while a gas boiler heats the building next door. Compressor heat recovery is among the most reliable retrofits in industrial energy management, and this lesson covers where the heat is, the two ways to catch it, and the demand-matching that decides whether it pays.

## Where the heat is

For an oil-injected screw compressor, the energy balance is remarkably consistent: roughly **72% of input power ends up in the cooling oil**, another 13% or so in the compressed air (removed by the aftercooler), a further few percent radiating from the package, and only a small fraction leaving as useful energy in the air itself. Practically, 70 to 90% of input power is recoverable, as warm air at 30 to 40 °C above ambient from the package cooling flow, or as hot water at up to 70 to 90 °C from the oil circuit with a suitable heat exchanger.

## Two recovery routes

**Ducted warm air** is the simple one: air-cooled packages already blow their heat somewhere, and ducting it into an adjacent workshop, warehouse or stores in winter (with a summer diverter flap) needs little more than sheet metal and a damper. Where the compressor house adjoins heated space, this is close to free heating and frequently the fastest payback on the entire site.

**Hot water from the oil circuit** suits sites with a year-round thermal demand: process washing, boiler feed pre-heat, space heating circuits, showers. A plate heat exchanger in the oil loop (factory option on new machines, retrofit kit on most existing screws) delivers water at up to 70 to 90 °C without affecting the compressor's own cooling.

  <Given>
    - A 90 kW compressor; 70% of input is practically recoverable as heat
    - Recovery is useful during the heating season: about 3,000 of its 5,000 running hours
    - Displaces gas heating at 85% boiler efficiency; gas £0.06/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The heat recovered and the annual saving.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Recoverable heat ≈ 90 × 3,000 × 0.70 = 189,000 kWh a year.

    Displaced gas = 189,000 ÷ 0.85 ≈ 222,000 kWh, worth about **£13,300 a year**. Ducting
    and dampers for an adjacent space typically cost a few thousand pounds, giving a
    payback measured in months. Note what did the work in this example: not exotic
    equipment, but the 3,000 hours of coincidence between compressor running and heating
    demand.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Demand matching decides everything

Recovered heat is only worth what it displaces, so the project stands or falls on **coincidence of supply and demand**. Three questions size it honestly: When does the compressor actually run (a summer-only compressor cannot heat a winter building)? What temperature does the demand need (air heating and washwater are easy; anything above ~85 °C is not)? And how far away is it (ducted air travels metres, not buildings; hot water pipes cost money per metre)? The strongest candidates are compressors running long hours beside spaces heated by [gas or electric heating](/courses/boilers-and-fired-systems), or feeding processes with constant hot-water appetite, as in the [brewery sector course](/sectors/breweries).

<Callout variant="key" title="You paid for this heat once already">
The framing that sells the project internally: the site has already bought this energy at electricity prices, roughly three times the gas price per kWh, and is currently throwing it away while buying the same kilowatt-hours again from the boiler. Recovery does not generate anything; it stops a double payment. On the platform's reference prices, every 10,000 kWh of compressor heat put to use against gas heating returns about £700 a year for hardware that mostly already exists.
</Callout>

Recovery closes the supply side of the course. What remains is keeping the whole system at its designed efficiency, which is the maintenance lesson, and assembling everything into a programme, which is the last.
