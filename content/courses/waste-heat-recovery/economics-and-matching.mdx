A waste heat recovery project works when **supply, demand, temperature, and timing all align.**

## The four conditions

**1. Supply exists:** Is there measurable waste heat, continuously or during operating hours?

**2. Demand exists:** Is there a thermal load (heating, cooling, DHW) that can use the heat?

**3. Temperature match:** Is the supply temperature useful for the demand? (You can't heat DHW at 80°C with 30°C waste heat.)

**4. Timing overlap:** Do supply and demand happen at the same time?

## Examples

**GOOD match:** Chiller running (summer, daytime) + domestic hot water demand (summer, daytime). Payback: 2–4 years.

**POOR match:** Industrial process (5–8 PM weekdays) + space heating (overnight, winter). Timing mismatch means recovery equipment idle most of the time. Payback: 15+ years.

## Economics

For a project with:
- Recovery cost: £50,000
- Annual energy recovered: 500 MWh
- Energy cost: £0.05/kWh
- Annual saving: 500 × 0.05 = £25,000
- **Payback: 2 years** ✓

But if annual energy recovered drops to 100 MWh (poor timing):
- Annual saving: £5,000
- **Payback: 10 years** (marginal, maybe not worth it)

**Lesson:** Improve timing match and utilisation to improve payback. Run processes when waste heat is available, or store heat for later use.

## Practical steps

1. Measure waste heat source (temperature, flow rate, hours/year)
2. Identify nearby thermal loads
3. Estimate overlap (hours both are running)
4. Size recovery equipment
5. Calculate payback; proceed if under 5 years (or 8 years if strategic)
