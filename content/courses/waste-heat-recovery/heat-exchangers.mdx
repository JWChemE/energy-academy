A **heat exchanger** transfers heat from one fluid (waste heat source) to another (thermal load). Selecting the right type and sizing it correctly is critical.

## Types of heat exchangers

**Plate (brazed or gasketed):** Two fluids separated by thin metal plates. Pros: compact, high effectiveness, easy cleaning. Cons: lower pressure rating than shell-and-tube. Best for liquid-to-liquid.

**Shell-and-tube:** One fluid in tubes, other in shell around tubes. Pros: robust, handles high pressure/temperature. Cons: larger, lower effectiveness. Best for high-pressure or high-temperature applications.

**Finned tube (air-cooled):** Tubes with fins for air-side heat transfer. Example: HVAC coils. Pros: good for air-side. Cons: fins collect dust (fouling).

## Effectiveness and approach temperature

**Effectiveness (ε):** Fraction of maximum possible heat transfer actually achieved.
- ε = (actual temp rise) / (max possible temp rise)
- Example: waste heat at 100°C, cold inlet at 20°C, exit at 50°C. Max possible rise = 80°C. Actual = 30°C. ε = 30/80 = 37.5%.

**Approach temperature:** The temperature difference between fluid exit and thermal load requirement.
- If DHW needs 60°C and HX can only deliver 55°C (5°C approach), HX is too small or effectiveness too low.

Larger (more expensive) HX increases effectiveness and lowers approach temperature.

## Fouling and maintenance

Dirty heat exchangers lose effectiveness. Water scaling, dust on fins, or corrosion reduce heat transfer.

**Solution:** Regular cleaning (chemical, mechanical, or both). Budget 5% of capital cost per year for maintenance.

## Selection and sizing

1. Define waste heat supply: temperature, flow rate (kg/s or m³/h)
2. Define load demand: inlet temperature, outlet temperature, flow rate
3. Use effectiveness-NTU relationships or software to size HX
4. Specify materials (stainless steel for corrosive, copper-brass for most, titanium for seawater)
5. Account for fouling (design for slightly larger effective area than minimum)
