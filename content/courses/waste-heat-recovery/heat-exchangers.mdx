Every waste heat recovery scheme comes down to one component doing the actual work: a heat exchanger, transferring heat from the waste stream to the load without mixing the two. Choosing the right type and sizing it correctly decides how much of the available heat you actually capture and how close you get to the temperature the load needs. This lesson covers the main types, the two numbers that describe how well an exchanger performs, and the fouling that quietly erodes both.

## The main types

Three families cover most recovery duties, trading compactness against robustness:

- **Plate exchangers** (brazed or gasketed) stack thin metal plates with the two fluids in alternate gaps. Compact, highly effective, and easy to clean (gasketed ones open up), they are the default for liquid-to-liquid duty, limited mainly by pressure and temperature rating.
- **Shell-and-tube** run one fluid through tubes and the other around them in a shell. Bulkier and less effective per unit size, but robust and happy at high pressures and temperatures, so they suit steam, high-grade process heat, and dirty fluids.
- **Finned-tube (air-side)** exchangers add fins to boost heat transfer to or from air, the form of most HVAC coils and [flue economisers](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/boiler-flue-recovery). Effective on the air side, but the fins collect dust and foul.

## Effectiveness and approach temperature

Two numbers describe how good an exchanger is at its job. **Effectiveness (ε)** is the fraction of the theoretically available temperature change that the exchanger actually delivers.

<WorkedExample title="reading effectiveness off temperatures">
  <Given>
    - Waste heat source enters at 100 °C; the cold stream being heated enters at 20 °C
    - The cold stream leaves at 50 °C
  </Given>
  <Find>The exchanger's effectiveness.</Find>
  <Solution>
    The most the cold stream could theoretically be raised is up to the source inlet:
    100 − 20 = 80 °C of potential. It actually rose 50 − 20 = 30 °C.

    Effectiveness ε = 30 ÷ 80 = **37.5%**. Fine for a cheap, small exchanger; a larger
    (more expensive) one would recover more of the 80 °C potential and push the outlet
    higher. Effectiveness is the dial you buy with heat-transfer area, and it trades
    directly against capital, which is why exchangers are sized to the [economics](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/economics-and-matching),
    not to some ideal.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

**Approach temperature** is the gap left between the exchanger's output and what the load actually needs. If the hot water load wants 60 °C and the exchanger can only deliver 55 °C, the 5 °C approach means it is too small or too fouled for the duty. Closing the approach means more area, so there is always a size-versus-performance trade to make.

## Fouling: the slow leak of performance

An exchanger's effectiveness is not fixed; it decays as surfaces foul. Water-side scaling, air-side dust, and corrosion all insulate the transfer surface and cut heat recovery, exactly the mechanism that fouls [condensers](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/maintenance-and-faults) and [compressor coolers](/courses/compressed-air/maintenance) elsewhere. The defences are the same: design for slightly more area than the minimum so there is margin, choose materials to suit the fluid (stainless for corrosive streams, copper-brass for general duty, titanium for seawater), and budget for regular cleaning, roughly 5% of capital a year is a reasonable maintenance provision.

<Callout variant="key" title="Buy effectiveness deliberately, then keep it clean">
An exchanger's effectiveness and approach temperature are design choices bought with area and capital, so size them against the project's economics rather than reaching for the biggest or the cheapest. Then protect what you bought: fouling silently erodes effectiveness over time, so a scheme commissioned at 40% effectiveness can be recovering far less within a couple of years if nobody cleans it. Select for the duty, allow margin for fouling, pick the right material, and maintain it, or the recovered heat quietly drains away through a dirty surface.
</Callout>
