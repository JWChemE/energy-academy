A boiler's flue is the most reliable waste heat source on most sites: hot, continuous whenever the boiler fires, and concentrated in one accessible duct. A conventional boiler sends its combustion gases up the stack at 150 to 200 °C, carrying away energy that was paid for as fuel. Two measures reclaim it: an economiser recovers the sensible heat, and condensing operation goes further by reclaiming the latent heat in the flue's water vapour. This lesson covers both, their economics, and the practical catches that decide whether they work.

## Economisers: the sensible heat

An economiser is a heat exchanger in the flue that transfers the gases' sensible heat to the boiler's feed or return water, cooling the flue and pre-warming the water so the burner has less work to do. It typically recovers around 5 to 6% of fuel input on a non-condensing boiler, and because the source is hot and continuous it is a straightforward retrofit where flue access allows. The [overview lesson's](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/heat-recovery-overview) example priced one at around £1,800 a year on a 500,000 kWh/yr boiler: modest for a small site, and scaling straight up with boiler size.

## Condensing: the latent heat

Cooling the flue far enough (below about 55 °C) condenses the water vapour that combustion produces, releasing its **latent heat**, the same large latent payload that makes [steam](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-basics) such a good heat carrier, around 2,250 kJ per kg of water. Condensing operation recovers roughly 10% of fuel input, more than a plain economiser, and leaves the flue at 50 to 60 °C instead of 180 °C.

    specification now.

## The catches that decide it

Condensing recovery only works if the boiler actually condenses, and two conditions govern that. First, the **return water must be cool enough**, below about 55 °C, or the flue never drops to its dew point and no condensation (hence no latent recovery) happens. This is why condensing boilers pair so well with [low-temperature heating](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/heating-modes) and underfloor systems, and why a condensing boiler feeding an old high-temperature circuit often never condenses and delivers little more than a standard one. Second, the condensate is mildly **acidic** (carbonic acid from dissolved CO₂), so the boiler and flue components in the condensing zone must be stainless steel or acid-resistant, and the condensate must drain safely. Get either wrong and the recovery either fails to happen or corrodes the plant.

<Callout variant="key" title="A condensing boiler that never condenses is just an expensive boiler">
The commonest disappointment in flue recovery is a condensing boiler installed on a heating system whose return water is too hot for it to condense, so it captures the sensible heat any economiser would and none of the latent heat it was bought for. The recovery lives entirely in the return-water temperature: keep it below the flue dew point (favouring lower-temperature emitters and [weather compensation](/courses/control-systems-and-bms/control-strategies)) and the latent heat is yours; let the circuit run hot and the premium is wasted. Specify the recovery and the heating system together, never in isolation.
</Callout>
