You've learned where waste heat comes from, how to grade it, how heat exchangers and recovery devices work, and the four conditions a project must meet to pay. This capstone sends you round a site as the recovery engineer — finding the heat going to waste, sizing what you could get back, and judging whether each project is actually worth doing.

A typical facility loses 20–30% of its fuel as waste heat, so the opportunities are everywhere. But a recovery project only earns when **supply, demand, temperature and timing all align** — and the skill is as much in spotting the bad matches as in sizing the good ones.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate** — read the brief and the panel: source temperatures, flows, effectiveness, sink loads, operating hours.
2. **Calculate** — work out the figure that decides it: the heat rate (Q = ṁ·cp·ΔT, or 1.2·V·ΔT for air), the effectiveness, the recovered energy, and the payback. The **reference data is on hand** — the heat-rate constants, effectiveness, the matching rules and prices — but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose** — pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe** — build an ordered work plan (no-cost/maintenance first, then capital).
5. **Verify** — score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="Supply, demand, temperature, timing">
Finding waste heat is easy; making it pay is about matching. Is there a continuous **supply**? A nearby **demand**? Is the source the right **temperature (grade)** for that sink? And do supply and demand happen at the same **time**? Get all four and the paybacks are often under a year. Miss one — the heat's too cool for the load, or it arrives when nothing needs it — and the best fix may be a thermal store, a reschedule, or simply a lower-grade sink, not the recovery kit itself.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **Sizing the recovery** — heat rate from flow and temperature, for water, air and flash steam.
- **Effectiveness** — what a heat exchanger actually delivers, and spotting a fouled one.
- **The classics** — flue economiser, chiller-condenser recovery, air-to-air (HRV), process-exhaust recovery.
- **The judgment calls** — a timing mismatch that wrecks utilisation, and a temperature mismatch where low-grade heat still earns its keep on preheat.

## Differential thinking

"There's loads of waste heat here" is not a project. A 35 °C source can't feed a 60 °C load no matter how much of it there is; heat available at 5 PM can't serve a load at 2 AM without storage; and an exchanger that used to work but doesn't now is fouled, not undersized. The decisive evidence is the numbers: the grade against the sink, the overlap between supply and demand, the effectiveness against what a clean exchanger should give.

## After you finish

- Which **number** decided each case — the heat rate, the effectiveness, the utilisation, the temperature grade?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, is it a maintenance job (clean the exchanger), a quick capital win (economiser, flash tank, HRV — often under a year), or a project that needs a store or a reschedule before it pays?
