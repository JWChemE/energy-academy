Waste heat recovery has a simple appeal: the energy has already been paid for once, at full fuel price, and recovering it means not buying the same heat again from a boiler. That is why well-matched recovery projects show some of the best paybacks in the whole field, often under two years and sometimes under one. This lesson makes the business case concrete, works a flue-gas example at the platform's reference prices, and sets out the typical paybacks and the practical obstacle (space and integration) that most often complicates them.

## The value of recovered heat

Recovered heat displaces fuel, so its value is the fuel it saves, at the boiler's efficiency. Displacing gas heating at £0.06/kWh and 85% boiler efficiency, every 100 kW of usefully recovered heat running continuously is worth roughly £62,000 a year. The scale is what makes recovery compelling: because the heat is a by-product the site is currently throwing away, almost all of that value is margin.

<WorkedExample title="a boiler-flue economiser">
  <Given>
    - A building burns 500,000 kWh of gas a year in its boiler; gas £0.06/kWh
    - A flue economiser recovers about 6% of fuel input as useful heat (preheating feed or return water)
    - Economiser installed cost ≈ £15,000
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual saving and payback.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Recovered heat ≈ 0.06 × 500,000 = 30,000 kWh a year, worth ≈ **£1,800 a year** in gas
    not burned. Payback ≈ 15,000 ÷ 1,800 ≈ **8 years**. Respectable rather than dramatic,
    which is honest for a modest source: the flue is only a fraction of the fuel, and this
    building is not large. Scale the same measure to a site burning several million kWh, or
    push the recovery deeper with [condensing](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/boiler-flue-recovery),
    and the payback shortens sharply. The lesson is that recovery economics track the size
    and grade of the source, so the biggest, hottest, steadiest sources are where to look
    first.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Typical paybacks

Recovery projects span a wide range, roughly in this order:

- **Steam flash and condensate recovery**: 1 to 3 years (covered in the [steam course](/courses/steam-and-condensate/condensate-return)).
- **Boiler flue condensing/economisers**: 2 to 8 years, depending on source size and how deep the recovery goes.
- **[Chiller condenser recovery](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/chiller-condenser-recovery)**: under 1 to 3 years where a simultaneous heat load exists.
- **[Process exhaust recovery](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/process-heat-recovery)**: often under 2 years on continuous, high-grade sources.

Anything under three years is almost always worth doing; the [economics and matching lesson](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/economics-and-matching) shows how to appraise the rest.

## The practical obstacle: space and integration

The commonest reason a good-on-paper project stalls is physical. Recovery equipment (heat exchangers, ducts, pipework, pumps, controls) has to fit into plant rooms and duct routes that were never designed for it, and connecting it to the existing hydraulic, electrical and control systems adds cost the energy calculation alone misses. This is why proximity between source and sink matters so much: a sink a few metres away is a cheap pipe run, while one across the site can cost more in pipework and pumping than the heat is worth. Realistic projects budget for the integration, not just the exchanger.

<Callout variant="key" title="You already paid for this heat; recovery just stops you buying it twice">
The framing that carries recovery projects through a finance committee: the site is not generating new energy, it is declining to pay twice for energy it already bought. Every kWh recovered and used is a kWh not purchased from the boiler, at full fuel price, which is why even modest recovery from a nearby source pays. The discipline is to chase the sources where the heat is large, warm and steady and the sink is close, because there the "free" energy is genuinely cheap to capture, and to let the space-and-integration cost, not just the energy, decide the marginal cases.
</Callout>
