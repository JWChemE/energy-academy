**Total cost of ownership (TCO)** captures capital, operating, maintenance, and disposal costs over the equipment's lifetime.

## Components

**Capital cost:** equipment purchase price + installation
**Operating cost:** energy (the main driver for most equipment)
**Maintenance cost:** servicing, cleaning, repairs, spare parts
**Replacement cost:** if major components fail mid-life
**Residual value:** scrap/resale value at end of life (negative cost)

## Example: Boiler comparison

**Conventional boiler (85% efficient):**
- Capital: £30k
- Annual fuel: 100,000 kWh ÷ 0.85 = 117,647 kWh × £0.05/kWh = £5,882/year
- Maintenance: £500/year (annual service, minor repairs)
- 15-year TCO: 30k + (5,882 + 500) × 15 − 2k (scrap) = **£97k**

**Condensing boiler (92% efficient, higher capital):**
- Capital: £45k
- Annual fuel: 100,000 kWh ÷ 0.92 = 108,696 kWh × £0.05/kWh = £5,435/year
- Maintenance: £600/year (more frequent flue cleaning, corrosion checks)
- 15-year TCO: 45k + (5,435 + 600) × 15 − 3k (scrap) = **£90k**

**Winner: Condensing boiler saves £7k over 15 years despite 15k higher capital cost.** Simple payback would show: (45k − 30k) / (5,882 − 5,435) = 10k / 447 = 22.4 years (misleading!). TCO shows the true picture: 2.3-year effective payback.

## Application to energy projects

Always ask:
- How long does equipment last?
- What does maintenance cost?
- Can components be refurbished or reused?
- What's the residual value?

A high-efficiency, low-maintenance option often wins TCO even if capital cost is higher.
