**A pound today is worth more than a pound tomorrow.** This is the cornerstone of financial decision-making.

## Why?

Because money today can be invested and earn a return. If you invest £100 at 5% interest, you have £105 in one year. So £100 today and £105 in one year are equivalent (at 5% discount rate).

Working backwards: if you're promised £105 in one year, and you require a 5% return, that's worth £100 today.

## Present value and discount rate

**Present Value (PV)** = Future Value / (1 + discount rate)^years

Example: Money in year 3 at 5% discount rate:
- PV = £1,000 / (1.05)^3 = £1,000 / 1.1576 = £864

So £1,000 in 3 years is worth £864 today (at 5% discount rate).

## Discount rate (hurdle rate)

Your **discount rate** is your required rate of return. For a company, it might be:
- Cost of borrowing (5–8%)
- Cost of capital (weighted average of debt and equity costs)
- Minimum required return (company policy, 8–12%)
- Risk-adjusted: a risky project might require 12%+

A typical energy project might use 6–10%.

## Why this matters for energy decisions

A 20-year boiler retrofit with savings of £5k/year is NOT worth £100k (20 × 5). Discounting that:
- At 5% rate: PV = £5k × 12.46 = £62,300
- At 10% rate: PV = £5k × 9.19 = £45,950

The same project is worth very different amounts depending on your discount rate and how you account for time.

## Real vs nominal

- **Nominal**: doesn't account for inflation (5% discount rate in a world with 2% inflation)
- **Real**: accounts for inflation (5% − 2% = 3% real return)

Energy savings typically grow with inflation (fuel prices rise). Using a real discount rate and real savings is more rigorous.
