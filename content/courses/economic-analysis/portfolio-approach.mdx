A real energy programme is not one project but a pipeline of them, competing for the same limited capital and management attention. Ranking that pipeline purely by [NPV](/courses/economic-analysis/npv-and-irr) treats every pound of value as identical, when in practice two projects with the same NPV can carry very different risk, deliver very different side-benefits, and fit the organisation's strategy in very different ways. The portfolio approach is how an energy manager sequences a whole programme so it manages cash, controls risk, and keeps momentum. This closing lesson covers the three factors NPV misses and the sequencing that follows from them.

## Risk and certainty

Two measures can share an NPV while one is a sure thing and the other a gamble. An LED retrofit's saving is close to arithmetic: proven technology, a wattage difference you can measure, savings that will materialise. An advanced controls upgrade with the same modelled NPV depends on commissioning quality and, worse, on operators not overriding it, so its real saving spans a wide range. Portfolio thinking prices that difference by holding uncertain projects to a **higher hurdle rate**: perhaps 8% for a proven measure, 12% or more for a behavioural or novel one. The [audit course's confidence grading](/courses/energy-audits/identify-opportunities) (measured, calculated, or literature-based) feeds directly into this, and the [measurement and verification](/courses/measurement-and-verification) plan is what converts an uncertain project into a bankable one over time.

## Co-benefits and strategic fit

Energy savings are often not a project's only, or largest, benefit. LED lighting cuts maintenance because lamps last far longer; controls and insulation improve comfort and reduce complaints; a modern boiler is more reliable than the thirty-year-old one it replaces; and every kWh saved cuts carbon that a [SECR or net-zero commitment](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/carbon-budgets) has to account for anyway. A measure with a modest energy IRR but strong co-benefits may rightly beat a narrowly higher-IRR project that does nothing else. Strategic fit matters too: some projects unlock others (a condensing boiler enables low-temperature heat that later suits a [heat pump](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/heating-modes)), and those enabling moves earn priority beyond their standalone numbers.

## Sequencing the programme

The practical output is a phased plan that lets early wins fund later ambition:

- **Phase 1: quick, certain wins.** Short-payback, low-risk measures (controls tuning, LED, leak repair). These build cash, credibility, and the track record that makes the next phase fundable.
- **Phase 2: medium-risk, medium-payback.** VFDs, [heat recovery](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/economics-and-matching), condensing conversions, part-funded by Phase 1's savings.
- **Phase 3: strategic capital.** Fabric, renewables, electrified heat: long paybacks justified by co-benefits and net-zero alignment rather than energy IRR alone.

This is the same sequence the [audit report](/courses/energy-audits/audit-report) recommends and for the same reason: a programme that self-finances and demonstrates results keeps getting approved, where a flat list of everything at once gets deferred.

<Callout variant="key" title="NPV ranks projects; judgement sequences a programme">
NPV is the right tool for choosing between two projects, but a programme is more than a sorted list. It has to stay within this year's cash, survive the failure of any single measure, and hold management's confidence long enough to reach the big, strategic projects at the end. Sequencing quick certain wins first is not financially optimal on a spreadsheet; it is what keeps the whole programme alive to deliver the later value. Manage the portfolio, not just the projects.
</Callout>

That completes the toolkit: payback to screen, discounting and NPV/IRR to decide, TCO and life-cycle costing to see the true cost, financing to pay for it, and the portfolio view to sequence the whole. The [diagnostics capstone](/courses/economic-analysis/diagnostics-capstone) puts a competing set of real measures in front of you and asks you to build the programme.
