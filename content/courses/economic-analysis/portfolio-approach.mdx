Not all projects are created equal. A **portfolio approach** considers risk, certainty, co-benefits, and strategic fit alongside financial metrics.

## Beyond NPV

Two projects with the same NPV may have very different characteristics:

**Project A:** LED retrofit, 50k GBP, 20-year savings guaranteed (technology proven), 15% IRR.
**Project B:** Advanced controls, 50k GBP, savings depend on commissioning and operator behaviour, 15% IRR, but high uncertainty.

Which is better? Same NPV, but A is safer. Portfolio thinking chooses A.

## Risk and certainty

- **High certainty:** LED, boiler replacement, insulation. Technology proven, savings quantifiable. Invest confidently.
- **Medium:** VFD on pump, condensing conversion. Proven tech, but site-specific variation.
- **Low:** Behavioural (occupancy controls, awareness), novel tech (not widely deployed). Savings may not materialise.

Assign different hurdle rates: maybe 6% for high-certainty, 10% for low-certainty projects.

## Co-benefits

Some projects have benefits beyond energy savings:
- **Comfort:** insulation and ventilation reduce drafts and improve air quality
- **Safety:** better lighting, safer equipment
- **Reliability:** modern boilers are more reliable than 30-year-old ones
- **Emissions:** carbon reduction (co-benefit of efficiency)

A lower-IRR project with strong co-benefits might be chosen over a higher-IRR but narrow project.

## Strategic alignment

- Is the project part of a net-zero roadmap?
- Does it enable future projects (e.g., condensing boilers enable low-temp radiant heating)?
- Does it improve marketability (green building credentials)?

## Portfolio sequencing

- Phase 1: High-certainty, short payback (quick wins, build confidence)
- Phase 2: Medium-risk, medium payback (fund from Phase 1 savings)
- Phase 3: Strategic, longer payback (co-benefits justify lower IRR)

This approach manages cash, reduces risk, and keeps momentum.
