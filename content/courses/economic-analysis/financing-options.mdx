A project worth doing still has to be paid for, and how it is funded changes both its true cost and whether it happens at all. Many good efficiency measures die not because they fail the [NPV test](/courses/economic-analysis/npv-and-irr) but because there is no capital in the budget this year. Understanding the funding routes, and their real costs, is what turns a sound business case into an installed project. This lesson covers the four main options and how to compare them.

## The four routes

**Cash** is simplest: no interest, no paperwork, outright ownership. Its cost is the opportunity cost, the other projects that capital could have funded, and its limit is obvious, since a fixed budget funds only so much before it runs out.

**A bank loan** spreads the cost over time so more projects can run in parallel, at the price of interest.

<WorkedExample title="what a loan really costs">
  <Given>
    - £50,000 borrowed at 7% over 5 years, repaid monthly
  </Given>
  <Find>The monthly payment and total interest.</Find>
  <Solution>
    The monthly payment works out to about **£990**, so total repaid ≈ £990 × 60 ≈
    **£59,400**, of which about **£9,400 is interest**. Borrowing added roughly 19% to the
    headline cost. That is worth paying whenever the project's [IRR](/courses/economic-analysis/npv-and-irr)
    comfortably exceeds the 7% loan rate (as most efficiency measures do) and the freed
    capital lets you run other worthwhile projects at the same time. Debt is leverage:
    good projects multiply it, marginal ones are sunk by it.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

**Leasing** hands you the equipment for a fixed monthly fee without owning it. It converts capital expenditure into a predictable operating cost, useful when capital is scarce or when the technology dates quickly, but a lease typically costs more over its term than buying outright, and you own nothing at the end.

**A performance contract (an ESCO arrangement, or in the UK often an Energy Performance Contract)** is the route for organisations with no capital and low appetite for risk. An Energy Services Company designs, funds and installs the improvements, and is repaid from the verified savings, frequently with a guaranteed minimum: if the savings fall short, the ESCO makes up the difference. You take little up-front risk and share the savings rather than keeping them all, and the contract can run ten to fifteen years. The saving verification is done to a formal standard, usually [IPMVP](/courses/measurement-and-verification), because the payments depend on it.

## Choosing between them

The comparison is not "which is cheapest in total" but "which fits the organisation's capital, risk appetite and portfolio". Cash suits a well-funded body doing a handful of high-return measures. A loan suits an organisation with a pipeline of good projects and access to cheap borrowing, wanting to do several at once. A lease suits fast-moving technology or genuinely capital-constrained budgets. An ESCO suits large, complex retrofits where the organisation wants the savings guaranteed and the risk transferred, and is willing to share the upside to get both.

<Callout variant="key" title="The right question is 'what unlocks the project', not 'what is cheapest'">
On a strict pounds basis, cash usually beats borrowing beats leasing beats an ESCO, because each route in turn buys something (parallelism, flexibility, risk transfer) with margin. But a project financed at a small premium and actually built beats a cheaper one that never gets funded. When capital is the binding constraint, the financing question is really "which route gets this saving installed this year", and a good measure funded by an ESCO still returns far more than the same measure sitting unbuilt in a business case. Match the route to the constraint, not to a spreadsheet ideal.
</Callout>

Financing decides how individual projects get paid for. The final lesson steps up to the whole pipeline: how to prioritise across many competing measures when NPV alone does not settle it.
