The gap between purchase price and lifetime cost is easiest to feel through worked examples, so this lesson takes two of the most common energy decisions, a lighting choice and a pump choice, and follows the money all the way through. In both, the option that looks expensive on the shelf is dramatically the cheaper one to own, and in both, simple payback and life-cycle costing tell such different stories that the contrast is the lesson.

## Example 1: LED versus halogen, over the life

<WorkedExample title="the £45 bulb that saves hundreds">
  <Given>
    - A fitting runs 8 hours a day (≈ 2,920 h/yr); electricity £0.20/kWh
    - Halogen lamp: £5, 60 W, lasts ~2,000 hours (roughly one year here)
    - LED lamp: £50, 10 W, lasts ~50,000 hours (well beyond the 15-year horizon)
  </Given>
  <Find>The 15-year cost of each, per fitting.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Energy per year: halogen 60 W × 2,920 h = 175 kWh ≈ £35; LED 10 W × 2,920 h = 29 kWh
    ≈ £6.

    Over 15 years the halogen also needs replacing roughly every year (≈ 15 lamps, £75),
    so its total ≈ £75 + £35 × 15 = **£600**. The LED needs one lamp: £50 + £6 × 15 =
    **£140**. The LED costs ten times as much to buy and saves about **£460 per fitting**
    over its life. Multiply by a few hundred fittings and the LED scheme is one of the
    largest, safest savings any building holds.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

Simple payback here is about four months, so this is a case where payback and life-cycle costing happen to agree: the project is a screaming yes on any metric. The life-cycle view adds the scale of the prize (the £460 that keeps accruing long after payback), which is what justifies doing the whole building at once rather than one failed lamp at a time.

## Example 2: variable-speed pump versus fixed pump and valve

Here payback and life-cycle costing agree on the direction but the life-cycle number reveals just how large the stakes are, because the [cube law](/courses/motors-and-drives/why-vfds) makes the running-cost gap enormous.

<WorkedExample title="the drive that saves more than it costs, many times over">
  <Given>
    - A pumping duty averages 50% flow; electricity £0.20/kWh; 6,000 running hours a year
    - Fixed pump throttled by a valve: £5,000 capital, draws ≈ 30 kW throughout (the valve wastes the surplus)
    - Variable-speed pump: £15,000 capital, power follows flow³, so ≈ 30 × 0.5³ ≈ 3.75 kW; £200/yr extra maintenance
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual running cost of each, and the 15-year totals.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Running cost: fixed ≈ 30 kW × 6,000 h × £0.20 = **£36,000/yr**; variable ≈ 3.75 kW ×
    6,000 h × £0.20 = **£4,500/yr**. The drive cuts the bill by £31,500 a year.

    15-year TCO: fixed ≈ £5,000 + £36,000 × 15 = **£545,000**; variable ≈ £15,000 +
    (£4,500 + £200) × 15 = **£85,500**. The drive costs £10,000 more up front and about
    **£460,000 less to own**. Payback on the premium is under four months. The cube law,
    not the price tag, decides this, and the price tag pointed the wrong way.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

<Callout variant="key" title="When running cost dominates, buy efficiency and never look back">
Both examples share one shape: a large annual running cost and a small capital difference, so the efficient option wins by a landslide over any realistic life. That shape describes most energy decisions, which is why "high capital for high efficiency" is the default that pays, and why life-cycle costing, not the purchase order, should hold the pen. The rare exceptions (very low running hours, genuinely short equipment life) are exactly the cases the [portfolio lesson](/courses/economic-analysis/portfolio-approach) flags for a second look.
</Callout>

Knowing a project is worth doing is only half the decision; the other half is how to pay for it, which the next lesson takes up.
