You've learned the tools of project appraisal, simple payback and its limits, the time value of money, NPV and IRR, total cost of ownership, financing options, and portfolio thinking. This capstone puts you in the role of the person who decides: turning the numbers into a defensible yes, no, or "this one first".

The thread through every case: **a pound today is worth more than a pound tomorrow.** Screen with payback if you like, but *decide* with NPV, IRR and whole-life cost, and don't be fooled by a dazzling IRR on a tiny project or a scary payback on a long-lived one.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate**: read the brief and the panel: costs, savings, lives, rates, annuity factors.
2. **Calculate**: work out the figure that decides it: the payback, the present value, the NPV, the risk-adjusted comparison. The **reference data is on hand**: payback, time value, NPV/IRR and annuity factors, TCO, financing, portfolio, but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose**: pick what the numbers really say.
4. **Prescribe**: make the call: proceed, accept, pick the higher-NPV project, finance it, or sequence it.
5. **Verify**: score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="Screen with payback, decide with NPV">
Simple payback is a fine first filter, but it ignores the time value of money, the project's life and its residual value, so it over-rewards short-life quick wins and wrongly rejects long-life winners. For the real decision, discount the cash flows: positive NPV means accept; IRR above your hurdle means accept; between competing projects, the highest NPV wins (not the highest IRR). Then look past the spreadsheet to financing and risk.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **Payback**: as a screen, and where it misleads.
- **Time value & NPV**: discounting savings with annuity factors, and the accept/reject rule.
- **IRR**: versus the hurdle rate, and why it flatters small projects.
- **Whole-life cost**: why a long payback can still be a strong NPV.
- **Financing & portfolio**: loan vs ESCO, and pricing risk to sequence projects.

## Differential thinking

The same project can look like a yes or a no depending on the lens. A high IRR can lose to a lower-IRR project with a bigger NPV; a 10-year payback can hide a +£16k NPV; a worthwhile project can stall for want of capital that a loan would happily provide; two equal NPVs can be very different bets once you price the risk. The decisive evidence is the discounted numbers and the risk behind them: not the headline payback or IRR.

## After you finish

- Which **number** decided each case: the NPV, the IRR margin, the present value, the risk-adjusted NPV?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** without the hints?
- For each, what would you actually recommend to the board, and could you defend it against "but the payback is too long" or "but the IRR is higher"?
