Net present value and internal rate of return are the two numbers a finance director actually expects to see on a capital request, and between them they answer the two questions payback cannot: does this project create value, and what return does it earn? Both are built directly on the [discounting](/courses/economic-analysis/time-value-of-money) from the previous lesson. This lesson defines them, works the example that catches a payback-approved loser, and settles when to use which.

## Net present value: does it create value?

**NPV** is the sum of every future cash flow, each discounted to today, minus the up-front cost. The decision rule is as simple as it is powerful:

> NPV positive → the project earns more than your discount rate → do it.
> NPV negative → it earns less → reject it, however tempting the payback looked.

<WorkedExample title="the 10-year payback that destroys value">
  <Given>
    - A boiler upgrade costs £40,000 and saves £4,000 a year for its 15-year life
    - The organisation's discount rate (hurdle rate) is 8%
    - Annuity factor, 15 years at 8%: 8.56
  </Given>
  <Find>Simple payback, NPV, and the verdict.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Simple payback = 40,000 ÷ 4,000 = **10 years**. On a 15-year life that might scrape
    through a payback screen.

    NPV: present value of savings = £4,000 × 8.56 ≈ £34,240; minus the £40,000 cost gives
    **NPV ≈ −£5,760**. The project *destroys* about £5,800 of value. The savings, spread
    over fifteen years and discounted at 8%, never add up to the £40,000 they cost.

    Payback said "acceptable"; NPV says "reject". NPV is right, because it counts the time
    value payback ignores. This is the exact trap the [payback lesson](/courses/economic-analysis/simple-payback-revisited)
    warned about, now shown in numbers.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Internal rate of return: what return does it earn?

**IRR** is the discount rate at which NPV comes out exactly zero, so it is the project's own built-in rate of return, expressed as a percentage. The rule mirrors NPV:

> IRR above your hurdle rate → accept. IRR below it → reject.

The boiler above earns an IRR of about **5.6%**: below the 8% hurdle, which is simply another way of seeing the negative NPV. A strong efficiency measure looks very different: the £15,000 VFD saving £12,000 a year from the [motors course](/courses/motors-and-drives/why-vfds) has an NPV near £88,000 at 8% and an IRR well above 50%. IRR appeals to finance because it speaks their language: "this project returns 50%" lands harder than "its NPV is £88,000".

## Which to use

The two usually agree, and where they diverge, **NPV wins** because it measures value in pounds rather than a percentage. Two cases show why. When projects differ in size, IRR can flatter the small one: a 60% return on £5,000 (NPV maybe £4,000) is worth less in absolute terms than a 20% return on £100,000 (NPV maybe £40,000), yet IRR ranks them backwards; for mutually exclusive choices, pick the highest NPV. IRR also misbehaves arithmetically when a project has unusual cash-flow patterns (a mid-life replacement cost can produce two IRRs or none). Use IRR to communicate and to test against a hurdle; use NPV to choose.

<Callout variant="key" title="Screen on payback, decide on NPV, communicate with IRR">
The three tools are a division of labour, not rivals. Payback filters the pipeline in seconds. NPV makes the actual accept-or-reject and rank decisions, in pounds, accounting for time. IRR carries the result into the finance conversation as a single familiar percentage. A capital request that shows all three, with the [savings themselves verified](/courses/measurement-and-verification) rather than assumed, is one a finance function can approve with confidence.
</Callout>
