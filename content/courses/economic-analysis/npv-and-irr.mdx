**NPV** and **IRR** are the rigorous tools for comparing energy projects.

## Net Present Value (NPV)

**NPV = sum of all discounted future cash flows − initial cost**

Example: 50k GBP boiler retrofit, 5k GBP savings/year for 15 years, 5% discount rate:
- Year 0: -50k
- Year 1: 5k / 1.05 = 4,762
- Year 2: 5k / 1.0576 = 4,530
- ... (and so on)
- Total discounted cash flows: ~62.3k

NPV = 62.3k − 50k = **+12.3k**

**If NPV > 0, the project earns more than your discount rate and should be done.** If NPV < 0, reject it.

## Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

**IRR is the discount rate at which NPV = 0.** In other words, it's the project's true rate of return.

For the boiler above, if NPV = 0 at 8% discount rate, then IRR = 8%.

**Decision rule: If IRR > hurdle rate, accept the project.**

If your hurdle rate is 6% and IRR is 8%, the project earns 2% more than required — worth doing.

## NPV vs IRR

**Use NPV when:**
- Comparing projects of different sizes (NPV shows absolute gain, IRR can mislead)
- Projects are mutually exclusive (pick the highest NPV)
- Discount rate is known and fixed

**Use IRR when:**
- Comparing projects on equal footing (both must exceed hurdle rate)
- Uncertainty about discount rate (IRR is independent)
- Reporting to finance (IRR feels familiar to investors)

**Caution:** IRR assumes reinvestment at the IRR rate, which may not be realistic. NPV is more theoretically sound.

## Typical energy project IRR

- LED retrofit: 50–100%+ IRR (payback under 1 year)
- VFD retrofit: 15–30% IRR (payback 2–4 years)
- Boiler replacement: 8–15% IRR (payback 5–10 years)
- Building insulation: 5–10% IRR (payback 10–20 years)

If your hurdle rate is 6%, all these are worthwhile (except possibly insulation in cooler climates).
