Simple payback, the project cost divided by its annual saving, is the most-used number in energy management, and for good reason: everyone understands it, it needs no assumptions about interest rates, and it sorts obvious winners from obvious losers in seconds. It is also the number that quietly kills good projects and waves through bad ones, because it ignores everything that happens after the money comes back. This lesson covers where payback earns its place and where it misleads, so you know when to reach for the heavier tools in the rest of the course.

## What payback does well

Payback answers one question honestly: how long until I get my money back? For screening, that is often enough. A measure repaying in under two or three years is almost always worth doing, and no amount of discounted-cash-flow arithmetic will change that conclusion, so running it would be wasted effort. Payback also communicates: a board that glazes over at "net present value" understands "it pays for itself in eighteen months" immediately. The [audit course](/courses/energy-audits/identify-opportunities) uses payback as its working currency for exactly these reasons.

The rule of thumb most practitioners carry: under three years, just do it; over seven years, look harder before committing; between three and seven, the fuller analysis earns its keep.

## Where payback misleads

The metric has one fatal blind spot: it stops counting the moment the cost is recovered, so it is completely blind to what a project does over its life.

<WorkedExample title="two projects, same payback, very different value">
  <Given>
    - Project A: an LED lighting scheme, £6,000, saving £3,000 a year, 15-year life
    - Project B: a niche control gadget, £6,000, saving £3,000 a year, but obsolete and scrapped after 3 years
  </Given>
  <Find>What payback says, and what it misses.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Both show a **2-year simple payback**, so payback rates them identically.

    Yet Project A goes on to save £3,000 a year for thirteen more years after paying for
    itself (roughly £39,000 of pure benefit); Project B recovers its cost just as its life
    ends and delivers almost nothing beyond. Payback saw two identical projects; they are
    worlds apart. The blind spot is always the same: **payback ignores project life**, and
    life is where the value lives.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

Payback's other omissions compound this. It treats a pound saved in year ten as equal to a pound saved in year one, which the [time value of money](/courses/economic-analysis/time-value-of-money) shows is wrong. It ignores residual value, rising fuel prices, and the pattern of savings over time. For a quick win none of that matters; for a large, long-lived capital decision, all of it does.

<Callout variant="key" title="Use payback to screen, not to decide the big ones">
The discipline is to match the tool to the stake. Screen every opportunity on payback: it is fast and it is right about the extremes. But once a measure is large, long-lived, or competing against other calls on the same capital, payback has told you all it can, and the decision moves to [NPV and IRR](/courses/economic-analysis/npv-and-irr). The classic expensive mistake is a "10-year payback on a 15-year boiler" that looks acceptable on payback and turns out, on the numbers, to destroy value. The next two lessons build the tools that catch it.
</Callout>
