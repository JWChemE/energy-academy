**Simple payback** — cost divided by annual savings — is the most-used energy metric. It's simple, but limited.

## When payback works

Payback is useful for:
- Quick screening: "Will this project pay for itself in under 5 years?"
- Comparing projects of similar size and lifetime
- When all you need is a rough answer
- Building intuition: "£10k cost, £2.5k/year savings = 4-year payback"

## When payback misleads

Payback ignores:
- **Time value of money** — it treats £1 saved in year 1 the same as year 10 (wrong)
- **Project lifetime** — a 5-year payback on a 20-year project is great; on a 5-year project, you've only just recovered your cost
- **Inflation and interest rates** — over 10+ years, these matter a lot
- **Residual value** — does the equipment have scrap value? Can it be reused?

## Example: LED vs halogen

- Halogen: £50 upfront, 60W, 2,000-hour life (~3 years). Cost: 50 GBP + (60 W × 3 yr × operating hours × £/kWh) = ~£200 total
- LED: £200 upfront, 10W, 50,000-hour life (~15 years). Cost: 200 GBP + (10 W × 15 yr × operating hours × £/kWh) = ~£250 total

Simple payback: (200 − 50) / (annual energy saving) = only 1–2 years. But the LED's **true value** is that it lasts 5× longer and uses 85% less energy over 15 years. Life-cycle costing captures this; simple payback doesn't.

## Rule of thumb

Use payback for screening. If payback < 3 years, the project is almost certainly worth doing. If payback > 7 years, be careful — use NPV to verify. For 3–7 years, NPV and IRR give you the full picture.
