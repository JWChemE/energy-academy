Dairy heat recovery starts from an unusual position: the sector's biggest recovery device is already installed. Every pasteuriser's regeneration section recovers ninety-odd percent of the largest thermal duty on site, which means the first job is not adding recovery but *defending* what exists, because regeneration degrades silently and expensively. Around that core sit the [foundations course's recurring matches](/courses/food-drink-foundations/heat-recovery-patterns), with dairy-specific streams: evaporator heat, cow water, and an ammonia plant rejecting heat beside a CIP system that buys it. This lesson works through the recovery hierarchy as a dairy auditor should.

## First, defend the regeneration

The [heat lesson](/courses/dairies/pasteurisers-and-uht) made the case; here is the audit practice. Regeneration effectiveness is computed from four temperatures the plant already measures (raw milk in, regenerated milk out, and the hot side's pair): log them, trend effectiveness monthly, and treat drift as a maintenance trigger. The failure is gradual (milk fouling, tired gaskets) and invisible to operations, because the product still reaches its validated temperature while the hot section silently absorbs the difference.

<WorkedExample title="eight silent points, priced">
  <Given>
    - A 20,000 L/h HTST line, total duty 1,473 kW, designed at 92% regeneration
    - Temperature logging finds 84%; the plant runs 4,000 h/yr; gas via an 85%-efficient hot section at £0.06/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The cost of the drift, and the value of restoring it.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Top-up at 92%: 8% × 1,473 ≈ 118 kW. At 84%: 16% × 1,473 ≈ 236 kW. The drift is
    **117.9 kW**, which over 4,000 hours is ≈471,500 kWh of heat, ≈554,700 kWh of gas:
    **£33,280 a year**.

    A plate-pack refurbishment (clean, re-gasket) at £15,000 pays back in **under six months**,
    and the monthly effectiveness trend then catches the next drift at one point instead of
    eight. This exact scenario is the first of this course's [diagnostic cases](/courses/dairies/two-quick-diagnostics).
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

The same defence applies to every regenerative exchanger on site: UHT preheaters, CIP recovery sections, cheese-vat heat exchangers. In a dairy, "heat recovery maintenance" outranks "heat recovery investment".

## Then, harvest the dairy's own streams

With regeneration defended, work the [matching method](/courses/food-drink-foundations/heat-recovery-patterns) down the site's streams:

- **Refrigeration desuperheat → CIP and washdown water.** The sector's standard pairing, with a dairy twist: the ammonia plant runs hardest exactly when milk flows, and CIP wants its heat a few hours later, so a preheat storage tank bridges the gap. Sizing and payback mirror the [food course's worked case](/courses/food-manufacturing/heat-recovery-in-food).
- **Evaporator condenser heat and condensate.** On powder sites the evaporator rejects large, clean, steady heat, and its condensate (cow water) leaves warm: boiler feed and CIP make-up are the natural sinks, as the [whey lesson](/courses/dairies/whey-and-coproducts) set out.
- **Pasteuriser cooling relief.** Remember that every recovered hot-side kilowatt at the regenerator is also a cold-side kilowatt the [ice water](/courses/dairies/ice-water-and-refrigeration) never has to remove: heat recovery in a dairy pays at both ends of the thermometer.
- **The boiler-house standards.** [Blowdown and condensate return](/courses/boilers-and-fired-systems/blowdown) apply unchanged, and older dairies usually have both to claim.

<Callout variant="key" title="In a dairy, maintenance is the biggest heat-recovery project">
The sector installed its great recovery device decades ago and then, on most sites, stopped watching it. Before any capital proposal, measure what the existing regeneration actually achieves against its design: the gap, priced at the hot section, is routinely the largest single finding in a dairy audit, and the fix is a service, not a project. Defend first, then extend.
</Callout>
