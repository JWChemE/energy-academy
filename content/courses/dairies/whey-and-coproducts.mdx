Make a kilogram of cheese and roughly nine litres of whey appear beside it, whether you wanted them or not. For most of the industry's history whey was a disposal problem, and in energy terms it still shapes cheese-site economics more than any machine on the floor: sent to drain it is catastrophically expensive effluent, processed it becomes valuable product at a real energy cost, and either way the site's numbers make no sense until whey is accounted for. This lesson covers the whey decision, the co-product logic behind it, and the sector's other hidden stream: the "cow water" that evaporators recover from the milk itself.

## Whey as effluent: the numbers that force the issue

The [foundations course](/courses/food-drink-foundations/trade-effluent-and-water) established strength-based charging; whey is its extreme case. Raw whey's organic strength (COD) runs to tens of grams per litre, on the order of a hundred times stronger than domestic sewage, so a cheese site casually draining whey would face effluent charges rivalling its energy bill, consent breaches, or both. That arithmetic, plus the value of what whey contains, is why essentially no UK site of scale drains it today: whey is collected, chilled and processed, and the only question is how far up the value chain.

## Whey as product: energy spent on purpose

Whey is mostly water carrying protein and lactose, so adding value means removing water, and the [previous lesson's ladder](/courses/dairies/evaporation-and-drying) applies verbatim. Ultrafiltration splits a protein concentrate (WPC, the sports-nutrition staple); reverse osmosis pre-concentrates; evaporation and spray drying finish powders. A cheese site that installs whey processing deliberately *increases* its energy per litre of milk while transforming its economics: new revenue, effluent charges collapsed, and (with membranes and MVR) water removed at the cheap end of the ladder. The energy manager's role is not to lament the new load but to benchmark it honestly: whey processing is a second production line, and per-litre figures that ignore it condemn the site for succeeding, the same allocation discipline the [benchmarking lesson](/courses/dairies/kwh-per-litre) demanded.

<WorkedExample title="what draining whey would cost, versus processing it">
  <Given>
    - A cheese site produces 40,000 L of whey a day, 300 days a year
    - Drained: strength charges at a plausible ~£3 per m³ of strong whey effluent (site-specific Mogden terms), plus consent risk
    - Processed: RO + evaporation removes ~35 t of water/day at an MVR-era ~£3/t, yielding saleable protein and lactose
  </Given>
  <Find>The comparison that decides the whey question.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Draining: 40 m³/day × £3 × 300 ≈ **£36,000 a year in effluent charges alone**, before the
    consent headaches, and every gram of protein and lactose thrown away with it.

    Processing: ~35 t/day × £3 × 300 ≈ **£31,500 a year of removal energy**, spent to *create*
    product revenue that typically dwarfs both figures. The energy bill rises; the business
    case is overwhelming. Whey is the sector's clearest demonstration that energy per litre
    is meaningless without asking what the litres became.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Cow water: the stream hiding in the milk

Evaporate milk and the water has to go somewhere: it condenses out of the evaporator as **condensate**, universally nicknamed *cow water*, warm, low-mineral, and produced in volumes that rival the site's mains supply. Powder sites treat and reuse it: as boiler feed (it arrives warm and soft, both virtues the [steam course](/courses/steam-and-condensate/condensate-return) prices), as CIP make-up and pre-rinse, and for cooling-tower duty, cutting mains water purchases, effluent volumes and water-heating gas simultaneously. Its warmth is also a textbook [foundations heat-recovery stream](/courses/food-drink-foundations/heat-recovery-patterns). On a large powder site, cow water can supply a substantial share of total water demand: the dairy literally drinks its own raw material.

<Callout variant="key" title="Follow the streams, not just the meters">
Whey and cow water are the sector's reminder that a dairy's balance is a mass balance before it is an energy one: litres in become product, co-product, condensate and effluent, and each destination carries its own energy and cost signature. A cheese or powder audit that stops at the gas and electricity meters misses the streams that decide them, which is exactly the [system-boundary thinking](/courses/mass-and-energy-balances/system-boundaries) this platform builds everything on.
</Callout>
