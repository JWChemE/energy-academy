## How the stages work

This capstone follows the same staged format as the [Energy Audits course's own capstone](/courses/energy-audits/field-audit-capstone), because a dairy audit *is* an energy audit: this course, and the [Food & Drink Foundations](/courses/food-drink-foundations/the-food-and-drink-sector) beneath it, supply everything sector-specific on top of that general method:

1. **Plan & Scope**: choose the audit level, draw the boundary where the sector's biggest footprint isn't (the farms stay outside), and plan hygiene inductions, the ammonia plant room and the overnight CIP shift before you walk in.
2. **On-Site**: the plant-room observations that matter in a dairy, plus twelve months of intake and utility data, spring flush included, with one figure missing for you to derive.
3. **Analyse & Normalise**: build the intake-and-weather models, compute the per-litre figures the CCA return needs, and prove with the model that May's extra gas was the cows' doing, not the plant's.
4. **Opportunities**: quantify the regeneration restoration and the ice-bank window from this course's diagnostics, met here on a real site, and keep every recommendation on the right side of the food-safety floors.
5. **Report & M&V**: what the report owes the CCA, the retailers and the board, and why per-litre claims that ignore the flush get unpicked by the first technical reviewer they meet.

<Callout variant="key" title="The flush is the examiner">
This capstone's data contains a 21% seasonal intake swing, and every stage tests whether you let it fool you: the model must carry an intake term, the benchmark must be read through it, the savings must be verified against an adjusted baseline, and the retailer summary must never claim spring's arithmetic as achievement. Handle the flush honestly and the rest of the audit is the method you already know.
</Callout>
