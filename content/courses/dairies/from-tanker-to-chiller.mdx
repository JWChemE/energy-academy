Milk is unlike any other raw material in the food and drink sector: it arrives already cold, already perishable, in tankers whose schedule the cows set rather than the factory, and it is 87% water that some sites will later spend fortunes removing. A dairy's energy story follows that milk from the reception bay to the chiller, and it varies more between dairy *types* than almost any other sector's does between whole industries. This lesson walks the milk through the plant, introduces the three site types whose energy profiles differ by an order of magnitude, and meets the seasonal rhythm (the spring flush) that every dairy model must respect.

## From tanker to chiller

This course assumes the [Food & Drink Foundations](/courses/food-drink-foundations/the-food-and-drink-sector): the HACCP floors, CIP and effluent rules apply here with full force. What is dairy-specific is the sequence:

1. **Reception.** Tankers discharge into raw-milk silos. The milk arrives at around 4 °C from farm bulk tanks and must be held cold: reception's energy is pumping plus silo refrigeration, modest but continuous.
2. **Separation and standardisation.** Centrifugal separators split cream from skim, and the two are recombined to the target fat content. Separators are serious electrical machines, running whenever milk flows.
3. **Homogenisation.** Milk is forced through a valve at 100 to 250 bar to break up fat globules: a high-pressure pump, and one of the site's larger point electrical loads.
4. **Heat treatment.** Pasteurisation (or UHT, or sterilisation): the thermal heart of the plant, and the [next lesson's](/courses/dairies/pasteurisers-and-uht) subject.
5. **Filling and packing.** Bottles, cartons and poly-bottles, with the packaging hall's usual [utilities tier](/courses/food-manufacturing/utilities-and-packaging).
6. **Cold chain to despatch.** Everything ends cold: chilled storage, chilled despatch, and the [ice-water systems](/courses/dairies/ice-water-and-refrigeration) that dominate the electrical base.

Around the milk runs the sector's standard second shift: nightly [CIP on every circuit](/courses/food-drink-foundations/cip-and-washdown), harsher in dairies than almost anywhere because milk fouls hot surfaces aggressively (protein and mineral deposits, the industry's "milk stone"), which is why dairy CIP recipes run hot and frequent, and why the [foundations' effluent lesson](/courses/food-drink-foundations/trade-effluent-and-water) applies with such force here.

## Three site types, three energy worlds

**Liquid milk dairies** (this course's running example) pasteurise, standardise and bottle. Their energy is comparatively modest per litre, split roughly evenly between gas (heat treatment top-up, CIP) and electricity (refrigeration, homogenisers, packing), with an efficient site processing a litre for well under a tenth of a kilowatt-hour.

**Cheese and fermented-product sites** add cheese vats (heat), long chilled maturation (electricity) and, above all, **whey**: for every kilogram of cheese, around nine litres of whey appear, and what a site does with it transforms both its energy and [effluent economics](/courses/dairies/whey-and-coproducts).

**Powder and ingredients sites** remove the water: evaporators and spray dryers turn milk or whey into powder, and in doing so consume energy per litre that liquid sites never approach. The [evaporation lesson](/courses/dairies/evaporation-and-drying) is devoted to why, and to the technology ladder that tames it.

The audit consequence is blunt: **never compare kWh per litre across site types.** The denominators match; the physics does not.

## The spring flush

Cows do not produce milk evenly. UK milk production peaks in April and May (fresh spring grass) and troughs in late autumn: a national swing of 15 to 25% that arrives at the dairy's reception bay whether the dairy likes it or not. A site processing 21% more milk in May than February will burn more gas and electricity in May with nothing running worse, and less in November with nothing running better.

<Callout variant="key" title="Model the flush or be fooled by it, twice a year">
The spring flush makes intake-normalised analysis non-negotiable in this sector: every spring flatters raw per-litre figures (fixed loads spread over more milk) and every autumn damns them, on a cycle the cows control. A dairy's energy model needs an intake term before anything else, exactly as this course's capstone will make you build, and any year-on-year claim that ignores the flush will be unpicked by the first competent reviewer it meets.
</Callout>
