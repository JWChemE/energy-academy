An organisation's energy and carbon impact extends through its supply chain, and its purchasing power is a lever for change. Setting supplier criteria - using procurement to require energy and carbon performance from suppliers - extends energy management beyond the organisation's own walls.

## Procurement as leverage

An organisation buys not just energy but goods, equipment, and services - all of which carry embedded energy and carbon (the scope 3 link). Its purchasing decisions therefore influence energy use far beyond its own operations:
- The efficiency of equipment it buys determines future energy costs
- The carbon performance of suppliers contributes to its supply-chain emissions
- Its requirements can drive suppliers to improve

This makes procurement a powerful lever - the organisation can use its buying power to require, reward, and drive energy and carbon performance throughout its supply chain (the policy supply-chain link).

## Whole-life cost, not purchase price

The foundational principle of energy-conscious procurement is buying on **whole-life cost**, not just purchase price:
- The cheapest equipment to buy is often expensive to run
- Energy-using equipment's lifetime energy cost can dwarf its purchase price
- Buying on purchase price alone systematically favours inefficient equipment

This addresses the **split-incentive** problem (the buildings and policy links): when the party buying equipment is not the party paying to run it, purchase price wins and efficiency loses. Procuring on whole-life cost - capital plus running cost over the equipment's life - corrects this, favouring efficient equipment that costs more upfront but far less over its life. Embedding whole-life costing in procurement is one of the highest-impact energy decisions an organisation can make.

## Energy and carbon criteria in purchasing

Beyond whole-life costing, organisations can build energy and carbon criteria into procurement:
- **Efficiency standards** for purchased equipment (minimum efficiency ratings)
- **Energy performance requirements** in specifications and tenders
- **Carbon criteria** in supplier selection (favouring lower-carbon suppliers and products)
- **Reporting requirements** - asking suppliers to disclose their energy and carbon performance

These criteria turn procurement from a cost-only exercise into one that drives energy and carbon outcomes - using the organisation's spending to pull the market toward efficiency and low carbon.

## Setting supplier expectations

For larger organisations especially, procurement can set expectations that drive supplier change:
- **Requiring** suppliers to meet energy and carbon standards
- **Requesting** suppliers measure and report their emissions
- **Favouring** suppliers with strong environmental performance in selection
- **Collaborating** with key suppliers on reduction
- **Setting** supplier codes of conduct including energy and carbon

The leverage depends on the organisation's buying power - a large buyer can require, a smaller one can favour and encourage (the policy supply-chain link). Either way, building energy and carbon into supplier selection signals that these matter and rewards suppliers who perform.

## Sustainable procurement policy

These practices are formalised in a **sustainable procurement policy** that embeds energy and carbon into how the organisation buys (the policy link):
- Whole-life costing as standard for energy-using purchases
- Energy and carbon criteria in tenders and supplier selection
- Supplier reporting and standards
- Alignment with the organisation's broader energy and carbon goals

This connects procurement to the organisation's energy strategy and policy, ensuring that buying decisions support rather than undermine its energy and carbon objectives. Without it, procurement defaults to lowest purchase price, quietly working against the organisation's efficiency goals.

## Driving market change

The collective effect of energy-conscious procurement extends beyond any single organisation:
- When buyers demand efficiency, suppliers compete to provide it
- When carbon performance affects sales, suppliers reduce their emissions
- Large buyers can shift entire markets toward efficiency and low carbon

This is procurement's wider power: aggregated across many buyers, energy and carbon criteria in purchasing drive the whole market toward better performance. The organisation's purchasing is not just an internal cost decision but a contribution to broader change (the net-zero link) - the demand side pulling the market toward efficiency and low carbon.

## Integrating with procurement practice

Realising this requires integrating energy and carbon into actual procurement processes (the next module on execution):
- Building criteria into tender documents and evaluation
- Training procurement staff to consider whole-life cost and carbon
- Working across the finance-procurement-operations boundary (the cross-functional link)
- Measuring and tracking supplier and product performance

Done well, supplier criteria turn the organisation's purchasing power into a force for energy and carbon improvement, extending energy management through the supply chain and into the market - a strategic dimension of procurement that reaches far beyond simply buying energy itself.
