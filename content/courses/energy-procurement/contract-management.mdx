Securing a good energy contract is only half the job - the value must be delivered and protected over the contract's life. Contract management, billing validation, and supplier-relationship management ensure the organisation actually receives what it negotiated.

## Why contract management matters

A well-negotiated contract delivers no value if it is not managed:
- Suppliers make billing errors (often in their favour)
- Consumption and costs drift without monitoring
- Contract terms go unenforced
- Renewal deadlines pass, defaulting to expensive out-of-contract rates

Active contract management protects the value secured in procurement, catches errors and overcharges, and ensures the organisation gets what it agreed. It is the often-neglected final stage that determines whether procurement's value is actually realised.

## Billing validation

A core contract-management task is **validating bills** - checking that what is charged matches what was agreed and consumed:
- **Rates** - are the contracted prices being applied correctly?
- **Consumption** - do the billed volumes match metered usage (the monitoring link)?
- **Charges** - are all the components (standing charges, demand charges, levies) correct?
- **Errors** - billing mistakes are common and often favour the supplier

Energy bills are complex (the tariff link), and errors are frequent. Systematically validating bills against the contract and metered consumption catches overcharges and errors - often recovering significant sums. This is one of the most reliably valuable contract-management activities.

## Monitoring consumption and cost

Contract management connects to the monitoring and targeting discipline (the M&T link):
- Tracking actual consumption against forecast and budget
- Identifying anomalies (unexpected rises - the link to faults and waste)
- Verifying that efficiency projects are reducing demand as planned (the M&V link)
- Managing demand to control costs (peaks, time-of-use - the tariff link)

Ongoing monitoring ensures the organisation understands and controls its actual energy use and cost throughout the contract, not just at the point of purchase. It links procurement to the wider energy management system.

## Managing demand against the contract

The contract and the consumption interact, and managing demand can capture value:
- **Demand charges** - managing peaks to reduce capacity charges (the tariff and storage links)
- **Time-of-use** - shifting consumption to cheaper periods
- **Volume tolerances** - staying within contracted volume bands to avoid penalties
- **Flexibility** - using demand flexibility to manage cost and exposure (the microgrid link)

Active demand management, informed by the contract structure, turns the contract from a passive arrangement into one whose terms are actively worked to the organisation's advantage.

## The supplier relationship

Beyond transactions, managing the supplier relationship adds value:
- **Communication** - resolving issues, addressing problems
- **Service** - ensuring good billing, account management, and support
- **Collaboration** - working with the supplier on efficiency, flexibility, and green options
- **Performance** - holding the supplier to service standards

A constructive supplier relationship can yield better service, smoother problem resolution, and better terms over time. While competition drives the headline deal, the ongoing relationship determines the quality of service and the ease of managing the contract.

## Renewal management

A critical, easily-missed contract-management task is managing renewal:
- **Tracking expiry dates** - so contracts do not lapse into expensive default rates
- **Starting early** - beginning the next procurement in good time to negotiate from strength (the tendering link)
- **Avoiding rollover** - automatic renewals or out-of-contract rates are typically expensive
- **Timing the market** - renewing when conditions are favourable (the risk link)

Letting a contract lapse into out-of-contract or deemed rates is a common, costly error. Diligent renewal management - tracking dates and starting the next procurement early - avoids this and ensures each contract is replaced with another competitively procured one.

## Closing the procurement loop

Contract management closes the procurement cycle that this course has traced: understanding tariffs, choosing contracts, managing risk, procuring clean energy, forecasting demand, tendering and negotiating, and finally managing the resulting contracts. Run well as a continuous cycle - not a periodic scramble at renewal - it ensures the organisation buys energy wisely, manages its price risk, supports its clean-energy and carbon goals, and captures the full value of every contract throughout its life.

## Procurement in the bigger picture

Energy procurement connects to the whole of energy management:
- It interacts with **efficiency** (using less reduces what must be bought and the exposure to price - the risk link)
- It supports **clean-energy and net-zero goals** (renewable procurement, PPAs - the net-zero link)
- It involves **cross-functional work** (finance, procurement, operations - the leadership link)
- It draws on **monitoring and verification** (validating bills, tracking consumption)

Buying energy well is not an isolated administrative task but an integral part of managing energy strategically - controlling a major cost, managing a business risk, and advancing the organisation's clean-energy ambitions. Mastered alongside the efficiency, strategy, and leadership disciplines of this academy, effective procurement completes the energy manager's command of both the demand and the supply sides of the organisation's energy.
