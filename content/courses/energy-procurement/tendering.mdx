With demand understood, the organisation must select suppliers and agree terms. The tendering and negotiation process - how energy is actually bought - determines the price and terms secured, and is where preparation translates into value.

## The procurement process

Buying energy involves a structured process:
1. **Define requirements** - the volume, profile, contract type, and any green requirements (the forecasting and contract links)
2. **Approach the market** - inviting suppliers to bid (tendering)
3. **Evaluate offers** - comparing on price, terms, service, and green credentials
4. **Negotiate** - improving terms and resolving details
5. **Award and contract** - selecting the supplier and finalising the agreement

A disciplined process - rather than simply renewing with the incumbent or accepting the first offer - is what secures competitive terms. Energy is a major cost, and a rigorous procurement process repays the effort.

## Tendering

**Tendering** invites suppliers to compete for the business:
- A clear specification of requirements (volume, profile, contract type, green needs)
- An invitation to multiple suppliers to bid
- A defined timeline and process
- Transparent evaluation criteria

Competition is the buyer's friend - inviting multiple suppliers to bid drives better prices and terms than accepting a single offer. The clarity of the specification matters: a well-defined tender gets comparable, competitive bids; a vague one gets inconsistent offers that are hard to compare.

## Using brokers and consultants

Many organisations use energy brokers or procurement consultants:
- They bring market knowledge, supplier relationships, and timing expertise
- They run the tendering and negotiation process
- They can access better terms through scale and expertise
- But they charge fees (sometimes built into the price) and their incentives must be understood

Brokers can add value, especially for organisations lacking in-house expertise, but the energy manager should understand how they are paid (commission can create conflicts of interest) and ensure their incentives align with the organisation's. Transparency on broker fees is important.

## Evaluating offers

Comparing supplier offers requires looking beyond the headline price:
- **Price** - but on a like-for-like basis (all components, the same volume and profile - the tariff link)
- **Contract terms** - the type, flexibility, and conditions (the contract-types link)
- **Service** - billing quality, account management, support
- **Green credentials** - the renewable backing, if required (the clean-energy link)
- **Supplier reliability** - financial stability and reputation

The cheapest headline price may not be the best deal once terms, service, and green credentials are considered. A structured evaluation against clear criteria ensures a fair comparison and a sound choice.

## Negotiation

Even after tendering, negotiation can improve the outcome:
- **Leverage** - competition between suppliers, and the organisation's volume (especially aggregated - the forecasting link)
- **Timing** - buying when market conditions are favourable (the risk link)
- **Terms** - negotiating not just price but flexibility, service, and conditions
- **Relationships** - a constructive supplier relationship can yield better terms over time

Preparation underpins negotiation: knowing the market, the organisation's demand and flexibility, and the alternatives gives the buyer a strong position. The work done in forecasting, understanding tariffs, and assessing risk all pays off here, in the strength it lends to the negotiation.

## Timing the market

Energy prices move, so timing matters (the risk-management link):
- Buying when the market is favourable secures better prices
- Flexible procurement spreads purchases to average out timing risk
- Market awareness informs when to fix and when to wait

Timing is part art, part discipline - no one consistently calls the market perfectly, which is why spreading purchases (the flexible approach) manages timing risk. But awareness of market conditions, and avoiding buying at obvious peaks, improves outcomes.

## Securing value

The goal of the whole process is securing the best value - not just the lowest price, but the best combination of price, terms, service, and (where required) green credentials, at an acceptable risk. This comes from:
- Thorough preparation (forecasting, market awareness)
- Genuine competition (tendering multiple suppliers)
- Rigorous evaluation (beyond headline price)
- Effective negotiation (leveraging position and timing)
- A deliberate balance of cost, risk, and other objectives

Done well, the procurement process turns energy buying from a passive renewal into an active pursuit of value - securing better terms on a major cost. The final lesson turns to managing the contracts once they are won, ensuring the value secured in procurement is actually delivered.
