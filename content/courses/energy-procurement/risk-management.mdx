Energy prices are volatile, and that volatility is a business risk. Managing energy price risk - protecting against damaging rises while not forgoing all opportunity - is a core part of procurement strategy, drawing on the contract types and active management.

## Energy price risk

Energy prices can move sharply and unpredictably, driven by:
- Fuel prices (gas, in particular, drives electricity prices)
- Weather (demand for heating and cooling)
- Geopolitical events and supply disruption
- Market dynamics and speculation

For an organisation with significant energy costs, this volatility is a real financial risk - a price spike can blow a budget or erode margins. Managing this risk is as important as managing the average cost, and is a distinct discipline within procurement.

## The goal: managing, not eliminating

Risk management does not mean eliminating all exposure (which usually means paying a premium for total certainty) - it means managing exposure to an acceptable level:
- Protecting against damaging price rises
- Not forgoing all opportunity to benefit from falls
- Matching the residual risk to the organisation's appetite and ability to bear it

The aim is a deliberate, managed risk position, not accidental exposure. An organisation might accept some volatility for lower average cost, or pay for more certainty if its budget cannot absorb spikes - the choice should be conscious.

## Hedging

**Hedging** is the core technique - taking positions that offset price risk:
- **Fixing** part or all of the price (via fixed contracts) locks in certainty for that portion
- **Buying forward** - purchasing energy in advance at agreed prices (the flexible-contract tranches)
- **Financial hedges** - instruments that offset price movements
- **Collars** - capping the maximum price while retaining some downside benefit (the contract-types link)

Hedging trades away some potential benefit (from price falls) in exchange for protection (against rises). The art is hedging enough to protect the organisation without over-paying for certainty it does not need.

## Spreading purchases over time

A key risk-management technique (central to flexible procurement) is spreading purchases across time:
- Buying energy in tranches at different times averages out price movements
- It avoids the risk of committing the whole volume at a single (possibly unfavourable) moment
- It reduces the impact of any single price point

This "averaging" approach reduces the risk of bad timing - rather than betting everything on one purchase, the organisation buys steadily, achieving a price closer to the average and avoiding the worst outcomes of mistiming.

## Diversification

Risk can also be managed by diversification:
- **Across time** - spreading purchases (as above)
- **Across contract types** - blending fixed and flexible
- **Across suppliers** - not depending on a single counterparty
- **Across fuels and sources** - including on-site generation and renewables (reducing market exposure - the renewables link)

A diversified position is more resilient to any single risk than a concentrated one. On-site renewable generation, in particular, reduces exposure to bought-energy price volatility - a risk-management benefit beyond its direct savings.

## Efficiency as risk reduction

A fundamental point that connects procurement to the whole curriculum: **using less energy reduces price risk**. The less energy an organisation buys, the less exposed it is to price volatility:
- Efficiency reduces the volume exposed to the market
- It cushions the impact of price spikes
- It is a hedge that also saves money (unlike financial hedges, which cost a premium)

This means energy efficiency is not just a cost saving but a risk-management strategy (the investment-case risk link). The most robust protection against energy price risk is needing less energy in the first place - making the technical efficiency work of the whole academy a procurement risk strategy too.

## A risk-management framework

Effective energy price risk management involves:
- **Assessing** the organisation's exposure and risk appetite
- **Setting a policy** for how much risk to bear and how to manage it
- **Implementing** through the right mix of contracts, hedging, and timing
- **Monitoring** the market and the position
- **Reviewing** and adjusting as conditions change

This framework turns risk management from reactive guesswork into a deliberate strategy, aligned with the organisation's appetite and integrated with its broader energy and procurement goals. Combined with the contract choices and the clean-energy procurement of the following lessons, it is what allows an organisation to buy energy not just cheaply but wisely - managing the volatility that makes energy cost a business risk.
