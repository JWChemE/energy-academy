The power purchase agreement is the principal way organisations buy renewable electricity at scale - a long-term contract that secures clean power, often at a stable price, while supporting new renewable generation. PPAs are central to corporate clean-energy procurement.

## What a PPA is

A **power purchase agreement (PPA)** is a long-term contract to buy electricity, typically from a specific renewable generator (a wind or solar farm), usually at a fixed or formula-based price over many years (often 10-15 years or more).

It connects an energy buyer directly with a renewable generator, providing:
- The buyer: long-term, often price-stable, renewable electricity
- The generator: the long-term revenue certainty needed to finance and build the project

PPAs are how many large organisations source significant renewable power and back their clean-energy commitments with real, additional generation.

## Why organisations use PPAs

PPAs offer several attractions:
- **Clean energy at scale** - sourcing large volumes of genuinely renewable power
- **Price stability** - a fixed or predictable price over a long term, hedging against market volatility (the risk-management link)
- **Additionality** - often supporting new renewable projects that would not otherwise be built, giving a credible claim to genuine impact (the credibility link)
- **Cost** - potentially competitive pricing, especially as renewables have become cheap
- **Strategic alignment** - backing net-zero and sustainability commitments with substance (the net-zero link)

The combination of clean energy, price stability, and credible impact makes PPAs powerful for organisations serious about both cost and carbon.

## Physical and virtual PPAs

PPAs come in two main forms:

**Physical PPA:** the electricity is physically delivered to the buyer (directly or through the grid). Suited to buyers who can take the power, often near the generator or through a supplier sleeving arrangement.

**Virtual PPA (financial PPA):** a financial contract that settles the difference between an agreed "strike" price and the market price, without physical delivery. The buyer continues to take grid power as normal but is financially hedged and credited with the renewable attributes. Suited to buyers wanting the price hedge and green credentials without the physical delivery complexity - common for organisations with many sites.

The choice depends on the buyer's structure, location, and objectives.

## Sleeved PPAs and suppliers

A **sleeved PPA** involves the buyer's energy supplier "sleeving" the renewable power from the generator to the buyer - managing the physical delivery, balancing, and any shortfall:
- The buyer gets the renewable power delivered through their supplier
- The supplier handles the complexity of matching generation to demand
- The buyer pays the generator (via the PPA) plus the supplier's sleeving fee

This makes physical PPAs practical for organisations that cannot directly take generation, bridging the generator and the buyer through the supplier relationship.

## Additionality and credibility

A key value of PPAs is **additionality** - the principle that the procurement supports *new* renewable generation that genuinely adds clean capacity to the system, rather than just relabelling existing power:
- A PPA that enables a new wind or solar farm to be built has real, additional impact
- This gives a far more credible clean-energy claim than simply buying certificates for existing generation (the next lesson)
- It connects the organisation's demand directly to new renewable supply

Additionality matters increasingly as stakeholders scrutinise clean-energy claims for genuine impact (the credibility link). A PPA backing new generation is among the most credible ways to claim renewable energy use.

## Challenges and considerations

PPAs are powerful but complex:
- **Long-term commitment** - contracts of a decade or more require confidence in future demand
- **Complexity** - negotiating and managing a PPA needs expertise (often specialist advice)
- **Risk** - generation is variable (weather-dependent), and price/volume risks must be managed
- **Scale** - traditionally suited to large buyers, though aggregation is opening PPAs to smaller organisations
- **Creditworthiness** - generators need confidence the buyer will honour the long contract

These mean PPAs require careful structuring and expertise, but for organisations with the scale and commitment, they are a leading route to credible, cost-effective renewable energy.

## PPAs in the clean-energy strategy

PPAs sit at the heart of serious corporate clean-energy procurement, complementing on-site generation (the renewables course) and green tariffs and certificates (the next lesson). For an organisation pursuing net zero, a PPA backing new renewable generation provides large-scale, price-stable, credibly additional clean power - a cornerstone of decarbonising purchased electricity (scope 2 - the net-zero link). Understanding PPAs is essential to procuring clean energy at the scale and credibility that ambitious carbon commitments demand.
