Before you can buy energy well, you must understand how it is priced. Energy tariffs are more complex than a single price per unit, and understanding their structure is the foundation of effective procurement.

## Energy is not one price

A common misconception is that energy has a single price. In reality, an energy bill is built from several components, and the way they are structured affects both the total cost and how the organisation can reduce it. Understanding these components is the starting point for buying energy intelligently.

## The components of an energy bill

A typical electricity bill comprises:
- **Unit rate (commodity cost):** the price per kWh of the energy itself - often the largest component, and the part exposed to wholesale market prices
- **Standing charge:** a fixed daily charge regardless of consumption
- **Capacity / demand charges:** charges based on peak demand (kW or kVA), not just total energy - significant for larger users
- **Network charges:** the cost of transmitting and distributing the energy
- **Policy costs and levies:** charges funding government energy and environmental programmes
- **Taxes:** such as the Climate Change Levy and VAT

Each component behaves differently and offers different opportunities to reduce cost. The commodity cost is reduced by buying well and using less; demand charges by managing peaks; some levies by qualifying for exemptions or efficiency schemes.

## Wholesale and retail

The **commodity** portion of the price derives from the **wholesale energy market**, where energy is traded:
- Wholesale prices vary continuously with supply and demand
- They can be volatile, driven by fuel prices, weather, and market conditions
- The retail price the organisation pays reflects the wholesale cost plus the supplier's costs and margin

How an organisation buys (the contract type - the next lesson) determines how much of this wholesale volatility it is exposed to. This is a central procurement decision.

## Time-of-use pricing

Increasingly, prices vary by time:
- **Time-of-use tariffs** charge different rates at different times (peak, off-peak)
- **Half-hourly settlement** for larger users prices each half-hour according to the market and network costs
- **Dynamic tariffs** track the wholesale market closely, varying continuously

Time-varying prices create both risk (exposure to expensive peak periods) and opportunity (shifting consumption to cheaper times - the link to demand flexibility and storage). Understanding the time structure of the tariff reveals these opportunities.

## Demand and capacity charges

For larger users, **demand charges** based on peak power (kW/kVA) can be a substantial part of the bill, distinct from the energy consumed:
- They charge for the peak demand the organisation places on the network
- They can be reduced by managing and reducing peaks (peak shaving - the storage and flexibility links)
- They make the *shape* of consumption matter, not just the total

This is why managing peak demand - through load shifting, storage, and avoiding coincident peaks - can cut costs even without reducing total energy. The tariff structure reveals where these savings lie.

## Why structure matters for procurement

Understanding tariff structure matters because it determines:
- **Where the cost is** - and therefore where savings can be found
- **What you are exposed to** - wholesale volatility, peak charges, levies
- **How to buy** - the contract type that suits the organisation's risk appetite (the next lesson)
- **How to reduce cost** - through buying, demand management, efficiency, and exemptions

A procurement strategy built without understanding the tariff structure is buying blind. With it, the organisation can target each component - buying the commodity well, managing demand charges, qualifying for levy exemptions, and shifting consumption to cheaper times. The following lessons build on this foundation: the contract types that determine market exposure, the management of price risk, and the procurement of clean energy.
