Renewable energy certificates let organisations claim the use of renewable electricity, but their credibility varies enormously. Understanding certificates - what they prove and what they do not - is essential to making honest clean-energy claims and avoiding greenwashing.

## What certificates are

When renewable electricity is generated, it produces two things: the electricity itself, and a **certificate** attesting to its renewable origin. In the UK these are **Renewable Energy Guarantees of Origin (REGOs)**; internationally, similar schemes exist (Guarantees of Origin in Europe, RECs in the US).

A certificate represents the renewable attribute of one unit (typically 1 MWh) of generation. It can be sold with the electricity or, in some markets, separately - allowing an organisation to buy certificates to "match" its consumption and claim renewable energy use.

## Green tariffs

Many suppliers offer **green tariffs** - electricity contracts marketed as renewable, backed by certificates:
- The supplier matches the customer's consumption with renewable certificates
- The customer can claim to use renewable electricity
- The tariff may cost the same as or slightly more than standard supply

Green tariffs are the simplest way for an organisation to claim renewable electricity - but their credibility depends entirely on what lies behind them, which varies widely.

## The credibility problem

Not all renewable claims are equal, and certificates sit at the heart of a credibility debate:
- **Strong claims** - backed by genuine additionality (a PPA enabling new generation, or a tariff sourcing power directly from specific renewable generators)
- **Weak claims** - backed only by cheap, unbundled certificates bought separately from the electricity, which may relabel existing generation without driving any new renewable capacity

The concern is **additionality**: does the purchase cause new renewable generation, or merely claim credit for generation that would have happened anyway? Buying cheap, unbundled certificates to "greenwash" standard grid power is widely criticised as a weak claim that drives no real change (the credibility link).

## Unbundled certificates and their critics

**Unbundled certificates** - sold separately from the electricity - are the most contested:
- They are cheap, making renewable claims easy and inexpensive
- But they often represent existing generation, adding no new renewable capacity
- Critics argue they allow organisations to claim "100% renewable" with little real impact
- Stakeholders increasingly scrutinise and discount such claims

The criticism does not mean certificates are worthless - they play a legitimate role in the system - but relying on cheap unbundled certificates for a headline green claim is increasingly seen as insufficient and potentially misleading.

## A hierarchy of credibility

Clean-electricity procurement can be ranked by credibility and impact:
1. **On-site renewable generation** - the most direct and credible (the renewables course)
2. **PPAs backing new generation** - large-scale, additional, credible (the PPA link)
3. **Green tariffs sourcing directly from specific renewable generators** - reasonably credible
4. **Green tariffs backed by bundled certificates** - moderate
5. **Unbundled certificates added to standard power** - the weakest, most criticised

The energy manager seeking genuine impact and credible claims should aim high on this hierarchy - prioritising on-site generation and additional PPAs over reliance on cheap certificates. The choice reflects whether the organisation wants real impact or merely a claim.

## Making honest claims

The integrity principle that runs through this academy applies to clean-energy claims:
- **Be honest** about what the procurement actually achieves
- **Prioritise additionality** - support genuinely new renewable generation
- **Avoid greenwashing** - do not make headline claims that cheap certificates cannot honestly support
- **Be transparent** about the basis of claims

As scrutiny of corporate environmental claims intensifies (from stakeholders, regulators, and standards), the credibility of clean-energy claims matters increasingly. An organisation whose "100% renewable" claim rests on cheap unbundled certificates risks accusations of greenwashing; one backing its claim with on-site generation and additional PPAs has a defensible, credible position (the net-zero credibility link).

## Certificates in context

Certificates and green tariffs have a legitimate place in the clean-energy toolkit - they provide a mechanism for tracking and claiming renewable attributes, and accessible green tariffs help build demand for renewables. But they are the weakest link in the credibility hierarchy when used alone, and the energy manager should understand both their utility and their limitations. Combined with the genuine additionality of on-site generation and PPAs, and used honestly, certificates form part of a credible clean-energy strategy. Used as a cheap cover for unchanged consumption, they invite the greenwashing criticism that undermines credibility. The distinction - and the honesty it demands - is central to credible clean-energy procurement.
