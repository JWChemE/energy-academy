Good procurement begins with knowing what you need to buy. Forecasting energy demand - and aggregating it across sites - is the foundation of effective purchasing, negotiation, and risk management.

## Why forecasting matters

You cannot buy energy well without knowing how much you need, when, and with what certainty. Demand forecasting underpins:
- **Contract sizing** - how much energy to contract for
- **Negotiation** - accurate volumes secure better terms
- **Risk management** - understanding exposure (the risk-management link)
- **Budgeting** - forecasting cost from forecast volume and price
- **Flexible procurement** - timing tranche purchases against expected demand

A poor forecast leads to over- or under-contracting, weak negotiating positions, and budget surprises. A good one is the foundation of confident, effective procurement.

## Building a demand forecast

Forecasting energy demand draws on:
- **Historical consumption** - past usage patterns as the starting point (the monitoring link)
- **Known changes** - planned growth, new sites, closures, equipment changes, efficiency projects
- **Drivers** - the factors that affect demand (production, occupancy, weather)
- **Efficiency plans** - the impact of planned energy projects in reducing future demand (the investment-case link)

A forecast combines the historical baseline with anticipated changes - growth that increases demand, efficiency projects that decrease it - to project future consumption. The energy manager's own efficiency programme is a key input: planned savings reduce the volume to be procured.

## Accuracy and uncertainty

Forecasts are estimates, and their uncertainty matters:
- **Over-forecasting** risks contracting for energy not needed (take-or-pay penalties, surplus)
- **Under-forecasting** risks shortfalls bought at unfavourable spot prices
- **Uncertainty** in the forecast affects how much flexibility to build into contracts

Good forecasting acknowledges uncertainty - providing a central estimate and a range, and building appropriate flexibility into procurement to handle the uncertainty. The more variable and uncertain the demand, the more flexibility the procurement strategy needs.

## Aggregation across sites

For multi-site organisations, **aggregation** is a powerful lever:
- Combining the energy demand of many sites into a single, larger procurement
- Larger volumes command better terms and more supplier interest
- Aggregation provides scale that individual small sites lack
- It enables access to options (like PPAs) that require scale (the PPA link)

A portfolio of small sites, each buying alone, gets poor terms; aggregated into one large purchase, the same demand commands far better prices and options. Aggregation is one of the simplest ways for a multi-site organisation to improve its energy procurement - turning scattered small demands into significant buying power.

## Demand profiling

Beyond total volume, the **shape** of demand matters (the tariff link):
- When energy is used (the load profile) affects cost, especially with time-of-use pricing and demand charges
- Understanding the profile reveals opportunities to shift load, reduce peaks, and match generation
- It informs the choice of tariff and contract

Forecasting therefore considers not just how much energy but its profile over time - when the demand occurs - which affects both cost and the opportunities to manage it (the flexibility and storage links).

## Forecasting and flexibility

Demand forecasting connects to the growing importance of demand flexibility:
- Knowing the demand profile reveals where load can be shifted (the storage and microgrid links)
- Flexible demand can be procured and managed to reduce cost and exposure
- Forecasting flexible and inflexible demand separately informs strategy

As energy systems become more dynamic, understanding and forecasting not just total demand but its flexibility becomes increasingly valuable - enabling the organisation to shift load to cheaper times and offer flexibility to the market.

## From forecast to procurement

The demand forecast feeds directly into the procurement process (the next lessons):
- It sizes the contracts and informs the negotiation
- It shapes the risk-management and tranche-buying strategy (the risk link)
- Aggregation strengthens the negotiating position
- It underpins the budget

Accurate forecasting and smart aggregation give the organisation the foundation for confident, effective procurement - knowing what it needs, buying at the right scale, and negotiating from a position of clarity and strength.
