How an organisation contracts for energy determines how much price risk it bears. The choice between fixed, flexible, and indexed contracts is one of the most consequential procurement decisions - balancing budget certainty against the chance of lower costs.

## The fundamental trade-off

Energy contracts sit on a spectrum between two poles:
- **Certainty** - locking in a known price, protecting against rises but forgoing falls
- **Opportunity** - exposure to the market, capturing falls but risking rises

No contract gives both. The choice reflects the organisation's priorities: does it value budget certainty above all, or is it willing to bear risk for the chance of lower cost? Understanding the contract types lets the organisation make this choice deliberately rather than by default.

## Fixed-price contracts

A **fixed-price contract** locks in a unit price for the contract term:
- **Certainty** - the price is known, aiding budgeting
- **Protection** - against price rises during the term
- **Simplicity** - straightforward to understand and manage
- **But:** no benefit if prices fall, and the supplier builds a risk premium into the fixed price

Fixed contracts suit organisations that value budget certainty, lack the resources to manage market exposure, or want simplicity. The trade-off is paying a premium for certainty and missing any price falls.

## Flexible procurement

**Flexible (or "flex") contracts** allow the organisation to buy its energy in tranches over time, rather than fixing the whole volume at once:
- The total volume is purchased in portions, at different times, at prevailing market prices
- This spreads the purchasing across the market, averaging out price movements
- It allows buying more when prices are low and less when high (active management)
- It requires expertise and active management (in-house or through a broker/consultant)

Flexible procurement suits larger organisations with the volume and expertise to manage it actively. It can achieve lower average costs than fixed contracts by buying strategically, but requires skill and accepts some price exposure.

## Indexed (pass-through) contracts

An **indexed contract** prices energy by reference to a market index, so the price tracks the wholesale market:
- The price varies with the market - falling when the market falls, rising when it rises
- It captures market falls but bears full exposure to rises
- It offers transparency (you pay the market price plus a known margin)
- It carries the most price risk

Indexed contracts suit organisations confident in their view of the market, able to bear volatility, or wanting full transparency and market exposure. They offer the most upside if prices fall but the most risk if they rise.

## The collar: capping the risk

Between full fixticity and full exposure, **collar arrangements** cap the risk while retaining some upside:
- A **cap** limits the maximum price (protection against rises)
- A **floor** limits the minimum (the cost of the protection)
- The price floats between, capturing some market benefit while bounding the risk

Collars and similar structured products let an organisation tailor its risk exposure - more flexible than a pure fixed contract, more protected than full market exposure. They are part of the toolkit for managing price risk (the next lesson).

## Matching contract to organisation

The right contract depends on the organisation's circumstances:
- **Budget certainty needed, limited expertise, smaller volume:** fixed-price
- **Larger volume, expertise available, willing to manage actively:** flexible
- **Confident market view, able to bear risk, wanting transparency:** indexed
- **Wanting bounded risk with some upside:** collar or structured

There is no universally best contract - it depends on the organisation's size, risk appetite, expertise, and priorities. The key is choosing deliberately, understanding the trade-off between certainty and opportunity that each contract represents.

## Beyond the contract

The contract type sets the framework, but good procurement also involves timing, market awareness, and risk management (the next lesson), as well as the supplier relationship and the procurement process (the final module). And increasingly, the choice of contract intersects with clean-energy goals - green tariffs and PPAs (the sustainable-procurement module) add another dimension to the decision. The contract type is the foundation of how an organisation buys energy and manages its exposure to the market - a deliberate strategic choice, not an administrative default.
