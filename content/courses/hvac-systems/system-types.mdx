HVAC systems come in many configurations, each with different efficiency
characteristics, control flexibility, and cost. Understanding the main types will
help you diagnose where energy is being wasted.

## The spectrum: all-air to all-water to heat pump

Think of systems on a spectrum from **completely air-based** to **completely liquid-
based**:

### All-air systems

A single central plant (rooftop or plant room) heats/cools and humidifies/dehumidifies
**all air** centrally, then distributes it through ducts to every zone.

**Pros:**
- Simple to understand and control
- Excellent for humidity control (central humidifier/dehumidifier)
- Good for clean environments (filters, air quality)

**Cons:**
- Large ductwork (takes space)
- Difficult to provide independent heating/cooling to different zones simultaneously
  (heating zone A while cooling zone B requires complex dampers)
- Fans are big and consume a lot of energy

**Typical efficiency:** 70–80% when considering duct losses. 20% of energy is lost
to duct leakage and fan work.

### Air-water systems (most common in modern buildings)

**Decentralized approach:**
- A central plant provides **hot water** and **chilled water**
- Terminal units (fan coil units, radiant panels) in each zone draw that water and
  use **local fans** (or none, in radiant systems) to deliver comfort
- A smaller amount of **outdoor air** is distributed for ventilation only

**Pros:**
- Zones can independently heat while others cool (huge efficiency gain)
- Better part-load efficiency (individual coils can modulate)
- Piping takes less space than ductwork

**Cons:**
- Can't directly control humidity in zones (condensation risk on coils if humidity
  rises)
- Requires good water treatment (scale, corrosion)

**Typical efficiency:** 80–85%.

### Radiant systems (ceiling/floor panels)

Chilled or heated water flows through tubing in the ceiling or floor. Comfort is
delivered via **radiation** rather than blown air.

**Pros:**
- Very low fan energy (no fans, just pump circulation)
- Excellent comfort (no draughts, even temperature field)
- Individual zone control

**Cons:**
- Very slow to respond to load changes (thermal mass)
- Humidity control difficult (condensation risk)
- Higher capital cost

**Typical efficiency:** 85–90% (low fan energy is the main advantage).

### Heat pump systems

Instead of a boiler + chiller, a **heat pump** extracts warmth from outdoor air (or
ground) and delivers it to zones.

**Pros:**
- One source for heating and cooling
- Efficiency improves with climate (better in UK than in tropics)
- Future-proof (electricity, not fossil fuel)

**Cons:**
- Higher capital cost
- Requires low-temperature distribution (radiators or radiant panels, not old high-
  temperature systems)
- Performance degrades in very cold weather (unless hybrid with backup boiler)

**Typical COP (coefficient of performance):** 3–4 (meaning 1 kW of electricity
delivers 3–4 kW of heat). Carbon intensity depends on grid mix.

## Sizing and oversizing

A common problem: HVAC systems are **oversized**.

Building design might specify "meet 21 °C in 99.6% of hours" (a very cold scenario).
But a building is comfortable at that setpoint only a few weeks per year. The rest
of the time, a smaller system would do.

**Oversized systems:**
- Run at part load most of the time (less efficient)
- Cycle frequently (burners/compressors on/off = transient losses)
- Cost more to buy, more to run

A properly sized system runs closer to design load more often, which improves
efficiency.

<Callout variant="key" title="Sizing to the right load is as important as picking the right technology">
A perfectly efficient heat pump that's twice the size you need is wasteful. A well-
matched, slightly simpler system often beats an over-engineered one.
</Callout>

## Distribution and controls

Even more important than the type of system is **how well it's controlled**:

- A poorly controlled all-air system with bad dampers wastes more than a well-
  controlled one
- An air-water system with stuck thermostatic valves functions like an over-sized
  all-air system (zones can't modulate)

You'll learn about controls in depth in the next module. For now, remember:
**system type is only half the equation; control architecture is the other half.**

## The trend: decentralisation and modulation

Modern buildings favour **decentralised, modulating systems** because:

1. **Zone independence**: heat one area while cooling another, not impossible
2. **Part-load efficiency**: individual units modulate to demand
3. **Resilience**: if one zone fails, others keep operating
4. **Retrofitability**: easier to upgrade zones piecemeal

The trend away from all-air systems (which dominated the 1990s–2000s) toward air-
water or heat-pump systems reflects this.
