Every HVAC system is built from the same basic components. Understanding what each
does, and where it wastes energy, gives you the diagnostic tools to walk into any
plant room and know what you're looking at.

## The central plant

### Boiler (for heating) or chiller (for cooling)

You've already learned about boilers. **Chillers** are essentially heat pumps
running backwards, using electricity (or in old plants, absorption systems using
waste heat) to cool water.

**Energy point:** a chiller running at design load might be 5–6 COP. At part load,
it degrades. On a 500-ton chiller running at 30% load most of the time, efficiency
can halve.

### Pumps

Water is distributed around the building via pumps. A pump consumes energy
proportional to:
- **Flow** (cubic metres per hour)
- **Pressure** (how hard the pump must push)

Energy consumed ∝ flow × pressure. **Many buildings run excessive flow and pressure**
(oversized distribution system).

**Energy point:** oversized pumps are massive waste. A 10 kW pump running 4,000
hours/year consumes 40 MWh and costs £5,000+/year. Variable-speed drives (adjusting
pump speed to demand) typically save 30–50%.

### Fans

In all-air systems, big fans move air around. In air-water systems, each zone has a
small fan (in the fan-coil unit) or none (radiant).

**Energy point:** fans are the biggest load in all-air systems: sometimes 30% of
total HVAC energy. Duct design and cleanliness hugely affect fan energy.

### Cooling towers (if the plant uses water-cooled chillers)

Rejects heat from the chiller to outdoor air via evaporative cooling.

**Energy point:** cooling-tower fans run year-round in plants with continuous
cooling load. In the UK, in winter, free cooling via the tower (instead of
refrigeration) saves massive amounts.

## Distribution

### Ducts (all-air systems)

Air travels from the central unit through ducts to zone delivery vents. If ducts are
leaky, ~20% of the air (and the energy that heated/cooled it) is lost to attics and
crawlspaces.

**Energy point:** duct sealing and insulation can recover 10–20% of HVAC energy on
all-air systems.

### Pipes (air-water and heating/cooling systems)

Water travels through insulated (or uninsulated!) pipes. Uninsulated hot pipes in
cold spaces lose heat continuously.

**Energy point:** insulating a 2-inch pipe running 50 metres in a cold basement saves
5–10 kW and costs ~£500. Very fast payback.

## Terminal units (zone delivery)

### Fan coil units (air-water systems)

A small coil + fan in each room or zone. Water flows through the coil; the fan
blows air across it.

**Energy point:** running fans continuously, even at low load, wastes energy. Modern
units have **thermostatic control valves** (turn off water flow when not needed) and
**variable-speed fans** (slow down as demand drops).

### VAV boxes (all-air systems)

Variable-air-volume dampers throttle the flow of air to each zone as the thermostat
demands. When a zone is satisfied, the damper closes and less air flows in.

**Energy point:** at part load, VAV boxes reduce air flow, which reduces fan energy.
But the system isn't perfect: some wasted air always leaks past dampers, and the
control can be crude.

### Radiant panels (heated/cooled ceiling or floor)

No fan, just warm or cool water in tubing. Comfort is radiant.

**Energy point:** zero fan energy. The main advantage.

## Controls: the beating heart

Every component above has controls:
- A **thermostat** senses temperature and opens/closes a valve or changes fan speed
- A **humidity sensor** may adjust dehumidification
- **Flow meters** may adjust pump or fan speed

The sophistication of these controls determines whether the system is efficient or wasteful.
A system with manual dampers and one building-wide thermostat (common in older
buildings) is always either over-heated or under-heated. A system with individual
zone thermostats and automatic dampers is much better.

**Energy point:** upgrading controls is often the fastest HVAC payback. Replacing a
fixed setpoint with weather compensation and zone control can save 10–20% of
heating/cooling energy for £5,000–15,000.

## Putting it together: a diagram

```
Central plant
(Boiler/chiller, pump, fan)
       ↓
Distribution (ducts or pipes, insulation)
       ↓
Terminal units (fans, coils, dampers)
       ↓
Zone (room or zone)
```

Each step has efficiency losses. Managing those losses (sizing, maintaining, controlling) is the discipline of HVAC management.
