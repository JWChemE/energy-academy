One of the biggest wins in HVAC efficiency is **using the outdoors to do your
cooling** instead of running the chiller. In the UK, this is possible for much of
the year.

## Outdoor air economizer (free cooling)

When outdoor air is cooler than your indoor setpoint, instead of cooling with a
chiller, you open dampers to admit more outdoor air. The cooler air cools the
building naturally.

**Example:** on a 12 °C spring day, if your building is 20 °C and needs cooling (from
internal gains — people, equipment), you can open the air intake to 100% outdoor air.
The cooler outdoor air brings the building temperature down at the cost of **fan
energy only**, not compressor energy.

Chiller energy to remove 50 kW: ~15 kW electric, costing ~£2/hour.
Fan energy to deliver the same with outdoor air: ~2 kW, costing ~£0.30/hour.

**Savings: 85% of cooling energy whenever the economizer is active.**

## When economizer works

**Outdoor temperature < indoor setpoint + delta:** the economizer is active.

In the UK:
- **Winter (Nov–Mar)**: outdoor air is usually cooler than 20 °C indoor setpoint,
  so economizer is on. No cooling needed unless there's solar gain or high internal
  load.
- **Spring/autumn (Apr–May, Sep–Oct)**: variable. Many days are economizer-capable.
- **Summer (Jun–Aug)**: some days are hot; some mild mornings are economizer-capable.

Rough estimate: **60% of the year, free cooling via economizer is possible in the
UK.**

<Callout variant="key" title="Free cooling can save 30–60% of cooling energy">
A building in the UK with a chiller might use 80 MWh/year for cooling. With a good
economizer, that could drop to 40 MWh. The investment (dampers, controls): £5,000–
10,000. Payback: 1–2 years.
</Callout>

## Enthalpy recovery (heat recovery ventilation)

In winter, you need fresh outdoor air for ventilation. But outdoor air is cold and
needs heating.

**Heat recovery ventilation (HRV)** captures the heat in the exhaust air (warm indoor
air being extracted) and uses it to preheat incoming fresh air.

- Incoming air: 0 °C outside → 15 °C after recovery (85% effectiveness typical)
- Heating load reduced by ~70% of the ventilation load

**Example:** a 100-person office needs ~1,000 L/s outdoor air. Heating that from 0
°C to 20 °C requires ~30 kW. An HRV cuts this to ~9 kW.

**Cost:** £8,000–15,000 for a typical office. **Savings:** 20–30 MWh/year heating.
**Payback:** 3–5 years.

HRV is not as fast as economizer, but it's valuable in winter when economizer can't
help.

## Hybrid systems

Modern control strategies combine economizer + chiller + HRV:

1. **Winter daytime:** economizer on (if mild), HRV active. Minimal heating/cooling.
2. **Winter night:** HRV active, minimum ventilation. Setpoint lowered.
3. **Summer daytime:** economizer on early morning (cool outdoor air); chiller off.
   As outdoor air warms, switch to chiller.
4. **Summer night:** economizer on fully (cool outdoor air passes through). Chiller
   off.

The control logic is simple: always ask "can I use outdoors before I use the
chiller?" If the answer is yes, use outdoor air. This automation can save 30–50% of
cooling energy compared to a plant with no economizer.

## Practical implementation

- **Check if your system has an economizer:** look for outdoor-air dampers that can
  open fully, and an outdoor-air temperature sensor. If missing, add them (usually
  cheap retrofits).
- **Verify the controls:** does the damper actually open when it should? Stuck
  dampers are common.
- **Measure the savings:** compare chiller energy before/after a sunny/cool day with
  economizer on vs off.

<Callout variant="tip">
If your building has a chiller and no economizer, installing one is often the
fastest efficiency win. Cost under £10k; savings £10k+/year.
</Callout>
