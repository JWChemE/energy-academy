**VAV** stands for variable-air-volume. In an all-air HVAC system, VAV boxes are
the mechanism that allow different zones to demand different amounts of air
(and thus heating/cooling) simultaneously.

## How VAV boxes work

A VAV box sits in the ductwork serving a zone. Inside is a damper that throttles
the airflow:

1. **Zone thermostat** measures room temperature
2. **If room is too cold**, thermostat opens the VAV damper → more air flows in
3. **If room is satisfied**, damper closes → less air flows in
4. **If room is too warm** (cooling needed), damper opens wider and more cool air flows

This allows **zone independence** — one zone can be heating while another is cooling,
without the central system fighting itself.

## The efficiency advantage

Compare:

**System without VAV:** central unit supplies 100% air regardless of load.
- All zones get full air flow, even when satisfied
- Excess air (and the energy to heat/cool it) is "dumped" — wasted
- Typical efficiency: 65–75%

**System with VAV:** central unit supplies air on demand.
- Each zone throttles its damper; total air flow drops as zones are satisfied
- Central fans run slower (smaller airflow), consuming much less energy
- Typical efficiency: 80–85%

**Savings: 10–20% of fan energy**, which is huge in all-air systems.

## VAV control problems

VAV systems only work efficiently if **controls are good**:

### Stuck dampers

A damper that's stuck open or closed → zone can't reach setpoint.
- Stuck open → zone can't cool down (damper won't close)
- Stuck closed → zone can't warm up

**Symptom:** one zone is always too hot or cold while others are fine. **Fix:**
mechanical cleaning or damper replacement, ~£500–1,500.

### Minimum airflow (ventilation requirement)

VAV dampers can't close fully — they must maintain **minimum outdoor air** for
ventilation and fresh air.

- **Minimum flow setting:** typical 20–30% of full flow
- If minimum is too high, you waste energy running air even when zone is satisfied
- If minimum is too low, air quality suffers

**Optimization:** lower minimum flow if occupancy is low or demand-controlled
ventilation is available.

### Duct static pressure

In a VAV system, closing some dampers can cause **duct pressure to rise**
(less flow out, same fan pushing). The fan must work harder.

**Solution:** a **static pressure sensor** in the duct adjusts **fan speed** to
maintain target pressure (typically 100–150 Pa). The fan slows down as dampers
close, saving energy.

Most modern VAV systems have this, but older ones don't — which means the fan runs
full speed even as dampers close.

**Retrofit static pressure control:** ~£3,000–5,000. **Savings:** 15–25% of fan
energy. **Payback:** 2–4 years.

## Typical VAV operation

**Peak load (hot summer afternoon, all zones want cooling):**
- All VAV dampers open
- System runs at full fan speed
- Duct pressure near maximum

**Part load (mild afternoon, some zones satisfied):**
- Some dampers close
- System recognizes lower pressure demand
- Fan slows down (if variable-speed drive installed)
- Energy use drops

**Light load (night, minimal ventilation):**
- All dampers nearly closed (minimum ventilation only)
- Fan slow, low energy

The efficiency gain from variable-speed fans is the biggest win in VAV systems.

## Troubleshooting VAV issues

| Problem | Likely cause | Fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| One zone always hot, others OK | Stuck damper | Clean/replace damper, ~£500–1,500 |
| High fan energy despite VAV | No static pressure control | Add variable-speed drive, ~£4,000–6,000 |
| Multiple zones can't reach setpoint | Damper calibration drift | Commission (adjust damper travel), ~£500–1,000 |
| Fresh-air quality poor | Minimum airflow set too low | Raise minimum setpoint, may require CO₂ control |
| Noise/drafts from a zone | Damper hunting/vibrating | Adjust control bandwidth, ~£200–500 |

Most VAV problems are **control commissioning issues**, not equipment failure. A
good commissioning engineer can fix 80% of VAV problems in one day for under £2,000.

<Callout variant="tip">
If your all-air HVAC system has VAV boxes and high fan energy, the problem is usually
no static pressure control or damper drift. These are cheap fixes (~£5,000) with huge
savings.
</Callout>
