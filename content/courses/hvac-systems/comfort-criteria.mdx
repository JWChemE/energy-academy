HVAC stands for Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning — systems that control
temperature, humidity, and air quality in buildings. Before we can understand how
to run them efficiently, we need to understand what "comfort" actually is and why
it's not just about temperature.

## The comfort envelope

Comfort depends on four things:

1. **Air temperature** (typically 19–23 °C in offices)
2. **Humidity** (typically 30–60% relative humidity)
3. **Air movement** (draughts are uncomfortable; stagnant air feels stuffy)
4. **Radiant temperature** (cold windows or hot surfaces cause discomfort via radiation)

If any one is off, people are uncomfortable — even if the others are perfect.

## Psychrometrics: the science of moist air

Air is never dry; it always contains some water vapour. The amount of vapour it can
hold depends on **temperature**. Warm air can hold more moisture than cold air.

Two key terms:

- **Relative humidity (RH)**: the amount of moisture in the air as a % of the
  maximum it could hold at that temperature. 50% RH means the air is "half full."
- **Dew point**: the temperature at which air becomes saturated (100% RH). Below
  that, moisture condenses.

<Callout variant="key" title="Why humidity matters for comfort and energy">
- **Too dry** (under 30% RH): skin and respiratory irritation, increased cold perception
- **Too moist** (>60% RH): stuffy feeling, mould risk, increased warm perception
- **Optimum**: 40–55% RH in offices

Many buildings run 20% RH in winter (dry) or 65% RH in summer (humid) because
they're not controlling humidity — HVAC systems need to **actively manage both
temperature and humidity**.
</Callout>

## Sensible vs latent loads

When you heat a room, you increase its **sensible heat** (temperature). When you
dehumidify it, you remove **latent heat** (the heat stored in water vapour).

- **Sensible heat** is easy to measure (thermometer) and control (turn up heating)
- **Latent heat** is invisible but significant. A 100-person office in summer loads
  the air with ~10 kW of moisture from breathing and perspiration

**The ratio of sensible to latent load** matters for HVAC design. In winter, a
building is mostly sensible (cold outside, low humidity inside naturally). In
summer, latent load can dominate (occupancy and outdoor humidity).

## Operative temperature and mean radiant temperature

**Operative temperature** is the combined effect of:
- Air temperature
- Radiant temperature (from walls, windows, people)

A person in a cold-window room at 21 °C air temperature might feel as cold as 18
°C because the window radiates cold. This is why:
- **Double-glazing** (warmer inner surface) improves comfort
- **Under-window heating** (radiators under windows) compensates for cold radiation

For comfort calculation, we use:

> **Operative T = (0.5 × air T) + (0.5 × mean radiant T)**

This is why building fabric (insulation, glazing) and HVAC are not separate — they
work together to deliver comfort.

## HVAC and the building envelope

A leaky, poorly insulated building needs a more powerful HVAC system to maintain
comfort. A well-insulated building with good windows needs less.

**Example:** an office building with:
- **Poor envelope** (U-values 0.5 W/m²K): winter heat loss 150 kW, summer cooling
  load 120 kW
- **Good envelope** (U-values 0.25 W/m²K): winter heat loss 75 kW, summer cooling
  load 60 kW

The "good envelope" building needs boilers and chillers only **half the size** —
savings that compound over 20+ years.

This is why energy management starts with the envelope. An HVAC system should be
sized to the **needs of a good building**, not over-sized to compensate for a poor
one.

## Air quality (a brief note)

HVAC also provides **fresh outdoor air** for breathing and odour control. Typical
requirements:
- **Offices**: 6–8 L/s per person (minimum ~25% of total air flow)
- **Kitchens / toilets**: higher, up to 100% outdoor air in exhaust zones

Balancing fresh-air intake with energy efficiency is a constant tension: more
outdoor air = more heating/cooling needed. Modern control strategies use **demand-
controlled ventilation** (adjust outdoor air based on CO₂, occupancy) to minimize
this waste.

<Callout variant="tip">
If you inherit an HVAC system running 100% outdoor air year-round, fixing that one
setting often saves 10–15% of heating/cooling energy.
</Callout>
