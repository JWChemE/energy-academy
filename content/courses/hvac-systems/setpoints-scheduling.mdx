The setpoint — the target temperature — is one of the most powerful levers in HVAC
efficiency. A 1 °C change in setpoint can change heating or cooling energy by 5–10%.

## Seasonal setpoints

Running 21 °C heating all winter and 21 °C cooling setpoint all summer is wasteful.

**Winter:** outdoors are cold. A 19–20 °C setpoint is comfortable with warm clothing,
reducing heating load significantly.

**Summer:** outdoors are warm. Cooling to 21 °C when 23–24 °C is acceptable reduces
cooling load.

**Spring/autumn:** mild weather allows much wider swings. 18–24 °C might be acceptable
on some days without any heating or cooling.

A building that manually adjusts setpoints by season — or better, allows automatic
adjustment — often saves 5–10% of HVAC energy.

## Weather compensation (automated seasonal reset)

Instead of manual adjustment, **weather compensation** automatically adjusts the
setpoint based on outdoor temperature:

- **Cold day (5 °C):** heating setpoint 21 °C (full heating)
- **Mild day (12 °C):** heating setpoint 18 °C (partial heating)
- **Warm day (18 °C):** no heating (use free cooling)

The relationship (setpoint vs outdoor T) is called a **reset curve**. Modern
controllers let you program this.

**Benefit:** no operator action needed; setpoint follows weather automatically.
**Typical savings:** 5–15% of heating/cooling energy.

<Callout variant="key" title="Weather compensation is one of the fastest paybacks">
Retrofit cost: ~£2,000–5,000 (outdoor sensor + control upgrade). Savings:
5–15% of HVAC energy (~50 MWh/year on a typical office). Payback: 1–2 years.
</Callout>

## Occupancy-based control

Many buildings are unoccupied at night, weekends, and during downtime. Running full
HVAC during those periods is waste.

**Occupancy reset:**
- **Occupied hours (e.g., 6am–8pm weekdays):** heating 21 °C, cooling 23 °C
- **Unoccupied (nights/weekends):** setpoint drifts to 17 °C (winter) or 27 °C
  (summer) — minimal conditioning

Or go further: if motion sensors detect no occupancy, setpoint shifts immediately.

**Savings:** 10–30% of HVAC energy (depending on occupancy profile).

**Cost:** occupancy sensors + automation: £5,000–15,000 for a building. **Payback:**
1–3 years on typical office.

## Night setback and pre-cooling

**Night setback:** lower heating (or raise cooling) setpoint at night. When the
building warms (or cools) as occupants arrive in the morning, it's cheaper to reach
setpoint gradually from a low base.

**Pre-cooling:** on mild nights in summer, cool the building down to 20 °C (using
outdoor air / economizer). During the day, allow it to coast up to 24 °C before
activating cooling. Uses only fan energy.

Both strategies **shift loads away from peak electricity hours** (mid-afternoon in
summer, early morning in winter), which can also lower demand charges.

## Deadband between heating and cooling

In intermediate weather (spring/autumn), you don't want heating AND cooling
competing.

A **dead band** between heating and cooling setpoints prevents this:
- Heating below 19 °C
- Cooling above 23 °C
- 19–23 °C: do nothing (allow passive operation)

This is especially useful during mild seasons when outdoor air is close to comfort
range.

## Practical implementation checklist

1. **Current setpoint:** what is it? Is it the same all year?
2. **Occupancy profile:** when is the building used? Can you set back nights/weekends?
3. **Outdoor sensor:** does your HVAC system have one? If not, adding one enables
   weather compensation.
4. **Controls software:** can you program reset curves? If controls are old (manual
   dampers, wall thermostat), upgrade to a BMS (building management system).

A building with no weather compensation or occupancy control is likely wasting
10–20% of HVAC energy. Retrofitting these is almost always a quick win.

<Callout variant="tip">
Before upgrading equipment, check setpoints and controls. Many HVAC "efficiency
problems" are actually control tuning problems that cost nothing to fix.
</Callout>
