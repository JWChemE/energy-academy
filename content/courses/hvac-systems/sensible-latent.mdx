A room might feel uncomfortable at 21 °C and 70% humidity even though the temperature
is correct. The problem is **latent load** — the water vapour in the air. To design
an efficient HVAC system, you must control both sensible (temperature) and latent
(humidity) loads. They require different strategies.

## Sensible cooling and heating

**Sensible** means you can feel it — a thermometer measures it.

To add heat (winter): blow warm air or run hot water through a coil.
To remove heat (summer): blow cool air or run cold water through a coil.

**The challenge:** the cooler the water, the more cooling you must apply. A chiller
running 5 °C water consumes more energy than one running 10 °C. So there's a
balance: how cold must you run to meet the sensible load?

## Latent cooling (dehumidification)

Moisture must be **removed from the air** to drop humidity. This happens when air is
cooled **below its dew point**. Water vapour condenses on the coil (like condensation
on a cold window), and the liquid drains away. The air leaves the coil dry and cold,
then is reheated (if necessary) to the target temperature.

This two-step process — cool to condense, then reheat — is called the **cooling-
dehumidification cycle**.

**The inefficiency:** you cool the air very cold (say, 8 °C) to remove moisture, then
you reheat it to 20 °C for comfort. All that reheating is wasted energy if you don't
need to remove moisture.

## Sensible vs latent ratio

Buildings produce sensible and latent loads in different ratios:

- **Winter**: mostly sensible (cold outside, dry because cold air holds little
  moisture). Latent load ~10% of sensible.
- **Summer**: mix of sensible (cooling) and latent (humidity). Latent can be
  30–50% of total.
- **Occupied office in summer**: occupants (breathing, perspiration) create latent
  load. 100 people = ~10 kW latent + ~20 kW sensible from their bodies and equipment.

## Control strategies for humidity

**Traditional approach: cool and reheat**
- Cool the air to ~8 °C to remove all moisture
- Reheat it to 20 °C for comfort

Simple but energy-intensive.

**Modern approach: desiccant or enthalpy recovery**
- Use a **desiccant wheel** (rotating wheel coated with silica gel) to remove
  moisture from outdoor air
- Reuse the "dry" air instead of over-cooling

Saves 30–50% of dehumidification energy, but more complex.

**Practical approach: accept wider humidity swings**
- Allow humidity to swing 30–60% instead of strict 45–55%
- Humans tolerate this range
- Greatly reduces cooling load

Many buildings over-control humidity, wasting cooling energy. A 10% relaxation in
humidity setpoint (say, 60% instead of 50%) often saves 5–10% of cooling energy
with no occupant complaint.

<Callout variant="key" title="Don't over-dehumidify">
Unless your building has a specific need (museum, clean room, server farm), relaxing
humidity setpoint is one of the fastest wins. Humidity at 65% is still comfortable.
</Callout>

## The psychrometric chart: how engineers visualize it

Engineers use a **psychrometric chart** — a graph with temperature on the x-axis and
humidity on the y-axis. Different moist-air properties (dew point, enthalpy, etc.)
are plotted as curves.

You don't need to be able to use one, but the key concept: cooling moves you down
(lower enthalpy) and left (lower temperature), removing sensible heat. To also
reduce humidity, you must cool below the dew point (the saturation line).

## Energy implications

To summarize:
- **Sensible control** (hot/cold coils): straightforward; efficiency depends on
  setpoint and part-load
- **Latent control** (dehumidification): expensive because you cool far below target
  then reheat
- **Best practice**: size HVAC for sensible load, allow wider humidity ranges,
  recover heat from dehumidification if latent load is high

A system designed to keep humidity strict 45–55% in a UK office (not essential) will
use 20–30% more cooling energy than one that allows 30–65%.
