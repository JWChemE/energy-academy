An HVAC system must satisfy the **thermal load** of a building. The load varies with
weather, occupancy, and time. Understanding where the load comes from — and what you
can control — is the key to running HVAC efficiently.

## Sources of heating load (winter)

**Transmission through the envelope:**
- Heat flows out through walls, roof, windows, doors. The colder outside, the higher
  the flow.
- Quantified by **U-values** (W/m²K) of each surface and **air-tightness**.
- Dominates heating load in poorly insulated buildings.

**Ventilation:**
- Fresh outdoor air enters at near-outdoor temperature, must be heated to indoor
  setpoint.
- In a leaky building, infiltration (uncontrolled air leakage) adds to this.
- Can be 20–40% of heating load.

**Note:** there's no internal cooling load in winter (people, equipment generate heat,
but it's a small comfort gain, not a net load).

Heating load ≈ (transmission loss) + (ventilation heat required)

## Sources of cooling load (summer)

**Transmission and solar gain:**
- Heat flows *in* through the roof and walls. Solar radiation on glazing adds to it.
- High solar gain on west-facing glass in summer afternoon can be huge.

**Ventilation and infiltration:**
- Warm outdoor air leaks in (or is provided for ventilation).

**Internal gains:**
- **People**: ~100 W per occupant (sensible) + ~50 W (latent, from breathing/
  perspiration)
- **Lighting**: ~15–50 W per m² (depending on density and control)
- **Equipment**: office equipment, servers, industrial processes

In modern offices, internal gains (people + equipment + lighting) can exceed
transmission + solar, especially with good insulation and shading.

**Latent gain:**
- Moisture from occupants and (outdoors in hot, humid climates)

Cooling load ≈ (transmission + solar) + (ventilation) + (people + equipment) + (latent)

## Load profiles: when you actually need HVAC

A building's load changes throughout the day and season. A typical office:

- **Winter, night**: ~20 kW heating (transmission only; no people)
- **Winter, occupied daytime**: ~50 kW heating (transmission + ventilation, offset
  partially by people/equipment)
- **Summer, night**: ~5 kW cooling (minimal load; windows open if allowed)
- **Summer, occupied hot afternoon**: ~80 kW cooling (transmission + solar + people)

Notice: peak cooling is much sharper than peak heating. A single hot day in June can
spike cooling load far above the annual average.

**HVAC systems are often sized for peak load** (a few days per year). They run at
10–30% of capacity most of the time. This is where part-load efficiency matters hugely.

<Callout variant="key" title="Load profiling saves energy">
If you measure or model your building's load profile, you can often relax setpoints
on mild days, turn off cooling when it's cool enough outside, and avoid oversized
equipment.
</Callout>

## Key controllables

From the sources above, you can control:

1. **Transmission** — improve envelope (insulation, glazing, air-sealing). Reduces
   both heating and cooling load.
2. **Solar gain** — install shading (blinds, overhangs). Reduces cooling.
3. **Ventilation rate** — lower outdoor-air flow (demand-controlled ventilation,
   CO₂ sensors). Reduces both heating and cooling.
4. **Setpoint** — lower heating setpoint, raise cooling setpoint. Cuts demand
   directly.
5. **Internal gains** — control lighting (occupancy sensors, daylighting controls),
   reduce plug loads (turn off unused equipment).

Improvements to 1–4 often have 1–2 year paybacks. Improvement to 5 is free if you
just discipline occupants.

## Load factors: degree-days and bin analysis

**Heating degree-days (HDD)** in winter = sum of (baseline T – outdoor T) for each
day. Baseline is typically 15.5 °C.

A UK office might accumulate 2,000 HDD/year. A Scottish one might see 3,000. A
London one might see 1,500.

The HDD of your location tells you how much heating capacity you need and how long
the heating season is.

**Cooling degree-days (CDD)** similarly quantify cooling need, though far less
significant in the UK than in hotter climates.

**Bin analysis** divides the year into temperature ranges and counts hours in each
bin. A building running HVAC in mild weather (say, 10–15 °C) might find that free
cooling or ventilation (no active heating/cooling) covers 30% of hours.

<Callout variant="tip">
If you're optimizing HVAC, commission a load analysis for your building. Knowing
your load profile often reveals quick wins (higher cooling setpoint on mild days,
lower heating on sunny mornings).
</Callout>
