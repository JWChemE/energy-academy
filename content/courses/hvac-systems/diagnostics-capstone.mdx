You've learned how HVAC systems work, where they waste energy, and the controls and free-cooling strategies that keep them efficient. This capstone drops you into the plant room as the auditor — and like real life, **you don't know what you'll find until you look.**

Some of these call-outs you'll crack with a quick calculation (the fan and pump affinity laws, a chiller COP, a free-cooling sum); others you'll reason out from the symptoms. All eight are about finding **improvements and optimisations** and proving what they're worth — exactly the judgement an energy manager needs walking a building.

## How the cases work

Each is a five-step loop:

1. **Investigate** — read the brief and the raw panel readings.
2. **Calculate** — where there's a number to find (fan power at part speed, COP, free-cooling kWh, ventilation load), work it out. The **reference data is on hand** — air-side heat, the affinity laws, COP and energy prices — but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but leaning on it means no clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose** — pick the root cause(s). The same symptom can have several causes, so use your figures and the evidence.
4. **Prescribe** — build an ordered work plan (no-cost controls first, then capital).
5. **Verify** — score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

## The optimisations you'll practise

- **Free cooling** — what a stuck economiser costs when the weather would cool the building for nothing.
- **Ventilation** — the heating/cooling penalty of over-supplying fresh air, and why CO₂ tells you the answer.
- **Fan & pump power** — the affinity laws, where slowing to 80% speed roughly halves the power. Static-pressure reset and VSDs.
- **Chiller COP** — reading efficiency from cooling duty and electrical input, and what a fouled condenser really costs.
- **Controls & scheduling** — simultaneous heating and cooling, running an empty building 24/7, and a drifted sensor quietly burning fuel.

<Callout variant="key" title="Power follows the cube of speed">
The single most valuable HVAC number to carry in your head: fan and pump power scale with the **cube** of speed. Slow a fan to 80% of its speed and it draws only 0.8³ ≈ 51% of the power; at 70%, about a third. That's why duct static-pressure reset and variable-speed drives are so powerful — and why a fan or pump running flat-out against throttled dampers or valves is almost always the biggest prize in the room.
</Callout>

## Differential thinking still applies

A hot, hard-working chiller might be a fouled condenser — or low refrigerant, or simply no chilled-water reset. A waterlogged-feeling zone running warm could be a drifted sensor — or genuinely undersized heating. A fan at full tilt might need a static reset — or just a filter change. The cases are built so the *decisive reading* (the CO₂ level, the condenser approach, the calibrated reference, the box positions) tells you which.

## After you finish

- Which **single reading** ruled the red herrings out in each case?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, what goes in the report — and which are no-cost controls versus capital projects worth scheduling?
