You cannot manage what you do not measure, and you cannot show improvement without a reference point. The energy baseline and energy performance indicators are the measurement foundation of an ISO 50001 system - how it tracks whether energy performance is actually improving.

## The energy baseline

The **energy baseline** is the reference point against which performance is measured - a quantified picture of energy use over a defined period, before improvement efforts (or at a defined starting point). All future performance is compared to it.

The baseline must account for the factors that affect energy use - weather, production, occupancy - so that genuine performance improvement can be distinguished from changes in conditions. This is exactly the baseline discipline of the Measurement and Verification course, applied as the foundation of the management system. A baseline that ignores these drivers produces misleading conclusions about whether performance has really improved.

## Energy performance indicators (EnPIs)

**Energy performance indicators (EnPIs)** are the metrics that quantify energy performance - the numbers you track to see whether you are improving. Good EnPIs:
- **Reflect performance, not just consumption** - normalised for the drivers (energy per unit of output, per m2, per degree-day), so they reveal efficiency rather than just activity
- **Are relevant** to the significant energy uses and objectives
- **Are measurable** reliably and repeatably
- **Are meaningful** to the people responsible

Examples: kWh per m2 for a building, kWh per tonne for a process, COP for a chiller. The right EnPI isolates efficiency from the noise of changing conditions.

## Consumption versus performance

A crucial distinction: **total consumption** can rise while **performance** improves, or fall while performance worsens:
- A factory making more product uses more total energy, but its energy *per unit* (the EnPI) may have improved
- A building in a cold winter uses more heating energy, but normalised for degree-days, its performance may be unchanged

This is why EnPIs are normalised - they measure how *efficiently* energy is used, separating real improvement from changes in weather, output, or occupancy. Tracking raw consumption alone misleads.

## Establishing them

Setting up the baseline and EnPIs involves:
1. **Collecting energy data** over a representative period (ideally 12+ months, for the full annual cycle)
2. **Identifying the relevant drivers** (weather, production, occupancy)
3. **Relating energy to those drivers** (regression analysis, as in M&T and M&V)
4. **Defining the EnPIs** that best reflect performance for each significant energy use
5. **Recording the baseline conditions** so future comparisons are fair

## Tracking improvement

Once established, the baseline and EnPIs become the system's measuring stick:
- Performance is tracked against the baseline, adjusted for current conditions
- Improvement (or deterioration) is revealed by the EnPI trend
- Targets are expressed in EnPI terms (e.g. "reduce kWh/m2 by 10%")
- Results feed the Check stage of PDCA and the management review

This continuous comparison is what makes ISO 50001 a performance-improvement system rather than just a set of good intentions. It connects directly to the Monitoring and Targeting course (the routine tracking) and the M&V course (verifying specific savings) - the measurement disciplines that give the management system its evidence and its credibility.

## The role in continual improvement

The baseline and EnPIs close the loop of continual improvement: they reveal where performance stands, whether actions are working, and where further effort is needed. Periodically, baselines may be reset (after major changes, or to reflect a new starting point), and EnPIs refined as understanding grows. This measurement foundation is what allows the organisation to demonstrate - to itself, to auditors, and to stakeholders - that its energy performance is genuinely, continually improving.
