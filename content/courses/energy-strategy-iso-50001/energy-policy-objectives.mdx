An energy management system begins with intent - a policy that commits the organisation to managing energy, and objectives and targets that turn that commitment into measurable goals. These give the whole system its direction.

## The energy policy

The **energy policy** is the foundation document of an ISO 50001 system - a short, high-level statement of the organisation's commitment to energy management. ISO 50001 requires it to:
- Be appropriate to the organisation's purpose and scale of energy use
- Commit to **continual improvement** in energy performance
- Commit to providing the resources and information needed
- Commit to complying with legal and other requirements
- Provide the framework for setting energy objectives and targets
- Be documented, communicated, and supported by top management

The policy is deliberately brief - it sets direction and commitment, not detail. (Setting up an effective policy is the subject of its own course; here it serves as the EnMS's anchor.)

## From policy to objectives

The policy's commitment to improvement is made concrete through **objectives and targets**:
- **Objectives** are the broad goals (e.g. "reduce energy intensity across the estate")
- **Targets** are the specific, measurable, time-bound expressions of those objectives (e.g. "reduce kWh/m2 by 10% within three years")

Objectives and targets translate good intentions into something you can plan for, measure against, and be held accountable to.

## Making targets SMART

Effective targets follow the SMART discipline:
- **Specific:** clearly defined (which energy, which boundary)
- **Measurable:** quantified, so progress can be tracked (the link to EnPIs and M&V)
- **Achievable:** ambitious but realistic
- **Relevant:** aligned with the policy and the business
- **Time-bound:** with a deadline

"Use less energy" is not a target; "reduce electricity use per unit of production by 8% by the end of 2027" is.

## Aligning with the business

Energy objectives are most powerful when aligned with broader business goals:
- Linked to **cost** reduction targets
- Linked to **carbon and sustainability** commitments (net zero)
- Linked to **risk** management (energy price and supply exposure)
- Linked to **operational** goals (reliability, resilience)

Targets that serve the organisation's wider strategy attract support and resources; targets that stand alone as "energy team goals" struggle for attention (the governance and leadership links).

## Action plans

Objectives and targets are realised through **action plans** that specify:
- **What** will be done (the specific measures and projects)
- **Who** is responsible
- **By when**
- **What resources** are needed
- **How the results will be verified**

Action plans are where strategy meets delivery - the bridge from a target on paper to projects on the ground. They draw on the whole technical curriculum: which measures, on which significant energy uses, to deliver the targeted savings.

## Cascading through the organisation

For objectives to drive action, they must cascade:
- Organisation-level targets break down into site, building, and system-level targets
- These connect to individual managers' objectives and accountability (the governance link)
- Progress is monitored and reviewed at each level

Cascaded this way, the policy's high-level commitment reaches all the way down to specific actions by specific people - turning a statement of intent into measurable, accountable improvement across the organisation.
