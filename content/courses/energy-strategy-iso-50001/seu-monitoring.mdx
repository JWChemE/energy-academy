Having identified the significant energy uses, the organisation must watch them closely. Monitoring and measuring SEUs reveals how they are performing, whether improvements are working, and when something has gone wrong.

## Why monitor SEUs

The significant energy uses determine the organisation's energy result, so they warrant focused, ongoing measurement:
- To **establish** their performance baseline and EnPIs
- To **track** whether performance is improving toward targets
- To **detect** deterioration (fouling, drift, faults) quickly
- To **verify** that improvement actions delivered (the M&V link)
- To **inform** management review and decisions

What gets measured gets managed - and the SEUs are precisely what most need managing.

## Sub-metering the SEUs

Whole-site meters are too coarse to manage individual SEUs - the signal of a single chiller or process is lost in the total. **Sub-metering** the significant uses provides the granular data needed:
- Dedicated meters on major equipment and processes
- Sub-meters by building, floor, or system
- Sufficient resolution (often half-hourly) to see patterns and anomalies

Sub-metering is an investment, so it follows the SEU priority - meter the vital few in detail rather than everything equally. This is the monitoring infrastructure that the Monitoring and Targeting course covers in depth, focused here on the significant uses.

## What to measure

For each SEU, monitor:
- **Energy consumption** over time
- **The relevant EnPI** (normalised performance - per unit, per degree-day, COP)
- **The driving variables** (load, output, weather) needed to interpret the energy
- **Operating parameters** that signal efficiency (flue temperature, pressure, COP)

Measuring the drivers alongside the energy is essential - without them, you cannot tell whether a change in consumption reflects performance or just changed conditions.

## Real-time and trend monitoring

Effective SEU monitoring combines:
- **Real-time / frequent data** to catch anomalies and faults quickly (a sudden rise in a chiller's energy signals a problem now)
- **Trend analysis** to reveal gradual changes (a slow decline in efficiency over months signals fouling or drift)

The BMS, sub-metering systems, and energy monitoring software provide this continuous view, often with automatic alerts when performance deviates from expectation.

## Detecting problems

Close monitoring of SEUs catches problems that would otherwise go unnoticed:
- **Equipment degradation** - efficiency slipping as something fouls or wears (the maintenance link)
- **Control drift** - setpoints creeping, schedules slipping
- **Faults** - a stuck valve, a failed sensor, a leak
- **Unexpected operation** - equipment running when it should not

Catching these early - through monitoring the SEUs that matter most - prevents the slow, expensive accumulation of waste.

## Regular review

SEU monitoring feeds regular review at multiple levels:
- **Operational review** (daily/weekly) - spotting and fixing immediate issues
- **Performance review** (monthly) - tracking EnPIs against targets
- **Management review** (periodic) - the formal ISO 50001 review of overall performance

The data from SEU monitoring is the evidence on which all these reviews depend - turning the management system's Check stage from guesswork into measured fact.

## Closing the loop

SEU monitoring closes the management loop: it establishes where the significant uses stand, reveals whether actions are improving them, detects when they degrade, and verifies the savings. Combined with SEU identification (knowing *what* to watch) and improvement action (the next lesson - acting on what you see), it makes the significant energy uses a focus of continual, measured improvement - exactly the disciplined, evidence-based management that ISO 50001 is designed to deliver.
