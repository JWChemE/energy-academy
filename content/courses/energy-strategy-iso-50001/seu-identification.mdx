Not all energy uses are equal. A small number usually dominate consumption, and focusing effort there delivers most of the savings. Identifying these significant energy uses is a central requirement of ISO 50001 and a powerful principle in its own right.

## What an SEU is

A **significant energy use (SEU)** is an energy use that accounts for substantial consumption, or that offers considerable potential for improvement. ISO 50001 requires organisations to identify their SEUs and focus management attention on them - because that is where the savings are.

## The Pareto principle

SEU analysis rests on the **Pareto principle** - the 80/20 rule. In most organisations, roughly 80% of energy is consumed by around 20% of the uses:
- A handful of large motors, a boiler, a chiller, a major process
- A few energy-intensive buildings in a large estate

Trying to manage everything equally wastes effort on trivial uses while the big consumers go unexamined. SEU analysis directs attention to the vital few that determine the result. Fix the SEUs and you have addressed the bulk of the energy; tinker with the trivial many and you achieve little.

## Conducting an energy review

SEUs are identified through an **energy review** - a systematic analysis of where energy goes:
1. **Gather data** on energy consumption across the organisation (bills, meters, sub-meters)
2. **Break down consumption** by site, building, system, and process
3. **Identify the largest uses** - the SEUs by consumption
4. **Identify high-potential uses** - those with significant savings opportunity, even if not the largest
5. **Rank and prioritise** them

This review draws directly on the energy-audit and monitoring disciplines from earlier courses - sub-metering, consumption analysis, and benchmarking.

## Significance by consumption and by opportunity

An energy use can be significant for two reasons:
- **Large consumption:** it simply uses a lot of energy (a major chiller, a furnace)
- **High improvement potential:** it may be smaller, but badly run, with large savings available

Both qualify as SEUs. A well-run large consumer and a poorly-run medium consumer may offer similar savings potential - so significance considers not just size, but the gap between current and achievable performance.

## Understanding the SEUs

For each SEU, ISO 50001 expects you to understand:
- The **current performance** and the relevant EnPI (kWh per unit, COP, etc.)
- The **variables** that affect its energy use (the drivers - load, weather, output)
- The **people** who affect its performance (operators, maintainers)
- The **improvement potential** and how to realise it

This understanding is what turns "this is a big energy user" into "here is specifically how we will improve it."

## Focusing resources

The practical power of SEU analysis is **focus**. With limited time, money, and attention, the organisation concentrates on the uses that matter:
- Detailed monitoring and sub-metering on the SEUs
- Maintenance attention prioritised on the SEUs (the maintenance link)
- Improvement projects targeted at the SEUs
- Management review focused on SEU performance

The trivial many are not ignored entirely, but they do not consume the effort that belongs to the vital few.

## The ongoing nature

SEUs are not fixed forever. As the organisation changes - new equipment, new processes, successful improvements - the significant uses shift. Today's biggest consumer, once optimised, may cede its place; a new process may become an SEU. The energy review is repeated periodically, keeping the focus on where energy *currently* goes and where the *current* opportunities lie. This continual re-focusing on the vital few is how ISO 50001 directs effort to maximum effect - the strategic heart of systematic energy management.
