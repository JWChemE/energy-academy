The [thermal demand lesson](/courses/breweries/steam-and-hot-water-demand) found two things worth recovering from every single batch: the sensible heat in the wort as it cools from boiling to pitching temperature, and the latent heat carried away in the vapour driven off during the boil itself. This lesson quantifies the second, and asks a bigger question about the brewhouse's whole energy supply.

## Recovering the boil-off vapour

Every batch's 60–90 minute boil evaporates water as vapour: 150.5 kWh of latent heat per 30 hL batch, calculated in the thermal demand lesson. Left to vent to atmosphere, that heat is gone entirely. A **vapour condenser** (essentially a heat exchanger fitted above the kettle, condensing the escaping vapour against incoming cold liquor) recovers a meaningful share of it, typically around 50–60% is achievable in practice, since capturing and condensing vapour is inherently less complete than a liquid-to-liquid exchange like the wort cooler.

> Recovered per batch = 150.5 kWh × 0.55 ≈ **82.8 kWh**
> Across 1,200 batches/year: 82.8 × 1,200 ≈ **99,300 kWh/year**
> At £0.06/kWh gas: **≈ £5,960/year**

<Callout variant="key" title="Two separate heat-recovery opportunities, not one">
Vapour condensing (this lesson) and wort-cooling heat recovery (quantified in [this course's diagnostic case](/courses/breweries/two-quick-diagnostics), worth roughly £14,000/yr for the same batch size) are genuinely independent projects: one captures heat leaving as vapour during the boil, the other captures heat leaving as hot water after the boil. A well-designed brewhouse retrofit does both, and the two paybacks add rather than compete, because they recover heat at different points in the process.
</Callout>

## Why breweries often suit CHP unusually well

Step back from individual heat-recovery projects and look at the brewhouse's whole demand profile: a brewery needs **steam or hot water and electricity simultaneously and fairly continuously**. The boiler runs through the brewing day while the glycol plant runs around the clock for fermentation and conditioning. That combination, steady heat demand alongside steady power demand, is exactly the load profile the [CHP & Cogeneration course](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/how-chp-works) identifies as the precondition for a good CHP business case.

Where a building with a spiky, weather-driven heat load (like most offices) makes CHP hard to size well, a brewery's comparatively steady, production-driven heat demand (running most of the brewing week, not just a few cold months) is a much better match. This doesn't mean every brewery should install CHP; the [CHP economics lesson](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/heat-demand) covers the sizing and spark-spread analysis needed to check a specific site properly. But it's worth putting on the table explicitly in a brewery audit, in a way it might not be for a typical commercial building.

## Bringing it together

A brewhouse energy review worth its fee checks, in order:

1. **Is the boil-off rate itself excessive?** (the [thermal demand lesson's](/courses/breweries/steam-and-hot-water-demand) first lever: a modern low-boil kettle cuts the latent-heat component before you even consider recovering it)
2. **Is vapour being condensed and recovered**, or vented?
3. **Is the cooled wort's heat being recovered**, or is the cooling water going to drain? (quantified in this course's diagnostic case)
4. **Does the site's combined heat-and-power demand profile justify a CHP feasibility study?**

Each of these is a genuinely separate lever, and a thorough audit checks all four rather than stopping at the first one found.
