Before you can manage a brewery's energy, you need to be able to follow a batch through the building — because unlike an office or a generic factory, a brewery's energy demand is dictated by a fixed sequence of process steps, each with its own temperature, its own duration, and its own utility. This lesson walks that sequence once; the rest of this course keeps coming back to it.

## From malted grain to packaged beer

**Malting** happens off-site, at a maltster — steeping, germinating and kilning barley into malt. It's outside a brewery's own energy bill and, as the [system boundaries lesson](/courses/mass-and-energy-balances/system-boundaries) would put it, outside the boundary of a brewery energy audit too. Everything from here on is what happens at the brewery itself.

1. **Milling** — the malt is crushed into grist. A short, low-energy mechanical step.
2. **Mashing** — grist is mixed with hot water (typically 65–72 °C) in the mash tun and held for 60–90 minutes. Enzymes in the malt convert starches into fermentable sugars. This is the first major heat demand of the day.
3. **Lautering** — the sweet liquid (wort) is separated from the spent grain husks, which are drained and usually sold on as animal feed.
4. **The wort boil** — the wort is boiled in the copper (kettle) for 60–90 minutes, with hops added at intervals. This single step is the brewhouse's single biggest thermal load, covered in detail in the next lesson.
5. **Whirlpool** — the boiled wort is spun to settle out solid trub (spent hops and protein) before it moves on.
6. **Wort cooling** — the near-boiling wort is rapidly cooled to fermentation "pitching" temperature (roughly 18–22 °C for ale, 7–13 °C for lager) through a plate heat exchanger, against cold water or glycol. This is the brewery's clearest heat-recovery opportunity, and gets its own lesson later in this course.
7. **Fermentation** — yeast is pitched and converts sugars to alcohol and CO₂ over several days to two weeks. The reaction is exothermic — it generates heat that must be actively removed to hold the target temperature, via cooling jackets on the fermentation vessel.
8. **Conditioning (maturation)** — the beer is held cold (often near 0 °C) for days to weeks to clarify and develop flavour. Lagers need longer, colder conditioning than ales — a real driver of cellar refrigeration load.
9. **Packaging** — kegging, casking, canning or bottling, frequently involving pasteurisation (heating then cooling) and always preceded by a CIP (clean-in-place) cycle of the vessel or line being used.

<Callout variant="key" title="Two loads dominate, and they're opposites">
The wort boil (step 4) is the brewery's biggest **thermal** load — steam or hot water, at high temperature, for a fixed duration every batch. Fermentation and conditioning (steps 7–8) are the brewery's biggest **electrical** load — refrigeration, running continuously, for days at a time. Almost everything in this course comes back to managing one of these two loads well, or connecting them to each other through heat recovery.
</Callout>

## Why the sequence matters for energy management

Unlike a building where load is driven mostly by occupancy and weather, a brewery's load is driven by **where a batch currently is in this sequence** — how many mash tuns are filling, how many coppers are boiling, how many fermenters are in peak fermentation versus settled conditioning. A walkthrough audit that doesn't track this sequence will see a snapshot of activity that looks arbitrary; one that does will see a predictable, repeating pattern tied directly to the brewing schedule.

<Callout variant="tip">
When you next read an energy bill or a half-hourly load profile for a brewery, try to name which stage of the process is driving each spike. A gas spike most likely means the copper is boiling; a sustained, flat electrical load most likely means the cellar's fermenters are holding steady; a short sharp electrical spike is more likely the packaging line or CIP pump starting up.
</Callout>
