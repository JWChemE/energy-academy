A brewery's total energy bill tells you almost nothing on its own — a brewery using 500,000 kWh a year could be excellent or terrible, depending on whether it made 10,000 hectolitres or 40,000. The way to compare breweries fairly, to each other and to their own history, is **energy intensity**: energy per hectolitre of beer produced. This is the same normalisation principle from the [monitoring & targeting course](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/normalisation), applied to the one driver that matters most in a brewery: production volume.

## Typical intensity ranges

Brewing industry benchmarking (broadly consistent across sources, though methods and site types vary) puts typical energy intensity in these ranges:

| Energy type | Typical range | What drives it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Thermal (gas) | ~15–30 kWh/hL | Mashing, boiling, CIP hot water, pasteurisation |
| Electrical | ~10–20 kWh/hL | Refrigeration, compressed air, pumps, packaging, lighting |

A brewery running near the bottom of both ranges is doing well; one running above the top of either range has real opportunities waiting to be found — exactly what the [thermal demand](/courses/breweries/steam-and-hot-water-demand) and [refrigeration demand](/courses/breweries/refrigeration-and-cooling-demand) lessons just quantified from first principles.

<Callout variant="tip">
You'll compute both of these intensities directly from real production and utility data in this course's capstone — 24 kWh/hL thermal and around 17.6 kWh/hL electrical, for a brewery you'll audit stage by stage.
</Callout>

## Why craft breweries run less efficiently than macro-breweries

Scale matters enormously in brewing, for the same reason it matters in almost every process industry: fixed losses don't shrink with output. A mash tun, a boiler, and a glycol chiller all have standing losses — radiated heat, standing electrical load, minimum practical batch sizes — that stay roughly constant whether the brewery makes 5,000 or 50,000 hectolitres a year. Spread over more production, those fixed losses become a smaller share of the total; spread over less, they dominate.

This is why a large, modern macro-brewery can often run at the low end of both benchmark ranges (well-optimised, huge production volumes diluting every fixed loss), while an equally well-run craft brewery, brewing smaller, more varied batches on the same fundamental equipment, will typically sit higher in both ranges — not through mismanagement, but through scale.

<Callout variant="key" title="Compare like with like">
Don't judge a 5,000 hL/year craft brewery against a benchmark built from 500,000 hL/year national brands. If you can, benchmark against breweries of a similar scale and process style (ale vs lager — lager's longer, colder conditioning genuinely costs more refrigeration energy per hectolitre than ale). Absent that, use the ranges above as a sense-check, not a verdict.
</Callout>

## The water-energy link

Brewing is also a water-intensive process — grain rinsing, CIP, cooling and general washdown all use water, and most of that water arrives cold and, for several of those uses, needs heating. The industry commonly reports a **water-to-beer ratio**: litres of water used per litre of beer produced. Efficient large breweries can approach 3:1; less efficient smaller sites can run 6:1 or higher.

This ratio matters for energy management directly: every litre of water heated for mashing, sparging or CIP carries its own energy cost (recall the [mass and energy balance](/courses/mass-and-energy-balances/conservation-of-mass) principle — track the water, and the energy tied to it usually follows). A brewery that reduces its water-to-beer ratio through better rinsing practice, right-sized CIP cycles, and water reuse is very often reducing its energy intensity at the same time, for free.

## Setting your own baseline

The single most useful first step in any brewery energy review is establishing this site's own historical intensity trend — plotting kWh/hL (thermal and electrical separately) over recent years or months, exactly as the [establishing your baseline lesson](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/establishing-baseline) describes. A rising trend, production held constant, is the clearest possible signal that something (fouling, drift, a failing control) needs investigating — before you ever compare to an external benchmark at all.
