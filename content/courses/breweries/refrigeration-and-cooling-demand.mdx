If the wort boil sets the gas bill, fermentation and conditioning set the electricity bill. Both are refrigeration loads — cooling jackets on vessels, run by a glycol (or ammonia) chiller plant — but they have very different shapes, and it's easy to guess wrong about which one actually costs more over a year. This lesson uses the [refrigeration COP concept](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/cop-and-performance) from the systems courses to find out.

## Fermentation: short, intense

Fermentation is exothermic — yeast converting sugar to alcohol releases real heat, and a cooling jacket has to remove it continuously to hold the target temperature (roughly 18–22 °C for ale, 7–13 °C for lager). For a 40 hL fermenter running at a typical peak rate of about 0.4 kW per hectolitre:

> Peak cooling duty = 40 hL × 0.4 kW/hL = **16 kW**

That peak doesn't hold for the whole fermentation — it's highest in the first few days as yeast activity peaks, then tapers off. Averaged across a 12-day active fermentation at roughly 6 kW:

> Thermal load = 6 kW × 12 days × 24 h = **1,728 kWh** (thermal, to be removed)

At a glycol chiller COP of 3.5 (squarely in the range from the [heat pump performance lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/cop-and-performance)):

> Electrical input = 1,728 ÷ 3.5 ≈ **494 kWh** per fermentation cycle

## Conditioning: long, gentle

Once fermentation finishes, the beer moves to a conditioning (or "lagering") tank, held near 0 °C for clarification and flavour development — far longer than active fermentation, but at a much gentler rate: mostly just holding against ambient heat gain through the tank and cellar insulation, say 2 kW for a comparable vessel:

> Thermal load = 2 kW × 14 days × 24 h = **672 kWh**
> Electrical input = 672 ÷ 3.5 ≈ **192 kWh** per conditioning cycle

## The non-obvious comparison

<Callout variant="key" title="Short and intense beats long and gentle here">
Despite running for less time, fermentation's electrical cost (≈494 kWh) is more than **2.5 times** conditioning's (≈192 kWh) for this vessel. It's tempting to assume the multi-week cold-conditioning period must be the bigger cost simply because it lasts longer — but intensity matters as much as duration, exactly the same lesson the [problem table algorithm](/courses/pinch-analysis/problem-table-algorithm) teaches about heat loads generally: you have to do the arithmetic, not just eyeball the schedule.
</Callout>

## Why this shapes the rest of the course

Two consequences follow directly from these numbers, and both come back later in this course:

- **The glycol plant is sized and run for fermentation's peak**, not conditioning's steady load — which is exactly why the glycol chiller's own condition (a fouled condenser, a poorly chosen setpoint) matters so much: it's working hardest during the highest-value part of the cycle.
- **Refrigeration, not the brewhouse, is usually the single biggest electrical end use on a brewery site** — a fact worth confirming with sub-metering (covered in the compliance and benchmarking lessons ahead), since a whole-site electricity meter bundles it in with packaging, pumps and lighting and hides exactly how dominant it is.

<Callout variant="tip">
When you next look at a brewery's electricity profile, expect it to look nothing like a typical office building's: instead of a daytime peak and a night-time trough, expect a fairly flat, continuous load — because fermentation and conditioning don't know or care what time it is.
</Callout>
