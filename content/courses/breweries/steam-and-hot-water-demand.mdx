The wort boil is the reason a brewery's gas bill looks the way it does. This lesson puts a number on the brewhouse's thermal demand, batch by batch, using nothing more than the sensible and latent heat calculations from the [Mass & Energy Balances course](/courses/mass-and-energy-balances/hvac-energy-balance) and the [steam properties lesson](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-basics).

## Three heating steps, one batch

Take a typical 30 hL batch and follow its heat demand through the brewhouse:

**1. Heating the brewing liquor.** Around 35 hL of water is heated from mains temperature (≈12 °C) to mash temperature (≈68 °C):

> Heat = mass × specific heat × ΔT = 3,500 kg × 4.18 kJ/kg·K × 56 °C ÷ 3,600 ≈ **227.6 kWh**

**2. Raising the wort to boiling.** After mashing and lautering, the wort itself (≈3,000 kg for a 30 hL batch) must rise from mash temperature to a full boil:

> 3,000 kg × 4.18 kJ/kg·K × (100 − 68) °C ÷ 3,600 ≈ **111.5 kWh**

**3. Maintaining the boil.** A traditional 60–90 minute boil evaporates around 8% of the batch volume — for 30 hL, that's 240 kg of water driven off as vapour, absorbing its full latent heat of vaporisation (2,257 kJ/kg, the same figure from the steam properties lesson):

> 240 kg × 2,257 kJ/kg ÷ 3,600 ≈ **150.5 kWh**

<Callout variant="key" title="Add it up, and the boil dominates">
Total process heat per batch: 227.6 + 111.5 + 150.5 ≈ **489.5 kWh**. Notice that raising and maintaining the boil (111.5 + 150.5 ≈ 262 kWh) is a bigger share than heating the mash liquor alone — the boil isn't just "the last step before cooling," it's the single largest heat demand in the whole brewhouse, batch after batch.
</Callout>

## From process heat to gas meter reading

That 489.5 kWh is useful heat delivered to water and wort — it isn't what the gas meter records, because the boiler itself isn't 100% efficient. At a typical 85% boiler efficiency (squarely in the range from the [boiler efficiency lesson](/courses/boilers-and-fired-systems/combustion-efficiency)):

> Gas input per batch = 489.5 ÷ 0.85 ≈ **575.9 kWh**

Scale that to 1,200 batches a year (a plausible schedule for a mid-size regional brewery) and the brewhouse alone accounts for roughly **691,000 kWh/year** of gas — before counting CIP, pasteurisation or space heating, covered in the next module.

## Where the energy actually goes

Look back at the three steps: mashing (227.6 kWh) is straightforward sensible heating — hard to avoid, though not impossible to trim with better insulation and faster liquor transfer. The boil (262 kWh combined) is where the real leverage sits:

- **Boil-off rate** is a direct dial on energy use — a modern low-boil-off kettle achieving 4% evaporation instead of 8% roughly halves the latent-heat component of the boil, a substantial, provable saving.
- **The evaporated vapour itself carries that 150.5 kWh/batch away** — venting it to atmosphere wastes exactly the heat you just paid to put into it. Capturing it (vapour condensing) is one of the two heat-recovery opportunities covered later in this course, alongside cooling the boiled wort back down.

<Callout variant="tip">
Whenever you're sizing or auditing a brewhouse boiler, work from the batch-level heat demand upward (as this lesson just did), not from a nameplate rating downward. It tells you not just how big the boiler needs to be, but exactly which of the three heating steps is worth attacking first.
</Callout>
