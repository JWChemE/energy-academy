Compressed air and the site's ancillary loads rarely dominate a brewery's energy bill the way the boiler or the glycol plant do — but they're everywhere, they're easy to leave running, and the [compressed air course](/courses/compressed-air/compression-fundamentals) elsewhere on this platform already covers the physics in full. This lesson is about *where* a brewery specifically uses air and ancillary power, not how compressors work.

## Where compressed air shows up in a brewery

- **Pneumatic valves and actuators** — CIP systems, transfer lines and the brewhouse itself are full of pneumatically-actuated valves, switching constantly during a brew or a clean.
- **Can and bottle handling** — packaging lines use compressed air for conveying, orienting, and blow-off (clearing debris or moisture from empty containers before filling).
- **Keg washing and filling** — pneumatic keg-washing equipment and some filling systems use compressed air directly.
- **General pneumatic tools** — maintenance and cellar work.

None of these are individually large, but a brewery's compressed air system typically runs for most of the production day, and — as the [compressed air course](/courses/compressed-air/compression-fundamentals) establishes — a poorly maintained system leaks continuously, 24 hours a day, whether or not the plant is actually producing.

<Callout variant="key" title="The same leak-cost math applies here">
A single 3 mm compressed-air leak at typical brewery supply pressure wastes on the order of 2–2.5 kW continuously — the exact figure and the method for finding leaks (ultrasonic detection, a leak-tagging walk during a quiet shutdown) are covered in full in the [compressed air course](/courses/compressed-air/compression-fundamentals). A brewery running its compressor 24/7 "just in case," rather than only when the packaging line or CIP system genuinely needs air, is one of the most common and cheapest-to-fix findings in a brewery audit.
</Callout>

## The ancillary loads that add up

Beyond compressed air, a handful of steady, easy-to-overlook loads round out the electricity bill:

- **Pumps** — wort, water, CIP and cellar transfer pumps run throughout the production day; oversized or un-controlled pumps (fixed speed where variable speed would do) waste energy exactly as the [motors and drives course](/courses/motors-and-drives/why-vfds) describes.
- **Cold store standing losses** — packaged product cold stores and any dedicated keg/cask chill rooms lose heat continuously through their fabric, entirely independent of production, in the same way a poorly insulated building does — see the [insulation systems course](/courses/insulation-systems/insulation-fundamentals).
- **Lighting** — production areas are often lit to a higher standard than necessary for the actual task, and cellars and stores are frequently lit around the clock out of habit rather than need.

## Why these matter for the audit, even though they're individually small

None of compressed air, pumping, cold-store losses or lighting will out-rank the brewhouse or refrigeration in a ranked opportunity list — but together, they routinely make up 10–20% of total site electricity, and they share one crucial property: **most of the fixes are cheap, fast, and don't touch the brewing process at all.** A leak repair, a lighting control upgrade, or fitting a VFD to an oversized pump can often be actioned in weeks, while the brewhouse heat-recovery project (covered later in this course) needs capital approval and a shutdown to install.

<Callout variant="tip">
In the ranked opportunity list you'll build in this course's capstone, don't let compressed air and ancillary loads get crowded out by the two headline systems. They're exactly the "quick wins" that build momentum and credibility before a client commits to the bigger capital projects.
</Callout>
