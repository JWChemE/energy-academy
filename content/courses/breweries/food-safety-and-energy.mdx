Every efficiency idea in this course has an unspoken boundary: food safety. Some of a brewery's biggest energy uses (CIP temperature, cold-chain integrity) exist because hygiene regulation requires them, not because nobody's got around to optimising them. Knowing exactly where that boundary sits is what separates a credible energy recommendation from an unsafe one.

## HACCP: the framework behind the limits

UK food and drink businesses, breweries included, operate under **HACCP** (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points) principles, retained in UK law from EU food hygiene regulation. HACCP requires identifying every point in the process where a hazard (microbial contamination, in brewing's case) could be introduced, and setting a **critical limit**: a measurable value, like a temperature or a cleaning concentration, that must never be crossed.

Two of the limits set this way land directly on top of the energy-intensive processes covered earlier in this course:

- **CIP temperature and duration**: the [CIP lesson](/courses/breweries/cip-and-packaging-energy) quantified a 75 °C caustic wash. That temperature (and the contact time held at it) isn't arbitrary: it's set to reliably kill the microorganisms that would otherwise contaminate the next batch. Lowering it to save energy without validating that it still achieves the required kill is a genuine food-safety incident waiting to happen, not an efficiency win.
- **Cold-chain temperature**: packaged product cold stores, and any pasteurised product awaiting despatch, have temperature limits tied to shelf life and microbiological safety, not just customer preference for a cold beer.

<Callout variant="warning" title="A HACCP critical limit is not a setpoint to be optimised">
Where the [refrigeration and glycol optimisation lesson](/courses/breweries/refrigeration-and-glycol-optimisation) later in this course talks about relaxing a setpoint, it always means relaxing within the range HACCP and process requirements already allow, never crossing a validated critical limit to save a few kWh. If you're not certain whether a limit is a genuine food-safety requirement or an inherited, unquestioned default, the answer is to ask a food safety professional or check the site's HACCP plan, not to guess.
</Callout>

## Where the genuine headroom actually is

This isn't a reason to treat CIP and cold-chain energy as untouchable: it's a reason to be precise about *where* the headroom is:

- **CIP frequency** is often more flexible than CIP temperature: cleaning a vessel that doesn't need it yet (a fixed weekly schedule regardless of actual use) wastes energy without any food-safety benefit at all: the earlier CIP lesson's "how often, and is it triggered by actual need?" question exists exactly to find this.
- **Fermentation and conditioning setpoints** are usually process and recipe decisions, not HACCP-mandated: there's often genuine room to review whether a 2 °C margin has been built in "just to be safe" without ever being validated against what the yeast strain and beer style actually need.
- **Packaging and cold-store setpoints** may have real HACCP-linked limits, but the *margin* held above the regulatory minimum is worth checking: a store running 3 °C colder than its actual requirement, "for safety," is spending energy on a safety margin nobody has ever confirmed is necessary.

## The audit discipline this creates

Every efficiency recommendation touching temperature, cleaning or the cold chain should carry an explicit answer to one question: **is this limit food-safety-mandated, or is it an inherited convention?** The first gets left alone; the second is a legitimate opportunity. Getting this distinction right, and being able to show the client you checked, is what makes a brewery energy audit credible to a food business, rather than a well-meaning outsider's list of generic energy tips.
