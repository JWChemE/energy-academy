A brewery doesn't just buy energy and water: it pays to get rid of what's left over, and that bill is driven by more than volume. Understanding how trade effluent is actually charged reveals a genuine, if easy to miss, financial link between energy efficiency and the water bill.

## Why breweries need a trade effluent consent

Any UK business discharging non-domestic wastewater to the public sewer needs a **trade effluent consent** from its local water/sewerage company, under the Water Industry Act 1991. Brewery effluent is a serious candidate for tight consent conditions, because it's:

- **High in BOD and COD** (biochemical and chemical oxygen demand), spent yeast, residual sugars, and cleaning chemicals are all highly organic, and treating that load at the local sewage works costs the water company real money.
- **Sometimes warm**: hot CIP rinse water and pasteuriser discharge can raise effluent temperature, which consent conditions typically cap.
- **Variable in pH**: caustic and acid CIP stages can each push pH outside normal limits if discharged without neutralising or blending.

A consent sets limits on flow rate, BOD/COD, temperature, pH and suspended solids, and in England and Wales, the trade effluent charge is calculated using a formula (commonly known as the **Mogden formula**) that charges for volume *and* for strength (how concentrated the BOD/COD load is) separately. A brewery discharging the same volume of water, but at twice the organic strength, pays significantly more: not because it used more water, but because it made the water company's job harder.

<Callout variant="key" title="Two charge components, two different levers">
Reducing the **volume** discharged (through water reuse) and reducing the **strength** of what's discharged (through better yeast/solids recovery, not dumping strong wash liquors down the drain) both lower the trade effluent bill, and they're largely independent levers, worth tackling separately.
</Callout>

## Where this connects directly to energy

The [wort heat-recovery case](/courses/breweries/heat-recovery-in-the-brewhouse) earlier in this course captures the heat from wort-cooling water rather than sending it to drain. Do that properly (recovering the *water* as well as its heat, reusing it as brewing liquor for the next batch rather than discharging it) and you cut the trade effluent bill on the **volume** side at the same time you cut the gas bill. For a brewery cooling roughly 30 hL of wort per batch across 1,200 batches a year, that's on the order of 3,600 m³/year of water that never needs to reach the drain (and be paid for) at all, if the recovery loop is designed to reuse the water rather than just extract its heat.

<Callout variant="tip">
When you scope a heat-recovery project in a brewery, always ask whether the water itself, not just its heat, can be reused. The energy saving alone often justifies the project: the trade effluent saving on top of it can meaningfully shorten the payback, and it rarely appears in a first-pass energy-only calculation.
</Callout>

## Practical steps

- **Know your consent limits**: flow, BOD/COD, temperature and pH, and check current discharge against them; consents are sometimes tighter than actual practice requires, and sometimes routinely breached without anyone noticing until enforcement action follows.
- **Segregate strong waste streams where practical**: spent yeast and strong CIP liquors disposed of separately (often to a specialist waste route, sometimes even sold as a by-product) rather than blended into general effluent can meaningfully cut the strength-based charge.
- **Cool hot discharges before they reach the sewer connection**, or better, recover that heat productively, both satisfy a temperature limit and capture value that would otherwise be thrown away twice: once as wasted energy, once as an avoidable effluent charge.
