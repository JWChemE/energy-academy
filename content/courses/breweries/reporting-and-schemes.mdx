## ESOS and SECR: mostly a large-brewery question

Both [ESOS](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/esos) (mandatory energy audits every four years) and [SECR](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/secr) (streamlined energy and carbon reporting) apply only to **large undertakings** — broadly, businesses with 250+ employees, or turnover and balance-sheet size above set thresholds.

<Callout variant="key" title="Most craft and regional breweries are SMEs, and exempt">
The overwhelming majority of UK breweries — the whole "craft beer" boom of the last fifteen years — are small or medium enterprises, comfortably below the ESOS and SECR employee and turnover thresholds. If you're auditing a brewery of the scale used in this course's capstone, ESOS and SECR compliance is very likely **not** a driver for the work — the client wants the audit because it saves them money, not because a regulator requires it. Large national and multinational brewing groups are a different matter entirely, and sit squarely in scope for both.
</Callout>

Get this right early in a scoping conversation — recommending a full ESOS-compliant Level 2 audit process to a client who isn't even in scope wastes their money on compliance rigour they don't need (though the *audit itself* is still worth doing on its financial merits alone).

## The brewing sector's Climate Change Agreement

The [Climate Change Levy (CCL) lesson](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/climate-change-levy) explained that CCL is charged on business energy use. What it didn't cover: several energy-intensive sectors, including parts of the brewing and malting industry, have historically held a **Climate Change Agreement (CCA)** — a sector-level voluntary agreement, negotiated (in brewing's case, generally through an industry trade body) between the sector and government, that gives a substantial discount on the CCL rate in exchange for the sector meeting agreed energy-efficiency or carbon targets.

CCAs are reviewed and renewed periodically, and eligibility and the specific target details change over their life — so always check current eligibility and terms for the specific site and time period, rather than assuming historic terms still apply. The mechanism itself, though, is a genuinely useful one to know: it means a qualifying brewery's efficiency improvements aren't just saving energy costs directly, they may also be protecting a preferential CCL rate the sector has negotiated collectively.

## Packaging producer responsibility

Increasingly significant, and less obviously "energy" but very much a live cost pressure for a packaging-heavy business: UK **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)** for packaging places a growing share of the cost of collecting and recycling packaging waste onto the businesses that put it on the market — bottles, cans, cardboard multipacks, and kegs are all in scope. This isn't an energy scheme, but it sits alongside energy costs as another line pushing breweries toward using less material and being more efficient with what they do use — reducing packaging weight and improving material choices reduces embodied energy and carbon as well as the EPR fee itself.

## The practical scoping checklist

Before writing any compliance section into a brewery audit report, confirm:

1. **Size** — employee count, turnover, balance sheet: is this brewery actually in scope for ESOS/SECR at all?
2. **Ownership** — a small brewery owned by a large group may be swept into the *group's* compliance obligations even if it wouldn't qualify alone.
3. **CCA status** — does this site (or its trade body membership) currently hold a Climate Change Agreement, and if so, what targets is it tracking against?
4. **Packaging** — is packaging producer responsibility a material cost line worth flagging alongside the energy findings, even though it's a different regulatory regime?

Getting this scoping right is itself part of a credible, brewery-specific audit — a generic "here's what UK energy law says" section, without checking which parts actually apply to this client, undermines the report's credibility just as much as a missed cross-pinch violation would.
