A net-zero goal decades away means nothing without a plan to reach it. A roadmap sequences the journey into phases - quick wins first, deep cuts later - turning a distant target into a series of concrete, scheduled steps. The sequencing is not arbitrary; it follows a logic that minimises cost and maximises effect.

## Why phasing matters

Net zero is a long journey - often two or three decades. A distant target alone drives no present action and risks the hard work being deferred until too late. A **roadmap** breaks the journey into phases with interim milestones, so the organisation knows what to do now, next, and later. The phasing turns "net zero by 2040" into a sequence of concrete, near-term steps - and the order of those steps matters enormously for cost and effectiveness.

## The sequencing logic

The net-zero roadmap follows a deliberate sequence (the net-zero-pathway, electrification, and renewable links):

1. **Reduce demand (efficiency first)** - cut energy use through fabric, efficiency, and behaviour
2. **Electrify** - replace fossil-fuel heat and transport with electric (heat pumps, EVs)
3. **Generate and procure renewably** - power it all with clean electricity
4. **Store and flex** - balance variable renewable supply with demand
5. **Address residual with removals** - balance the genuinely hard-to-eliminate emissions

This order is not arbitrary - each step makes the next cheaper and easier. It is the unifying logic that connects the whole curriculum's technical and strategic disciplines into a coherent path.

## Why efficiency comes first

Reducing demand first is the foundation (the net-zero-pathway and MAC-curve links):
- It is usually the cheapest abatement - often negative-cost, paying for itself (the MAC-curve link)
- It reduces the demand that must then be electrified and supplied renewably
- A smaller demand means smaller, cheaper heat pumps, less renewable generation, less storage
- It delivers savings that fund and motivate the later, harder steps

Skipping efficiency means over-sizing and over-spending on everything that follows - electrifying and powering waste. This is why "fabric first" and the efficiency disciplines (the technical courses) precede the rest. The cheapest, most profitable measures come first, building momentum and funding.

## Why electrify before fully decarbonising supply

Electrification comes next (the electrification link):
- It shifts energy onto electricity, which can be decarbonised
- Doing it after efficiency means smaller, cheaper electrified systems
- Electrified systems get cleaner automatically as the grid and renewables decarbonise
- It sizes the renewable generation needed for the true future electrical load

Electrifying after reducing demand, but as a distinct phase before relying wholly on renewables, sequences the shift onto clean electricity sensibly.

## Quick wins and momentum

A crucial practical aspect of phasing is starting with quick wins (the leadership and investment-case links):
- **Early phases** focus on cheap, fast-payback, often profitable measures (efficiency)
- These build credibility, momentum, and savings
- The savings and credibility fund and justify the larger, harder, later investments
- Visible early progress sustains support and engagement (the leadership link)

This sequencing - quick wins first, deep cuts later - is both economically logical (cheapest first - the MAC-curve link) and strategically wise (building the momentum and funding for the hard parts). It connects the roadmap to the leadership journey of building support over time.

## Interim milestones

A roadmap needs interim milestones, not just a distant end date (the targets link):
- **Near-term targets** (e.g. 30% reduction by 2027) drive present action
- **Medium-term targets** (e.g. 60% by 2032) maintain trajectory
- **The end goal** (net zero by 2040) as the destination

Interim milestones create accountability now, reveal slippage early, and turn the distant goal into a series of checkable steps. Without them, a long-term target invites procrastination - the hard work deferred until it is too late to achieve. The milestones force steady progress and allow course correction.

## The hard-to-abate residual

The roadmap recognises that the last emissions are the hardest (the MAC-curve and offset links):
- **Early phases** tackle the cheap, easy abatement (efficiency, then electrification and renewables)
- **Later phases** confront the harder, more expensive emissions
- **The residual** - genuinely hard-to-eliminate emissions (some industrial heat, some scope 3) - is addressed last, with removals (the offset link)

The roadmap should be realistic that the final reductions are the toughest, sequencing the easier abatement first and confronting the hard residual - and the role of removals - later. This honesty about the hard part is essential to a credible roadmap (the credibility link).

## Building the roadmap

Constructing a net-zero roadmap involves:
- **Baseline** - measuring current emissions across scopes (the carbon-accounting link)
- **Target** - a credible net-zero goal, science-aligned, with interim milestones (the net-zero-definition link)
- **Options** - identifying and prioritising measures (the MAC-curve link)
- **Sequence** - phasing them (efficiency, electrify, renewables, store, residual)
- **Investment** - the financing for each phase (the financing course)
- **Milestones** - interim targets and a delivery schedule
- **Governance** - accountability for delivery (the governance link)

The result is a phased, costed, scheduled plan that turns the net-zero goal into concrete action - knowing what to do now, next, and later, in the order that minimises cost and maximises effect.

## The roadmap as the integrating plan

The net-zero roadmap is, in a sense, the integrating plan of the whole academy. It sequences the efficiency, electrification, and renewable measures of the technical courses; draws on the investment-case and financing courses to fund each phase; relies on the leadership, policy, and change courses to drive and govern it; and uses the carbon accounting and credibility disciplines to measure and report it. The phased roadmap - efficiency first, then electrify, then renewables, then store and flex, with removals for the residual - is how all these disciplines combine into a credible, deliverable path to net zero. The final lessons address the role of offsets and removals for the residual, and the transparent reporting that makes the whole journey credible.
