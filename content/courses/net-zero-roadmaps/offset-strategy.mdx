Even after deep reductions, some emissions remain that are genuinely hard to eliminate. Offsets and removals address this residual - but their use is fraught with credibility risks. Using them properly, as a last resort for the residual rather than a substitute for reduction, is essential to credible net zero.

## The role of offsets

After an organisation has reduced its emissions as far as possible - through efficiency, electrification, and renewables - a **residual** of hard-to-eliminate emissions usually remains. **Offsets and removals** address this residual, balancing it so the net effect is zero. The critical principle (the net-zero-definition link) is that offsets are for the *residual only* - the genuinely hard-to-eliminate emissions after deep reduction - not a substitute for the reduction itself.

## Removals versus avoidance offsets

A fundamental distinction determines credibility (the net-zero-definition link):

**Carbon removals** - actually taking carbon out of the atmosphere:
- Nature-based: reforestation, afforestation, soil carbon, restoring ecosystems
- Technological: direct air capture (DAC), bioenergy with carbon capture (BECCS)
- These genuinely remove carbon, truly balancing residual emissions

**Avoidance offsets** - paying to prevent emissions elsewhere:
- Funding renewable energy, preventing deforestation, capturing methane
- These reduce emissions compared to a baseline but do not remove carbon

For genuine net zero, residual emissions must be balanced by **removals**, not just avoidance offsets - because only removals actually take out the carbon the residual emissions add. Avoidance offsets have a role in the broader journey (funding reductions elsewhere) but do not, strictly, neutralise residual emissions. Credible net-zero standards increasingly require removals for the residual.

## The credibility minefield

Offsets are a notorious source of greenwashing and must be used carefully (the credibility link). Common problems:
- **Additionality** - would the offset's reduction have happened anyway? (if so, it is not a real additional saving)
- **Permanence** - will the carbon stay removed? (a forest can burn or be felled, re-releasing the carbon)
- **Verification** - is the offset real, measured, and verified?
- **Double-counting** - is the same reduction counted by more than one party?
- **Over-reliance** - using cheap offsets to avoid real reduction (the central credibility failure)

Poor-quality offsets - non-additional, impermanent, unverified - undermine the whole net-zero claim. The history of carbon offsetting is littered with credibility scandals, making careful, high-quality offset use essential.

## The over-reliance trap

The single biggest credibility failure is **over-reliance on offsets** instead of reduction (the net-zero-definition link):
- A "net zero" achieved mostly through cheap offsets, with little real reduction, is greenwashing
- Offsets are not a licence to keep emitting while paying to look clean
- The credibility test is the ratio of genuine reduction to offsetting

Credible: deep reduction (90%+) with a small residual balanced by high-quality removals.
Not credible: modest reduction with the bulk "offset" by cheap, questionable credits.

This is the heart of the matter: offsets must be the last resort for the genuine residual, after the hard work of real reduction - not a shortcut that avoids it. An organisation reaching for offsets before exhausting reduction is not pursuing genuine net zero.

## Using offsets and removals well

To use offsets and removals credibly:
- **Reduce first** - exhaust genuine reduction before offsetting (the roadmap link)
- **Minimise the residual** - keep the amount offset as small as possible
- **Prioritise removals** - use genuine carbon removals for the residual, not just avoidance offsets
- **Ensure quality** - additionality, permanence, verification, no double-counting
- **Be transparent** - disclose clearly what is reduced and what is offset, and the quality of the offsets (the credibility link)
- **Treat as transitional** - some offsets (avoidance) may bridge the journey while removals and reduction scale up, but the goal is deep reduction with removals for the residual

## The quality question

Offset quality varies enormously, and using high-quality offsets is essential:
- **Certified** to recognised, rigorous standards
- **Additional** - genuinely causing reductions that would not otherwise happen
- **Permanent** - with the carbon durably removed or stored
- **Verified** - independently measured and confirmed
- **Transparent** - clear about what they represent

Cheap, low-quality offsets are worse than useless - they create a false claim of neutrality while delivering little real benefit, exposing the organisation to greenwashing criticism. Investing in genuine, high-quality removals for the residual - even though they cost more - is what makes the offsetting credible.

## Offsets in the credible roadmap

In a credible net-zero roadmap, offsets and removals play a specific, limited, last-resort role (the roadmap and net-zero-definition links):
- The roadmap prioritises deep reduction (efficiency, electrification, renewables)
- The residual is minimised through that reduction
- High-quality removals balance the genuine residual
- The whole is transparently reported (the credibility link)

Used this way - as a limited, high-quality, transparent means of balancing the genuine residual after deep reduction - offsets and removals legitimately complete the journey to net zero. Used as a cheap substitute for reduction, they invite the greenwashing criticism that destroys credibility. The distinction - reduction first, removals for the residual, quality and transparency throughout - is central to a credible net-zero strategy, and leads directly to the final lesson on credibility and reporting.
