Electrification shifts energy onto electricity; renewable energy ensures that electricity is clean. Together they decarbonise energy. This lesson covers the renewable transition - sourcing clean electricity and managing the shift to a renewable energy system.

## Renewables complete the picture

Electrification (the previous lesson) moves energy onto electricity, but electricity is only low-carbon if it is generated cleanly. **Renewable energy** - solar, wind, and other zero-carbon sources - provides that clean electricity. Electrification and renewables are therefore two halves of the same strategy: shift energy onto electricity, and make that electricity renewable. Neither alone achieves net zero; together they decarbonise energy.

## Sourcing renewable electricity

An organisation can access renewable electricity through several routes (the procurement and renewables courses):
- **On-site generation** - solar PV (and occasionally wind) generating clean power directly (the renewables course) - the most direct and credible
- **Power purchase agreements (PPAs)** - long-term contracts for renewable power, often backing new generation (the PPA link) - credible and scalable
- **Green tariffs and certificates** - buying renewable-backed supply (the REGO link) - credibility varies
- **The decarbonising grid** - the grid itself is adding renewables, so bought electricity gets cleaner over time

A serious renewable transition typically combines on-site generation, PPAs, and the decarbonising grid, prioritising the more credible, additional routes (the credibility link) over reliance on cheap certificates.

## The credibility hierarchy

As the procurement course established, renewable claims vary in credibility (the REGO link):
- **On-site generation** - most direct and credible
- **PPAs backing new generation** - credible, additional, scalable
- **Green tariffs from specific renewable generators** - reasonably credible
- **Unbundled certificates** - weakest, criticised as greenwashing

For a credible net-zero claim, the renewable transition should prioritise genuine, additional renewable energy - on-site generation and PPAs that support new capacity - over cheap certificates that relabel existing power. This connects directly to the credibility of the whole net-zero claim (the credibility lesson).

## On-site generation

On-site renewable generation (the renewables course) is the most direct route:
- **Solar PV** on roofs and land - the most common and accessible
- Generating clean power used directly on site (high-value self-consumption - the renewables course)
- Reducing both emissions and energy cost
- Demonstrably additional and credible

On-site generation is a cornerstone of the renewable transition - directly displacing grid electricity with clean power, with the strongest credibility and the co-benefit of reduced energy cost (the investment-case link).

## The system transition

The renewable transition is part of a broader transformation of the energy system (the microgrid and storage links):
- From centralised fossil generation to distributed renewable generation
- From steady fossil output to variable renewable output, needing storage and flexibility (the storage link)
- From passive consumers to active participants generating, storing, and flexing demand (the microgrid link)
- Toward smarter grids balancing variable supply and flexible demand

An organisation's renewable transition - generating, storing, and flexibly using clean energy - is part of this system-wide shift. Storage and demand flexibility (the storage and microgrid links) become important to match variable renewable supply with demand, turning the building into an active node in a renewable energy system.

## Sector coupling and integration

The renewable transition increasingly integrates previously separate energy uses (sector coupling):
- Electrified heat (heat pumps) and transport (EVs) become flexible electrical loads
- These can be shifted to times of abundant renewable generation (the storage and flexibility links)
- Storage (thermal and battery) bridges the gap between variable supply and demand
- The whole system is optimised together

This integration - coupling heat, transport, and power, with storage and flexibility - is how a renewable-powered energy system balances variable supply with demand. The organisation's electrified, renewable, flexible energy system becomes part of a smarter, integrated whole (the microgrid link).

## Renewables in the roadmap

Renewable energy is a central pillar of the net-zero roadmap, working with efficiency and electrification (the roadmap-phases and net-zero-pathway links):
- **Efficiency** reduces the demand (so less renewable generation is needed)
- **Electrification** shifts energy onto electricity (so it can be renewable)
- **Renewables** provide the clean electricity to power it all
- **Storage and flexibility** balance variable renewable supply with demand

This is the technical heart of net zero: an efficient, electrified energy system powered by renewable electricity, balanced by storage and flexibility. The renewable transition - sourcing genuine, additional clean electricity through on-site generation and PPAs, and integrating it with electrified, flexible demand - is how the energy an organisation uses is ultimately decarbonised. Combined with the efficiency and electrification that precede it, it eliminates the energy emissions that dominate most organisations' footprints, leaving only the hardest residual for removals (the offset link).
