The single most important technical move in most net-zero plans is electrification - replacing the direct burning of fossil fuels with electricity that can be decarbonised. Understanding electrification, especially of heat and transport, is central to decarbonisation.

## Why electrification is central

Most emissions come from burning fossil fuels - gas for heat, petrol and diesel for transport, fuel for processes. As long as energy comes from combustion, it produces carbon. **Electrification** breaks this link by replacing fossil-fuel combustion with electricity:
- Electricity can be generated from zero-carbon sources (renewables, the decarbonising grid)
- So electrified energy becomes cleaner as the electricity supply decarbonises
- Unlike a gas boiler (locked to fossil fuel for its life), an electrified system gets greener automatically over time

This is why electrification is the central technical strategy for net zero: it shifts energy onto electricity, which can be - and increasingly is - decarbonised. The carbon problem becomes a question of cleaning up electricity generation, which is happening, rather than of countless individual combustion sources, which is far harder.

## Electrifying heat

Heat is the biggest electrification challenge and opportunity for most buildings (the renewables and CHP links):
- **Heat pumps** replace gas boilers, providing heating (and cooling) from electricity
- Their efficiency (COP of 3-4 - the heat-pump renewable lesson) means they deliver 3-4 units of heat per unit of electricity, harvesting renewable environmental heat
- Run on clean electricity, electrified heat is low-carbon; and it gets cleaner as the grid decarbonises

Replacing fossil-fuel heating with heat pumps is the cornerstone of decarbonising building heat - and one of the more challenging measures, requiring suitable emitters (the efficiency link), good building fabric (the insulation link), and electrical capacity. But it is essential: heat is too large a source to leave on fossil fuel in a net-zero plan.

## Electrifying transport

Transport electrification replaces fossil-fuel vehicles with electric ones:
- **Electric vehicles (EVs)** replace petrol and diesel fleet vehicles
- Run on clean electricity, they are far lower-carbon, and get cleaner as the grid decarbonises
- They also require charging infrastructure and electrical capacity

For organisations with vehicle fleets, electrification is the route to decarbonising transport emissions (scope 1). It connects to the broader electrification of the energy system and to managing the resulting electrical demand (the load and storage links).

## The grid-decarbonisation synergy

Electrification works because the electricity supply is decarbonising (the carbon-accounting and CHP links):
- As grids add renewables, the carbon intensity of electricity falls
- Every electrified system automatically gets cleaner as this happens
- The carbon case for electrification strengthens over time

This synergy is crucial. Electrifying now, even while the grid is not fully clean, locks in declining emissions as the grid decarbonises over the system's life. It also shifts the strategic question from "how do we decarbonise thousands of combustion sources?" to "how do we clean up electricity generation?" - a more tractable, system-level problem being actively addressed (the renewables link).

## The efficiency-first principle

Electrification should follow efficiency, not precede it (the roadmap-phases and net-zero-pathway links):
- Reducing demand first (efficiency, fabric) means smaller, cheaper electrified systems
- A well-insulated building needs a smaller heat pump, working at lower temperatures (higher COP)
- Electrifying an inefficient building is expensive and may strain electrical capacity

This is why the net-zero sequence is efficiency first, then electrify (the roadmap link): reducing demand makes electrification cheaper, easier, and more effective. Electrifying a wasteful building or process before improving its efficiency wastes money and clean electricity.

## Challenges of electrification

Electrification is powerful but challenging:
- **Cost** - heat pumps and EVs can have higher upfront costs (the investment-case and financing links)
- **Electrical capacity** - electrifying heat and transport increases electrical demand, sometimes needing supply upgrades
- **Suitability** - some applications (high-temperature industrial heat) are hard to electrify with current technology
- **Timing and disruption** - replacing heating and vehicles takes time and investment

These challenges mean electrification is a multi-year journey, sequenced within the roadmap, requiring investment (funded through the mechanisms of the financing course) and planning. Some hard-to-electrify emissions (high-temperature process heat, certain transport) may need other solutions (hydrogen, biofuels) or remain part of the residual.

## Electrification in the roadmap

Electrification is a central pillar of the net-zero roadmap (the roadmap-phases link):
- It comes after efficiency (which reduces the demand to be electrified)
- It works with renewable generation (clean electricity to power it - the renewables link)
- It is the main route to decarbonising heat and transport (large emission sources)
- It gets cleaner over time as the grid decarbonises

Combined with renewable energy (the next lesson) and underpinned by efficiency, electrification is how most of an organisation's emissions are ultimately eliminated - shifting energy onto an electricity supply that is being decarbonised. It is the technical heart of the journey to net zero.
