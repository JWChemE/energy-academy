"Net zero" is widely claimed but often poorly understood. A credible net-zero commitment means something specific - deep emissions reductions, with only a limited residual balanced by removals. Understanding what net zero genuinely means is essential to setting a credible target.

## What net zero means

**Net zero** means reducing emissions as close to zero as possible, and balancing the small remaining (residual) emissions with an equivalent amount of carbon removal from the atmosphere - so the net effect on atmospheric carbon is zero.

The crucial points are:
- **Deep reduction first** - net zero is primarily about reducing emissions, drastically
- **Residual only** - only the genuinely hard-to-eliminate emissions remain
- **Removals balance the residual** - the small remainder is balanced by removing carbon from the atmosphere
- **Net effect zero** - emissions added equal carbon removed

This is a high bar, and much that is labelled "net zero" falls short of it. Understanding the genuine definition is essential to distinguishing credible commitments from greenwashing.

## Reduction versus offsetting

The defining principle of credible net zero is the priority of **reduction over offsetting**:
- The vast majority of the journey to net zero must come from actually reducing emissions
- Offsetting (paying for reductions or removals elsewhere) is reserved for the small residual that cannot be eliminated
- A "net zero" achieved mostly through offsetting, with little real reduction, is not credible

This is the heart of the credibility question (the credibility lesson). Compare:
- **Credible:** 90% emissions reduction + 10% balanced by genuine removals
- **Not credible:** 30% reduction + 70% offset by cheap, questionable credits

Both might claim "net zero," but only the first represents genuine decarbonisation. The ratio of real reduction to offsetting is the key test of a net-zero claim's integrity.

## Removals versus avoidance offsets

Within offsetting, a critical distinction (the offset-strategy lesson):
- **Removals** - actually taking carbon out of the atmosphere (reforestation, direct air capture) - genuinely balance residual emissions
- **Avoidance offsets** - paying to avoid emissions elsewhere (preventing deforestation, funding renewables) - reduce emissions compared to a baseline but do not remove carbon

True net zero requires balancing residual emissions with **removals**, not just avoidance offsets. Avoidance offsets have a role in the journey but do not, strictly, achieve net zero for residual emissions - because they do not remove the carbon those residual emissions add. This distinction is increasingly emphasised in credible net-zero standards.

## Net zero versus carbon neutral

"Net zero" and "carbon neutral" are often confused:
- **Carbon neutral** - a broader, often weaker term, frequently achieved largely through offsetting, sometimes with little reduction
- **Net zero** - a more rigorous standard, requiring deep reduction with only residual emissions offset by removals, usually aligned with science-based pathways

The terms are not interchangeable. "Carbon neutral" claims, often based heavily on offsets, have attracted greenwashing criticism. "Net zero," properly defined and aligned with science, sets a higher, more credible bar. The energy leader should understand the distinction and aim for genuine net zero, not weaker neutrality claims.

## Science-based targets

Credible net zero is increasingly defined by alignment with **science-based targets** (the credibility lesson):
- Targets consistent with limiting warming to 1.5C, as the science requires
- Following recognised methodologies (such as the Science Based Targets initiative, SBTi)
- Specifying the pace and depth of reduction science demands
- Defining net zero rigorously (deep reduction, limited residual, removals)

Science-based targets give net-zero commitments external rigour and credibility, grounding them in what the climate science actually requires rather than arbitrary or convenient goals. Aligning with them is a key mark of a credible commitment.

## A credible net-zero target

Putting this together, a credible net-zero target:
- **Covers the material emissions** - including significant scope 3 (the scopes link)
- **Prioritises deep reduction** - the bulk from actually cutting emissions
- **Limits the residual** - only genuinely hard-to-eliminate emissions remain
- **Uses removals for the residual** - not cheap avoidance offsets
- **Aligns with science** - the pace and depth the science requires
- **Sets interim milestones** - near-term targets, not just a distant date (the roadmap and targets links)
- **Is transparent** - honest accounting and reporting (the credibility link)

A target meeting these criteria is credible; one that is mostly offsets, ignores scope 3, lacks interim milestones, or is not science-aligned is not. The distinction matters enormously as scrutiny of net-zero claims intensifies.

## The definition as foundation

Understanding what net zero genuinely means - deep reduction, limited residual, removals, science-aligned - is the foundation for setting a credible target and building an honest roadmap. It guards against the greenwashing that an ill-defined "net zero" invites, and sets the demanding but necessary standard that the rest of this course works toward: the pathways to deep reduction (electrification, renewables), the sequencing of a credible roadmap, the proper use of offsets and removals, and the transparent reporting that makes the whole effort credible. Net zero, properly understood, is not a label to claim but a rigorous goal to achieve.
