With many possible measures competing to cut emissions, an organisation needs a way to prioritise. The marginal abatement cost curve ranks the options by cost-effectiveness, revealing where to start and how far each measure goes - a central tool for planning decarbonisation.

## The prioritisation problem

A net-zero plan involves many possible measures - efficiency, electrification, renewables, process changes, offsets - each with a different cost and a different amount of carbon saved. With limited budget and capacity, the organisation must decide which to do, and in what order. The **marginal abatement cost (MAC) curve** is the standard tool for this, ranking measures by their cost-effectiveness at cutting carbon.

## What a MAC curve shows

A **MAC curve** plots all the available abatement measures, ranked by their cost per tonne of CO2 saved:
- **The horizontal axis** shows the cumulative carbon abated (tonnes of CO2)
- **The vertical axis** shows the cost per tonne of each measure
- **Each bar** represents a measure - its width the carbon it saves, its height the cost per tonne

The measures are ranked from cheapest (left) to most expensive (right), forming a staircase. Reading left to right shows the order in which to deploy measures for maximum carbon reduction per pound, and how much total abatement is available at each cost level.

## Negative-cost measures

A striking feature of most MAC curves is that some measures have **negative cost** - they save money as well as carbon:
- Many energy efficiency measures pay for themselves through energy savings (the investment-case link)
- These appear below the axis - they cost less than nothing per tonne (they generate net savings)
- They should obviously be done first - they reduce both emissions and cost

This is a powerful insight: a significant amount of carbon can often be abated at negative cost, through energy efficiency that pays for itself. These "no-regret" measures are the natural starting point - they save money while cutting carbon, funding and motivating the rest of the journey (the roadmap-phases link).

## Reading the curve

The MAC curve guides decarbonisation strategy:
- **Start at the left** - the cheapest (often negative-cost) measures first
- **Work rightward** - progressively more expensive measures as cheaper ones are exhausted
- **See the total** - how much abatement is available, and at what cumulative cost
- **Identify the gap** - how far the available measures get toward the target, and what the hardest, most expensive abatement (or removals) must cover

This reveals both the sequence (cheapest first) and the scale of the challenge (whether available measures reach the target, or whether expensive abatement and removals are needed for the last, hardest emissions).

## The shape of the curve

The typical shape tells a story:
- **Negative-cost measures** (efficiency) on the left - do these first
- **Low-cost measures** (some renewables, electrification as costs fall) next
- **Rising costs** as you move right - the harder, more expensive abatement
- **Very expensive measures** on the far right - the hardest-to-abate emissions, where removals and offsets may come in (the offset link)

This shape underpins the sequencing logic of a net-zero roadmap: do the cheap, often profitable measures first, then progressively tackle the more expensive ones, reserving offsets and removals for the residual that is genuinely hardest and most expensive to eliminate (the net-zero-definition link).

## Limitations of MAC curves

MAC curves are valuable but have limitations to bear in mind:
- **Static snapshot** - they reflect current costs, which change (technologies get cheaper - the renewables link)
- **Cost focus** - they rank by cost, but other factors matter (ease, risk, co-benefits, dependencies)
- **Interactions** - measures interact (efficiency reduces the renewable needed), which simple curves may not capture
- **Uncertainty** - the costs and savings are estimates

So the MAC curve is a guide, not a rigid prescription. It should be combined with consideration of co-benefits (the investment-case link), dependencies, risk, and strategic factors - and updated as costs change. But as a tool for prioritising measures by cost-effectiveness, it is central to planning a cost-effective path to net zero.

## From curve to roadmap

The MAC curve feeds directly into building a net-zero roadmap (the roadmap-phases link):
- It identifies the measures and their cost-effectiveness
- It suggests the sequence (cheapest first)
- It reveals the scale of the challenge and where removals must fill the gap
- It informs the investment case for the programme (the investment-case link)

Combined with the specific decarbonisation pathways - electrification and renewables, covered next - and the sequencing logic of a roadmap, the MAC curve helps turn the goal of net zero into a prioritised, cost-effective plan of action: which measures, in which order, to cut emissions as far and as cost-effectively as possible, with removals reserved for the hardest residual. It is one of the most useful planning tools in the decarbonisation toolkit.
