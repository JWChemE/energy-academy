Net zero requires measuring emissions accurately: you cannot manage, target, or reduce what you have not quantified. The measurement method itself (scopes, emission factors, the GHG Protocol and ISO standards) is taught in the [Carbon Accounting Fundamentals course](/courses/carbon-accounting/why-count-carbon); this lesson applies it at roadmap level, establishing the baseline and tracking machinery on which every target and claim in this course depends.

## What carbon accounting is

**Carbon accounting** is the process of measuring an organisation's greenhouse gas emissions - converting its activities (energy used, fuel burnt, goods bought, miles travelled) into quantities of carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e). It establishes the emissions baseline, tracks progress, and provides the numbers for targets and reporting. It is the measurement foundation of net zero, analogous to the energy baselines of the M&V and ISO 50001 courses, applied to carbon.

## From activity to emissions

The basic method converts activity data into emissions using emission factors:

Emissions = activity data x emission factor

- **Activity data** - the quantity of the activity (kWh of electricity, litres of fuel, tonnes of material, miles travelled)
- **Emission factor** - the emissions per unit of activity (kgCO2e per kWh, per litre, per tonne, per mile)

For example, electricity consumption (kWh) multiplied by the grid emission factor (kgCO2e/kWh) gives the emissions from that electricity. Summed across all activities and scopes, this builds the organisation's total carbon footprint.

## Emission factors

**Emission factors** are central to carbon accounting:
- They convert each activity into its emissions
- They come from published sources (government, the GHG Protocol, databases)
- They vary by activity, fuel, region, and over time
- The **grid electricity factor** is particularly important and changes as grids decarbonise (the electrification and CHP links)

Using accurate, appropriate, up-to-date emission factors is essential to credible accounting. The grid factor especially matters: as it falls with grid decarbonisation, the emissions from purchased electricity (scope 2) fall too, and the relative merits of electrification versus combustion shift (the CHP carbon lesson).

## CO2e and the greenhouse gases

Carbon accounting measures not just carbon dioxide but all greenhouse gases, expressed as **carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e)**:
- Different gases (methane, nitrous oxide, refrigerants) have different warming effects
- Each is converted to its CO2 equivalent using its global warming potential (GWP)
- This gives a single, comparable measure across all gases

This matters because some activities emit potent non-CO2 gases - refrigerant leaks (scope 1) can have a large CO2e impact despite small quantities, and methane from some processes is highly potent. Accounting in CO2e captures the full warming impact, not just carbon dioxide.

## Establishing the baseline

A key output of carbon accounting is the **emissions baseline** - the reference point against which reduction is measured (the M&V parallel):
- A comprehensive measurement of emissions across the relevant scopes
- For a defined base year
- Against which targets are set and progress tracked

The baseline must be robust and well-documented, because all net-zero targets and progress claims are measured against it. As with energy baselines, it may need adjustment for significant structural changes (acquisitions, divestments) to remain a fair reference - the carbon equivalent of the M&V non-routine adjustment.

## The scope 3 challenge

Carbon accounting for scopes 1 and 2 is relatively straightforward (fuel and energy data are usually available). **Scope 3 is far harder** (the scopes link):
- Data is often unavailable or estimated (suppliers' emissions, product use)
- It relies on assumptions, averages, and supplier data of varying quality
- It spans many categories, each needing its own method
- It is less precise and harder to verify

Despite the difficulty, material scope 3 must be accounted for to be credible (the credibility link). Methods range from rough spend-based estimates (emissions per pound spent) to more accurate activity-based and supplier-specific data. Improving scope 3 data over time is part of the journey - starting with estimates and refining them as supplier engagement and data improve.

## Accuracy, consistency, and improvement

Good carbon accounting balances accuracy with practicality:
- **Consistency** - using consistent methods and boundaries over time, so progress is comparable
- **Accuracy** - using the best available data and factors, improving over time
- **Transparency** - documenting methods, assumptions, and boundaries
- **Materiality** - focusing effort on the most significant emissions

Perfect accuracy, especially for scope 3, is unattainable; the goal is robust, consistent, transparent accounting that improves over time and is good enough to drive and demonstrate genuine reduction. As with M&V, integrity matters - honest, conservative accounting builds credibility; manipulated or selective accounting undermines it (the credibility link).

## The foundation for action

Carbon accounting underpins the whole net-zero effort:
- It establishes **where emissions come from** (informing where to act - the pathways)
- It sets the **baseline** for targets (the credibility lesson)
- It tracks **progress** toward net zero
- It provides the numbers for **reporting and disclosure** (the credibility lesson)

Just as energy management depends on measuring energy, decarbonisation depends on measuring carbon. With a robust emissions baseline and accounting in place, the organisation can define what net zero means for it (the next lesson), identify where to act (the pathways), and track and report its progress credibly. Carbon accounting is the measurement foundation on which credible net zero is built.
