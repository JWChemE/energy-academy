Net zero begins with knowing what you emit. The greenhouse gas scopes - 1, 2, and 3 - provide the standard framework for categorising emissions, and understanding them is the foundation of any credible decarbonisation plan.

## Why scopes matter

Before an organisation can reduce its emissions, it must define and measure them - and that requires a consistent framework. The **greenhouse gas (GHG) scopes**, established by the GHG Protocol, are the globally recognised way to categorise an organisation's emissions. They determine what is counted, prevent double-counting, and frame the whole net-zero effort. Getting the scopes right - especially the boundary of what is included - is the essential first step.

## Scope 1: direct emissions

**Scope 1** covers direct emissions from sources the organisation owns or controls:
- Fuel burnt on site (gas boilers, furnaces)
- Company vehicles (fleet)
- Process emissions
- Refrigerant leaks

These are emissions the organisation produces directly - the fuel it burns and the gases it releases. They are usually the most directly controllable, addressed by efficiency, electrification (replacing combustion - the electrification lesson), and process change.

## Scope 2: indirect emissions from energy

**Scope 2** covers indirect emissions from purchased energy:
- Electricity bought from the grid
- Purchased heat, steam, or cooling

The organisation does not produce these emissions directly (they occur at the power station), but it causes them through its energy consumption. Scope 2 is addressed by using less energy (efficiency) and by decarbonising the energy bought - through renewable procurement and on-site generation (the procurement and renewables links). As grids decarbonise, scope 2 emissions fall automatically, but procurement choices can accelerate this.

## Scope 3: value-chain emissions

**Scope 3** covers all other indirect emissions across the value chain - everything not in scopes 1 and 2:
- Purchased goods and services (embedded emissions)
- Capital goods
- Transport and distribution
- Business travel and commuting
- Waste
- Use of sold products
- End-of-life treatment

Scope 3 is the broadest, most complex, and often the largest category - frequently 70-90% of total emissions for many organisations (the policy supply-chain link). It is also the hardest to measure and influence, since it lies outside the organisation's direct control. But ignoring it means addressing only a fraction of the real impact.

## The dominance of scope 3

For most organisations, scope 3 dominates total emissions:
- A retailer's emissions are mostly in the products it sells and their supply chains
- A service firm's are in its purchases, travel, and the buildings it uses
- A manufacturer's are often in the use of its products and its supply chain

This has a profound implication: a net-zero plan addressing only scopes 1 and 2 - the organisation's own operations and energy - misses the majority of its impact. Credible net zero must grapple with scope 3, despite its difficulty (the credibility link). This is one of the most important and challenging aspects of decarbonisation.

## Setting boundaries

A key decision is the organisational and operational **boundary** - what to include:
- **Organisational boundary** - which operations, sites, and entities are counted (equity share, control)
- **Operational boundary** - which scopes and categories are included
- **Scope 3 categories** - which of the many scope 3 categories are material and included

These boundaries determine what "net zero" means for the organisation. A narrow boundary (scopes 1 and 2 only) is easier but less credible; a comprehensive boundary (including material scope 3) is harder but honest. Stakeholders increasingly scrutinise boundaries, expecting material scope 3 to be included (the credibility link).

## Scopes as the foundation

The GHG scopes are the foundation of the entire net-zero effort:
- They define **what is counted** (the boundary)
- They frame **where emissions come from** (direct, energy, value chain)
- They guide **where to act** (the pathways - the following lessons)
- They are the basis for **measurement, targets, and reporting** (the carbon accounting and credibility lessons)

Every credible net-zero plan begins by defining and measuring emissions across the relevant scopes, with an honest boundary that includes material scope 3. This framing determines everything that follows - the accounting, the targets, the pathways, and the credibility of the whole endeavour.
