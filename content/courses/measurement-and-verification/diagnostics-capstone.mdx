You've learned what a baseline is, how to normalise for weather and other drivers, the four IPMVP options, how to calculate adjusted savings, and the pitfalls that overstate results. This capstone puts you in the M&V role, turning raw data into a credible, defensible saving, and refusing to claim more than really happened.

The thread through every case is **integrity**: a saving is the energy you *avoided* using, measured against an adjusted baseline under the same conditions, never raw bills year-on-year. The most damaging error in energy management is overstatement, and these cases are built to test whether you'll resist it.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate**: read the brief and the panel: the baseline model, the drivers, the actual energy, the conditions.
2. **Calculate**: work out the figure that decides it: the adjusted baseline, the real saving, the overstatement, the noise floor. The **reference data is on hand**: the savings equation, baselines, normalisation, IPMVP options, pitfalls, but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose**: pick what's really going on: weather luck, a boundary change, noise, rebound, decay.
4. **Prescribe**: choose the correct M&V response, and reject the tempting inflated claim.
5. **Verify**: score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="Report what really happened">
Every saving is adjusted baseline minus actual: what the building *would* have used under this period's conditions, minus what it did. Normalise for weather and every other real driver, make non-routine adjustments when static factors change, choose the IPMVP option that fits, quote savings with their uncertainty, and verify on an ongoing basis. And when the measured number falls short of the engineering promise, report the measured number. Overstated savings erode credibility the moment the bills fail to fall.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **The savings equation**: adjusted baseline minus actual, with single and multiple drivers.
- **Weather luck**: why raw year-on-year comparisons miscredit the seasons.
- **IPMVP**: matching the option to the project's scale and value.
- **Non-routine adjustments**: correcting the baseline when a static factor changes.
- **Rigour**: savings versus the baseline noise floor, persistence decay, and the rebound effect.

## Differential thinking

A reported "saving" might be a real avoided-energy figure, a mild winter in disguise, a number buried in baseline noise, or an engineering prediction that rebound has eroded. The decisive evidence is the arithmetic against an honest, adjusted baseline, and the recurring decision is whether to claim the inflated figure or the true one. The right answer is almost always the smaller, defensible number.

## After you finish

- Which **number** decided each case: the adjusted baseline, the overstatement, the noise, the rebound?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each, what would you actually report, and would it survive an independent reviewer and a performance-contract dispute?
