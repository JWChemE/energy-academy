The final stage of M&V turns analysis into credibility: documenting the method, reporting the savings clearly, and where the stakes are high, having the work independently verified.

## The M&V plan: agree it first

Good M&V begins before the project, with an **M&V plan** that sets the rules in advance:
- The chosen IPMVP option (A, B, C, or D)
- The measurement boundary
- The baseline period and model
- The independent variables (drivers) to be used
- How non-routine adjustments will be handled
- The reporting frequency and format
- Responsibilities and data sources

Agreeing all this upfront - especially where a performance contract is involved - prevents the disputes that arise when methods are chosen after seeing results. It is the single most important step for credible, contestable savings.

## Reporting savings

Savings reports should be clear, regular, and honest:
- **State the savings** in energy (kWh) and cost, for the period and cumulatively
- **Show the method** - the adjusted baseline, the actual energy, the adjustments made
- **Quote the uncertainty** - savings with a confidence interval, not false precision
- **Explain variances** - why savings differ from prediction (rebound, weather, operational issues)
- **Flag drift** - if savings are decaying, say so, and investigate

Good reporting builds trust through transparency. Burying the method or quoting a single confident number without context invites scepticism - and rightly so.

## Independent verification

Where savings carry financial or reputational weight, **independent third-party verification** adds credibility:
- An independent party reviews the baseline, method, and calculations
- It checks that the M&V plan was followed and adjustments made properly
- It confirms the savings are defensible

Independent verification matters most for:
- **Energy performance contracts**, where payment depends on the savings - both parties need a trusted, neutral figure
- **Compliance and ESG reporting**, where external stakeholders rely on the numbers
- **Public claims**, where credibility is on the line
- **Large investments**, where the next round of funding depends on a proven track record

Self-reported savings always carry a suspicion of bias; independent verification removes it.

## Verification in performance contracts

In an **energy performance contract (EPC)**, the provider is paid from the savings, so the M&V is the heart of the deal. The contract specifies the M&V method in detail, often with guaranteed savings and an independent verifier. Get the M&V right and the contract runs smoothly; get it vague and it ends in dispute over whose figure is correct. This is M&V at its most consequential - real money rides on every assumption.

## Connecting to the wider picture

M&V reporting feeds the broader energy-management system:
- Verified savings justify the **investment case** for the next project (the link to the leadership courses)
- They populate **compliance and net-zero reporting** with defensible numbers
- They reveal which measures worked, guiding strategy
- Ongoing verification detects **persistence and drift**, linking to maintenance and continuous improvement

## The value of doing it well

Done properly, M&V transforms energy management from assertion into evidence. It proves that projects delivered, builds the credibility that unlocks further investment, settles contracts fairly, and underpins honest reporting. The effort of a sound M&V plan, careful adjustment, transparent reporting, and - where it matters - independent verification, is repaid many times over in trust, funding, and the confidence that the savings are real. In a field where it is easy to claim success and hard to prove it, M&V is what makes energy management accountable.
