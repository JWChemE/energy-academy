Every saving is measured against a baseline - a picture of how much energy was used before the project. Getting the baseline right is the foundation of all M&V; get it wrong and every saving figure is wrong.

## What a baseline is

The **baseline** is the energy consumption of the building or system before the energy-saving measure, characterised well enough that you can estimate what it *would have* used under any later set of conditions. It is not just a single number - it is a model of how energy use related to the things that drive it.

## Why a single number is not enough

Suppose last year a building used 500,000 kWh. Is that the baseline? Only partly - because energy use depends on conditions that change:
- A cold winter pushes heating up
- More occupants or longer hours push usage up
- Higher production pushes industrial energy up

If you simply compare this year's 450,000 kWh to last year's 500,000 and claim 50,000 kWh saved, you are wrong if this year was milder, emptier, or quieter. The baseline must capture *how* energy use responds to these factors, so it can be adjusted to match current conditions.

## The baseline as a model

A good baseline relates energy use to its **independent variables** - the measurable factors that drive it:
- **Weather** (degree-days) for heating and cooling
- **Occupancy** or operating hours
- **Production volume** for industrial sites

Often this takes the form of a simple equation (from regression analysis) - for example, energy = base load + a coefficient x degree-days. This model lets you ask: "given this year's weather, what would the old building have used?" - the adjusted baseline against which the saving is measured.

## Choosing the baseline period

The baseline period must:
- Be **long enough** to capture the full range of operating conditions - normally **at least 12 months**, so all seasons are represented
- Be **representative** of normal operation (avoid periods with unusual events - a long shutdown, a major fault)
- Have **good data** - reliable energy records and the matching driver data (weather, occupancy, production)

Twelve continuous months is the usual minimum because it captures a full annual cycle of weather and operation.

## Baseline conditions and boundary

Two things must be defined and recorded with the baseline:
- The **measurement boundary** - exactly what is included (a single chiller, a building, a whole site). Savings are measured within this boundary.
- The **baseline conditions** - the weather, occupancy, and operation during the baseline, so later adjustments are made from a known starting point.

## The static factors

Beyond the variable drivers, note the **static factors** - things assumed constant (building size, equipment inventory, operating practices). If a static factor changes later (an extension is built, a new production line added), the baseline must be formally adjusted - a "non-routine adjustment" covered later. Recording the static factors at baseline is what makes such adjustments possible.

## Getting it right

The baseline is the reference for everything that follows. Invest in getting it right:
- A full, representative period (12+ months)
- Good energy and driver data
- A sound relationship between energy and its drivers
- Clearly defined boundary and static factors

A robust baseline makes honest, defensible savings possible. A weak one undermines every figure built on it - which is why M&V puts so much emphasis on establishing it well.
