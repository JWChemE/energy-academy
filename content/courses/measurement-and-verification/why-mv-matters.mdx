You implement an energy project; the bills go down - or do they? Proving that a saving is real, and quantifying it credibly, is the job of measurement and verification (M&V). Without it, energy savings are just claims.

## The problem M&V solves

Energy consumption changes for many reasons - weather, occupancy, production, behaviour - not just your project. After a retrofit, this year's bill might be lower because of your work, or because it was a mild winter, or because the building was half-empty. **M&V** is the discipline of separating the saving caused by your project from all the other things that move energy use.

## Why it matters

Credible savings figures underpin almost everything in energy management:

- **Justifying investment:** finance directors fund projects whose returns are proven. A track record of verified savings unlocks the next budget.
- **Performance contracts:** energy performance contracts (EPCs) pay the provider from the savings - so the savings must be measured and agreed, or there is nothing to pay against and endless dispute.
- **Compliance and reporting:** schemes, ESG reporting, and net-zero claims require defensible numbers, not estimates.
- **Continuous improvement:** you can only manage what you measure - M&V reveals which projects worked and which did not.
- **Protecting savings:** verified savings that later drift reveal a problem (controls drifting back, equipment fouling) before it erodes the whole benefit.

## The cost of getting it wrong

Without proper M&V, organisations:
- Claim savings that did not happen (and lose credibility when bills do not fall)
- Miss savings that did happen (and under-value good projects)
- Cannot settle performance contracts fairly
- Repeat ineffective measures and abandon effective ones

Overstated savings are especially damaging - they erode trust and undermine the case for future investment when reality fails to match the claim.

## What M&V involves

At its heart, M&V follows a simple logic:

Savings = (baseline energy, adjusted to current conditions) - (actual energy used)

The "adjusted to current conditions" is the crucial part. You estimate what the building *would have* used under this year's weather, occupancy, and production *without* the project, then compare it to what it actually used. The difference is the verified saving.

## The IPMVP framework

M&V is formalised in the **International Performance Measurement and Verification Protocol (IPMVP)** - the globally recognised framework that gives structure, options, and rigour to the process. The rest of this course works through it: establishing baselines, adjusting for the factors that affect energy use, calculating savings honestly, and avoiding the pitfalls that overstate results.

## The mindset

Good M&V is fundamentally about honesty and rigour - resisting the temptation to claim the mild winter as your project's success, accounting fairly for everything that changed, and producing numbers that withstand scrutiny. It is the discipline that turns energy management from a matter of faith into a matter of evidence.
