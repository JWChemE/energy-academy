A robust baseline is built from good data and sound analysis. This lesson covers the practical work of developing one - collecting data, finding the relationships, and avoiding the traps.

## Collecting the data

A baseline needs two matched data sets over the baseline period:

**Energy data:**
- Utility bills or, far better, meter readings (ideally half-hourly or daily)
- Sub-meter data where available, for the specific system in scope
- Enough history - normally at least 12 continuous months

**Independent variable (driver) data, over the same period:**
- Weather data (temperatures, to derive degree-days)
- Occupancy or operating hours
- Production volumes for industrial sites
- Anything else that demonstrably drives energy use

The two must align in time so you can relate energy to its drivers period by period.

## Cleaning the data

Raw data is rarely ready to use. Before analysis:
- **Check for gaps** (missing readings) and fill or exclude them properly
- **Identify anomalies** - meter errors, estimated bills, spikes from one-off events
- **Exclude unrepresentative periods** - a long shutdown, a major fault, commissioning of unrelated plant
- **Verify units and meter boundaries** match the measurement boundary

Bad data produces a bad baseline, so cleaning is time well spent. But beware: exclude only genuine anomalies, not inconvenient data that happens not to fit - that is manipulation, not cleaning.

## Finding the relationship: regression

The core analytical tool is **regression analysis** - finding the equation that best relates energy use to its drivers. The simplest and commonest is energy against degree-days:

Energy = base load + (coefficient x degree-days)

- The **base load** is energy independent of weather (lighting, equipment, hot water)
- The **coefficient** captures how much energy rises with each degree-day (the weather-sensitive heating or cooling)

Plotting energy against degree-days should give points clustering around a straight line. The fitted line is the baseline model.

## Judging the model

A good baseline model:
- **Fits well** - the data points lie close to the line. The **R-squared** value (0-1) measures this; above about 0.75 is usually considered a good fit for monthly energy data
- **Makes physical sense** - the base load and coefficients are plausible
- **Uses the right drivers** - if weather alone fits poorly, occupancy or production may be needed (multi-variable regression)

A poor fit signals a missing driver, noisy data, or that the energy use is not well explained by the chosen variables - a warning to investigate before relying on the baseline.

## Multiple drivers

Many buildings need more than one variable. A factory's energy might depend on both degree-days *and* production volume; an office on degree-days *and* occupancy. **Multi-variable regression** handles this, producing a model with a coefficient for each driver. Include drivers that genuinely and significantly affect energy - but avoid over-fitting with spurious variables that happen to correlate by chance.

## Documenting the baseline

Finally, record everything:
- The baseline period and why it was chosen
- The measurement boundary
- The data sources and any cleaning done
- The regression model, its drivers, and its goodness of fit
- The static factors assumed constant

This documentation makes the baseline transparent, defensible, and adjustable later. A well-developed, well-documented baseline is the bedrock on which credible savings rest - and the foundation for the adjustments covered next.
