M&V is not one-size-fits-all. The IPMVP framework offers four options, each suited to different projects, balancing accuracy against cost. Choosing the right one is a key M&V skill.

## The IPMVP framework

The **International Performance Measurement and Verification Protocol (IPMVP)** is the global standard for M&V. It defines four options - A, B, C, and D - distinguished by *what* you measure (a single piece of equipment or the whole facility) and *how* you determine savings (measurement or calculation). The choice depends on the project's complexity, the value of the savings, and how much measurement is justified.

## Option A: Retrofit Isolation, Key Parameter Measurement

Isolate the affected equipment and measure only the **key parameter**, estimating the others.

- Example: a lighting retrofit - measure the new power draw (key parameter), estimate the operating hours
- Savings = change in measured parameter x estimated factors
- **Best for:** simple measures where one parameter dominates and others can be reasonably estimated
- **Strength:** low cost; **limitation:** relies on estimated values

## Option B: Retrofit Isolation, All Parameter Measurement

Isolate the affected equipment and measure **all** relevant parameters.

- Example: the same lighting retrofit, but meter both power *and* operating hours continuously
- Savings = fully measured energy before vs after, within the isolation boundary
- **Best for:** individual measures where accurate, sub-metered proof is worth the cost
- **Strength:** accurate for that equipment; **limitation:** more metering cost, only covers the isolated system

## Option C: Whole Facility

Measure energy at the **whole-building (utility meter) level** and use regression to separate the saving from other influences.

- Example: a package of measures across a building - compare adjusted baseline to actual whole-building energy
- Savings = adjusted baseline energy - actual energy, using the utility meters
- **Best for:** whole-building projects, multiple interacting measures, large savings relative to total use
- **Strength:** captures everything, uses existing meters; **limitation:** needs savings large enough (typically 10%+) to be visible above the noise, and good baseline modelling

## Option D: Calibrated Simulation

Use an **energy simulation model** of the building, calibrated against measured data, to estimate savings.

- Example: a new building, or where no usable baseline exists (so you cannot measure "before")
- Savings = simulated baseline - simulated (or actual) post-project energy
- **Best for:** new construction, missing baseline data, or complex interactions hard to isolate
- **Strength:** works without a measured baseline; **limitation:** requires simulation expertise and careful calibration

## Choosing the right option

The choice balances several factors:

- **What changed:** a single system favours A or B (isolation); whole-building packages favour C
- **Savings size:** small savings need isolation (A/B) to be visible; large whole-building savings suit C
- **Baseline data:** no baseline pushes toward D (simulation)
- **Value and budget:** higher-value projects (and performance contracts) justify more measurement (B over A, or rigorous C)
- **Accuracy needed:** more measurement means more cost but more confidence

## A practical guide

- **Simple equipment swap, modest value:** Option A
- **Single system, accuracy matters (e.g. performance contract):** Option B
- **Whole-building or multiple measures, large savings:** Option C
- **New build or no baseline:** Option D

## The principle

Whichever option, the goal is the same: a credible, defensible saving figure for a reasonable cost. IPMVP's value is in providing a recognised, structured way to get there - so the method, its boundary, and its assumptions are transparent and agreed in advance, especially where money (a performance contract) depends on the result. The chosen option is documented in an **M&V plan** before the project, locking in how savings will be determined and avoiding disputes later.
