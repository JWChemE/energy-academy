M&V is as much about integrity as arithmetic. Savings can be overstated - deliberately or accidentally - in many ways. Knowing the common pitfalls is essential to producing numbers that withstand scrutiny.

## Boundary changes

The **measurement boundary** defines what is included. Savings go wrong when something changes inside the boundary that was not part of the project:
- A new production line is added inside the boundary, raising energy and masking the saving
- Equipment unrelated to the project is removed, falsely inflating the saving
- The boundary is quietly redrawn between baseline and reporting

The fix is **non-routine adjustments**: when a static factor changes (an extension, new equipment, a process change), the baseline must be formally adjusted to reflect it. Failing to make non-routine adjustments is a leading cause of wrong savings figures.

## Ignoring or under-counting drivers

Omitting a significant driver overstates or understates savings:
- Claiming a saving when really the building was less occupied or made less product
- Not normalising for weather and crediting a mild winter to the project
- Missing a driver that changed between periods

Every significant, changed driver must be in the model. The classic error is comparing raw energy bills year on year with no adjustment at all.

## Weather luck

A mild winter or cool summer lowers energy for reasons nothing to do with any project. Without weather normalisation, this "weather luck" gets miscredited as a saving (or, in a harsh year, hides a real one). Always normalise for weather - never compare raw consumption across years of different weather.

## The rebound effect

After an efficiency measure, behaviour sometimes changes in ways that consume some of the saving:
- A better-insulated, cheaper-to-heat building gets heated to a higher temperature
- More efficient lighting gets left on longer because it "costs less"
- Improved comfort leads to longer operating hours

This **rebound effect** (or "take-back") is real and often 5-20% of the predicted saving. It is not an error in the M&V - the measured saving correctly reflects it - but it explains why measured savings can fall short of engineering predictions, and it must be understood when setting expectations.

## Selective data and cherry-picking

Savings can be inflated by manipulating data:
- Excluding "inconvenient" high-consumption reporting periods while keeping favourable ones
- Choosing an unrepresentative baseline period (an unusually high-consumption year makes any later year look better)
- Excluding data as "anomalous" only when it hurts the saving

The discipline is to set the rules - baseline period, exclusion criteria, boundary, drivers - **in advance** in the M&V plan, and apply them consistently. Decisions made after seeing the results invite bias.

## Over-fitting the baseline model

Adding spurious variables to a regression can make it fit the historical data beautifully while predicting badly. An over-fitted model with too many drivers chases noise rather than real relationships. Keep models as simple as the physics allows - include genuine drivers, resist the temptation to add variables just to raise R-squared.

## Optimism in predictions versus measurement

Engineering predictions of savings are often optimistic - ideal conditions, perfect operation, full persistence. Measured savings frequently come in lower because of rebound, imperfect operation, and real-world conditions. M&V's role is to report what *actually* happened, not to defend the original prediction. Resisting pressure to make the measurement match the promise is part of M&V integrity.

## Persistence and drift

A saving proven in year one may not persist:
- Controls drift back to old settings
- Equipment fouls and loses efficiency
- New staff override the efficient setup

Claiming the year-one saving for years two through ten without continued verification overstates the lifetime benefit. Ongoing M&V detects this drift - linking back to maintenance and performance tracking.

## The overstatement trap

The thread through all these pitfalls is **overstatement** - claiming more saving than truly occurred. Overstated savings are the most damaging error in energy management: they erode credibility when bills fail to fall, they corrupt performance-contract settlements, and they undermine the case for future investment. The antidote is rigour and honesty: clear boundaries, all relevant drivers, weather normalisation, rules set in advance, simple defensible models, non-routine adjustments when static factors change, and an unflinching commitment to reporting what really happened.
