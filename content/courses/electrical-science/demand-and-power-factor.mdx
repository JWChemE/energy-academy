Your electricity bill has two main charges: **energy (kWh)** and **demand (kW)**. Managing demand is often a bigger lever than managing total kWh.

## How demand charges work

Utilities measure your peak power draw in fixed intervals (usually 30 minutes). The **highest 30-minute peak in the month** sets your demand charge for that month.

If your peak is 100 kW in any 30-minute window, you're charged for 100 kW all month, even if you're usually at 50 kW.

Example: A 500 kW peak in January = ~£5,000–10,000/month depending on your contract. Cut the peak to 450 kW and you save ~£500–1,000/month.

## Why this matters

A single event — all compressors or HVAC units starting simultaneously — can spike demand and cost you thousands for the whole billing period.

## Practical demand-management tactics

1. **Load shifting** — spread start-ups. Stagger boiler firing, start compressors one at a time, delay non-essential equipment.
2. **Reduce peak loads** — turn off non-essential equipment during peak hours (typically 8am–8pm in summer).
3. **Improve power factor** — reduces apparent power (kVA), which utilities sometimes charge for alongside kW.
4. **Energy storage** — charge a thermal store or battery at off-peak, discharge at peak. Smooths the load.

Cost of implementing: £2,000–5,000 for controls to stagger loads. Savings: £5,000–20,000/year depending on your peak. Payback: often under 1 year.

<Callout variant="key" title="Demand management is often more valuable than energy reduction">
Cutting peak demand by 10 kW saves ~£1,000/month. Cutting total energy by 10% (say, 5,000 kWh) saves ~£500/month at typical rates. Peak is more valuable.
</Callout>

## Checking your bill

Look at:
- **kWh** used (bottom of the bill)
- **Demand** or **kW** peak (often labeled as "maximum demand" or "contracted capacity")
- **Power factor charge** (if you have one; it's often hidden as a % adjustment to kWh rates)

If your facility runs 24/7, demand management is less valuable (you're always at high load). If it's office hours only, demand spikes are common and very worth managing.
