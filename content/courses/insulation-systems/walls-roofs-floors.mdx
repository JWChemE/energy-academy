The opaque elements of a building - walls, roof, and floor - carry the bulk of fabric heat loss. Each is insulated differently, and each has its own pitfalls.

## Walls

Wall construction dictates the approach:

- **Cavity walls:** inject insulation into the gap (mineral fibre or polystyrene bead). Cheap, quick, paybacks under 5 years - provided the cavity is clear, dry, and exposed conditions suit filling.
- **Solid walls:** add insulation internally or externally (covered in detail later). Far more expensive but essential for the millions of pre-1920s solid-wall buildings.
- **Framed walls:** insulate between and over the studs, taking care that the studs themselves do not become repeating thermal bridges.

## Roofs

The roof is often the cheapest, highest-impact element because heat rises and roof areas are large.

- **Cold loft:** lay insulation between and over the ceiling joists (270-300 mm of mineral wool). Simple and cheap. Keep it clear of eaves ventilation and never compress it.
- **Warm/room-in-roof:** insulate at the rafter line with rigid board or between/under rafters, maintaining a ventilation path or using a breathable membrane to manage moisture.
- **Flat roofs:** "warm deck" (insulation above the structure) is preferred over "cold deck" to avoid condensation risk.

## Floors

Ground floors lose heat at their perimeter most of all.

- **Suspended timber floors:** fit insulation between the joists, supported on netting or battens, with attention to draught-sealing.
- **Solid floors:** add rigid insulation above (raising floor level) or incorporate it when a slab is replaced. Perimeter insulation matters most.

## The continuity rule

Whatever the element, the overriding principle is **continuity of the insulation layer**. Gaps, compressions, and unsealed junctions create thermal bridges and air paths that disproportionately undermine performance - a 5% gap can add far more than 5% to heat loss. Detailing at junctions between wall, roof, and floor is where good and poor insulation jobs diverge.

## Moisture, always

Adding insulation changes where surfaces sit relative to the dew point. Every element needs its moisture behaviour checked - vapour control on the warm side where needed, ventilation paths maintained, breathable build-ups for older fabric - so that saving energy does not invite condensation and decay.
