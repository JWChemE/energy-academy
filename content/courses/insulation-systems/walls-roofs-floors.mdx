The opaque elements of a building, walls, roof and floor, carry the bulk of fabric heat loss, and each is insulated differently with its own pitfalls. This lesson works through the practical insulation of each element and the two rules that apply to all of them: continuity of the insulation layer, and managing moisture. The [buildings-and-envelope course](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/insulation-retrofit) covers the whole-building retrofit strategy and economics; this lesson is the element-by-element craft.

## Walls

Wall construction dictates the approach:

- **Cavity walls**: inject insulation into the gap (mineral fibre or polystyrene bead). Cheap, quick, paybacks under five years, provided the cavity is clear, dry, and the exposure suits filling.
- **Solid walls**: add insulation internally or externally. Far more expensive but essential for the millions of pre-1920s solid-wall buildings, and covered in detail in the [envelope course](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/insulation-retrofit).
- **Framed walls**: insulate between and over the studs, taking care that the studs themselves do not become a repeating [thermal bridge](/courses/insulation-systems/thermal-bridges).

## Roofs

The roof is often the cheapest, highest-impact element, because heat rises and roof areas are large.

- **Cold loft**: lay insulation between and over the ceiling joists (270 to 300 mm of mineral wool). Simple and cheap. Keep it clear of the eaves ventilation and never compress it, since compressed mineral wool loses much of its value.
- **Warm or room-in-roof**: insulate at the rafter line with rigid board, or between and under the rafters, maintaining a ventilation path or using a breathable membrane to manage moisture.
- **Flat roofs**: a "warm deck" (insulation above the structure) is preferred over a "cold deck" to avoid condensation risk within the build-up.

## Floors

Ground floors lose heat most at their perimeter.

- **Suspended timber floors**: fit insulation between the joists, supported on netting or battens, with attention to draught-sealing at the edges.
- **Solid floors**: add rigid insulation above (raising the floor level) or incorporate it when a slab is replaced. Perimeter insulation matters most, because that is where the heat escapes to the cold ground beyond the building line.

## The two rules that apply everywhere

Whatever the element, two principles override the details. First, **continuity of the insulation layer**: gaps, compressions and unsealed junctions create thermal bridges and air paths that disproportionately undermine performance, so a 5% gap can add far more than 5% to heat loss. Detailing at the junctions between wall, roof and floor is where good and poor insulation jobs diverge. Second, **moisture, always**: adding insulation changes where surfaces sit relative to the dew point, so every element needs its moisture behaviour checked, vapour control on the warm side where needed, ventilation paths maintained, breathable build-ups for older fabric, so that saving energy does not invite condensation and decay.

<Callout variant="key" title="Insulate to a plan for heat and moisture together">
Each element has its own method, but the two failure modes are shared: a broken insulation line, and mismanaged moisture. Both are cheap to get right at the design stage and expensive, sometimes destructive, to fix afterwards. Plan the continuous insulation line and the vapour and ventilation strategy for the whole element before installing anything, and check the junctions to the neighbouring elements, because that is where continuity and moisture control most often break down.
</Callout>

The last element is the transparent one, where the trade-offs are sharpest: windows and glazing.
