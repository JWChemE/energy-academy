Good pipe insulation is as much about installation quality as material choice. Poorly fitted lagging underperforms, lets in moisture, and falls apart - wasting the investment.

## Fitting the sections

Rigid pipe sections must be:
- Sized correctly to the pipe outside diameter - too loose leaves air gaps, too tight crushes the material
- Butted tightly at end joints, with staggered longitudinal seams
- Secured with banding, wire, or adhesive tape rated for the temperature
- Finished with a protective cladding (foil, PVC, or metal) where exposed to weather or mechanical damage

Gaps and crushed sections are the commonest defects - each one a small uninsulated bridge bleeding heat.

## Bends, tees, and reducers

These need purpose-made mitred or moulded sections, not improvised wrapping. A neatly mitred elbow keeps the insulation continuous around the bend; a sloppy one leaves cold spots and looks like (and is) a heat leak.

## Valves and flanges: removable jackets

Valves and flanges must be insulated but also reachable for maintenance. The solution is **removable insulation jackets** (sometimes called valve jackets or mattresses): tailored, lace-up covers filled with insulation that wrap the fitting and unclip for servicing. They pay back fast because bare fittings lose so much heat, and they get refitted properly after maintenance - unlike rigid insulation, which is often hacked off and never replaced.

## Weather and mechanical protection

Outdoor and exposed insulation needs durable cladding to keep rain out (wet insulation is useless) and to resist knocks. Aluminium or stainless cladding on industrial lines, with sealed joints shedding water downward.

## Maintaining the system

Insulation degrades: cladding splits, jackets go missing after maintenance, sections get crushed or soaked. A periodic survey - ideally with a thermal-imaging camera - finds the hot spots where insulation has failed or was never reinstated. Restoring missing pipe and valve insulation is consistently one of the quickest paybacks an energy manager can find, often under a year.

## The maintenance trap

The classic failure is insulation removed for a repair and never put back. Bare patches accumulate over years until plant rooms run hot and energy bleeds away. A simple rule - "if you take it off, put it back" - plus jackets on anything serviced regularly, prevents most of the loss.
