Good pipe insulation is as much about installation quality as material choice. Poorly fitted lagging underperforms, lets in moisture, and falls apart, wasting the whole investment, and the single most common defect is not a material failure at all but insulation removed for a repair and never put back. This lesson covers fitting the sections, handling awkward fittings with removable jackets, protecting exposed runs, and the maintenance discipline that keeps the savings from bleeding away over the years.

## Fitting the sections

Rigid pipe sections must be sized correctly to the pipe's outside diameter (too loose leaves air gaps, too tight crushes the material and destroys its performance), butted tightly at end joints with staggered longitudinal seams, secured with banding, wire or temperature-rated tape, and finished with a protective cladding where exposed to weather or mechanical damage. Gaps and crushed sections are the commonest defects, and each is a small uninsulated bridge bleeding heat, the same [continuity principle](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/thermal-bridging) that governs building insulation.

Bends, tees and reducers need purpose-made mitred or moulded sections, not improvised wrapping. A neatly mitred elbow keeps the insulation continuous around the bend; a sloppy one leaves cold spots that are, quite literally, heat leaks.

## Valves and flanges: removable jackets

Valves and flanges must be insulated but also reachable for maintenance, and this tension is where most fittings end up bare. The solution is **removable insulation jackets** (sometimes called valve jackets or mattresses): tailored, lace-up covers filled with insulation that wrap the fitting and unclip for servicing. They pay back fast because bare fittings lose so much heat (a single bare valve can lose as much as several metres of pipe), and, crucially, they get refitted properly after maintenance, unlike rigid insulation, which is often hacked off and never replaced.

## Weather and mechanical protection

Outdoor and exposed insulation needs durable cladding to keep rain out (wet insulation is useless, its conductivity soars) and to resist knocks. Aluminium or stainless cladding on industrial lines, with sealed joints arranged to shed water downward, is standard practice.

## Maintaining the system, and the trap that defeats it

Insulation degrades over time: cladding splits, jackets go missing after maintenance, sections get crushed or soaked. A periodic survey, ideally with a **thermal-imaging camera**, finds the hot spots where insulation has failed or was never reinstated, and restoring it is consistently one of the quickest paybacks an energy manager can find, often under a year, as the [pipe-lagging economics](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-sizing) showed.

<Callout variant="key" title="If you take it off, put it back">
The classic and most expensive failure in pipe insulation is not a design error but a maintenance habit: lagging removed for a repair and never replaced. Bare patches accumulate over years until plant rooms run hot and energy bleeds away invisibly, one forgotten section at a time. Two rules prevent nearly all of it: a standing instruction that insulation removed for work is reinstated as part of the job, and removable jackets on anything serviced regularly so there is no excuse to leave it bare. A thermal-imaging walk of the plant room once a year finds whatever slips through.
</Callout>
