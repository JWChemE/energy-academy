Before you can justify insulation, you have to quantify the heat being lost. The calculations are straightforward and underpin every insulation decision.

## The U-value

The **U-value** measures how readily heat passes through a construction, in W/m2K. It is the reciprocal of the total thermal resistance of all the layers:

U = 1 / (Rsi + R1 + R2 + ... + Rso)

where each R is a layer's resistance (thickness / conductivity) and Rsi, Rso are the inside and outside surface resistances. Lower U means better insulation.

## Heat loss through a surface

The steady-state heat loss through any element is:

Q = U x A x dT

- Q = heat loss (watts)
- U = U-value (W/m2K)
- A = area (m2)
- dT = temperature difference inside to outside (K)

A 50 m2 wall at U = 1.5 W/m2K with a 20 K difference loses 1,500 W. Insulate it to U = 0.3 and the loss drops to 300 W - an 80% reduction.

## Annual energy from degree-days

To turn a heat-loss rate into annual energy, use **degree-days**, which capture how cold the year is. Annual loss through an element is approximately:

Energy (kWh) = U x A x degree-days x 24 / 1000

This lets you convert a fabric improvement directly into kWh and pounds saved per year, ready for a payback calculation.

## Pipework and curved surfaces

Heat loss from pipes and vessels uses the same principle but cylindrical geometry, so loss is expressed per metre of pipe at a given temperature and insulation thickness. Standards such as BS 5422 tabulate these directly, avoiding hand calculation - covered in the pipework module.

## Putting it to work

The method is always the same: establish the current U-value (or pipe loss), calculate the improved value with insulation added, find the difference in annual energy, and weigh it against the cost. Every insulation business case starts here.
