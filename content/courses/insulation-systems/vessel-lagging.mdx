Tanks, vessels, and other large hot surfaces lose heat across their whole area. Lagging them follows the same principles as pipework but at larger scale, and the savings can be substantial.

## Why vessels matter

Hot-water cylinders, buffer stores, calorifiers, process tanks, and boilers all present large surface areas at elevated temperature. An unlagged 1,000 litre hot-water tank at 70 C can lose 1-2 kW continuously - running into thousands of pounds a year, and heating the plant room rather than the process.

## Calculating the loss

The method is the surface heat-loss equation applied to the vessel area:

Q = U x A x dT

For a bare steel tank, U is high (the steel offers almost no resistance), so loss is governed by the surface area and temperature. Adding 50-100 mm of insulation typically cuts the loss by 85-95%, exactly as for a wall - just wrapped around a tank.

## Methods of lagging vessels

- **Rigid preformed sections** for cylindrical tanks
- **Flexible mineral-wool mattresses** strapped around irregular vessels
- **Removable insulation jackets** for vessels needing access (the same approach as valves, scaled up)
- **Sprayed or injected insulation** in some industrial applications

The choice depends on temperature, shape, and the need for maintenance access.

## Access and inspection

Vessels often have manways, instruments, sightglasses, and fittings that must stay accessible. Removable jackets around these allow inspection without destroying the insulation - again avoiding the trap of lagging that is hacked off and never replaced.

## Hot and cold vessels

As with pipes, cold vessels (chilled buffer tanks, refrigeration receivers) need a vapour barrier to stop condensation soaking the insulation. Hot vessels need temperature-rated, non-combustible materials, particularly near boilers and flues.

## A reliable payback

Vessel lagging shares the appeal of pipe lagging: continuous loss from a hot surface, cheap to address, fast to repay. A thermal-imaging survey of any plant room usually reveals unlagged or poorly lagged tanks glowing on the camera - each one an easy, quick-return project.
