Tanks, vessels and other large hot surfaces lose heat across their whole area, and because that area is large the losses can be substantial. Lagging them follows exactly the same principles as pipework, at bigger scale, and it shares pipework's great appeal: a continuous, hour-after-hour loss from a hot surface that is cheap to address and fast to repay. This lesson covers why vessels matter, how the loss is calculated, the lagging methods, and the access requirements that make removable jackets essential.

## Why vessels matter

Hot-water cylinders, buffer stores, calorifiers, process tanks and boilers all present large surface areas at elevated temperature. An unlagged 1,000 litre hot-water tank at 70 °C can lose 1 to 2 kW continuously, and unlike a pipe it is heating the plant room rather than delivering anything useful.

<WorkedExample title="lagging a hot-water tank">
  <Given>
    - A 1,000 litre tank at 70 °C losing ≈ 1.5 kW bare; insulation cuts the loss by ≈ 90%
    - Heated continuously by electric immersion at £0.20/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual value of insulating it.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Saving ≈ 1.5 × 0.9 = 1.35 kW, continuously. Over a full year (8,760 h) at electric
    immersion prices: 1.35 × 8,760 × £0.20 ≈ **£2,365 a year**. Even fed from a gas boiler
    at 85% over 6,000 hours it is worth ≈ £570 a year, against an insulation jacket costing
    a fraction of that. A single unlagged tank is one of the most reliable quick wins a
    [plant-room survey](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-installation) turns up.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Calculating the loss

The method is the [surface heat-loss equation](/courses/insulation-systems/heat-loss-calculation) applied to the vessel's area: Q = U × A × ΔT. For a bare steel tank U is high (the steel offers almost no resistance), so the loss is governed by surface area and temperature. Adding 50 to 100 mm of insulation typically cuts the loss by 85 to 95%, exactly as for a wall, just wrapped around a tank.

## Methods of lagging vessels

- **Rigid preformed sections** for cylindrical tanks
- **Flexible mineral-wool mattresses** strapped around irregular vessels
- **Removable insulation jackets** for vessels needing access, the same approach as valves, scaled up
- **Sprayed or injected insulation** in some industrial applications

The choice depends on temperature, shape and the need for maintenance access.

## Access, and hot versus cold

Vessels often have manways, instruments, sightglasses and fittings that must stay accessible, so removable jackets around these allow inspection without destroying the insulation, again avoiding the [trap](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-installation) of lagging that gets hacked off and never replaced. And as with pipes, cold vessels (chilled buffer tanks, refrigeration receivers) need a **vapour barrier** to stop condensation soaking the insulation, while hot vessels near boilers and flues need temperature-rated, non-combustible materials.

<Callout variant="key" title="A glowing tank on a thermal camera is money you can see">
Vessel lagging shares the whole appeal of pipe lagging: a continuous loss from a hot surface, cheap to fix, fast to repay, and easy to find. A [thermal-imaging](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-installation) survey of any plant room usually reveals unlagged or poorly lagged tanks glowing on the camera, each one an easy, quick-return project. The larger the vessel and the hotter it runs, the more it is worth, and the more obvious it is once you point a camera at it.
</Callout>

Insulation on hot surfaces saves energy and, as the next lesson shows, protects people from burns, sometimes demanding more insulation than energy alone would.
