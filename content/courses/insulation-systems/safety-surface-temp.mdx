Insulation does more than save energy. On hot pipes and vessels it also protects people from burns, and that protection is a legal requirement in its own right, sometimes demanding more insulation than the energy economics alone would justify. Where energy and safety point the same way, which is usually, insulating a hot surface saves money and makes it safe to touch. But where they diverge, safety is the hard constraint that wins. This lesson covers the burn risk, the safe-touch standard, and what to do when safety drives the thickness.

## The burn risk

Human skin is damaged quickly by contact with hot surfaces, and how quickly depends on temperature and contact time:

- Above about 70 °C, a burn can occur in roughly one second
- At 60 °C it takes several seconds
- Around 48 to 50 °C is generally regarded as the safe threshold for sustained contact

Bare steam and hot-water pipes routinely run at 80 to 180 °C, far into the dangerous range, and an unlagged pipe or valve at head height beside a walkway is an accident waiting to happen as well as an energy loss.

## The safe-touch standard

Guidance underpinning **BS EN ISO 13732-1** and HSE advice sets a maximum touch temperature for accessible hot surfaces, commonly around **50 to 60 °C** depending on the surface material and the likely contact (bare metal feels hotter and burns faster than a painted or coated surface at the same temperature). Insulation is sized so that its outer surface stays at or below this limit wherever people can reach it, which is a genuine design calculation, not just "add some lagging".

## When safety drives the thickness

Sometimes the thickness needed for a safe touch temperature exceeds the [economic thickness](/courses/insulation-systems/economic-thickness) for energy. On a very hot pipe in an accessible area, you may add insulation purely to bring the surface temperature down, even though the extra energy saving beyond the economic optimum is small. Where the required thickness becomes impractical (very hot flues and exhausts), **guarding**, physical barriers or cages, keeps people away from the hot surface instead, and warning labels flag surfaces that remain hot.

In practice: insulate accessible hot pipes and vessels to a safe surface temperature, use guards where insulation alone cannot achieve it, label surfaces that stay hot, and pay special attention at head height, along walkways, and at maintenance access points where people work close to the plant.

<Callout variant="key" title="Safety is a hard requirement; energy is an optimisation">
Most of the time, insulating a hot surface saves energy *and* makes it safe to touch, so the two goals reinforce each other and the [energy calculation](/courses/insulation-systems/heat-loss-calculation) sets the thickness. But they are not the same kind of requirement. Energy economics tell you the thickness that maximises return, a figure you can trade against other priorities. Safe-touch temperature is a legal duty under health-and-safety law, which you cannot trade away. Where the safe-touch limit demands more insulation than the energy payback alone would justify, the safety case simply wins, and where insulation cannot achieve it, guarding must.
</Callout>

The safety logic extends to cold surfaces too: very cold pipes can cause cold burns and, more commonly, condensation that drips and creates slip hazards, both addressed by insulation with a sound [vapour barrier](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-sizing).
