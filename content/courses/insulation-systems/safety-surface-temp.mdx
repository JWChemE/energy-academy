Insulation does more than save energy - on hot pipes and vessels it also protects people from burns. Surface-temperature safety is a requirement in its own right, sometimes demanding more insulation than energy economics alone would.

## The burn risk

Human skin is damaged quickly by contact with hot surfaces. The risk depends on temperature and contact time:
- Above about 70 C, a burn can occur in roughly one second
- At 60 C it takes several seconds
- Around 48-50 C is generally regarded as the safe touch threshold for sustained contact

Bare steam and hot-water pipes routinely run at 80-180 C - far into the dangerous range.

## The safe-touch standard

Guidance (such as that underpinning BS EN ISO 13732 and HSE advice) sets a maximum touch temperature for accessible hot surfaces, commonly around **50-60 C** depending on the surface material and likely contact. Insulation is sized so that its outer surface stays at or below this limit wherever people can reach it.

## When safety drives the thickness

Sometimes the thickness needed for safe touch exceeds the economic thickness for energy. On a very hot pipe in an accessible area, you may add insulation purely to bring the surface temperature down, even though the extra energy saving is small. Where insulation thickness becomes impractical, **guarding** - physical barriers or cages - keeps people away from hot surfaces instead.

## Personnel protection in practice

- Insulate accessible hot pipes and vessels to a safe surface temperature
- Use guards or barriers where insulation alone cannot achieve it (very hot flues, exhausts)
- Apply warning labels on surfaces that remain hot
- Pay special attention at head height, walkways, and maintenance access points

## Cold surfaces too

The safety logic extends to cold surfaces: very cold pipes can cause cold burns and, more commonly, condensation that drips and creates slip hazards. Insulation with a vapour barrier addresses both.

## The dual benefit

Surface-temperature safety and energy efficiency usually pull in the same direction - insulating a hot pipe saves energy *and* makes it safe to touch. The discipline is to remember that safety is a hard requirement: where the safe-touch limit demands more insulation than the energy payback alone would justify, the safety case wins.
