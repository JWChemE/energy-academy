In an insulated building, the weak points are the bridges, the places where heat sidesteps the insulation through a more conductive path or a geometric corner. This lesson looks at thermal bridges from the insulation-craft angle: what makes them form, why insulating the main surfaces can make them worse, and the detailing that designs them out. The [buildings-and-envelope course](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/thermal-bridging) covers the whole-building context and the condensation standards; here the focus is on keeping the insulation layer itself continuous.

## What a thermal bridge is

A **thermal bridge** is a localised area of higher heat flow, caused either by a more conductive material penetrating the insulation (a steel beam, a concrete balcony, a metal fixing) or by geometry (an external corner, where more heat escapes than from a flat area of the same footprint). Heat takes the path of least resistance, and it concentrates wherever the insulation is interrupted or bypassed.

## Why they matter in insulation work

Bridges can add 10 to 30% to the heat loss calculated from the plain element U-values if they are ignored, so an insulation job that hits its target U-values on every surface can still underperform badly at the junctions. Worse, they create cold inner surfaces that collect condensation and grow mould, cause visible "ghosting" (dark staining that tracks the cold studs or junctions), and chill nearby occupants.

The retrofit twist matters especially here: adding insulation to the main surfaces makes any untreated bridge *relatively* colder, because the surrounding fabric is now warm while the bridge still conducts. A junction that was harmless in an uninsulated wall can become a condensation risk once the wall around it is insulated. Insulation work can therefore *create* a bridge problem that did not visibly exist before, which is why bridges must be addressed as part of the insulation, not left as an afterthought.

## Common bridge locations

- Window and door reveals, lintels, and sills
- Wall-to-floor and wall-to-roof junctions
- Balconies and canopies that penetrate the wall
- Steel or concrete structural elements crossing the insulation line
- Service penetrations and fixings

## Designing them out

- Keep the insulation layer **continuous**, overlapping it around junctions.
- Use **thermal breaks**: insulated lintels, cavity closers, low-conductivity structural connectors at balconies.
- Insulate window reveals and carry wall insulation onto the frame.
- For [internal wall insulation](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/insulation-retrofit), return the insulation a short distance onto party walls and around openings to lengthen the heat path and warm the junction.

Designers verify junctions using the **temperature factor (fRsi)**, a measure of how warm the inner surface stays; keeping fRsi above about 0.75 holds the surface clear of the dew point and prevents mould. **Accredited construction details** provide pre-calculated, safe junctions and are the simplest route to bridge-free insulation.

<Callout variant="key" title="Insulation is only as good as its continuity">
The craft rule for insulation detailing is the same at every scale, pipe, vessel or building: the layer must be continuous, because a bridge or gap disproportionately undermines the whole. A 5% break in coverage can add far more than 5% to the heat loss, and on a cold surface it invites condensation as well. Draw or trace the insulation line around every junction without lifting the pen; wherever you have to, you have found a bridge to design out before it becomes a mould stain.
</Callout>
