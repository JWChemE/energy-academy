Choosing the right insulation means matching a material's properties to the application: temperature, moisture, fire risk, space and budget all pull on the decision, and no single material wins everywhere. A material that is ideal in a loft is wrong on a steam pipe, and one that suits a below-ground wall would be a fire risk near a flue. This lesson covers thermal conductivity, the common materials and their niches, and the properties beyond conductivity that often decide the choice.

## Thermal conductivity (lambda)

The key property is **thermal conductivity (lambda, W/mK)**, how readily the material conducts heat. Lower is better, meaning less thickness is needed for the same thermal resistance:

- Vacuum insulation panels: 0.004 to 0.008 W/mK (exceptional, costly, fragile)
- Phenolic foam / PIR: 0.018 to 0.024 W/mK
- Polystyrene (EPS/XPS): 0.030 to 0.038 W/mK
- Mineral wool (glass or rock): 0.032 to 0.044 W/mK
- Natural fibres (wood, sheep wool, hemp): 0.038 to 0.045 W/mK

Lower conductivity buys thinner build-ups, which matters where space is tight (internal wall insulation, floors) and is worth paying for there, but is often not worth the premium in a loft where thickness is free.

## Common materials and their niches

**Mineral wool** (glass or rock): cheap, non-combustible, good acoustically, and vapour-open. The default for lofts, cavities and pipework. Rock wool handles higher temperatures, making it ideal for [industrial pipe lagging](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-sizing).

**Rigid foam boards** (PIR, phenolic): high performance in thin sheets, ideal where space is tight (internal wall insulation, floors, flat roofs). Their combustibility means fire detailing needs care, especially in tall buildings.

**Polystyrene** (EPS/XPS): moisture-resistant, used below ground, under floors and in external wall systems. XPS resists water better than EPS.

**Natural materials** (wood fibre, hemp, cellulose): breathable and low in embodied carbon, well suited to older breathable buildings where [managing moisture](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/insulation-retrofit) matters more than minimum thickness.

## Beyond conductivity

Conductivity is only the headline; four other properties frequently decide the material:

- **Fire performance**: non-combustible (mineral wool) versus combustible foams, critical in tall buildings and near hot surfaces.
- **Moisture behaviour**: does it absorb water and lose performance, or stay dry? Is it vapour-open or does it act as a vapour barrier?
- **Temperature limit**: industrial lagging on steam lines needs materials rated to hundreds of degrees, calcium silicate, rock wool, or aerogel.
- **Embodied carbon**: the emissions to manufacture the insulation, increasingly weighed against the operational carbon it saves over its life.
- **Durability and compressive strength**: floor and roof insulation must bear loads without crushing, which would destroy its performance.

<Callout variant="key" title="There is no universally best insulant, only the right one for the job">
The material follows the application, not a league table of conductivity. Loft: mineral wool, cheap and thick. Tight internal wall: phenolic board, thin and high-performance. Damp or below-ground: XPS. Hot industrial pipe: rock wool or calcium silicate, rated for the temperature and non-combustible. Heritage breathable wall: wood fibre, to keep the fabric drying. The right answer balances conductivity against fire, moisture, temperature, space and cost, and the balance is different for every surface on site.
</Callout>
