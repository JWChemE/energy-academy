Choosing the right insulation means matching material properties to the application - temperature, moisture, fire risk, space, and budget all influence the decision.

## Thermal conductivity (lambda)

The key property is **thermal conductivity (lambda, W/mK)** - how readily the material conducts heat. Lower is better, meaning less thickness for the same resistance.

- Vacuum insulation panels: 0.004-0.008 W/mK (exceptional, costly, fragile)
- Phenolic foam / PIR: 0.018-0.024 W/mK
- Polystyrene (EPS/XPS): 0.030-0.038 W/mK
- Mineral wool (glass/rock): 0.032-0.044 W/mK
- Natural fibres (wood, sheep wool, hemp): 0.038-0.045 W/mK

## Common materials and their niches

**Mineral wool** (glass or rock): cheap, non-combustible, good acoustic performance, vapour-open. The default for lofts, cavities, and pipework. Rock wool handles higher temperatures - ideal for industrial pipe lagging.

**Rigid foam boards** (PIR, phenolic): high performance in thin sheets, ideal where space is tight (internal wall insulation, floors, flat roofs). Combustibility and fire detailing need care.

**Polystyrene** (EPS/XPS): moisture-resistant, used below ground, under floors, and in external wall systems. XPS resists water better than EPS.

**Natural materials** (wood fibre, hemp, cellulose): breathable and low embodied carbon, well suited to older breathable buildings where managing moisture matters more than minimum thickness.

## Beyond conductivity

- **Fire performance:** non-combustible (mineral wool) versus combustible foams - critical in tall buildings and near hot surfaces.
- **Moisture behaviour:** does it absorb water (losing performance) or stay dry? Is it vapour-open or a vapour barrier?
- **Temperature limit:** industrial lagging on steam lines needs materials rated to hundreds of degrees - calcium silicate, rock wool, or aerogel.
- **Embodied carbon:** the emissions to make the insulation, increasingly weighed against the operational carbon it saves.
- **Durability and compressive strength:** floor and roof insulation must bear loads without crushing.

## Matching material to job

There is no universally best insulant. Loft: mineral wool, cheap and thick. Tight internal wall: phenolic board. Damp or below-ground: XPS. Hot industrial pipe: rock wool or calcium silicate. Heritage breathable wall: wood fibre. The right answer balances conductivity against fire, moisture, temperature, space, and cost.
