More insulation always saves more energy, but each added millimetre saves less than the last while costing the same to install. Somewhere between "not enough" and "wasteful excess" is an optimum, the economic thickness, and finding it is what separates an engineered specification from a guess. This lesson explains the principle of diminishing returns, how to locate the optimum, what shifts it, and why the practical constraints sometimes overrule the pure economics.

## The principle of diminishing returns

Heat loss falls with insulation thickness, but not linearly. The first 50 mm might cut the loss in half; the next 50 mm cuts the *remainder* in half, a smaller absolute saving. Plot cumulative energy saving against thickness and the curve flattens out. Meanwhile the cost of insulation rises roughly linearly with thickness, since each extra layer costs about the same to buy and fit. The two trends going in opposite directions are what create an optimum.

## Finding the optimum

**Economic thickness** is where the marginal cost of adding more insulation equals the marginal value of the energy it would save over the chosen payback period. Add the falling energy-cost curve to the rising insulation-cost curve, and the total-cost curve has a minimum; that minimum is the economic thickness.

Below it, you are leaving cheap savings on the table, each additional millimetre would still pay for itself. Above it, you are spending more on insulation than you will ever recover in energy. The [heat-loss method](/courses/insulation-systems/heat-loss-calculation) provides the energy side of the calculation, and the fuel price and payback period provide the value side.

## What shifts the optimum

The economic thickness increases when:

- Energy prices are high (each saved kWh is worth more)
- The surface is hot (industrial pipes) or runs continuously (more hours of loss)
- The acceptable payback period is long
- The fuel is carbon-priced or emissions are explicitly valued

It decreases for low temperatures, intermittent operation, cheap energy, or short required paybacks. Because the surface temperature and running hours matter so much, industrial steam lines justify far thicker insulation than a domestic heating pipe.

<WorkedExample title="why hot industrial pipe justifies thick insulation">
  <Given>
    - A steam main at 150 °C; standards such as BS 5422 tabulate its economic thickness directly by pipe size and temperature
    - For a 100 mm pipe at this temperature the table calls for roughly 80 to 100 mm of insulation
  </Given>
  <Find>Why the optimum sits so high, and what going thinner costs.</Find>
  <Solution>
    The surface is very hot and (on a steam system) runs continuously, so every millimetre
    of insulation saves a large, year-round quantity of heat, pushing the optimum out to
    80 to 100 mm. Going thicker than the table yields little extra saving; going *thinner*
    wastes heat continuously, often with a payback under a year for the missing insulation.
    On hot, continuous industrial services the economics point firmly toward generous
    thickness, which is exactly why the [pipe-lagging lesson](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-sizing)
    treats bare hot pipe as one of the best paybacks on any site.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

<Callout variant="key" title="The economic optimum is a floor, not the final answer">
Economic thickness gives the financial optimum, but other limits then intervene, and they usually push the specification *up*, never down. Available space, [surface-temperature safety](/courses/insulation-systems/safety-surface-temp) (preventing burns), condensation control on cold pipes, and the standard product thicknesses actually manufactured all constrain the final choice. Treat the economic thickness as the minimum a sound business case supports, then reconcile it with these practical requirements, taking whichever demands the most insulation.
</Callout>
