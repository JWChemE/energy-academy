More insulation always saves more energy - but each added millimetre saves less than the last, while costing the same to install. Somewhere there is an optimum: the economic thickness.

## The principle of diminishing returns

Heat loss is inversely related to insulation thickness. The first 50 mm might cut loss by half; the next 50 mm cuts the remainder by half again - a smaller absolute saving. Plot cumulative energy saving against thickness and the curve flattens. Meanwhile the cost of insulation rises roughly linearly with thickness.

## Finding the optimum

**Economic thickness** is where the marginal cost of adding more insulation equals the marginal value of the energy it saves over the chosen payback period. Add the energy-cost curve (falling) to the insulation-cost curve (rising) and the total-cost curve has a minimum - that minimum is the economic thickness.

Below it, you are leaving cheap savings on the table. Above it, you are spending more on insulation than you will ever recover in energy.

## What shifts the optimum

The economic thickness increases when:
- Energy prices are high (each saved kWh is worth more)
- The surface is hot (industrial pipes) or runs continuously
- The acceptable payback period is long
- The fuel is carbon-priced or emissions are valued

It decreases for low temperatures, intermittent operation, cheap energy, or short required paybacks.

## Industrial pipework example

For a steam main, standards such as BS 5422 tabulate economic thicknesses directly by pipe size and temperature. A 100 mm pipe at 150 C might call for 80-100 mm of insulation. Going thicker yields little extra saving; going thinner wastes heat continuously, often with paybacks under a year for the missing insulation.

## Don't forget the practical limits

Economic thickness is the financial optimum, but other limits intervene: available space, surface-temperature safety (preventing burns), condensation control on cold pipes, and standard product thicknesses. The final specification reconciles the economic optimum with these practical constraints.
