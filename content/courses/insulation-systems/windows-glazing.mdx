Within an insulation strategy, windows are the element where the least resistance is achievable and the trade-offs are sharpest. They must balance heat loss, solar gain, daylight, and ventilation.

## Why windows are the weak point

Even excellent triple glazing (around 0.8 W/m2K) loses far more heat per square metre than an insulated wall (0.2 W/m2K). You cannot insulate a window to wall standards while keeping it transparent, so the strategy is to optimise rather than eliminate the loss.

## The performance levers

- **Glazing layers:** single (around 5.0), double (1.2-1.6), triple (0.8-1.0 W/m2K).
- **Low-E coatings:** reflect indoor heat back inside, the biggest single improvement.
- **Gas fills:** argon (or krypton) cut convection in the cavity.
- **Warm-edge spacers:** reduce the bridge around the glass perimeter.
- **Insulated frames:** the frame can be the weakest part - specify thermally broken or multi-chamber profiles.

The headline figure is the **whole-window U-value (Uw)**, combining glass, frame, and spacer.

## The solar trade-off

Glazing is unique in admitting useful solar heat (quantified by the g-value). In a heating-dominated building, a south window with good solar gain can be a net energy benefit over the year despite its heat loss. The insulation strategy therefore differs by orientation: maximise gain on the south, minimise loss everywhere, and control overheating where glazing is extensive.

## Secondary glazing for heritage

Where original windows must be kept (listed buildings, conservation areas), **secondary glazing** - a discreet internal pane - roughly halves the heat loss and cuts draughts and noise, at far lower cost and disruption than replacement, while preserving the historic window.

## Where windows sit in the plan

On pure heat-loss economics, glazing upgrades pay back slowly and come late in the insulation sequence. But combined with comfort, condensation, and noise benefits - and especially when bundled with other works or when frames are failing anyway - they become a sensible part of the whole-building insulation strategy.
