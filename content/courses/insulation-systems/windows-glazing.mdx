Within an insulation strategy, windows are the element where the least thermal resistance is achievable and the trade-offs are sharpest: they must balance heat loss, solar gain, daylight and ventilation all at once, and you cannot insulate them to wall standards while keeping them transparent. This closing element lesson covers why windows are the weak point, the levers that improve them, and the low-disruption route for heritage buildings. The [buildings-and-envelope course](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/window-types) covers glazing performance in full; here the focus is where glazing fits within an insulation strategy.

## Why windows are the weak point

Even excellent triple glazing (around 0.8 W/m²K) loses far more heat per square metre than an insulated wall (0.2 W/m²K). You cannot insulate a window to wall standards and still see through it, so the strategy is to *optimise* rather than eliminate the loss, and to be deliberate about how much glazing a facade carries in the first place.

## The performance levers

The [envelope course](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/window-types) details these; in summary, the levers are glazing layers (single ≈ 5.0, double 1.2 to 1.6, triple 0.8 to 1.0 W/m²K), [low-E coatings](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/glazing-coatings) (the single biggest improvement, reflecting indoor heat back inside), gas fills (argon or krypton to cut convection in the cavity), warm-edge spacers (reducing the bridge around the glass perimeter), and insulated frames (often the weakest part, so specify thermally broken or multi-chamber profiles). The headline figure is the **whole-window U-value (Uw)**, which combines glass, frame and spacer.

## The solar trade-off

Glazing is unique among fabric elements in admitting useful solar heat, quantified by the [g-value](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/solar-gain). In a heating-dominated building, a south-facing window with good solar gain can be a net energy benefit over the year despite its heat loss, gaining more in winter sun than it loses through the glass. The insulation strategy therefore differs by orientation: maximise gain on the south, minimise loss everywhere, and control overheating where glazing is extensive. This is the one fabric element where "more insulation everywhere" is not automatically right.

## Secondary glazing for heritage

Where original windows must be kept (listed buildings, conservation areas), **secondary glazing**, a discreet internal pane fitted behind the existing window, roughly halves the heat loss and cuts draughts and noise, at far lower cost and disruption than replacement, while preserving the historic window. It is often the only route that satisfies both conservation and energy requirements, and it avoids the fifty-year payback that [full replacement](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/window-replacement) suffers on energy grounds alone.

<Callout variant="key" title="Optimise glazing; do not try to insulate it like a wall">
Windows are the deliberate weak point in the insulation line, and the strategy accepts that rather than fighting it. You cannot make transparent glass perform like an insulated wall, so the craft is to minimise the loss (low-E, argon, warm-edge, insulated frames, sensible glazing areas), turn orientation to advantage by welcoming winter solar gain where it helps, and reach for [secondary glazing](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/window-replacement) where replacement is barred. On pure heat-loss economics, glazing pays back slowly and sits late in any insulation plan; its case is usually made by comfort, condensation and noise, and by bundling it with other works.
</Callout>
