You've learned to calculate heat loss, choose materials by their conductivity, find the economic thickness, lag pipes and vessels, control condensation on cold lines, and insulate hot surfaces to a safe touch temperature. This capstone walks you round a plant with a thermal camera, finding the heat bleeding away and prescribing the right fix for each.

Insulation is where the cheapest, fastest-payback projects in any facility hide, bare hot surfaces bleed heat continuously, and a thermal-imaging survey of any plant room lights them up. But "add insulation" isn't always the whole answer: the thickness is set by economics *and* by safety, the material by temperature, moisture and fire, and the job is only done if the fittings are covered and the lagging survives the next maintenance visit.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate**: read the brief and the panel: temperatures, lengths, areas, conductivities, surface temperatures.
2. **Calculate**: work out the figure that decides it: the heat loss (Q = U·A·ΔT, or W/m for pipes), the saving, the payback, the thickness for a target resistance. The **reference data is on hand**: heat-loss relationships, bare-pipe losses, conductivities, the economic-thickness idea, safe-touch limits, but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose**: pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe**: build an ordered work plan (reinstate/maintain first, then capital).
5. **Verify**: score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="Right thickness, right material, actually fitted">
Lagging a hot surface saves energy *and* makes it safe to touch: usually the same direction. But get the details right: insulate to the economic thickness (not thinner, not gold-plated), match the material to the temperature, moisture and fire risk, cover the awkward valves and fittings with removable jackets, put a vapour barrier on anything cold, and make sure the lagging actually goes back on after maintenance. The biggest losses are usually the simplest: bare pipe, bare valves, and lagging hacked off and forgotten.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **Pipe and vessel loss**: W/m for bare pipe, Q = U·A·ΔT for tanks, and the ~90% that lagging recovers.
- **Economic thickness**: under-insulated pipe is a hidden loss, but diminishing returns mean there's a point past which thicker doesn't pay.
- **Cold pipes**: why a sweating chilled line is a vapour-barrier problem, not a thickness problem.
- **Materials**: matching conductivity to a space constraint, and temperature/fire to the application.
- **Safety**: surface-temperature limits, and why safety can demand more insulation than energy alone.
- **Maintenance**: the lagging that gets cut off and never replaced.

## Differential thinking

"It's losing heat, add insulation" misses half the cases. A sweating cold pipe doesn't need more mineral wool: it needs a vapour barrier and closed-cell material. A pipe in a tight void doesn't need more thickness: it needs a lower-conductivity material. Thin lagging needs topping up to the economic thickness, but not beyond it. And a glowing bare patch after an overhaul isn't a design gap: it's lagging that was removed and never reinstated. Read the evidence: the temperature, the moisture, the space, the surface you can touch.

## After you finish

- Which **observation** decided each case: the temperature, the dripping, the space available, the surface you could touch?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, is it a maintenance job (reinstate, fit jackets), a thickness or material change, or a safety requirement that stands regardless of the energy payback?
