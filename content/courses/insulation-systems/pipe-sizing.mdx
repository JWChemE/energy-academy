Bare hot pipes and vessels bleed heat continuously, usually in plant rooms where nobody sees the cost, and pipe insulation (lagging) is one of the cheapest, fastest-payback measures in any facility. The heat is being lost every hour the system runs, the fix is well-understood, and the payback is often under a year. This lesson covers the scale of the loss, how thickness is chosen from standards, which material suits which service, and why cold pipes need a completely different approach.

## The scale of the loss

A bare 100 mm steam pipe at 150 °C loses roughly 0.4 to 0.6 kW per metre into a 20 °C room. That sounds small until you multiply it by the length of a real run.

<WorkedExample title="what a bare steam main costs">
  <Given>
    - 100 m of bare 100 mm steam pipe losing ≈ 0.5 kW/m
    - System runs 6,000 h/yr; heat from a boiler at 85% efficiency, gas £0.06/kWh
    - Lagging cuts the loss by ≈ 90%
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual value of lagging the run.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Bare loss ≈ 100 × 0.5 = 50 kW, continuously. Lagging saves ≈ 45 kW, which as fuel is
    45 × 6,000 ÷ 0.85 ≈ 318,000 kWh of gas a year, worth about **£19,000 a year**. Against
    a lagging cost of a few thousand pounds, the payback is a matter of weeks. This is why
    a [thermal-imaging survey](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-installation) of any plant
    room, glowing bare pipe everywhere, so often reveals five-figure savings sitting in
    plain sight.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Choosing thickness

Pipe insulation thickness is selected from standards such as **BS 5422**, which tabulate the [economic thickness](/courses/insulation-systems/economic-thickness) by pipe diameter, operating temperature and ambient conditions. Higher temperatures and larger pipes justify thicker insulation. Typical values run from 25 to 30 mm on small low-temperature pipes up to 80 to 120 mm on large high-temperature mains. The standard is simply the economic-thickness principle applied to cylinders, pre-calculated so you do not have to.

## Choosing material

- **Mineral / rock wool sections**: the workhorse for hot pipes, rated to high temperatures, non-combustible.
- **Calcium silicate**: for very high temperatures (steam, exhaust), rigid and durable.
- **Phenolic / PIR**: efficient on lower-temperature heating and chilled pipes.
- **Elastomeric (closed-cell rubber)**: flexible and vapour-tight, the standard for chilled and refrigeration lines where condensation control matters.
- **Aerogel**: very thin for its performance, used where space is tight.

## Cold pipes are different

On chilled-water and refrigeration pipes, the enemy is not just heat gain but **condensation**. Because the pipe is colder than the surrounding air, any moisture that reaches it condenses, so the insulation must include a continuous **vapour barrier** on its *outer* surface. Any breach lets moist air reach the cold pipe, condensing water that soaks the insulation, destroys its performance, and corrodes the pipe underneath, often invisibly until the pipe fails. Closed-cell elastomeric materials with fully sealed joints are specified for exactly this reason, and the vapour barrier's integrity matters more than the raw thickness.

<Callout variant="key" title="Hot pipes leak heat; cold pipes drink water">
The two failure modes are opposites and demand opposite defences. On a hot pipe, the vapour is inside and escaping, so you insulate to keep heat in and safe-touch temperatures down. On a cold pipe, the vapour is outside and trying to get in, so the *vapour barrier* is the critical component and any gap in it is progressive: condensation soaks the insulation, which then conducts better, which makes it colder and wetter still. Specify closed-cell material with sealed joints on anything below ambient, and never treat a cold line like a warm one.
</Callout>

Valves, flanges and bends are often left bare because they are awkward, yet a single bare valve can lose as much heat as several metres of pipe. Removable jackets solve this, and installation quality generally is the next lesson.
