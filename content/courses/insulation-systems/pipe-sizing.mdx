Bare hot pipes and vessels bleed heat continuously, often in plant rooms where nobody notices. Pipe insulation (lagging) is one of the cheapest, fastest-payback measures in any facility.

## The scale of the loss

A bare 100 mm steam pipe at 150 C loses roughly 400-600 W per metre to a 20 C room. Over a 100 m run, that is 40-60 kW lost continuously - tens of thousands of pounds a year. Lagging it cuts the loss by 90% or more.

## Choosing thickness

Pipe insulation thickness is selected from standards such as **BS 5422**, which tabulate the economic thickness by:
- Pipe diameter
- Operating temperature
- Ambient conditions

Higher temperatures and larger pipes justify thicker insulation. Typical values run from 25-30 mm on small low-temperature pipes up to 80-120 mm on large high-temperature mains. The standard balances the insulation cost against the value of heat saved over the payback period - the economic-thickness principle applied to cylinders.

## Choosing material

- **Mineral/rock wool sections:** the workhorse for hot pipes, rated to high temperatures, non-combustible.
- **Calcium silicate:** for very high temperatures (steam, exhaust), rigid and durable.
- **Phenolic/PIR:** efficient on lower-temperature heating and chilled pipes.
- **Elastomeric (closed-cell rubber):** flexible, vapour-tight - the standard for chilled and refrigeration lines where condensation control matters.
- **Aerogel:** very thin for its performance, used where space is tight.

## Cold pipes are different

On chilled-water and refrigeration pipes, the enemy is not just heat gain but **condensation**. The insulation must include a continuous vapour barrier on its outer surface; any breach lets moist air reach the cold pipe, condensing water that soaks the insulation, destroys its performance, and corrodes the pipe. Closed-cell elastomeric materials with sealed joints are specified for this reason.

## Don't forget the fittings

Valves, flanges, and bends are often left bare because they are awkward to lag - yet a single bare valve can lose as much heat as several metres of pipe. Removable insulation jackets cover them while still allowing maintenance access, and they are covered next.
