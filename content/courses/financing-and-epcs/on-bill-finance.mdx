On-bill financing is an elegant mechanism that funds energy efficiency through the utility bill itself - repaying the cost from the savings, so the customer is better off from day one. It is one of several innovative models expanding access to energy finance.

## The idea

**On-bill financing** lets a customer fund energy efficiency improvements through their utility (energy) bill:
- The utility (or a partner) funds the efficiency measures
- The cost is repaid through a charge added to the customer's energy bill
- Crucially, the efficiency savings are designed to exceed the repayment charge
- So the customer's total bill (energy plus repayment) is lower than before - they are cash-flow positive immediately

The customer gets the improvements with no upfront cost and an immediately lower bill, while the financing is repaid automatically through the billing relationship they already have.

## Why it is powerful

On-bill financing overcomes several barriers at once:
- **No upfront capital** - the measures are funded externally
- **Immediate benefit** - savings exceed repayments, so the customer gains from day one
- **Simplicity** - repayment through the existing bill, no separate loan to arrange
- **Accessibility** - uses the utility relationship, reaching customers who might not access conventional finance

This combination - no upfront cost, immediate net saving, and simplicity - makes it particularly effective for smaller organisations and households that lack capital or appetite for conventional financing.

## The bill-neutral or bill-positive principle

The key design principle is that the savings should exceed the repayment, making the customer **bill-positive** (or at least bill-neutral):
- Before: high energy bill
- After: lower energy use (savings) + repayment charge = a total bill lower than before
- The customer pays less overall while the measures are paid off

This is the elegance of the model - the customer never pays more, so there is no financial barrier or sacrifice. It directly addresses the reluctance to invest in efficiency when capital is scarce or payback feels distant.

## On-bill repayment variants

There are variations on the model:
- **On-bill financing** - the utility provides the capital
- **On-bill repayment** - a third party provides the capital, repaid through the utility bill
- **Tariff-based** - the charge attaches to the meter/property rather than the customer, so it can transfer to a new occupant (addressing the split-incentive and tenure problems)

The property-attached variants are particularly interesting because they solve the problem of customers who might move before the measures are paid off - the obligation (and the benefit) stays with the property.

## Where it fits

On-bill financing is especially suited to:
- **Smaller organisations and SMEs** lacking capital or finance access
- **Households** (in residential energy efficiency programmes)
- **Standard efficiency measures** with reliable savings (insulation, lighting, heating)
- **Utility-led programmes** promoting efficiency among their customers

It is widely used in utility and government efficiency programmes precisely because it removes the upfront-cost barrier that deters so many smaller customers from investing in efficiency.

## Limitations

On-bill financing has constraints:
- **Availability** - it depends on utilities or programmes offering it, which varies by region
- **Scale** - typically suited to smaller, standard measures rather than large complex projects
- **Savings certainty** - the bill-positive promise depends on the savings materialising (the M&V link)
- **Programme design** - the terms and the qualifying measures are set by the programme

It is one tool among many - excellent for its niche (smaller, standard measures, capital-constrained customers) but not a universal solution.

## Part of the innovation

On-bill financing is one of a growing range of innovative mechanisms designed to expand access to energy finance and overcome the persistent barrier of upfront capital. Alongside the public-private and carbon-finance models in the following lessons, it reflects the broader trend of financial innovation making energy efficiency accessible to those who lack capital - turning the savings themselves into the means of funding the improvements. For the energy manager, knowing these mechanisms exist - and where they apply - widens the toolkit for getting projects funded, especially for smaller sites and organisations where conventional finance is hard to access.
