The shared-savings contract is a major variant of the energy performance contract, in which client and ESCO split the savings rather than the client guaranteeing a fixed payment. It allocates risk and reward differently, with its own advantages and trade-offs.

## How shared savings works

In a **shared-savings EPC**, the savings the project generates are divided between the client and the ESCO in agreed proportions (for example, 50/50, or 70/30):
- The ESCO funds and implements the measures
- The savings are measured against the baseline (the M&V link)
- The savings are **shared** between the parties in the agreed split, over the contract period
- After the contract, the client typically keeps all the savings

Both parties' returns depend on the actual savings achieved - so both share the risk and the reward.

## Shared savings versus guaranteed savings

The two main EPC models allocate risk differently:

**Guaranteed savings:**
- The ESCO guarantees a savings level; if actual savings fall short, the ESCO compensates
- The client gets certainty (protected from shortfall)
- The client typically takes on the financing (and so the credit risk)
- Suits clients wanting certainty and able to finance

**Shared savings:**
- Savings are split; both parties' returns depend on actual performance
- The ESCO typically finances the project (no client capital or credit needed)
- Both share the upside and the risk of underperformance
- Suits clients wanting no capital outlay and willing to share the savings

The key practical difference is often **who finances** and **who bears the credit risk**: in classic shared savings, the ESCO finances, so the client needs no capital and takes on no debt - attractive for organisations that cannot or will not borrow.

## Advantages of shared savings

- **No client capital or borrowing** - the ESCO finances, keeping debt off the client's books
- **Aligned interests** - both parties gain from higher savings, motivating the ESCO to maximise (not just meet) performance
- **Shared upside** - if savings exceed expectations, the client shares the bonus
- **Accessibility** - suits clients who cannot finance or guarantee

## Disadvantages and cautions

- **Higher cost of capital** - the ESCO's financing is often more expensive than the client could obtain itself, and this cost comes out of the savings
- **Less certainty** - the client's savings depend on performance (no guarantee of a fixed outcome)
- **Shared upside** - the client gives up a share of the savings it might have kept by financing itself
- **ESCO risk** - the ESCO takes on more risk (financing plus performance), which it prices in

The fundamental trade-off: shared savings removes the capital and financing burden from the client, but at the cost of sharing the savings and accepting the ESCO's higher financing cost. A client with cheap capital of its own may do better with guaranteed savings (financing itself); one with no capital may find shared savings the only route.

## The role of measurement

As with all EPCs, **measurement and verification is central** (the M&V link) - the shared savings can only be split fairly if they are measured accurately. The same M&V rigour applies: a robust baseline, clear adjustment rules, a defined method, and often independent verification. In shared savings, both parties have a direct financial stake in the measured figure, making clear, agreed M&V even more critical to avoiding dispute.

## Choosing the model

The choice between shared and guaranteed savings (and other variants) depends on the client's circumstances:
- **Capital availability** - no capital favours shared savings (ESCO finances)
- **Borrowing capacity and appetite** - unwillingness to take on debt favours shared savings
- **Cost of capital** - cheap own capital favours guaranteed savings (self-finance)
- **Risk appetite** - desire for certainty favours guaranteed savings
- **Desire for upside** - willingness to share for no capital favours shared savings

## Part of the toolkit

Shared savings, guaranteed savings, and their variants give the energy manager a range of EPC structures to match different circumstances. The unifying principle is the same: a specialist ESCO delivers savings, paid from those savings, with the risk and reward allocated by the contract structure. Combined with the conventional financing routes and the emerging models, EPCs in their various forms are among the most powerful tools for funding energy projects - especially where capital is the binding constraint. The skill is choosing and structuring the model that best fits the organisation's capital position, risk appetite, and goals.
