Energy performance contracts transfer much risk to the ESCO, but they are not risk-free for the client. Long-term contracts based on measured savings create their own complexities, and understanding the risks - and how to manage them - is essential to a successful EPC.

## The savings shortfall question

The defining risk an EPC addresses is the **savings shortfall** - the danger that the measures do not deliver the predicted savings. In a well-structured EPC:
- **Guaranteed savings** contracts place this risk on the ESCO - if savings fall short, the ESCO compensates
- This is the EPC's great strength: the client is protected from underperformance

But the protection is only as good as the contract and the measurement behind it. The detail of how shortfall is defined, measured, and remedied is where the risk really lies.

## The M&V dispute risk

Because payment depends on measured savings, **disputes over measurement and verification** are the most common EPC problem (the M&V link):
- How exactly are savings measured?
- How is the baseline defined and adjusted?
- What happens when conditions change (weather, occupancy, production)?
- Who decides if a shortfall has occurred?

If the M&V method is vague or contested, the contract can descend into argument over whose savings figure is correct. **Mitigation:** specify the M&V method in precise detail upfront (the IPMVP framework), agree the baseline and adjustment rules clearly, and often use independent verification. The rigour of the M&V provisions is the single most important protection against dispute.

## Baseline and adjustment risk

A particular M&V risk is the **baseline and its adjustments**:
- The baseline determines what "savings" means - get it wrong and every payment is wrong
- Conditions change over a long contract (the building's use, occupancy, production) - requiring adjustments (the non-routine adjustments of the M&V course)
- Disputes arise over whether changes are accounted for fairly

**Mitigation:** invest in a robust, well-documented baseline; specify clearly how routine and non-routine adjustments will be handled; and agree the process for changes in advance. The M&V course's baseline discipline is directly applied here.

## Long-term commitment risk

EPCs are long-term contracts (often 5-15 years), which creates risks:
- **Flexibility** - the organisation is committed for years; what if its needs change, it relocates, or restructures?
- **Counterparty risk** - what if the ESCO fails during the contract?
- **Relationship** - a long contractual relationship that must work over time

**Mitigation:** include sensible exit and change provisions; check the ESCO's financial strength and track record; and structure the relationship for ongoing collaboration, not just a transaction.

## Measurement boundary and interaction risk

Risks arise around what is in and out of scope:
- **Boundary** - exactly which energy and which measures the contract covers (the M&V boundary)
- **Interaction** - how the contracted measures interact with other changes the organisation makes
- **Client actions** - if the client changes operations in ways that affect savings

**Mitigation:** define the boundary precisely, and agree how client-driven changes outside the ESCO's control are handled (so the ESCO is not penalised, or credited, for changes it did not cause).

## Quality and persistence risk

Beyond the headline savings:
- **Quality** - are the measures well-designed and installed, or cheap solutions that disappoint?
- **Persistence** - do the savings last, or decay over the contract (drift, fouling - the maintenance link)?
- **Maintenance** - who maintains the measures to keep them performing?

**Mitigation:** specify quality standards and maintenance responsibilities; the performance-linked payment itself incentivises the ESCO to ensure persistence (it is paid from savings throughout), but the contract should make maintenance obligations explicit.

## Aligning interests

The fundamental risk-management principle of an EPC is **aligning interests** through the payment mechanism:
- The ESCO is paid from savings, so it is motivated to deliver and sustain them
- Guarantees place performance risk on the party best able to manage it (the ESCO)
- M&V provides the objective measurement that keeps both parties honest

A well-structured EPC aligns the ESCO's profit with the client's savings, so both want the same outcome. The risks above arise mainly when this alignment is imperfect - vague measurement, unclear boundaries, or poorly handled change. Careful contracting addresses them.

## The key to success

Successful EPCs share common features:
- **Precise, agreed M&V** - the single most important protection (the M&V link)
- **A robust, documented baseline** with clear adjustment rules
- **Clear scope and boundaries**
- **A financially sound, reputable ESCO** with a track record
- **Sensible flexibility and exit provisions**
- **Explicit quality and maintenance standards**

Get these right, and an EPC delivers savings with capital and risk transferred to a motivated specialist - a powerful proposition. Get them wrong, and the long-term, measurement-based nature of the contract creates disputes and disappointment. The diligence is in the contracting, where the rigour of measurement and the clarity of terms determine success.
