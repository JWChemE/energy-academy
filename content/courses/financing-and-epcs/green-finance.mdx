A growing market of finance is dedicated specifically to environmental and energy projects - often on better terms than conventional funding. Green finance can lower the cost of capital for energy projects and connect them to investors actively seeking such opportunities.

## What green finance is

**Green finance** is funding earmarked for projects with environmental benefits - energy efficiency, renewables, low-carbon technology, and similar. It has grown rapidly as investors, lenders, and regulators increasingly prioritise sustainability. For the energy manager, it offers a pool of capital that actively wants to fund the very projects they are trying to deliver - often on preferential terms.

## Green loans and bonds

The main instruments:
- **Green loans** - loans whose proceeds are dedicated to qualifying green projects, often with preferential interest rates or terms
- **Green bonds** - bonds issued to raise capital for green projects, bought by investors seeking sustainable investments

Both channel debt finance specifically to environmental projects. The "green" label typically requires the proceeds to be used for qualifying projects, with reporting on their use and impact - a discipline that brings transparency but also administrative requirements.

## The preferential terms

Green finance can offer better terms than conventional debt:
- **Lower interest rates** - sometimes a small discount ("greenium") reflecting strong investor demand and lower perceived long-term risk
- **Favourable terms** - longer tenors, better conditions
- **Access to capital** - tapping investors specifically seeking green assets, widening the available funding

Even a small rate reduction improves a project's economics (lowering the cost of capital and raising NPV - the appraisal link). And the access to dedicated green capital can fund projects that would struggle for conventional finance.

## ESG-linked finance

Beyond project-specific green loans, **sustainability-linked finance** ties the cost of general corporate borrowing to environmental performance:
- **Sustainability-linked loans** - where the interest rate falls if the organisation meets agreed environmental targets (e.g. carbon reduction)
- This rewards the whole organisation's progress, not just a specific project
- It connects energy and carbon performance directly to financing cost - a powerful incentive

This links the energy manager's work to the organisation's cost of borrowing: hitting carbon targets literally reduces the interest the organisation pays - a compelling argument for energy investment (the investment-case and governance links).

## Verification and standards

Green finance requires demonstrating that the money genuinely funds green projects:
- **Standards and frameworks** (such as the Green Loan Principles and Green Bond Principles) define what qualifies
- **Verification** - often independent - confirms the green credentials
- **Reporting** on the use of proceeds and the environmental impact achieved

This brings rigour and credibility (and counters greenwashing concerns) but also administrative effort. The energy manager's measurement and verification work (the M&V link) supports the impact reporting green finance requires - another reason robust M&V matters.

## The growing market and its drivers

Green finance is expanding rapidly, driven by:
- Investor demand for sustainable assets
- Regulatory pressure and disclosure requirements
- Corporate net-zero commitments needing funding (the net-zero link)
- The growing recognition that climate risk is financial risk

For the energy manager, this means an increasingly favourable funding environment - more capital, on better terms, actively seeking energy and carbon projects. Understanding and accessing this market is an increasingly valuable skill.

## Accessing green finance

To tap green finance, an organisation typically:
- Identifies qualifying projects (energy efficiency, renewables)
- Works with lenders or arrangers offering green products
- Meets the relevant standards and verification requirements
- Reports on the use of proceeds and impact

The energy manager's role is to identify and develop the qualifying projects, provide the technical and savings evidence (the investment-case and M&V links), and support the impact reporting. Green finance then provides funding - often cheaper and more readily than conventional routes - turning the organisation's environmental ambitions into funded projects. It is one of the most favourable financing routes available for energy work, and increasingly central to how energy and carbon projects are funded.
