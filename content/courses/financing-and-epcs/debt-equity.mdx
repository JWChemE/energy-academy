Energy projects need funding, and the most fundamental choice is between debt and equity - borrowing money or using owned capital. Understanding these two basic routes, and how they combine, underpins all project financing.

## The two basic routes

All financing ultimately derives from two sources:
- **Debt:** borrowed money that must be repaid with interest, regardless of how the project performs. The lender takes limited risk and earns a fixed return (interest).
- **Equity:** the organisation's own capital (or an investor's), which earns a return only if the project succeeds. The equity holder takes the risk and the reward.

Most projects use a blend of the two, and the balance between them shapes the cost, risk, and structure of the financing.

## Debt financing

Borrowing to fund a project means:
- **Repayment** of the principal over time
- **Interest** paid on the outstanding balance - the cost of the debt
- **Obligation** regardless of project performance - the debt must be serviced even if savings disappoint
- **Security** - lenders often require the asset or organisation as security

Debt is generally **cheaper** than equity (lenders accept lower returns for lower risk), so debt financing can lower the overall cost of capital. But it adds fixed obligations and risk - the project must generate enough to service the debt. For energy projects with reliable savings, debt is often attractive.

## Equity financing

Using owned capital (or bringing in equity investors) means:
- **No repayment obligation** - the capital is at risk in the project
- **Return only if successful** - equity earns from the project's success
- **Higher cost** - equity holders require higher returns to compensate for the risk they bear
- **Ownership and control** - equity may involve sharing ownership (for external investors)

Equity is more expensive but more flexible - it carries no fixed repayment burden. Internal equity (the organisation's own funds) competes with other uses of that capital (the opportunity cost / hurdle-rate link).

## The cost of capital and gearing

The blend of debt and equity determines the overall **cost of capital** - the weighted average of the cost of debt and the cost of equity (the WACC). Because debt is cheaper than equity:
- More debt (higher **gearing**) lowers the average cost of capital - improving project economics
- But more debt also raises risk and fixed obligations

There is a balance: some debt lowers the cost of capital beneficially, but too much creates financial risk. The right gearing depends on the project's reliability and the organisation's appetite for risk. For energy efficiency, with its relatively predictable savings, moderate debt financing often makes sense.

## Internal versus external capital

A practical distinction for energy managers:
- **Internal capital** - the organisation's own funds, competing against all other internal uses (new equipment, expansion, etc.). Energy projects often lose this competition not on merit but on priority (the investment-case link).
- **External capital** - debt or investment raised specifically for the project, which may not compete with internal priorities and can unlock projects that internal capital constraints would block

This is why external financing (the rest of this course) matters: it can fund good energy projects that internal capital rationing would otherwise stall - a frequent obstacle the energy manager must overcome.

## Matching financing to the project

The choice of debt, equity, or a blend depends on:
- **Project reliability** - predictable savings support debt (the obligations can be safely met)
- **Cost of capital** - cheaper debt improves economics, within prudent limits
- **Capital availability** - constrained internal capital pushes toward external debt
- **Risk appetite** - the organisation's tolerance for fixed obligations
- **Balance-sheet considerations** - the impact on financial ratios (the investment-case link)

## The foundation for what follows

Debt and equity are the building blocks from which all the more sophisticated financing structures in this course are assembled - green loans (debt with environmental terms), energy performance contracts (third-party financing repaid from savings), and the emerging models. Understanding the basic trade-off - debt is cheaper but carries fixed obligations, equity is flexible but costlier - is the foundation for choosing among them.
