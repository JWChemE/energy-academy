As carbon gains a price - through markets, taxes, and internal mechanisms - it becomes a source of value that can help fund energy projects. Carbon finance turns emissions reductions into revenue or avoided cost, strengthening the case for investment.

## Carbon as a financial factor

For most of energy management's history, the value of a project was measured in energy saved. Increasingly, the **carbon** saved has financial value too - through carbon markets, carbon taxes, and internal carbon pricing. This adds a new revenue or savings stream to energy projects:
- Reducing emissions can generate carbon credits (revenue)
- Reducing emissions avoids carbon taxes and costs (savings)
- Pricing carbon internally improves the apparent return of low-carbon projects

Carbon finance, broadly, is the set of mechanisms that turn emissions reductions into financial value - strengthening the investment case for energy and decarbonisation projects.

## Carbon markets and credits

**Carbon markets** put a price on emissions and allow the trading of allowances or credits:
- **Compliance markets** (such as emissions trading schemes) cap emissions and let participants trade allowances - so reducing emissions frees allowances to sell, or avoids buying them
- **Voluntary markets** let organisations buy credits to offset emissions, creating demand for projects that reduce or remove carbon

For an organisation covered by a compliance scheme, reducing energy use and emissions has direct financial value - fewer allowances needed. For project developers, generating verified carbon reductions can produce sellable credits (though the integrity of offsets is contested - the net-zero link).

## Carbon taxes and avoided costs

Many jurisdictions price carbon through **taxes and levies** on fuel and emissions:
- These add to the cost of energy, especially fossil fuels (the UK's various carbon and energy taxes)
- Reducing energy use and emissions therefore avoids these costs
- As carbon prices rise (and they are generally expected to), the value of avoiding them grows

This means an energy project's savings include not just the energy cost but the avoided carbon cost - and that avoided cost rises over time as carbon is priced more heavily. Including rising carbon costs in the project baseline (the investment-case link) strengthens the case, sometimes substantially.

## Internal carbon pricing

A growing number of organisations apply an **internal carbon price** - a notional cost assigned to their own emissions in decision-making:
- It makes the carbon impact of decisions financially visible
- Low-carbon projects look better when carbon is priced into the appraisal
- It prepares the organisation for rising external carbon costs
- It aligns investment decisions with carbon goals (the net-zero link)

By pricing carbon internally, an organisation tilts its investment decisions toward low-carbon options - giving energy efficiency and decarbonisation projects an advantage that reflects their true value in a carbon-constrained future. This is a powerful tool for the energy manager: it embeds the value of carbon reduction into every investment decision.

## Carbon value in the investment case

The practical implication for the energy manager is to include carbon value in the investment case (the investment-case link):
- **Avoided carbon costs** (taxes, allowances) as part of the savings
- **Rising carbon prices** over the project life (as with energy price escalation)
- **Internal carbon price** where the organisation applies one
- **Carbon credit revenue** where projects qualify

Counting the carbon value alongside the energy value gives a fuller, larger return - often decisive for low-carbon projects that look marginal on energy savings alone. As carbon pricing strengthens, this carbon value becomes an increasingly important part of the case.

## The growing importance

Carbon finance is becoming more significant as:
- Carbon prices rise and spread to more sectors
- Net-zero commitments make carbon reduction a strategic priority (the net-zero link)
- Carbon markets mature and expand
- Disclosure and regulation make carbon a financial reality

For the energy manager, this means carbon is increasingly a source of financial value, not just an environmental concern - and incorporating it into project appraisal and financing strengthens the case for the very projects they champion. The energy transition is, in part, the steady conversion of carbon from an unpriced externality into a priced financial factor - and carbon finance is how that value is captured to help fund the projects that reduce emissions.

## Connecting finance to strategy

Carbon finance closes the loop between this financing course and the strategic courses that follow. As carbon gains financial value, the funding of energy projects becomes inseparable from the organisation's carbon strategy and net-zero ambitions. The mechanisms in this course - from simple leasing to carbon pricing - are the financial tools that fund the journey. The next courses turn to procuring energy itself, changing organisational behaviour, and mapping the road to net zero - the strategic context in which all this financing is ultimately deployed.
