The energy performance contract is one of the most powerful financing mechanisms in energy management. It lets an organisation deliver energy savings with no upfront capital and little risk, by transferring both to a specialist provider paid from the savings themselves.

## The core idea

An **energy performance contract (EPC)** is an agreement under which an **energy services company (ESCO)** designs, funds, and implements energy-saving measures, and is paid from the savings those measures generate. The defining feature is that payment is linked to performance - the savings must materialise for the ESCO to be paid.

This transforms the proposition for the client organisation:
- **No (or little) upfront capital** - the ESCO funds the project
- **Performance risk transferred** - the ESCO, not the client, bears the risk that savings disappoint
- **Savings guaranteed** - many EPCs guarantee a minimum level of savings

The organisation gets the energy improvements and savings without finding the capital or carrying the technical risk.

## The ESCO

An **energy services company (ESCO)** is a specialist that delivers energy efficiency as a service:
- It **audits** the site and identifies savings opportunities
- It **designs** the package of measures
- It **funds** the project (directly or by arranging finance)
- It **implements** the measures
- It **measures and verifies** the savings (the M&V link)
- It is **paid from the savings** achieved

The ESCO's expertise and its financial stake in the outcome align its interests with delivering real savings - it only profits if the savings are genuine.

## How an EPC works

A typical EPC follows this structure:
1. The ESCO conducts a detailed energy audit and proposes a package of measures
2. A contract is agreed, specifying the measures, the guaranteed savings, the payment mechanism, and the M&V method
3. The ESCO funds and implements the measures
4. The savings are measured and verified against the baseline (the M&V link)
5. The ESCO is paid from the savings - over a contract period (often 5-15 years)
6. After the contract, the organisation keeps all the savings

The contract aligns payment with performance, so the ESCO is motivated to deliver and sustain genuine savings throughout the term.

## Guaranteed savings versus shared savings

EPCs come in two main forms:
- **Guaranteed savings:** the ESCO guarantees a level of savings; if the actual savings fall short, the ESCO makes up the difference. The client gets certainty; the ESCO bears the performance risk.
- **Shared savings:** the savings are split between the client and the ESCO in agreed proportions. The risk and reward are shared (explored further in a later lesson).

Guaranteed-savings contracts are common where the client wants certainty; shared-savings where both parties want to share the upside. The choice shapes how risk and reward are allocated.

## The appeal

EPCs are powerful because they overcome the main barriers to energy investment simultaneously:
- **Capital barrier** - no upfront capital needed (the ESCO funds it)
- **Technical risk barrier** - the ESCO bears the performance risk
- **Expertise barrier** - the ESCO brings specialist capability
- **Verification barrier** - the ESCO handles M&V

For an organisation lacking capital, expertise, or risk appetite, an EPC can unlock energy savings that would otherwise be impossible. This is why EPCs are widely used in the public sector and large estates, where capital is constrained but savings potential is large.

## The central role of M&V

Because payment depends on savings, the **measurement and verification** of those savings is the heart of an EPC (the M&V link). The contract must specify precisely how savings will be measured - the baseline, the adjustments, the method (IPMVP) - because real money depends on the answer. Disputes over M&V are the commonest cause of EPC problems, which is why the M&V provisions are negotiated carefully and often involve independent verification. The rigour of the M&V course is nowhere more consequential than in an EPC.

## The trade-offs

EPCs are not free value - the ESCO takes a share of the savings as its return for funding, risk, and expertise. So:
- The client shares (or forgoes, during the contract) some of the savings
- The total cost over the contract may exceed doing it in-house with own capital
- The client commits to a long-term contractual relationship

The trade-off is paying for the capital, risk transfer, and expertise the ESCO provides. For organisations that have these themselves, in-house delivery may be cheaper; for those that do not, the EPC's value is in making the project happen at all.
