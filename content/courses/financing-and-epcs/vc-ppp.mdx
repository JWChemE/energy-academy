Beyond conventional and performance-based finance, energy projects can tap venture capital for innovation and public-private partnerships for large-scale infrastructure. These models fund the projects that are too novel, too large, or too strategic for ordinary financing.

## Funding innovation: venture capital

**Venture capital (VC)** funds new and growing companies developing innovative energy technologies and business models:
- It provides equity investment to high-potential, higher-risk ventures
- It targets innovation - new technologies, novel business models, scalable solutions
- It accepts high risk for the potential of high returns
- It has poured into "cleantech" - renewables, storage, efficiency technology, energy software

For the energy field, VC matters because it funds the development of the technologies and services that energy managers will later deploy - the next generation of efficient equipment, smart controls, storage, and energy-as-a-service models. It funds the supply side of innovation rather than individual building projects.

## The cleantech investment wave

Climate concern and the energy transition have driven substantial venture and growth capital into clean energy:
- New efficiency and renewable technologies
- Energy storage and smart-grid solutions
- Energy management software and analytics
- Novel business models (energy-as-a-service, peer-to-peer, virtual power plants - the microgrid link)

This investment accelerates the development of the tools and services available to energy managers, continually expanding what is technically and commercially possible. While most energy managers do not raise VC themselves, they benefit from - and increasingly procure from - the innovative companies it funds.

## Public-private partnerships

**Public-private partnerships (PPPs)** combine public and private resources to fund large-scale energy infrastructure and programmes:
- The public sector provides support - funding, guarantees, policy, or de-risking
- The private sector provides capital, expertise, and delivery
- Together they fund projects too large, too long-term, or too strategic for either alone

PPPs are used for major energy infrastructure - district heating networks, large renewable projects, city-wide efficiency programmes, and the kind of strategic decarbonisation projects that underpin net-zero plans (the net-zero link).

## Why PPPs matter for energy

Large-scale energy and decarbonisation projects often face barriers that PPPs help overcome:
- **Scale** - too large for a single organisation's capital
- **Long horizons** - paybacks over decades that private capital alone finds hard
- **Strategic value** - public benefits (decarbonisation, resilience) beyond private return
- **Risk** - levels of risk that need public de-risking to attract private capital

By combining public support (funding, guarantees, policy certainty) with private capital and delivery, PPPs make viable the large, strategic, long-term projects - district energy, area-wide retrofit, major renewables - that are central to decarbonising at scale but hard to fund conventionally.

## Government co-investment and support

Related to PPPs is the broader role of public funding in energy:
- **Grants and subsidies** that reduce project costs (the investment-case link)
- **Loan guarantees** that de-risk private lending
- **Public funds and green banks** that co-invest in energy and decarbonisation
- **Policy support** (incentives, carbon pricing) that improves project economics

This public support can be decisive for projects that are strategically important but marginally economic - tipping them into viability by reducing cost or risk. Energy managers, especially in the public sector and on large projects, should be aware of the grants, guarantees, and co-investment available, which can transform a project's economics.

## Scale and strategic projects

The unifying theme of these models is funding at scale and for the future:
- **VC** funds the innovation that creates tomorrow's energy solutions
- **PPPs** fund the large-scale infrastructure that decarbonises cities and systems
- **Public co-investment** de-risks and enables strategically important projects

While most day-to-day energy projects use the conventional and performance-based finance of earlier lessons, the largest and most strategic projects - and the development of the technologies behind all of them - rely on these innovation and partnership models. They are part of the broader financial ecosystem that funds the energy transition, and the energy leader pursuing ambitious, large-scale decarbonisation (the net-zero link) will encounter them. Understanding the full landscape - from a simple lease to a public-private district-heating partnership - completes the picture of how energy projects, large and small, get funded.
