Equipment suppliers often offer to finance the very equipment they sell - letting an organisation acquire efficient plant with little or no upfront capital. Vendor finance and leasing are accessible, common routes that sidestep the capital-budget constraint.

## The capital obstacle

Many good energy projects stall not because they fail the return test but because the upfront capital is not available - it competes with other priorities for a limited budget (the investment-case link). Vendor finance and leasing address this directly by spreading the cost over time, removing or reducing the upfront capital barrier. The same project that could not get scarce capital becomes affordable as a series of payments.

## Leasing

**Leasing** lets an organisation use equipment while paying for it over time, rather than buying it outright:
- **Operating lease** - effectively renting the equipment; the lessor retains ownership, and the lease may be off-balance-sheet (subject to accounting rules). Payments are an operating expense.
- **Finance lease** - more like buying on credit; the organisation effectively acquires the asset and the associated obligation, usually on-balance-sheet.

Leasing converts a large capital outlay into manageable periodic payments (CAPEX into OPEX - the investment-case link), often funded directly from the energy savings the equipment generates.

## Vendor financing

**Vendor financing** is where the equipment supplier itself provides or arranges the funding:
- The supplier offers the equipment with a financing package
- The organisation pays over time, often with the supplier or a partner finance house carrying the credit
- It bundles the purchase and the finance into one transaction

Vendor finance is convenient (one-stop), accessible (the supplier is motivated to make the sale), and common for energy-efficient equipment - lighting, HVAC, motors, controls. Suppliers use it as a sales tool, which can mean competitive terms.

## Paying from the savings

The elegance of these routes for energy projects is that the payments can often be matched to the savings:
- The efficient equipment reduces energy costs
- Those savings fund the lease or finance payments
- Ideally, the saving exceeds the payment, so the organisation is cash-flow positive from the start

This "savings-funded" structure means the project pays for itself out of the very savings it generates, with little or no net cost to the organisation - a compelling proposition that removes the capital barrier entirely (a principle taken further by energy performance contracts).

## Advantages

Vendor finance and leasing offer:
- **No (or low) upfront capital** - overcoming the budget constraint
- **Predictable payments** - spread over time, often matched to savings
- **Accessibility** - readily available, especially through suppliers
- **Balance-sheet benefits** - some structures keep the asset off-balance-sheet (subject to accounting rules)
- **Simplicity** - particularly vendor finance, bundling purchase and funding

## Disadvantages and cautions

These routes also carry trade-offs:
- **Higher total cost** - financing is not free; the total paid exceeds the cash price (the cost of capital)
- **Commitment** - ongoing payment obligations
- **Terms scrutiny** - vendor finance terms should be compared to alternatives, as convenience can mask a higher cost
- **Ownership** - operating leases mean you do not own the asset

The convenience and accessibility come at a cost, so the terms should be compared against other financing (debt, green loans). The right choice balances the value of avoiding upfront capital against the higher total cost.

## Where they fit

Vendor finance and leasing suit:
- Organisations with constrained capital budgets
- Standard, well-understood equipment (lighting, HVAC, motors)
- Projects with reliable savings that can fund the payments
- Situations where speed and simplicity matter

They are among the most accessible financing routes - often the practical answer to "the project is worth doing but we have no capital." Combined with the performance-contract and emerging models in the following lessons, they form part of the toolkit that lets the energy manager fund projects despite capital constraints - turning good ideas that lack budget into delivered savings.
