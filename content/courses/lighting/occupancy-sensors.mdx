The cheapest light is the one that is switched off. Occupancy and presence sensors turn lights off automatically when nobody needs them, capturing savings that depend on no one remembering to flick a switch.

## How they work

Two main detection technologies:

- **PIR (passive infrared):** senses the movement of body heat across its field of view. Cheap, reliable, but needs line of sight and detects movement rather than mere presence - sit very still and it may switch off.
- **Microwave (ultrasonic) sensors:** emit a signal and detect changes in its reflection, sensing finer movement and seeing around obstacles. More sensitive, slightly more prone to false triggers.

**Dual-technology** sensors combine both, requiring agreement between them to avoid false on/off switching - the best choice for awkward spaces.

## Occupancy versus vacancy

- **Occupancy (auto-on/auto-off):** lights come on when you enter and off when you leave.
- **Vacancy (manual-on/auto-off):** you switch on manually, but the sensor switches off when the space empties. This saves more, because lights only come on when someone actually wants them - useful where daylight is often sufficient.

## Where they pay best

Savings are largest in spaces that are intermittently occupied but often lit:
- Toilets, changing rooms: 40-65% savings
- Storerooms, plant rooms: 40-60%
- Corridors and stairwells: 30-50%
- Meeting rooms, cellular offices: 20-45%

In a constantly occupied open-plan office, savings are smaller - there is little "empty" time to exploit.

## Getting the settings right

- **Time delay:** too short and lights flick off while people are still there (annoying, and frequent switching shortens some lamp lives); too long and savings shrink. LEDs tolerate frequent switching, so shorter delays of 5-15 minutes are practical.
- **Sensitivity and coverage:** aim the sensor to cover the whole occupied area without seeing corridors or neighbours through doorways.
- **Dimming to standby:** in corridors, dimming to a low level when empty (rather than full off) balances safety and saving.

## Combining with other controls

Occupancy sensing is most powerful combined with daylight harvesting (next lesson): lights come on only when the space is both occupied *and* too dark. Together these two strategies routinely halve lighting energy in suitable spaces, with paybacks commonly under three years.
