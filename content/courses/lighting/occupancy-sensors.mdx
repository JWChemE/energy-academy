The cheapest light is the one that is switched off. Occupancy and presence sensors turn lights off automatically when nobody needs them, capturing savings that would otherwise depend on someone remembering to flick a switch, which reliably does not happen. Combined with efficient lamps, controls are what turn a good [LED retrofit](/courses/lighting/led-retrofit) into a great one. This lesson covers how the sensors work, the important occupancy-versus-vacancy distinction, where they pay best, and the settings that make or break them.

## How they work

Two main detection technologies, often combined:

- **PIR (passive infrared)**: senses the movement of body heat across its field of view. Cheap, reliable, but needs line of sight and detects movement rather than mere presence, so someone sitting very still may trigger a switch-off.
- **Microwave (or ultrasonic) sensors**: emit a signal and detect changes in its reflection, sensing finer movement and seeing around obstacles. More sensitive, slightly more prone to false triggers.
- **Dual-technology** sensors combine both and require agreement between them before switching, avoiding false on/off, the best choice for awkward spaces.

## Occupancy versus vacancy

This distinction quietly decides how much you save:

- **Occupancy (auto-on/auto-off)**: lights come on when you enter and off when you leave.
- **Vacancy (manual-on/auto-off)**: you switch on manually, but the sensor switches off when the space empties.

Vacancy saves more, because lights only come on when someone actually wants them, which is especially valuable in daylit spaces where the occupant often does not need the electric light at all. Auto-on is convenient but switches lights on every time anyone passes, even at midday by a window.

## Where they pay best

Savings are largest in spaces that are intermittently occupied but often left lit:

- Toilets, changing rooms: 40 to 65% savings
- Storerooms, plant rooms: 40 to 60%
- Corridors and stairwells: 30 to 50%
- Meeting rooms, cellular offices: 20 to 45%

In a constantly occupied open-plan office, savings are smaller, because there is little "empty" time to exploit. A quick sizing: a 1 kW store lit 4,000 hours a year, with a sensor cutting the lit time in half, saves 2,000 kWh, or **£400 a year** at £0.20/kWh, for a sensor costing a fraction of that.

## Getting the settings right

- **Time delay**: too short and lights flick off while people are still there (annoying, and frequent switching shortens some lamp lives); too long and the savings shrink. LEDs tolerate frequent switching, so shorter delays of 5 to 15 minutes are practical.
- **Sensitivity and coverage**: aim the sensor to cover the whole occupied area without seeing corridors or neighbours through doorways, a classic cause of lights that never switch off.
- **Dimming to standby**: in corridors, dimming to a low level when empty (rather than full off) balances safety and saving.

<Callout variant="key" title="Vacancy control plus daylight is the winning combination">
Occupancy sensing is most powerful combined with [daylight harvesting](/courses/lighting/daylight-harvesting): lights come on only when the space is *both* occupied and too dark. Using vacancy (manual-on) rather than occupancy (auto-on) in daylit spaces compounds this, since the occupant simply never switches the lights on when the daylight suffices. Together, these two strategies routinely halve lighting energy in suitable spaces, with paybacks commonly under three years, and they catch the waste that no efficient lamp alone can address: light burning in an empty or already-bright room.
</Callout>
