You cannot manage lighting energy you have not measured. A short survey with simple instruments turns vague impressions into hard numbers that justify projects and verify their results.

## Measuring light levels

A **lux meter** (illuminance meter) reads the light falling on the working plane. Use it to:
- Check actual levels against the design target for the task
- Find over-lit areas (an immediate saving opportunity) and under-lit ones (a comfort and safety issue)
- Map illuminance across a space on a grid to reveal uneven distribution

Hold the sensor on the working plane (desk height, or floor for circulation), shade it from your own shadow, and let the reading settle. Compare against the EN 12464-1 target for that task.

## Measuring power

The energy depends on the electrical load, not the light output:
- **Plug-in power meters** for individual luminaires or circuits on a socket
- **Clamp meters** to read current on a lighting circuit (power = voltage x current x power factor)
- **Sub-metering or the BMS** for whole lighting loads over time

Measuring power before and after a retrofit is what proves the saving - the link to measurement and verification.

## Calculating energy and savings

Combine power with operating hours:

Energy (kWh) = power (kW) x hours of use

For a retrofit, the saving is the difference in power multiplied by annual hours, valued at the electricity tariff. Add the avoided relamping and maintenance for the full picture. Measuring actual operating hours (with a data logger or the BMS) rather than assuming them keeps the figures honest.

## Verifying control savings

Controls savings (occupancy, daylight, scheduling) are harder to predict and easiest to overstate. Verify them by logging the lighting circuit before and after, capturing how the controls behave in real use - the proportion of hours lights are actually off or dimmed. This both confirms the saving and exposes badly commissioned controls that are not delivering.

## A simple audit routine

1. Walk the building, noting lamp types, fitting counts, and obvious waste
2. Lux-meter readings against task targets, room by room
3. Power measurements on representative circuits
4. Log operating hours over a typical week or two
5. Identify over-lit areas, inefficient lamps, and missing controls
6. Quantify each opportunity in kWh and pounds, ranked by payback

This evidence base turns "the lighting looks old" into a costed, prioritised programme - and, after works, proves the savings were real.
