You cannot manage lighting energy you have not measured, and a short survey with simple instruments turns vague impressions ("the lighting looks old") into hard numbers that justify projects and, afterwards, prove they worked. Lighting is unusually easy to measure: a lux meter and a power meter, plus a note of the operating hours, are enough to build a costed, ranked programme. This lesson covers measuring light levels, measuring power, calculating savings, and verifying that control savings are real.

## Measuring light levels

A **lux meter** (illuminance meter) reads the light falling on the working plane. Use it to check actual levels against the [design target](/courses/lighting/lux-and-efficacy) for the task, to find over-lit areas (an immediate saving opportunity) and under-lit ones (a comfort and safety issue), and to map illuminance across a space on a grid to reveal uneven distribution. Hold the sensor on the working plane (desk height, or floor for circulation), shade it from your own shadow, let the reading settle, and compare against the BS EN 12464-1 target for that task. Over-lit spaces are common and are a saving hiding in plain sight.

## Measuring power

The energy depends on the electrical load, not the light output, so measure the watts, not the lumens:

- **Plug-in power meters** for individual luminaires or circuits on a socket
- **Clamp meters** to read current on a lighting circuit (power = voltage × current × power factor, the [three-phase arithmetic](/courses/electrical-science/three-phase-power) where relevant)
- **Sub-metering or the BMS** for whole lighting loads over time

Measuring power before and after a retrofit is what *proves* the saving, the link to [measurement and verification](/courses/measurement-and-verification), and it catches the hidden [ballast losses](/courses/lighting/ballasts-drivers) that nameplate lamp wattages miss.

## Calculating energy and savings

Combine power with operating hours: energy (kWh) = power (kW) × hours of use. For a retrofit, the saving is the difference in power multiplied by annual hours, valued at the electricity tariff, plus the avoided relamping and maintenance for the full picture. Measuring actual operating hours (with a data logger or the BMS) rather than assuming them keeps the figures honest, since assumed hours are the commonest source of an overstated business case.

## Verifying control savings

Controls savings (occupancy, daylight, scheduling) are harder to predict and easiest to overstate, because they depend on how the building is actually used. Verify them by logging the lighting circuit before and after, capturing how the controls behave in real use: the proportion of hours the lights are genuinely off or dimmed. This both confirms the saving and exposes badly commissioned controls that are not delivering, a common and invisible failure.

<Callout variant="key" title="A simple audit routine turns impressions into a programme">
Six steps build the evidence base: walk the building noting lamp types, fitting counts and obvious waste; take lux-meter readings against task targets, room by room; measure power on representative circuits; log operating hours over a typical week or two; identify over-lit areas, inefficient lamps and missing controls; then quantify each opportunity in kWh and pounds, ranked by payback. This turns "the lighting looks old" into a costed, prioritised programme, and, after the works, into proof that the savings were real. It is the [audit method](/courses/energy-audits/measurement-and-logging) applied to one system.
</Callout>
