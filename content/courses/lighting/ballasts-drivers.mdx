Lamps rarely connect straight to mains power. Between the supply and the light sits control gear, a ballast for fluorescent, a driver for LED, and its efficiency and quality matter more than people realise. On an LED fitting the driver is usually the first thing to fail and a large part of what separates a good product from a bad one, yet it is invisible on the shelf. This lesson covers why control gear exists, the fluorescent ballast choice, and how LED driver quality decides an installation's real performance.

## Why control gear exists

A fluorescent tube needs a high striking voltage to start and then current limiting, or it would draw ever-increasing current and destroy itself. An LED needs a stable, regulated DC current, quite unlike mains AC. The ballast or driver provides this conditioning, and because it sits in the power path, its own losses and reliability feed straight into the installation's performance.

## Fluorescent ballasts

- **Magnetic (wire-wound) ballasts**: the old technology. They work but waste 10 to 20% of the lamp's power as heat in the ballast itself, and cause visible flicker.
- **Electronic ballasts**: lighter, more efficient, run lamps at high frequency to eliminate visible flicker, and enable dimming. Replacing magnetic with electronic ballasts is itself an energy measure.

Ballast losses are real energy: a magnetic ballast on a 58 W tube might draw an extra 10 W continuously, which is why an "old 58 W fluorescent" often really means about 68 W at the meter, a distinction that matters when you calculate a [retrofit saving](/courses/lighting/led-retrofit).

## LED drivers

The **LED driver** converts mains AC to the regulated low-voltage DC the LEDs need, and its quality determines much of an LED product's real performance:

- **Efficiency**: good drivers are 85 to 92% efficient; poor ones waste more and run hotter.
- **Lifetime**: the driver, not the LED, is usually the first thing to fail, cheap electrolytic capacitors cooked by heat. Driver quality is why two outwardly identical LED fittings can have very different real lifespans.
- **Dimming capability**: drivers must be compatible with the dimming protocol (mains dimming, 0 to 10 V, DALI) for [controls](/courses/lighting/daylight-harvesting) to work at all.
- **Power factor and harmonics**: quality drivers maintain high power factor and low harmonic distortion, avoiding penalties and supply problems when many are installed.

## Power factor and the wider system

Large numbers of cheap drivers with poor power factor draw extra apparent current and inject harmonics into the building's electrical system, causing losses and equipment problems elsewhere, a link back to the [power factor](/courses/electrical-science/power-factor) and [harmonics](/courses/motors-and-drives/vfd-installation) issues in the electrical courses. Specifying gear with power factor above 0.9 and low harmonic distortion protects the whole installation, not just the light fitting.

<Callout variant="key" title="The driver is where a cheap LED reveals its true cost">
When two LED fittings look identical and one is half the price, the difference is usually the driver: its efficiency, its thermal design, and the quality of its capacitors. A poor driver runs hot, fails years early, dims badly or not at all, and drags down the building's power factor. Since the driver is invisible at purchase, specify it deliberately, good efficiency, high power factor, the right dimming protocol, and proven thermal design, and favour reputable products with real photometric and reliability data. The lamp gets the attention; the driver decides the lifespan.
</Callout>
