In daylit spaces, electric light is often burning needlessly while the sun does the job for free. Daylight harvesting measures the available daylight and dims the electric lighting to suit, keeping the illuminance on the task constant while cutting the energy. The occupant never notices a change in light level; the building simply stops paying for light the sky is already providing. This lesson covers the principle, why dimming beats switching, the savings by zone, and the commissioning that so often decides whether the system delivers.

## The principle

A **photosensor** measures the light level on the working plane. When daylight contributes, the control system dims the electric lighting so the combined level just meets the target (say 500 lux). As clouds pass or evening falls, the electric lighting fades back up. The user always gets the same illuminance; the building uses only the electricity needed to top up the daylight, which on a bright day near a window can be almost nothing.

## Why dimming, not switching

Crude systems simply switch luminaires off when daylight is sufficient, but the abrupt change is distracting and the control can "hunt" on and off near the threshold, flicking as a cloud passes. **Continuous dimming** is smoother and far less noticeable; occupants rarely realise it is happening, which is exactly the point. Unobtrusive control is control that stays switched on rather than being disabled by an annoyed occupant.

## The savings

Savings depend on how much daylight actually reaches the space:

- Perimeter zones (within ~4 to 6 m of windows): 30 to 60% lighting savings
- Spaces under rooflights or atria: up to 60 to 70%
- Deep-plan interiors with little daylight: minimal, since harvesting only helps where daylight arrives

This is why daylight harvesting is **zoned**: only the luminaires near glazing are controlled by daylight, while interior rows are not, because dimming an interior row against a perimeter sensor would leave it too dark.

## Designing it well

- **Zone by daylight availability**: separate control for window rows, mid-room, and interior.
- **Place sensors carefully**: they should see the working plane, not direct sun or a bright window, or they misread the level and over-dim.
- **Commission properly**: set the target level and the dimming response on site, in real conditions. Poor commissioning is the main reason daylight systems disappoint, and the [measurement lesson's](/courses/lighting/measurements) before-and-after logging is how you catch a system that is not delivering.
- **Combine with [occupancy sensing](/courses/lighting/occupancy-sensors)**: dim for daylight *and* switch off when empty.

<Callout variant="key" title="Design the daylight in, then let the controls step back">
Harvesting only captures what the building provides, so the largest gains come from designing daylight *into* the building, light shelves, rooflights, high windows, light-coloured surfaces that bounce daylight deeper, and then zoning the electric lighting so perimeter rows can dim or switch off as that daylight arrives. Good daylighting cuts lighting energy and improves wellbeing and productivity at the same time, one of the most valuable moves in low-energy design. Harvesting simply ensures the electric lighting steps back to let the free daylight count, instead of burning alongside it. The recurring failure is commissioning: a daylight system set up badly quietly reverts to running the lights at full, so verify it with logged data.
</Callout>

Occupancy and daylight handle presence and brightness. The final control layer is time: making sure lights are only on during the hours a space is used at all, which is the next lesson.
