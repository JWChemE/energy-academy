In daylit spaces, electric light is often burning needlessly while the sun does the job for free. Daylight harvesting measures the available daylight and dims the electric lighting to suit - keeping illuminance constant while cutting energy.

## The principle

A **photosensor** measures the light level on the working plane. When daylight contributes, the control system dims the electric lighting so the combined level just meets the target (say 500 lux). As clouds pass or evening falls, the electric lighting fades back up. The user always gets the same illuminance; the building uses only the electricity needed to top up daylight.

## Why dimming, not switching

Crude systems simply switch luminaires off when daylight is sufficient, but the abrupt change is distracting and the control can "hunt" on and off near the threshold. **Continuous dimming** is smoother and far less noticeable - occupants rarely realise it is happening, which is exactly the point.

## The savings

Savings depend on how much daylight reaches the space:
- Perimeter zones (within ~4-6 m of windows): 30-60% lighting savings
- Spaces under rooflights or atria: up to 60-70%
- Deep-plan interiors with little daylight: minimal - harvesting only helps where daylight actually arrives

This is why daylight harvesting is zoned: only luminaires near glazing are controlled by daylight, while interior rows are not.

## Designing it well

- **Zone by daylight availability:** separate control for window rows, mid-room, and interior.
- **Place sensors carefully:** they should see the working plane, not direct sun or a bright window, or they misread the level.
- **Commission properly:** set the target level and the dimming response on site, in real conditions. Poor commissioning is the main reason daylight systems disappoint.
- **Combine with occupancy sensing:** dim for daylight *and* switch off when empty.

## Daylight as a design asset

Beyond controls, the building itself can be designed to bring daylight deeper - light shelves, rooflights, high windows, light-coloured surfaces. Good daylighting reduces lighting energy, but also improves wellbeing and productivity, making it one of the most valuable moves in low-energy design. Harvesting simply ensures the electric lighting steps back to let that daylight count.
