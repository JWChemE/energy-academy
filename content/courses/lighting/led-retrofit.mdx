Replacing old lamps with LED is one of the most reliable energy projects available: mature technology, large savings, and paybacks often under three years and sometimes under one. It is the measure that tops more [audit opportunity lists](/courses/energy-audits/identify-opportunities) than any other. But doing it well takes more than buying the cheapest bulbs, and the difference between a good retrofit and a poor one is mostly in specification and design rather than the decision to use LED. This lesson covers the savings case, what drives the payback, the lamp-versus-luminaire choice, and the quality pitfalls.

## The savings case

LED draws 50 to 80% less power than the technology it replaces while lasting several times longer:

- Halogen downlight (50 W) to LED (6 W): 88% saving
- Linear fluorescent (58 W tube) to LED (22 W): 60% saving, plus the [ballast losses](/courses/lighting/ballasts-drivers) removed
- Metal-halide high-bay (400 W) to LED (150 W): 60%+ saving

<WorkedExample title="an office lighting retrofit">
  <Given>
    - 200 fittings, each 58 W fluorescent plus ballast ≈ 68 W, replaced with 22 W LED
    - 3,000 operating hours a year; electricity £0.20/kWh; retrofit ≈ £40/fitting
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual saving and simple payback.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Old load: 200 × 68 W = 13.6 kW. New load: 200 × 22 W = 4.4 kW. Saving = 9.2 kW ×
    3,000 h = 27,600 kWh, worth **£5,520 a year**. Cost ≈ 200 × £40 = £8,000, so payback ≈
    **1.5 years**, before counting the reduced relamping and maintenance that LED's long
    life adds on top. A typical office retrofit cuts lighting energy by 50 to 65%, and the
    economics only improve with longer hours and higher-wattage lamps.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Payback drivers

The economics improve with long operating hours (warehouses and 24/7 areas pay back fastest), high lamp wattage being replaced (high-bay, floodlighting), high access cost for relamping (high ceilings, where LED's long life avoids costly access), and [adding controls](/courses/lighting/occupancy-sensors) at the same time to compound the saving. Paybacks range from under a year for high-hour industrial lighting to three to five years for low-hour spaces.

## Lamp replacement versus luminaire replacement

- **Retrofit lamps** ("tubes" or "corn" lamps): swap the lamp, keep the fitting. Cheapest and quickest, but you inherit old, possibly inefficient optics and must address the existing ballast (bypass or compatible).
- **New LED luminaires**: replace the whole fitting. More cost and disruption, but better optics, integrated drivers, controls-ready, and a clean warranty. Usually the better long-term choice for major refurbishments.

## Quality pitfalls

Cheap LED disappoints in ways easy to miss at purchase: poor lifespan from inadequate [driver and thermal design](/courses/lighting/ballasts-drivers), bad colour rendering or the wrong colour temperature making spaces look grim, flicker causing discomfort and headaches, overstated lumen claims delivering less light than promised, and glare from bare, undiffused LEDs. Specify reputable products with verified lumen output, CRI above 80, low flicker, a suitable colour temperature, and a meaningful warranty (five years or more). Independent schemes and photometric data help separate good products from bad.

<Callout variant="key" title="Don't relamp; redesign">
A retrofit is a chance to do better than like-for-like, and treating it as a straight swap leaves money on the table. LED's efficiency and directionality often mean *fewer* fittings can do the job; [daylight and occupancy controls](/courses/lighting/daylight-harvesting) can be added while the electrician is already there; and light levels can be corrected where the old scheme over- or under-lit. Approaching the retrofit as a mini redesign, matching the [right levels](/courses/lighting/lux-and-efficacy) to each task and adding controls, captures the full saving rather than just the lamp swap.
</Callout>

Better hardware is only part of the saving. The next lesson turns to getting the design itself right (the right light for the task, without glare or over-lighting), then to measuring performance, and finally to the controls that ensure efficient lights are only on when and where they are needed.
