The lamp, or light source, is where electricity becomes light, and the technology chosen sets the efficiency, lifespan, light quality and running cost of the whole installation. The story of lighting over the last two decades is the story of one technology, LED, overtaking everything else on almost every measure at once. This lesson walks the main types, the light-quality dimensions beyond raw efficacy, and why the remaining skill is specifying *good* LED rather than choosing a technology at all.

## Incandescent and halogen

The original electric light: a filament heated until it glows. Cheap to buy, excellent colour rendering, but desperately inefficient, since around 90% of the energy becomes heat rather than light. Halogen is a marginally better variant. Both are now largely phased out for general lighting under efficiency regulations.

- Efficacy: 10 to 25 lm/W
- Life: 1,000 to 2,000 hours

## Fluorescent

An electric discharge through mercury vapour produces ultraviolet, which a phosphor coating converts to visible light. For decades the commercial workhorse, in T8 and the slimmer, more efficient T5 tubes.

- Efficacy: 50 to 100 lm/W
- Life: 10,000 to 20,000 hours
- Drawbacks: contains mercury, needs a [ballast](/courses/lighting/ballasts-drivers), output dips in the cold, and has a finite number of switching cycles

## LED

A light-emitting diode converts electricity directly to light in a semiconductor, now the default for virtually all new and retrofit lighting.

- Efficacy: 100 to 180 lm/W
- Life: 30,000 to 50,000+ hours
- Strengths: instant start, dimmable, directional, robust, no mercury, excellent at low temperatures
- Considerations: quality varies widely, and the [driver](/courses/lighting/ballasts-drivers) and thermal design determine real-world life

## Light quality dimensions

Choosing a lamp is not just efficacy and life; three quality dimensions decide whether a space works:

- **Colour temperature (kelvin)**: warm white (2,700 to 3,000 K) for hospitality and homes; neutral (4,000 K) for offices; cool (5,000 to 6,500 K) for industrial and task areas.
- **Colour rendering index (CRI)**: how faithfully colours appear under the light, from 0 to 100. Offices want CRI above 80; retail, galleries and clinical settings 90+.
- **Glare and distribution**: how the light is delivered affects comfort as much as how much of it there is, the subject of the [design lesson](/courses/lighting/design-integration).

<Callout variant="key" title="The technology choice is settled; the specification choice is not">
LED dominates because it leads on nearly every axis at once: efficacy, life, controllability, switching and, increasingly, colour quality. So the real skill is no longer choosing a technology but specifying a *good* LED product and integrating it with [controls](/courses/lighting/occupancy-sensors), because cheap LED can disappoint on colour, flicker, glare and lifespan in ways that are easy to miss at purchase. "We fitted LED" is not the end of the decision; which LED, with what CRI, colour temperature, flicker performance and driver quality, is where the value is won or lost.
</Callout>

Between the lamp and the mains sits the control gear, a ballast or driver, whose quality matters more than most people realise, and that is the next lesson.
