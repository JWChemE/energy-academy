Lighting accounts for 15-40% of electricity use in many commercial buildings. Managing it well starts with understanding how light is measured and how efficiently it is produced.

## The key quantities

- **Lumen (lm):** the total amount of visible light a source emits.
- **Lux (lx):** illuminance - lumens landing on each square metre of a surface (1 lux = 1 lm/m2). This is what matters for seeing a task.
- **Luminous efficacy (lm/W):** how much light you get per watt of electricity. The efficiency metric for lighting.

A lamp might emit 4,000 lumens (its output) while delivering 500 lux on a desk (the useful illuminance there), drawing 40 watts (giving 100 lm/W efficacy).

## Recommended light levels

Standards (such as EN 12464-1) set task-based illuminance targets:
- Circulation, corridors: 100-150 lux
- Warehouse storage: 150-200 lux
- General office, reading: 300-500 lux
- Detailed work, drawing offices: 750-1,000 lux
- Fine inspection, surgeries: 1,000+ lux

Lighting to the right level is itself an efficiency measure: over-lighting a space to 700 lux when 500 is specified wastes around 40% of that lighting energy.

## Efficacy across technologies

The efficacy gap between technologies is enormous:
- Incandescent: 10-15 lm/W
- Halogen: 15-25 lm/W
- Compact fluorescent: 50-70 lm/W
- Linear fluorescent (T5): 90-100 lm/W
- LED: 100-180 lm/W and still improving

Replacing a 15 lm/W incandescent with a 130 lm/W LED delivers the same light for under a fifth of the energy - the basis of the LED retrofit case.

## Quality matters too

Efficacy is not the whole story. Good lighting also needs the right colour, good colour rendering, controlled glare, and even distribution. The next lesson covers lamp types and these quality dimensions, which determine whether a space is both efficient and pleasant to work in.
