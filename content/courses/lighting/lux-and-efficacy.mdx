Lighting accounts for 15 to 40% of electricity use in many commercial buildings, and it is one of the easiest loads to cut, because the technology has improved so dramatically. Managing it well starts with understanding how light is measured and how efficiently it is produced, since the whole business case rests on getting the right amount of light to the task for the least electricity. This lesson covers the three quantities that matter, the light levels tasks actually need, and the efficacy gap that makes the LED case so strong.

## The key quantities

- **Lumen (lm)**: the total amount of visible light a source emits.
- **Lux (lx)**: illuminance, the lumens landing on each square metre of a surface (1 lux = 1 lm/m²). This is what matters for seeing a task.
- **Luminous efficacy (lm/W)**: how much light you get per watt of electricity, the efficiency metric for lighting.

A lamp might emit 4,000 lumens (its output) while delivering 500 lux on a desk (the useful illuminance there), drawing 40 watts (giving 4,000 ÷ 40 = 100 lm/W efficacy). Keep the three distinct: output is what the lamp produces, illuminance is what reaches the task, and efficacy is how efficiently the electricity became light.

## Recommended light levels

Standards such as **BS EN 12464-1** set task-based illuminance targets, and lighting to the right level is itself an efficiency measure:

- Circulation, corridors: 100 to 150 lux
- Warehouse storage: 150 to 200 lux
- General office, reading: 300 to 500 lux
- Detailed work, drawing offices: 750 to 1,000 lux
- Fine inspection, surgeries: 1,000+ lux

Over-lighting a space to 700 lux when 500 is specified wastes around 40% of that lighting energy, for no benefit and often for a less comfortable, over-bright room. Designing and checking to the task level, not above it, is a saving in its own right, taken up in the [design lesson](/courses/lighting/design-integration).

## Efficacy across technologies

The efficacy gap between technologies is enormous, which is the entire basis of the [LED retrofit case](/courses/lighting/led-retrofit):

- Incandescent: 10 to 15 lm/W
- Halogen: 15 to 25 lm/W
- Compact fluorescent: 50 to 70 lm/W
- Linear fluorescent (T5): 90 to 100 lm/W
- LED: 100 to 180 lm/W and still improving

Replacing a 15 lm/W incandescent with a 130 lm/W LED delivers the same light for under a fifth of the energy. That factor-of-five (or more) is why lighting so often tops an [audit's ranked opportunities](/courses/energy-audits/identify-opportunities).

<Callout variant="key" title="Right light, right place, right level, efficiently produced">
Efficient lighting is not just about efficient lamps; it is the product of two things. First, delivering the *right illuminance* for the task, since over-lighting to 700 lux where 500 is needed throws away 40% regardless of how efficient the lamp is. Second, producing that light *efficiently*, where the five-fold efficacy gap between old and modern sources lives. A good lighting project attacks both: correct the levels and upgrade the technology, and the savings multiply.
</Callout>

Efficacy is not the whole story, though. Good lighting also needs the right colour, good colour rendering, controlled glare and even distribution, which the next lesson on lamp types covers, since those determine whether a space is both efficient and pleasant to work in.
