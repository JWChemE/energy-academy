Some of the simplest lighting savings come from a clock: ensuring lights are on only during the hours a space is actually used. Time scheduling, especially integrated with the building management system, catches the lights that occupancy and daylight controls miss.

## The wasteful default

Without scheduling, lights tend to follow human memory - which fails. Cleaners switch everything on at 5am and it stays on; a display stays lit all weekend; external lighting burns through bright mornings. Scheduling replaces unreliable memory with a reliable timetable.

## Basic time control

A **time switch or astronomical timer** turns circuits on and off to a schedule. Astronomical timers track sunrise and sunset by date and location - ideal for external and security lighting, which should follow daylight rather than a fixed clock through the changing seasons.

## BMS integration

Connecting lighting to the **building management system** unlocks far more:
- **Occupancy-based schedules** aligned with the building's working hours, with separate weekday, weekend, and holiday patterns
- **Zone-by-zone control** so unoccupied wings stay dark while occupied areas are lit
- **Override with timeout:** an occupant working late presses a button for an extra hour or two, after which the lights revert to off - preventing all-night burning
- **Integration with security and HVAC** so the whole building powers down together out of hours

## Cleaning and out-of-hours strategy

A common waste is whole floors lit for a single cleaner. Better strategies:
- Schedule cleaning during occupied hours where possible
- Use occupancy sensing so only the cleaner's current area is lit
- Provide "clean lighting" - a reduced set of luminaires - rather than full illumination

## Layering the controls

Time scheduling is the outer layer of a good control hierarchy:
1. **Schedule** sets when lighting is permitted at all
2. **Occupancy sensing** switches within those hours based on presence
3. **Daylight harvesting** dims based on available daylight
4. **Manual override** lets occupants adapt, with automatic timeout

Each layer catches waste the others miss. Together they ensure lights are on only where, when, and as brightly as genuinely needed - the foundation of an efficient lighting installation.
