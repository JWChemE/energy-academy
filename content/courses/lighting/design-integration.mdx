Energy-efficient lighting is not just about efficient lamps. Once the [LED retrofit](/courses/lighting/led-retrofit) has settled the technology, the design decides whether the installation is genuinely efficient and pleasant to be in: the right light, in the right place, for the task, without glare or over-lighting. Good lighting design saves energy and improves the space at the same time, and the two goals are usually the same goal. This lesson covers designing for the task, avoiding over-illumination, controlling glare, and using surfaces and daylight.

## Light for the task, not the room

The biggest design saving is matching illuminance to need. Lighting an entire warehouse to the level required at a single inspection bench wastes enormous energy, because the whole floor is lit to the standard only one point needs. Two approaches fix it:

- **Task-ambient lighting**: a modest general (ambient) level across the space, with brighter local task lighting only where detailed work happens. The desk gets 500 lux; the circulation around it gets 200.
- **Zoning by use**: different areas lit to their own [task requirements](/courses/lighting/lux-and-efficacy) rather than a blanket level everywhere.

## Avoiding over-illumination

Over-lighting is common and costly: every excess lux is wasted energy, and over-bright spaces can be uncomfortable as well. Design to the standard for the task, not above it, and verify on site with a [lux meter](/courses/lighting/measurements) after installation. Allow for the **maintenance factor** (light output falls as fittings age and collect dirt) by designing to the *maintained* level rather than over-specifying "to be safe", which just bakes in permanent over-lighting to compensate for a decline that good cleaning would prevent.

## Glare and visual comfort

Efficient lighting that causes glare is bad lighting. The **Unified Glare Rating (UGR)** quantifies discomfort glare, and offices target UGR below 19. Achieve it with recessed or louvred fittings that hide the bright source from normal view, diffusers on LED fittings to spread the emission (bare LEDs are point sources and glare badly), indirect lighting bounced off ceilings for soft even illumination, and careful placement relative to screens to avoid reflections.

## Surfaces and daylight

Light-coloured walls, ceilings and floors reflect light around a space, so less is needed; a room with dark surfaces can need substantially more lighting power for the same perceived brightness. Even, well-distributed light avoids the gloomy patches that tempt people to add more fittings. And the best-lit spaces use electric light to *supplement* [daylight](/courses/lighting/daylight-harvesting), not replace it: designing for good daylight, and zoning the electric lighting so perimeter rows can dim or switch off, means the installed lighting works with the building rather than fighting it.

<Callout variant="key" title="Deliver exactly what people need to see, no more">
Good lighting design is a double win: it uses less energy *and* produces a more comfortable, productive environment, because the same discipline that removes over-lighting and glare also removes waste. The method is to think about what people actually need to see and feel, then deliver exactly that, the right level for the task, low glare, even distribution, good surfaces, working with daylight, using efficient sources and controls. Over-lighting is not "generous"; it is wasted energy that often makes a space less pleasant, not more. Design to the task, and verify it on site.
</Callout>
