Energy-efficient lighting is not just about efficient lamps - it is about delivering the right light, in the right place, for the task, comfortably. Good design saves energy and improves the space at the same time.

## Light for the task, not the room

The biggest design saving is matching illuminance to need. Lighting an entire warehouse to the level required at a single inspection bench wastes enormous energy. Better approaches:

- **Task-ambient lighting:** a modest general (ambient) level across the space, with brighter local task lighting only where detailed work happens. The desk gets 500 lux; the circulation around it gets 200.
- **Zoning by use:** different areas lit to their own requirements rather than a blanket level everywhere.

## Avoiding over-illumination

Over-lighting is common and costly - every excess lux is wasted energy, and over-bright spaces can be uncomfortable. Design to the standard for the task, not above it, and verify on site with a lux meter after installation. Allow for the **maintenance factor** (light output falls as fittings age and dirty) by designing to the maintained level, not over-specifying "to be safe."

## Glare and visual comfort

Efficient lighting that causes glare is bad lighting. The **Unified Glare Rating (UGR)** quantifies discomfort glare; offices target UGR below 19. Achieve it with:
- Recessed or louvred fittings that hide the bright source from normal view
- Diffusers on LED fittings to spread the emission
- Indirect lighting bouncing off ceilings for soft, even illumination
- Careful placement relative to screens to avoid reflections

## Surfaces and distribution

Light-coloured walls, ceilings, and floors reflect light around a space, so less is needed. A room with dark surfaces can need substantially more lighting power for the same perceived brightness. Even, well-distributed light avoids the gloomy patches that tempt people to add more fittings.

## Daylight integration

The best lit spaces use electric light to supplement daylight, not replace it. Designing for good daylight - and zoning electric lighting so perimeter rows can dim or switch off - means the installed lighting works with the building rather than fighting it.

## Comfort and energy together

Done well, efficient lighting design is a double win: it uses less energy *and* produces a more comfortable, productive environment. The discipline is to think about what people need to see and feel, then deliver exactly that - no more, no less - with efficient sources and good controls.
