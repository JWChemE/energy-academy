You've learned how light is measured, why LED wins, and how the controls layers (scheduling, occupancy and daylight) stack up. This capstone walks you round a building as the lighting auditor, meter in hand, to find the waste and cost the fixes.

Lighting is 15–40% of electricity in many commercial buildings, and almost all of the waste falls into a simple hierarchy: the wrong **level** of light, the wrong **source**, or light on at the wrong **time** or in the wrong **place**. Some of these you'll crack with a quick sum; others you reason out from what you see and measure.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate**: read the brief and the panel: lux readings, fitting counts and wattages, hours, control type.
2. **Calculate**: work out the figure that decides it: how far over target a space is lit, a retrofit's saving and payback, out-of-hours burning, the value of occupancy or daylight control, or ballast losses. The **reference data is on hand**: efficacy, lux targets, control savings, prices, but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose**: pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe**: build an ordered work plan (no-cost first, then capital).
5. **Verify**: score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="Level, source, time, place">
Before you reach for a retrofit, ask the four questions in order. Is the space lit to the right **level** (not 50% over target)? Is it the most efficient **source**? Is it on only at the right **time** (schedule), only when someone's there (occupancy), and only as bright as the daylight requires (harvesting)? The cheapest light is the one switched off, so the controls layers usually beat new lamps for payback, and a recommissioning screwdriver can beat them both.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **Right level**: lux against task targets, and why being 50% over only needs a 33% cut.
- **Right source**: the LED retrofit case, where high wattage × long hours gives a sub-2-year payback.
- **Right time**: out-of-hours burning and the BMS schedule that stops it.
- **Right place**: occupancy sensing in intermittently used spaces, and zoned daylight dimming on the perimeter.
- **Quality, not just quantity**: magnetic ballast losses, and the cheap-LED trap where the driver fails long before the diode.
- **Verify the controls**: logging a circuit to expose controls that were fitted but never commissioned.

## Differential thinking

A high lighting bill could be over-lighting, an inefficient source, lights left on, or controls that don't work, and the fix for each is completely different. The decisive evidence is what you measure: the lux reading against target, the wattage on the clamp meter versus the lamp rating, the logged hours the lights are actually on. A space that's "too bright and expensive" might need dimming, not a retrofit; controls that "don't seem to save anything" might just need retuning, not replacing.

## After you finish

- Which **measurement** decided each case: the lux reading, the fitting wattage, the logged hours?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, is it a no-cost change (dim to target, recommission), a control upgrade (schedule, occupancy, daylight), or a capital retrofit that has to earn its payback?
