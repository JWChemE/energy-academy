The [streams lesson](/courses/pinch-analysis/streams-and-approach-temperature) told you ΔTmin is a design choice, not a fixed constant, and asked you to take it on faith for a while. This lesson shows exactly why it's a choice — by running the same process through the problem table algorithm at three different ΔTmin values and pricing the result.

## The same process, three approach temperatures

Take the four-stream process from earlier in this course (H1 180→40 °C CP 3.0; H2 150→60 °C CP 1.5; C1 50→190 °C CP 2.0; C2 30→170 °C CP 4.0) and re-run the [problem table algorithm](/courses/pinch-analysis/problem-table-algorithm) at three different values of ΔTmin:

| ΔTmin | Minimum hot utility | Minimum cold utility |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 10 °C | 285 kW | 0 kW |
| 20 °C | 315 kW | 30 kW |
| 30 °C | 355 kW | 70 kW |

Every column comes from exactly the same method as before — only the ΔTmin fed into the temperature-shifting step has changed. The pattern is unmistakable: **tightening ΔTmin always reduces both utilities** (more heat can be recovered between process streams instead of bought in), and **loosening it always increases both** (less recovery, more utility). At ΔTmin = 10 °C here, the process is even self-sufficient in cold utility — needing none at all.

## Why tighter isn't automatically better

If minimising utility were the only goal, you'd always choose the smallest ΔTmin possible. But a smaller approach temperature means a smaller temperature difference driving heat transfer at every exchanger in the network, which means more heat-transfer area — bigger, more expensive exchangers — is needed to move the same amount of heat. Somewhere between "wastefully loose" and "unaffordably tight" sits an economic optimum.

## Pricing the trade-off

Using gas at £0.06/kWh for hot utility, a low-cost cooling source at £0.01/kWh for cold utility, and 8,000 operating hours a year:

| ΔTmin | Hot utility cost | Cold utility cost | Total utility cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 10 °C | 285 × 8,000 × £0.06 = £136,800 | £0 | £136,800/yr |
| 20 °C | 315 × 8,000 × £0.06 = £151,200 | 30 × 8,000 × £0.01 = £2,400 | £153,600/yr |
| 30 °C | 355 × 8,000 × £0.06 = £170,400 | 70 × 8,000 × £0.01 = £5,600 | £176,000/yr |

Now add a typical (illustrative) annualised capital cost for the heat-exchanger network at each ΔTmin — rising steeply as the approach temperature tightens, because area requirements grow sharply as the temperature driving force shrinks:

| ΔTmin | Annualised capital cost | Total annual cost (utility + capital) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 10 °C | £90,000/yr | £136,800 + £90,000 = **£226,800** |
| 20 °C | £50,000/yr | £153,600 + £50,000 = **£203,600** |
| 30 °C | £35,000/yr | £176,000 + £35,000 = **£211,000** |

<Callout variant="key" title="The classic U-shaped trade-off">
Here, 20 °C gives the lowest total annual cost — cheaper than both the tighter (10 °C) and looser (30 °C) alternatives. This is the shape you should expect: utility cost falls steadily as ΔTmin tightens, capital cost rises steadily, and the sum has a minimum somewhere in between. The exact optimum depends entirely on your site's utility prices and exchanger costs — there is no universal "correct" ΔTmin, only the one that minimises total cost for *your* numbers.
</Callout>

## The practical takeaway

Run this comparison with your own site's utility prices and a rough capital-cost estimate (even a simple "cost roughly doubles as ΔTmin halves" rule of thumb is enough for a first pass) before committing to a ΔTmin for a detailed design. The [Economic Analysis course](/courses/economic-analysis/npv-and-irr) covers the rigorous way to compare options like these over a project's life using NPV rather than a simple annual-cost snapshot — worth applying here if the numbers are close, exactly as you'd apply it to any other capital-versus-running-cost decision.
