Every example in this course so far has designed a network from a blank sheet of paper. Almost every real application of pinch analysis is the opposite: a plant that already exists, already has pipework and heat exchangers bolted down, and simply needs to use less energy than it currently does. This lesson is about that far more common situation.

## The retrofit method, step by step

The approach mirrors the [energy audit process](/courses/energy-audits/why-audit) you already know, with pinch analysis as the calculation engine at its centre:

1. **Build the stream table from the real plant.** Measure (or take from process data) the supply temperature, target temperature, and flow rate of every stream that's heated or cooled anywhere on site, exactly the [stream classification](/courses/pinch-analysis/streams-and-approach-temperature) from earlier in this course.
2. **Run the targeting calculation.** The [problem table algorithm](/courses/pinch-analysis/problem-table-algorithm) gives you the minimum hot and cold utility this process could ever need, at a sensible ΔTmin for the site.
3. **Compare the target to what the site actually uses.** This is the number that matters: if the site currently burns fuel for 800 kW of hot utility but the target says 500 kW is achievable, there's a 300 kW gap: a quantified, defensible opportunity, in exactly the same spirit as the [opportunity-ranking table](/courses/energy-audits/identify-opportunities) from the audits course.
4. **Look for existing golden-rule violations first.** Before proposing any new equipment, check whether the current network already has a cooler operating above the pinch, a heater operating below it, or an exchanger spanning across it, exactly the violations from [the golden rules lesson](/courses/pinch-analysis/the-golden-rules). These are often the cheapest fixes available: re-piping an existing connection, not buying a new exchanger.
5. **Propose new matches only where the gap remains.** Once existing violations are corrected, whatever utility gap is left points to genuinely new heat-recovery opportunities, new exchangers, sized and matched using the [network design principles](/courses/pinch-analysis/matching-at-the-pinch) from earlier in this course.

## Why retrofit is different from new design

A greenfield design can put any exchanger anywhere pipework allows. A retrofit inherits:

- **Existing equipment with sunk capital**: an exchanger installed five years ago for a different reason may already (by luck) be doing useful heat recovery, or may be sitting exactly where the golden rules say it shouldn't be.
- **Physical layout constraints**: the ideal match on paper might require running a pipe the length of the site, which the targeting calculation knows nothing about and a site visit will reveal immediately.
- **A phased investment path.** Rather than one large project, retrofit opportunities are usually delivered as several smaller ones (fix the cheapest golden-rule violation first, then add the next most attractive new match) exactly the [portfolio approach](/courses/economic-analysis/portfolio-approach) to sequencing projects.

<Callout variant="key" title="The target doesn't have to be hit all at once">
A pinch study on an existing site rarely leads straight to a single "install this network" recommendation. More often it produces a ranked list: correct this misplaced cooler (near-zero cost, quick payback), add this new exchanger between these two streams (moderate cost, good payback), consider this larger network reconfiguration only if a major turnaround or expansion is already planned (higher cost, justified only alongside other work). The target from step 2 tells you the ceiling; the ranked list tells you how to climb toward it economically.
</Callout>

## Where the value concentrates

In practice, the biggest retrofit wins usually cluster around exactly the two things this course has spent the most time on:

- **Golden-rule violations**: because fixing them can be nearly free (re-routing existing pipework) while the [cross-pinch penalty lesson](/courses/pinch-analysis/the-golden-rules) showed every misplaced kilowatt costs *two* kilowatts of utility.
- **The largest hot-utility or cold-utility users on site**, because a percentage improvement there is worth far more in absolute terms than the same percentage anywhere else: the same significant-energy-use logic behind the [maintenance prioritisation lesson](/courses/maintenance/maintenance-plan) elsewhere on this platform.

This closes the fundamentals of pinch analysis. The capstone that follows puts you in exactly this retrofit auditor's position: real stream data, an existing network with problems hiding in it, and the job of finding them.
