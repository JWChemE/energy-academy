A single stream, plotted as temperature against the heat it carries, is just a straight line. The power of pinch analysis comes from combining *all* the hot streams into one curve, and *all* the cold streams into another: the **composite curves**, so you can see the whole process's heating and cooling needs on one picture.

## The process to combine

Take a small process with four streams:

| Stream | Type | Supply (°C) | Target (°C) | CP (kW/°C) | Heat load (kW) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| H1 | Hot | 180 | 40 | 3.0 | 420 |
| H2 | Hot | 150 | 60 | 1.5 | 135 |
| C1 | Cold | 50 | 190 | 2.0 | 280 |
| C2 | Cold | 30 | 170 | 4.0 | 560 |

## Building the hot composite curve

List every temperature at which a hot stream starts or ends (180, 150, 60, 40), and work down from the highest. In each temperature band, add up the CP of every hot stream that's actually present in that band, and multiply by the band's width to get the heat released there:

| Temperature band | Streams present | Combined CP (kW/°C) | Heat in this band (kW) | Cumulative heat (kW) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 180 → 150 °C | H1 only | 3.0 | 3.0 × 30 = 90 | 90 |
| 150 → 60 °C | H1 + H2 | 4.5 | 4.5 × 90 = 405 | 495 |
| 60 → 40 °C | H1 only | 3.0 | 3.0 × 20 = 60 | 555 |

The **hot composite curve** is this cumulative-heat column plotted against temperature: it starts at (180 °C, 0 kW) and ends at (40 °C, 555 kW), matching the two streams' combined total load exactly. Notice the curve gets *steeper* in the middle band, where both hot streams are releasing heat at once (a bigger combined CP means more heat per degree).

## Building the cold composite curve

Exactly the same process, for the cold streams:

| Temperature band | Streams present | Combined CP (kW/°C) | Heat in this band (kW) | Cumulative heat (kW) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 190 → 170 °C | C1 only | 2.0 | 2.0 × 20 = 40 | 40 |
| 170 → 50 °C | C1 + C2 | 6.0 | 6.0 × 120 = 720 | 760 |
| 50 → 30 °C | C2 only | 4.0 | 4.0 × 20 = 80 | 840 |

The **cold composite curve** runs from (30 °C, 0 kW) up to (190 °C, 840 kW): the two cold streams' combined heating requirement.

<Callout variant="key" title="Two curves, one picture">
Plot both composite curves on the same axes, temperature up the side, cumulative heat along the bottom. The hot composite curve sits somewhere above the cold composite curve everywhere (heat can only flow from hot to cold), and the vertical gap between them at any point is the temperature difference available for heat exchange there. Where that gap is at its *narrowest*, you've found the pinch: the tightest constraint in the whole process.
</Callout>

## Reading the picture, qualitatively

Look at where the two curves' cumulative-heat ranges overlap: the cold composite needs heat from 0 up to 840 kW; the hot composite supplies heat from 0 up to 555 kW. Because the cold demand (840 kW) is bigger than the hot supply (555 kW), the curves can't be lined up so that heat exchange alone satisfies everything: some external heating (hot utility) and, depending on exactly where the curves sit relative to each other, possibly some external cooling (cold utility) too, will be unavoidable. Exactly how much of each, and exactly where the curves come closest together, is what the next lesson calculates precisely.

<Callout variant="tip">
You don't need to draw the composite curves with a ruler to get a correct pinch analysis: the numerical method in the next lesson gives you the exact same answer through arithmetic alone. But the picture is invaluable for building intuition and for presenting a result to someone who'd rather see a chart than a cascade table, much like the [Sankey diagram](/courses/mass-and-energy-balances/sankey-diagrams) you met earlier makes an energy balance easy to communicate at a glance.
</Callout>
