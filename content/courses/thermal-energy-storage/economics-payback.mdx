Thermal storage stores energy, not money, and it only pays when the value of shifting energy in time exceeds the cost of the store and its losses. Because storage saves nothing on a flat tariff and its benefits are spread over years, the appraisal needs care. This closing lesson sets out the core economic logic, what to include on both sides, a worked cooling example, the plant-downsizing bonus that often clinches it, and how tariffs make or break the case.

## The core economic logic

Storage has no energy saving in the simple sense: you put energy in and take slightly less out later. Its value comes entirely from **time arbitrage**, buying or making energy when it is cheap and using it when it would be expensive. The annual benefit is roughly (price when discharging − price when charging) × energy shifted per year − the cost of losses. A wide price spread between peak and off-peak is the single biggest driver of viability; on a flat tariff, storage saves nothing.

## What to include

A proper appraisal weighs benefits (energy cost saved by shifting from peak to off-peak, [demand-charge](/courses/motors-and-drives/demand-management) reduction from cutting peak load, avoided or downsized plant capital, efficiency gains such as [cooler night-time chiller operation](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/cooling-storage), and enabling cheap or renewable energy that would otherwise be wasted) against costs (capital for the store, controls, integration and space; round-trip losses; and maintenance and any auxiliary pumping).

<WorkedExample title="an ice store's payback">
  <Given>
    - An ice store shifts 500 kWh of cooling per day from a £0.25/kWh peak period to £0.08/kWh off-peak
    - A 260-day cooling season; store cost £60,000 to £90,000
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual arbitrage saving and rough payback.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Daily saving ≈ (0.25 − 0.08) × 500 = **£85**, or roughly **£22,000 a year** over a
    260-day season, before counting the demand-charge saving and the smaller chiller it
    allowed. Against a £60,000 to £90,000 store, that alone points to a payback of a few
    years, and it is strengthened considerably by the plant downsizing below. The result
    is entirely driven by the £0.17/kWh spread: halve the spread and the payback doubles,
    which is why tariff structure dominates every storage decision.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## The plant-downsizing bonus, and tariffs

The avoided capital from a smaller chiller or heat pump often transforms the economics. Because storage covers the peak, the plant is sized to the average rather than the maximum load, and this capital saving can offset much of the store's cost immediately, turning a marginal energy-arbitrage case into a clear winner. But everything hinges on the tariff: a large peak/off-peak spread or high demand charges make storage attractive, flat or narrow tariffs make it struggle, and the increasingly common dynamic time-of-use tariffs both improve the case and reward smart control.

<Callout variant="key" title="Appraise over the life, and verify how it is actually operated">
Because storage is capital-heavy with benefits spread over years and utterly dependent on the tariff, judge it with [NPV and IRR](/courses/economic-analysis/npv-and-irr) over its life rather than simple payback alone, and run sensitivities on the price spread and utilisation. Then, crucially, verify the saving after installation with [measurement and verification](/courses/measurement-and-verification): unlike a boiler or a motor, a store's benefit depends entirely on *how it is operated*, charging at the right hours and discharging at the peak. A store controlled badly, charging at the wrong times or sitting half-used, delivers a fraction of its modelled value, so the control strategy is as much a part of the business case as the hardware.
</Callout>
