The humble water tank is the workhorse of thermal storage: cheap, safe, and effective, sensible water storage underpins most building heat and hot-water systems. Getting it right matters more than its simplicity suggests, because a poorly designed or badly connected tank can waste much of the value it should deliver. This lesson covers why water, the roles a tank plays, the stratification that determines its real performance, and losses and sizing.

## Why water

Water is an almost ideal sensible storage medium: high specific heat (4,180 J/kgK, so it stores a lot per degree), cheap and universally available, safe and non-toxic, easy to pump, and it works across the whole building temperature range. Using the [sensible-storage equation](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/sensible-latent), a 1,000 litre tank holding water across a 50 °C band stores around **58 kWh**, enough to ride a building through demand peaks or an off-peak charging window.

## Buffer tanks and their roles

- **Hot-water cylinders** store domestic hot water, decoupling the spiky draw-off from steady heating.
- **Buffer vessels** on heating systems prevent boilers and heat pumps from short-cycling, store off-peak heat, and hydraulically separate circuits.
- **Thermal stores** combine these roles, often charged by multiple sources (boiler, [heat pump](/courses/renewable-energy/heat-pump-renewable), solar) and supplying both heating and hot water.

For heat pumps especially, a buffer tank is often essential: it provides volume for stable operation, defrost cycles, and time-of-use charging.

## Stratification: the key to performance

The single most important design feature is **thermal stratification**, keeping hot water at the top and cold at the bottom in distinct layers rather than mixed. It matters because you draw the hottest water from the top at usable temperature, while the heat source sees the coldest water at the bottom, working efficiently, which is vital for condensing boilers and heat pumps that need low return temperatures. A stratified store effectively holds more usable energy than a mixed one of the same volume. Stratification is preserved by tall, slim tank geometry, low-velocity diffuser inlets that do not stir the water, and connecting flows at the right heights; poor pipework that mixes the layers wastes much of the store's value.

<Callout variant="key" title="A mixed tank throws away half its value; keep the layers separate">
Stratification is where good and bad thermal stores diverge. A well-stratified tank delivers hot water at full temperature from the top while feeding cold water to the heat source at the bottom, which is exactly what a [heat pump or condensing boiler](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/heating-modes) needs to run efficiently. A tank whose layers are stirred by high-velocity inlets or badly placed connections delivers lukewarm water and hands warm water back to the source, crippling its efficiency. Tall geometry, gentle diffuser inlets, and connections at the right heights preserve stratification; get them wrong and an expensive store performs like a small one.
</Callout>

## Losses and sizing

A store only helps if it keeps its heat. Standing losses from a poorly insulated tank are continuous, day and night, so good factory-insulated tanks, well-lagged connections, and avoiding thermosyphon losses through pipes (using loops or valves) keep the stored energy where it belongs, a direct link to the [insulation course](/courses/insulation-systems/vessel-lagging). Sizing then balances the energy you need to store against available space and structural floor loading (water is heavy) and standing losses (a bigger tank loses more in absolute terms). Size to the genuine storage need: oversizing wastes space and raises standing losses, while undersizing fails to capture the off-peak or solar energy available.
