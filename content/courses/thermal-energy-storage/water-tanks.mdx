The humble water tank is the workhorse of thermal storage. Cheap, safe, and effective, sensible water storage underpins most building heat and hot-water systems - and getting it right matters more than its simplicity suggests.

## Why water

Water is an almost ideal sensible storage medium:
- High specific heat (4,180 J/kgK) - stores a lot per degree
- Cheap and universally available
- Safe, non-toxic, easy to pump
- Works across the building temperature range

A 1,000 litre tank holding water across a 50 C band stores around 58 kWh - enough to ride a building through demand peaks or off-peak charging windows.

## Buffer tanks and their roles

- **Hot-water cylinders** store domestic hot water, decoupling draw-off from heating.
- **Buffer vessels** on heating systems prevent boilers and heat pumps from short-cycling, store off-peak heat, and hydraulically separate circuits.
- **Thermal stores** combine these roles, often charged by multiple sources (boiler, heat pump, solar) and supplying heating and hot water.

For heat pumps especially, a buffer tank is often essential - it provides volume for stable operation, defrost cycles, and time-of-use charging.

## Stratification: the key to performance

The single most important design feature is **thermal stratification** - keeping hot water at the top and cold at the bottom, in distinct layers rather than mixed. Stratification matters because:
- You draw the hottest water from the top, at usable temperature
- The heat source sees the coldest water at the bottom, working efficiently (vital for condensing boilers and heat pumps, which need low return temperatures)
- A stratified store effectively holds more usable energy than a mixed one

Stratification is preserved by tall, slim tank geometry, low-velocity diffuser inlets that do not stir the water, and connecting flows at the right heights. Poor pipework that mixes the layers wastes much of the store's value.

## Insulation and losses

A store only helps if it keeps its heat. Standing losses from a poorly insulated tank can be significant - continuous loss, day and night. Good factory-insulated tanks, well-lagged connections, and avoiding thermosyphon losses through pipes (using loops or valves) keep the stored energy where it belongs - a direct link to the insulation course.

## Sizing

Tank size balances:
- The energy you need to store (off-peak window, peak demand, solar capture)
- Available space and structural floor loading (water is heavy)
- Standing losses (a bigger tank loses more in absolute terms)

Size to the genuine storage need - oversizing wastes space and increases standing losses, while undersizing fails to capture the off-peak or solar energy available.
