The most common thermal storage in any building is for domestic hot water and space heating. Done well, these stores cut energy bills, enable low-carbon heat, and keep plant running efficiently, and they are increasingly essential rather than optional as buildings move to heat pumps. This lesson covers hot-water storage, buffering space heating, the heat-pump partnership, solar thermal integration, and off-peak electric heating.

## Domestic hot water storage

Hot-water demand is spiky (showers in the morning, washing through the day), while it is cheaper and more efficient to heat water steadily. A **hot-water cylinder** bridges the two: it heats water during efficient periods (off-peak, when the heat pump runs well, or when solar is available) and delivers it on demand at the tap, however spiky the draw-off. For heat pumps and [solar thermal](/courses/renewable-energy/solar-thermal), storage is essential, because both produce heat gently over time and need a store to meet instantaneous demand.

## Buffering space heating

A **buffer tank** on the heating circuit serves several purposes at once: it prevents short-cycling (stopping boilers, and especially heat pumps, from switching on and off rapidly, which wastes energy and wears the plant), enables off-peak charging (storing heat made on a cheap tariff for use during peak hours), handles defrost (giving a heat pump a reservoir to draw on during defrost cycles), and provides hydraulic separation (decoupling the heat source flow from the building's variable demand).

## The heat-pump partnership

Storage and heat pumps are natural partners. A heat pump runs most efficiently when it operates steadily at a low flow temperature into a [well-stratified store](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/water-tanks), charging during warmer parts of the day or cheaper tariff periods, then supplying heating and hot water from the store. The store lets a smaller heat pump meet a larger peak, and shifts its running to the cheapest, most efficient times, improving both cost and seasonal performance ([SCOP](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/cop-and-performance)). This is why almost every well-designed heat-pump installation now includes a buffer, and why the two technologies recur together throughout the curriculum.

## Solar thermal and off-peak electric

[Solar thermal](/courses/renewable-energy/solar-thermal) collectors produce heat when the sun shines, rarely matching demand, so a store, often a twin-coil cylinder with solar charging the lower coil, banks the solar heat for later use and maximises the useful solar contribution. Traditional **night-storage heaters** are sensible storage in brick or ceramic, charged on cheap overnight electricity and releasing heat through the day; modern high-heat-retention versions with better controls and insulation continue the principle and pair naturally with [time-of-use tariffs](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/load-shifting).

<Callout variant="key" title="The same four rules govern every heating and hot-water store">
Effective heating and hot-water storage always comes down to the same four things, whatever the technology charging it: good [stratification](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/water-tanks) (hot at top, cold at bottom, so efficient sources see cold return water), thorough [insulation](/courses/insulation-systems/vessel-lagging) to minimise standing losses, correct sizing to the genuine storage need, and controls that charge at the cheapest and cleanest times and discharge when demand peaks. Get these right and a simple tank becomes a genuine energy and cost saver that also unlocks heat pumps and solar; get them wrong and the same tank quietly wastes what it was meant to save.
</Callout>

Heating storage is the everyday case; cooling storage, next, is often where the economics are strongest.
