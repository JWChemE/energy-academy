The most common thermal storage in any building is for domestic hot water and space heating. Done well, these stores cut energy bills, enable low-carbon heat, and keep plant running efficiently.

## Domestic hot water storage

Hot water demand is spiky - showers in the morning, washing through the day - while it is cheaper and more efficient to heat water steadily. A **hot-water cylinder** bridges this:
- Heats water during efficient periods (off-peak, when the heat pump runs well, when solar is available)
- Delivers it on demand at the tap, however spiky the draw-off

For heat pumps and solar thermal, storage is essential: both produce heat gently over time and need a store to meet instantaneous demand.

## Buffering space heating

A **buffer tank** on the heating circuit serves several purposes:
- **Prevents short-cycling:** stops boilers and especially heat pumps switching on and off rapidly, which wastes energy and wears the plant
- **Enables off-peak charging:** stores heat made on cheap tariff for use during peak hours
- **Handles defrost:** gives a heat pump a heat reservoir to draw on during defrost cycles
- **Hydraulic separation:** decouples the heat source flow from the building's variable demand

## The heat-pump connection

Storage and heat pumps are natural partners. A heat pump runs most efficiently when it operates steadily at a low flow temperature into a well-stratified store, charging during warmer parts of the day or cheaper tariff periods, then supplying heating and hot water from the store. The store lets a smaller heat pump meet a larger peak, and shifts its running to the cheapest, most efficient times - improving both cost and seasonal performance (SCOP).

## Solar thermal integration

Solar thermal collectors produce heat when the sun shines, rarely matching demand. A store - often a twin-coil cylinder with solar charging the lower coil - banks the solar heat for later use, maximising the useful solar contribution. Without storage, much solar heat would arrive when there is no demand and be wasted.

## Off-peak electric heating

Traditional **night-storage heaters** are sensible storage in brick or ceramic, charged on cheap overnight electricity and releasing heat through the day. Modern high-heat-retention versions with better controls and insulation continue this principle, and pair naturally with time-of-use tariffs.

## Getting the most from it

The keys to effective heating and hot-water storage are the same throughout this course: good stratification (hot at top, cold at bottom for efficient sources), thorough insulation to minimise standing losses, correct sizing to the genuine storage need, and controls that charge at the cheapest, most efficient times and discharge when demand peaks. These turn a simple tank into a genuine energy and cost saver.
