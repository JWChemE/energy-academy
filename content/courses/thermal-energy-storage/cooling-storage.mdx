Cooling is where thermal storage often delivers its strongest economic case. By making and storing coolth when conditions are favourable, buildings cut both their energy cost and the size of their cooling plant, and the plant-downsizing saving alone can justify the store. This lesson covers why cooling storage pays, ice and chilled-water storage, the chiller-downsizing bonus, the efficiency gain from night operation, and where it fits best.

## Why cooling storage pays

Cooling demand and electricity prices often peak together: the hot afternoon drives both air-conditioning load and grid prices to their daily maximum. Storage breaks this expensive coincidence. **Make coolth off-peak** (run the chiller overnight on cheap electricity, in cooler night air so it runs more efficiently, and store the output) and **discharge on-peak** (meet the afternoon cooling demand from the store, avoiding expensive peak electricity and high demand charges). Both the energy price and the [demand charge](/courses/motors-and-drives/demand-management) are avoided, which is why cooling storage often beats heating storage on economics.

## Ice and chilled-water storage

The dominant technology is [ice storage](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/phase-change), exploiting the large latent heat of melting ice (334 kJ/kg): the chiller freezes water in a tank overnight, and during the day the ice melts to chill water circulating to the cooling system. A compact ice tank stores the equivalent of a far larger chilled-water tank. Where space allows, **chilled-water storage** is simpler, a large stratified tank of cold water charged overnight and drawn down by day, but it needs much more volume for the same stored cooling, the sensible-versus-latent trade-off again.

## Downsizing the chiller

A powerful benefit is **plant downsizing**. Without storage, the chiller must be big enough to meet the instantaneous peak cooling load. With storage, a smaller chiller runs for longer hours (including overnight) to build the store, and the store covers the peak. A smaller chiller means lower capital cost, lower connected electrical load, and often a smaller supply connection, and sometimes the storage system largely pays for itself through this saving alone.

<WorkedExample title="the night-operation efficiency bonus">
  <Given>
    - Making coolth at night rather than during the hot afternoon
  </Given>
  <Find>Why the same cooling costs less energy, not just cheaper energy.</Find>
  <Solution>
    A chiller rejects its heat to the outside air, so its efficiency depends on how hot
    that air is. At night the ambient temperature is lower, the [condensing temperature
    falls](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/water-cooled-systems), and the chiller's
    COP rises, so it uses *less electricity* to make the same quantity of coolth. Night
    charging therefore delivers a double benefit: it shifts the load to cheap off-peak
    hours *and* produces the cooling more efficiently. The energy saving from cooler
    operation stacks on top of the tariff saving from load shifting, which is why cooling
    storage economics are frequently so strong.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

<Callout variant="key" title="Cooling storage is often the most compelling storage business case">
Cooling storage combines three savings that rarely align so well elsewhere: avoided peak-tariff electricity, avoided or reduced demand charges, and a smaller (cheaper) chiller, on top of the efficiency gain from night-time operation. It suits buildings with sharp afternoon cooling peaks (offices, retail), sites on time-of-use tariffs or with high demand charges, schedulable process cooling, and new plant rooms where chiller downsizing can be designed in from the start. Where those conditions hold, the [economics](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/economics-payback) often make cooling storage one of the clearer wins in the whole storage field.
</Callout>
