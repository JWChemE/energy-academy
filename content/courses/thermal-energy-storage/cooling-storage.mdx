Cooling is where thermal storage often delivers its strongest economic case. By making and storing coolth when conditions are favourable, buildings cut both their energy cost and the size of their cooling plant.

## Why cooling storage pays

Cooling demand and electricity prices often peak together - the hot afternoon drives both air-conditioning load and grid prices to their daily maximum. Storage breaks this expensive coincidence:
- **Make coolth off-peak** (overnight): run the chiller on cheap electricity, in cooler night air (so it runs more efficiently), and store the output
- **Discharge on-peak** (afternoon): meet the cooling demand from the store, avoiding expensive peak electricity and high demand charges

## Ice storage

The dominant technology is **ice storage**, exploiting the large latent heat of melting ice (334 kJ/kg):
- The chiller freezes water in a tank overnight
- During the day, the ice melts to chill water circulating to the cooling system
- A compact ice tank stores the equivalent of a far larger chilled-water tank

## Chilled-water storage

Where space allows, **chilled-water storage** is simpler: a large, stratified tank of cold water charged overnight and drawn down by day. It avoids the complexity of ice-making but needs much more volume for the same stored cooling - the sensible-versus-latent trade-off again.

## Downsizing the chiller

A powerful benefit is **plant downsizing**. Without storage, the chiller must be big enough to meet the instantaneous peak cooling load. With storage, a smaller chiller runs for longer hours (including overnight) to build the store, then the store covers the peak. A smaller chiller means lower capital cost, lower connected electrical load, and often a smaller supply connection - sometimes the storage system largely pays for itself through this saving alone.

## Efficiency gains

Making coolth at night is more efficient than by day: cooler ambient temperatures mean the chiller rejects heat more easily, improving its COP. So night-time charging not only shifts the load to cheap hours but also uses less energy to produce the same cooling - a double benefit.

## Where it fits best

Cooling storage suits:
- Buildings with sharp afternoon cooling peaks (offices, retail)
- Sites on time-of-use tariffs or with high demand charges
- Process cooling with predictable, schedulable loads
- New plant rooms where chiller downsizing can be designed in

Combined with the efficiency of night-time operation and the avoided peak charges, cooling storage is frequently one of the more compelling storage business cases - quantified in the economics lesson next.
