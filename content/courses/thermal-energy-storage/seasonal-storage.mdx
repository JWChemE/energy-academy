Most thermal storage shifts energy by hours; seasonal (interseasonal) storage shifts it by months, capturing abundant summer heat for use in winter. It is technically demanding and slow to repay, but it is central to some of the most ambitious low-carbon heat schemes, because it solves a mismatch no daily store can touch. This lesson covers the seasonal mismatch, the scale-and-loss physics that make it inherently large, the main technologies, and where it makes sense.

## The seasonal mismatch

Solar energy is plentiful in summer when heat demand is lowest, and scarce in winter when demand peaks. Seasonal storage bridges this gap: collect and store heat through the summer, then draw it down through the heating season (the same logic can store winter cold for summer cooling). It is the storage answer to the fundamental problem that renewable heat and heat demand are half a year out of phase.

## Why it is inherently large-scale

Storing heat for months runs into two hard problems, and the second dictates the scale:

- **Volume**: the quantity of energy is enormous, demanding very large stores, thousands of cubic metres of water or large volumes of ground.
- **Heat loss**: over months, even well-insulated stores lose heat. The key physics is that **larger stores lose proportionally less**, because volume (which holds the energy) grows faster than surface area (through which heat leaks). A store ten times bigger has only about five times the surface area per unit of energy.

That geometry means seasonal storage only works at large scale: it is inherently a community or district technology, not a single-building one. A house-sized seasonal store would lose most of its heat before winter; a district-sized one keeps it.

## The main technologies

- **Tank / pit thermal energy storage (TTES/PTES)**: giant insulated water tanks or lined pits, sometimes covered and built into the ground, used in Danish district-heating schemes storing summer solar for winter.
- **Borehole thermal energy storage (BTES)**: an array of [boreholes](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/ground-storage) turns a block of ground into a heat store, charged in summer and discharged in winter via heat pumps.
- **Aquifer thermal energy storage (ATES)**: uses natural groundwater in an aquifer, with warm and cold wells, to store and recover large quantities of heat and cold seasonally.

<Callout variant="key" title="A strategic, long-horizon investment, not a quick win">
Seasonal storage has high capital cost and turns its heat over only once a year, so simple paybacks are long, often 10 to 20+ years. The case rarely rests on straightforward energy arbitrage; it rests on long asset life, carbon savings, and the value of decarbonising heat where few other options exist. This is why seasonal storage appears in net-zero district schemes, campuses and new developments where a ground store can be built in from the start, rather than in routine building upgrades. Judge it as strategic low-carbon infrastructure with a decades-long horizon, using [lifetime appraisal](/courses/economic-analysis/npv-and-irr), not as a simple payback project, or it will always look uneconomic.
</Callout>
