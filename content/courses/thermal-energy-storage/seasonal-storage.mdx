Most thermal storage shifts energy by hours. Seasonal (interseasonal) storage shifts it by months - capturing abundant summer heat for use in winter. It is technically demanding but central to some of the most ambitious low-carbon schemes.

## The seasonal mismatch

Solar energy is plentiful in summer when heat demand is lowest, and scarce in winter when demand peaks. Seasonal storage bridges this gap: collect and store heat through the summer, then draw it down through the heating season. The same logic can store winter cold for summer cooling.

## The fundamental challenge: scale and loss

Storing heat for months runs into two hard problems:

- **Volume:** the quantity of energy is enormous, demanding very large stores - thousands of cubic metres of water, or large volumes of ground.
- **Heat loss:** over months, even well-insulated stores lose heat. The key insight is that **larger stores lose proportionally less**, because volume (energy) grows faster than surface area (loss). Seasonal storage therefore only works at large scale - it is inherently a community or district technology, not a single-building one.

## The main technologies

- **Tank/pit thermal energy storage (TTES/PTES):** giant insulated water tanks or lined pits, sometimes covered and built into the ground. Used in Danish district-heating schemes storing summer solar for winter.
- **Borehole thermal energy storage (BTES):** an array of boreholes turns a block of ground into a heat store, charged in summer and discharged in winter via heat pumps.
- **Aquifer thermal energy storage (ATES):** uses natural groundwater in an aquifer, with warm and cold wells, to store and recover large quantities of heat and cold seasonally.

## Where it makes sense

Seasonal storage suits:
- District heating networks with a summer heat source (solar thermal, waste heat)
- Large institutions - campuses, hospitals - with both heating and cooling demands (ATES is very effective at balancing the two)
- New developments where ground stores can be built in from the start

## The economics

Capital cost is high and the heat turns over only once a year, so simple paybacks are long (often 10-20+ years). The case rests on long asset life, carbon savings, and the value of decarbonising heat where few other options exist. Seasonal storage is rarely a quick financial win - it is a strategic, long-horizon investment in low-carbon heat, which is why it features in net-zero district schemes rather than routine building upgrades.
