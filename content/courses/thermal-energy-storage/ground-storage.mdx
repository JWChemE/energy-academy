The ground itself can be a vast thermal store. Ground and aquifer storage systems use the earth's stable temperature and large volume to store heat and coolth, often seasonally, at scales no tank could match.

## Why the ground

A few metres down, ground temperature is stable year-round (around 10-14 C in the UK) and the volume available is effectively unlimited. This makes the ground ideal for large-scale and seasonal storage, charged in one season and discharged in another, almost always working with heat pumps that lift the modest ground temperature to useful levels.

## Borehole thermal energy storage (BTES)

An array of closed-loop **boreholes** - vertical pipes 50-200 m deep - turns a block of ground into a heat store. Fluid circulating through the loops deposits heat into the ground in summer and extracts it in winter.

- Charged with summer solar or waste heat, discharged for winter heating
- Can also store winter cold for summer cooling
- The store grows more efficient over years as the surrounding ground reaches a stable temperature profile
- Best at large scale (district schemes, campuses) where the volume-to-loss ratio is favourable

## Aquifer thermal energy storage (ATES)

**ATES** uses natural groundwater in a permeable aquifer as an open-loop store. It typically runs two sets of wells:
- A **warm well** and a **cold well**
- In summer: cold groundwater is pumped up to cool the building, returning warmed to the warm well
- In winter: the stored warm water is pumped up (via heat pump) for heating, returning cooled to the cold well

ATES is remarkably effective for buildings with balanced heating and cooling demands - offices, hospitals, data centres - recovering the heat rejected in summer to serve winter heating and vice versa. It requires a suitable aquifer and regulatory approval to protect the groundwater.

## Ground-source heat pump synergy

Ground storage and ground-source heat pumps are closely linked. A standard GSHP simply draws from the ground; a storage system deliberately *charges* the ground with surplus heat (or cold) so the heat pump has a warmer (or cooler) source to work from later - improving its efficiency and the system's overall performance.

## Economics and suitability

Ground stores involve significant capital - drilling boreholes or wells is expensive - and long paybacks, so they suit:
- New developments where ground arrays are installed from the outset
- Sites with balanced heating and cooling (ATES excels here)
- District-scale schemes pursuing low-carbon heat
- Long-horizon owners valuing decades of low-running-cost, low-carbon thermal supply

The reward is access to large-scale, low-carbon heating and cooling using stored thermal energy and the stable temperature of the earth - a cornerstone technology for decarbonising heat at scale.
