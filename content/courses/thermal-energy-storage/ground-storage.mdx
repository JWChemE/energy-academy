The ground itself can be a vast thermal store. Ground and aquifer storage systems use the earth's stable temperature and effectively unlimited volume to store heat and coolth, often seasonally, at scales no tank could match. Almost always they work with heat pumps, which lift the modest ground temperature to a useful level. This lesson covers why the ground works, the two main approaches (borehole and aquifer), their synergy with heat pumps, and their economics.

## Why the ground

A few metres down, ground temperature is stable year-round (around 10 to 14 °C in the UK) and the available volume is effectively unlimited. This makes the ground ideal for large-scale and [seasonal storage](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/seasonal-storage), charged in one season and discharged in another. Because the ground temperature is modest, these systems pair with heat pumps that upgrade it to useful heating (or use it directly for cooling).

## Borehole thermal energy storage (BTES)

An array of closed-loop **boreholes**, vertical pipes 50 to 200 m deep, turns a block of ground into a heat store. Fluid circulating through the loops deposits heat into the ground in summer and extracts it in winter. Charged with summer solar or waste heat and discharged for winter heating (or storing winter cold for summer cooling), a BTES store grows *more* efficient over the first few years as the surrounding ground reaches a stable temperature profile. It works best at large scale (district schemes, campuses), where the favourable [volume-to-loss ratio](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/seasonal-storage) applies.

## Aquifer thermal energy storage (ATES)

**ATES** uses natural groundwater in a permeable aquifer as an open-loop store, typically running two sets of wells, a **warm well** and a **cold well**. In summer, cold groundwater is pumped up to cool the building and returned warmed to the warm well; in winter, the stored warm water is pumped up (via heat pump) for heating and returned cooled to the cold well. ATES is remarkably effective for buildings with balanced heating and cooling demands, offices, hospitals, data centres, recovering the heat rejected in summer to serve winter heating and vice versa. It requires a suitable aquifer and Environment Agency approval to protect the groundwater.

## Ground storage and heat-pump synergy

Ground storage and [ground-source heat pumps](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/ground-source-heat-pumps) are closely linked but not the same. A standard GSHP simply draws heat from the ground. A storage system deliberately *charges* the ground with surplus heat (or cold) so the heat pump has a warmer (or cooler) source to work from later, which raises its [COP](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/cop-and-performance) and the system's overall performance. Storage turns the ground from a passive source into an active, seasonally managed reservoir.

<Callout variant="key" title="Balanced heating and cooling is where ground storage shines">
Ground stores involve significant capital (drilling boreholes or wells is expensive) and long paybacks, so they suit new developments where the array is installed from the outset, district-scale low-carbon schemes, and long-horizon owners. But the standout case is a building or site with **balanced heating and cooling demand**, where ATES in particular recovers the summer's rejected heat to serve the winter and vice versa, doing two jobs with one store. Where cooling in summer and heating in winter roughly balance, ground storage approaches a closed loop that moves heat between the seasons almost for free, which is why it is a cornerstone technology for decarbonising heat and cooling at scale.
</Callout>

With the technologies covered, the final lessons apply storage to the everyday cases: hot water and heating, cooling, and the economics that decide them all.
