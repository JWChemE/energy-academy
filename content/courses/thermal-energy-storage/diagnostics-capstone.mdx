You've learned the difference between sensible and latent storage, how load shifting and demand-charge reduction create value, why stratification and insulation make or break a tank, and why seasonal storage only works at scale. This capstone sends you round a site as the storage engineer — sizing stores, building business cases, and spotting the projects that don't actually pay.

The golden rule runs through every case: **storage stores energy, not money**. It earns only from a *time value* — a price spread, a demand charge, or avoided plant — and only if it's the right medium, well stratified, well insulated, and charged when energy is cheap. Engineer a perfect store against a flat tariff and you've saved nothing.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate** — read the brief and the panel: loads, temperatures, tariffs, volumes, surface areas.
2. **Calculate** — work out the figure that decides it: storage capacity (Q = m·c·ΔT, or latent density), the arbitrage and demand savings, the standing loss, the surface-to-volume ratio. The **reference data is on hand** — sensible/latent capacity, the load-shift value, stratification and scale rules, prices — but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose** — pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe** — build an ordered work plan (controls/operation first, then capital).
5. **Verify** — score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="A store earns from a time value, not from thin air">
Before you specify a tank, ask what time value you're chasing: a peak/off-peak price spread, a demand charge, a smaller chiller? Then make sure the engineering delivers it — the right medium for the space, stratification so the energy is usable, insulation so it survives the wait, and controls that charge at the cheapest hours. Miss the value (flat tariff) or miss the engineering (mixed tank, leaky store, backwards schedule) and the cleverest store saves nothing.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **Sizing** — sensible (m·c·ΔT) versus the ~10× density of latent ice storage.
- **The business case** — energy arbitrage, demand-charge reduction, and the plant-downsizing bonus.
- **The economics test** — why a flat tariff kills the case, and a time-of-use tariff makes it.
- **Design** — stratification (a mixed tank delivers its average, not its peak) and standing losses.
- **Scale & operation** — why seasonal storage needs district scale, and how a backwards charging schedule throws the whole benefit away.
- **Materials** — matching a PCM's transition temperature to the application.

## Differential thinking

"It's a store, it must save energy" misses most of these. A store on a flat tariff saves nothing; a mixed tank that's "full" delivers almost nothing usable; an ice store charged by day costs more than no store at all; a PCM specified at the wrong melt point never engages. The decisive evidence is the numbers: the price spread, the tank's average temperature, the charging schedule, the transition temperature.

## After you finish

- Which **number** decided each case — the price spread, the surface-to-volume ratio, the mixed temperature, the melt point?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, is it a no-cost operational change (reschedule charging), a design fix (stratification, insulation, PCM choice), or a capital project (the store itself) that has to earn its time value?
