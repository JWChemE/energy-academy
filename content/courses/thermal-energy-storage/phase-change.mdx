Where space is tight or a constant temperature is needed, latent storage using phase-change materials and ice offers far higher energy density than a water tank. The trade-off is cost and complexity, so latent storage is chosen when sensible storage genuinely cannot do the job rather than as a default. This lesson covers phase-change materials and their transition temperatures, ice storage for cooling, PCMs built into building fabric, and the practical challenges.

## Phase-change materials (PCMs)

A **PCM** stores energy by melting and releases it by freezing, all at a near-constant transition temperature. Because the [latent heat of fusion](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/sensible-latent) is large, a PCM stores several times more energy per litre than sensible water storage over the same temperature band. The common types trade density against stability:

- **Salt hydrates**: high energy density, non-flammable, cheaper, but can suffer phase separation and supercooling over many cycles.
- **Paraffins (organic)**: stable over thousands of cycles and predictable, but flammable and lower density.
- **Bio-based PCMs**: newer, more sustainable alternatives.

The defining feature is the **transition temperature**: a PCM is chosen so it melts and freezes at exactly the temperature the application needs, around 22 °C for keeping a room comfortable, or higher for hot-water and process storage. Choose the wrong transition temperature and the material never changes phase in service, so it delivers only feeble sensible storage.

## Ice storage for cooling

The most widespread latent application is **ice storage** for [cooling](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/cooling-storage). Melting ice absorbs 334 kJ/kg, enormous compared with chilled water, so systems make ice overnight on cheap power and melt it to provide cooling during the expensive, high-demand afternoon. Ice-on-coil (ice builds on submerged coils, then melts to chill water) and encapsulated or ice-harvesting variants suit different scales. A small ice store can replace a much larger chilled-water tank for the same cooling capacity, the density advantage in action.

## PCMs in building fabric

PCMs can be built into walls, ceilings and ceiling tiles to add thermal mass to lightweight buildings. The PCM melts as the room warms during the day (absorbing heat and capping the peak temperature) and refreezes at night (releasing heat to be ventilated away). This passive use can cut cooling demand and smooth temperature swings without any tank at all, a link to the [thermal-mass](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/solar-gain) role that heavyweight construction plays naturally.

<Callout variant="key" title="Choose latent only when sensible cannot do the job">
Latent storage brings real difficulties that a water tank does not: low thermal conductivity (PCMs charge and discharge slowly and often need fins or encapsulation to move heat fast enough), cycling instability (some materials degrade or phase-separate over many cycles), higher material and containment cost, and the need to contain expansion on melting. So the rule is to reach for latent storage only where sensible storage genuinely cannot meet the need: where space is too tight for the required water volume, where a stable temperature is essential, or where the high density of ice transforms the [cooling economics](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/cooling-storage). Otherwise a water tank is usually simpler, cheaper and more reliable.
</Callout>
