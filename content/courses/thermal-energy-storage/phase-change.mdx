Where space is tight or a constant temperature is needed, latent storage using phase-change materials and ice offers far higher energy density than a water tank. The trade-off is cost and complexity.

## Phase-change materials (PCMs)

A **PCM** stores energy by melting and releases it by freezing, all at a near-constant transition temperature. Because the latent heat of fusion is large, a PCM stores several times more energy per litre than sensible water storage over the same temperature band.

Common PCM types:
- **Salt hydrates:** high energy density, non-flammable, cheaper - but can suffer phase separation and supercooling over many cycles
- **Paraffins (organic):** stable over thousands of cycles, predictable - but flammable and lower density
- **Bio-based PCMs:** newer, more sustainable alternatives

The defining feature is the **transition temperature**: a PCM is chosen so it melts/freezes at exactly the temperature the application needs - around 22 C for keeping a room comfortable, or higher for hot-water and process storage.

## Ice storage for cooling

The most widespread latent application is **ice storage** for cooling. Melting ice absorbs 334 kJ/kg - enormous compared with chilled water. Systems make ice overnight on cheap power and melt it to provide cooling during the expensive, high-demand afternoon.

- **Ice-on-coil:** ice builds on coils submerged in a tank, then melts to chill water
- **Ice-harvesting and encapsulated ice:** variants suited to different scales

A small ice store can replace a large chilled-water tank for the same cooling capacity - the density advantage in action.

## PCMs in building fabric

PCMs can be built into walls, ceilings, and ceiling tiles to add thermal mass to lightweight buildings. The PCM melts as the room warms during the day (absorbing heat and capping the peak temperature) and refreezes at night (releasing heat to be ventilated away). This passive use can cut cooling demand and smooth temperature swings without any tank at all.

## Practical challenges

Latent storage is not without difficulties:
- **Low thermal conductivity:** PCMs charge and discharge slowly; they often need fins or encapsulation to move heat in and out fast enough
- **Cycling stability:** some materials degrade or separate over many cycles
- **Cost:** materials and containment cost more than a simple water tank
- **Containment:** PCMs expand on melting and must be safely encapsulated

## When to choose latent

Choose latent storage when sensible storage cannot meet the need: where space is too tight for the required water volume, where a stable temperature is essential, or where the high density of ice transforms the cooling economics. Otherwise, a water tank is usually simpler and cheaper.
