The main reason to store thermal energy is to move consumption in time - generating or buying energy when it is cheap and using it when it would otherwise be expensive. This is load shifting, and it is where storage earns its keep.

## The time-value of energy

Energy is not the same price at all hours. Electricity tariffs vary across the day, and demand charges penalise peak consumption. Storage lets you decouple the time of purchase from the time of use:

- **Charge off-peak:** make and store heat or coolth when electricity is cheap (overnight) or when on-site renewables are generating
- **Discharge on-peak:** draw from the store during expensive peak hours, avoiding high-tariff purchase

## Cooling example

A building chills water or makes ice overnight on cheap off-peak power, storing it. During the hot afternoon - when both cooling demand and electricity prices peak - it melts the ice instead of running the chiller. The chiller can be smaller (it runs longer, off-peak, rather than meeting the instantaneous peak), and the expensive peak electricity is avoided.

## Heating example

A thermal store charged overnight (by a heat pump on cheap tariff, or off-peak electric heating) supplies the morning heating peak without drawing power at the worst time. This is the logic behind night-storage heating and, increasingly, heat-pump systems with buffer tanks on time-of-use tariffs.

## Peak demand reduction

Beyond the energy price, storage cuts **peak demand** itself, which matters because:
- Many tariffs include a capacity or demand charge based on the highest peak
- A lower peak can mean a smaller, cheaper supply connection and smaller plant
- It eases strain on the local network

Shifting load from peak to off-peak can substantially cut the demand-charge portion of a bill, sometimes worth more than the energy saving.

## Working out the value

The saving from load shifting is the price difference between charging and discharging periods, multiplied by the energy shifted, minus the round-trip losses of the store (heat leaks away over time). A wide peak/off-peak price spread makes storage attractive; a flat tariff makes it pointless. The economics lesson later in this course works this through in detail.

## Beyond cost: enabling renewables and flexibility

Load shifting also lets a building soak up its own variable renewable generation (storing midday solar heat for evening use) and offer flexibility to the grid - reducing demand at system peaks. As tariffs become more dynamic and grids more renewable, the value of being able to shift thermal load in time is rising.
