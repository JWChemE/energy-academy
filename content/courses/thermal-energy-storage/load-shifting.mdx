The main reason to store thermal energy is to move consumption in time: generating or buying energy when it is cheap and using it when it would otherwise be expensive. This is load shifting, and it is where storage earns its keep. It also cuts peak demand, which on many commercial tariffs is worth as much as the energy saving itself. This lesson covers the time-value of energy, worked examples in heating and cooling, peak-demand reduction, and how to work out the value.

## The time-value of energy

Energy is not the same price at all hours. Electricity tariffs vary across the day, and [demand charges](/courses/motors-and-drives/demand-management) penalise peak consumption. Storage lets you decouple the time of purchase from the time of use: **charge off-peak** (make and store heat or coolth when electricity is cheap overnight, or when on-site renewables are generating) and **discharge on-peak** (draw from the store during expensive peak hours, avoiding high-tariff purchase).

The two everyday cases:

- **Cooling**: a building makes ice or chilled water overnight on cheap off-peak power, then during the hot afternoon (when both cooling demand and electricity prices peak) it melts the ice instead of running the chiller. The chiller can be smaller, because it runs longer and off-peak rather than meeting the instantaneous peak, and the expensive peak electricity is avoided, the [cooling-storage](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/cooling-storage) case in full.
- **Heating**: a [thermal store](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/water-tanks) charged overnight (by a [heat pump](/courses/renewable-energy/heat-pump-renewable) on cheap tariff, or off-peak electric heating) supplies the morning heating peak without drawing power at the worst time.

## Peak demand reduction

Beyond the energy price, storage cuts **peak demand** itself, which matters because many tariffs include a capacity or maximum-demand charge based on the highest peak, a lower peak can mean a smaller and cheaper supply connection and smaller plant, and it eases strain on the local network. Shifting load from peak to off-peak can substantially cut the demand-charge portion of a bill, sometimes worth more than the energy saving.

## Working out the value

The saving from load shifting is the price difference between the charging and discharging periods, multiplied by the energy shifted, minus the round-trip losses of the store (heat leaks away over time). A wide peak/off-peak spread makes storage attractive; a flat tariff makes it pointless, the [economics lesson](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/economics-payback) works this through in detail.

<Callout variant="key" title="Load shifting also unlocks your own renewables and grid flexibility">
Load shifting is not only about avoiding expensive hours; it is what lets a building actually use its own variable renewable generation, storing midday solar heat for the evening rather than exporting it cheaply, and what lets the building offer [flexibility](/courses/renewable-energy/microgrid) to the grid by reducing demand at system peaks. As tariffs become more dynamic and grids more renewable, the value of being able to shift thermal load in time is rising, so a store justified today on a simple peak/off-peak spread often gains value over its life as tariffs sharpen.
</Callout>
