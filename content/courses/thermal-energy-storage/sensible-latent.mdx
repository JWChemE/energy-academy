Thermal energy storage lets you separate when energy is generated from when it is used. The two fundamental ways to store heat or "coolth" are sensible and latent storage.

## Sensible heat storage

**Sensible storage** raises the temperature of a material - you feel (sense) the temperature change. The energy stored is:

Q = m x c x dT

- m = mass (kg)
- c = specific heat capacity (J/kgK)
- dT = temperature change (K)

Water is the classic medium: cheap, safe, and with a high specific heat of 4,180 J/kgK. Heating 1,000 litres of water by 40 C stores about 46 kWh. Other media include concrete, rock, and molten salts for high-temperature applications.

## Latent heat storage

**Latent storage** uses the energy absorbed or released when a material changes phase - usually melting and freezing - at constant temperature. The **latent heat of fusion** is large compared with sensible heat, so far more energy is stored in a small temperature band.

Melting ice absorbs 334 kJ/kg at 0 C - equivalent to heating the same water by about 80 C sensibly. This density is why ice storage is so compact for cooling, and why **phase-change materials (PCMs)** are attractive where space is limited.

## Comparing the two

- **Sensible:** simple, cheap, proven (a hot-water tank). But low energy density means large volumes, and the temperature falls as it discharges.
- **Latent:** high energy density and near-constant temperature during discharge. But materials cost more, can be harder to charge/discharge (poor conductivity, phase separation), and must be matched to the right transition temperature.

## Choosing a medium

The choice follows the application:
- Building heating/hot water: sensible water storage, cheap and adequate
- Cooling with tight space: ice or PCM latent storage for density
- Keeping a space at a set temperature: PCM tuned to that temperature
- High-temperature industrial/solar: molten salt (sensible) or high-temperature PCM

## Why store at all

Storage exists to exploit a difference in time-value: charge when energy is cheap, abundant, or being wasted; discharge when it is expensive or scarce. The following lessons explore that economic logic - load shifting, peak reduction, and seasonal storage - and the technologies that deliver it.
