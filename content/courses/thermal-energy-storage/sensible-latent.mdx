Thermal energy storage lets you separate when heat (or "coolth") is generated from when it is used, which is what turns a cheap off-peak hour or a burst of midday solar into useful energy later. There are two fundamental ways to store thermal energy, sensible and latent, and the choice between them runs through every technology in this course. This lesson covers both, compares them, and explains why storage exists at all.

## Sensible heat storage

**Sensible storage** raises the temperature of a material, so you literally feel (sense) the change. The energy stored is Q = m × c × ΔT, where m is mass (kg), c is specific heat capacity (J/kgK), and ΔT is the temperature change (K).

<WorkedExample title="storing heat in water">
  <Given>
    - 1,000 litres of water (1,000 kg), specific heat 4,180 J/kgK
    - Heated through a 40 °C band
  </Given>
  <Find>The energy stored.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Q = 1,000 × 4,180 × 40 = 167,200,000 J ≈ **46 kWh**. That is enough to ride a small
    building through a demand peak or an off-peak charging window. Water is the classic
    medium (cheap, safe, high specific heat), and this simple equation underpins the
    [water-tank sizing](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/water-tanks) later in the course.
    Other sensible media include concrete, rock, and molten salts for high-temperature use.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Latent heat storage

**Latent storage** uses the energy absorbed or released when a material changes phase, usually melting and freezing, at constant temperature. The **latent heat of fusion** is large compared with sensible heat, so far more energy is stored in a small temperature band. Melting ice absorbs 334 kJ/kg at 0 °C, equivalent to heating the same water by about 80 °C sensibly. That density is why ice storage is so compact for [cooling](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/cooling-storage) and why [phase-change materials](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/phase-change) are attractive where space is tight.

## Comparing the two

- **Sensible**: simple, cheap, proven (a hot-water tank). But low energy density means large volumes, and the temperature falls as it discharges.
- **Latent**: high energy density and near-constant temperature during discharge. But materials cost more, can be harder to charge and discharge (poor conductivity, phase separation), and must be matched to the right transition temperature.

The choice follows the application: sensible water storage for building heating and hot water (cheap and adequate); ice or PCM for cooling where space is tight; a PCM tuned to a target temperature for keeping a space comfortable; and molten salt or high-temperature PCM for industrial and solar heat.

<Callout variant="key" title="Storage sells time, not energy">
Storage does not create or save energy in itself; you put energy in and take slightly less out later. Its whole value comes from exploiting a difference in the *time-value* of energy: charge when energy is cheap, abundant, or being wasted, and discharge when it is expensive or scarce. That is why storage pays only where prices or availability vary in time, and why a flat tariff makes it pointless. The following lessons develop that economic logic, load shifting, peak reduction, and seasonal storage, and the technologies that deliver it.
</Callout>

The reason to store at all is time-value, which the next lesson develops into the core storage strategy: load shifting.
