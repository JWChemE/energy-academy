Before improving a steam system you need to know where its energy currently goes, and the honest answer on most sites is: nobody has checked for years. A structured efficiency assessment follows the energy from the gas meter to the process and back, attributing losses to each stage: combustion, blowdown, distribution, traps and condensate. This lesson covers the generation end, where the first and largest losses live, and sets up the loss inventory the rest of the course fills in.

## Boiler efficiency: the flue tells the story

A shell boiler in good order converts around 80 to 85% of its fuel's energy into steam; the balance leaves mostly up the flue. Flue gas analysis, a routine service measurement with a portable analyser, reads the two numbers that matter: **flue temperature** (heat leaving; every ~20 °C of excess roughly costs 1% efficiency) and **oxygen content** (excess air; air that was heated and discharged without burning anything). High flue temperature points to fouled heat transfer surfaces or an economiser opportunity; high oxygen points to burner tuning. The [boilers course](/courses/boilers-and-fired-systems) covers combustion in depth; for the steam assessment, the required output is simply the measured efficiency figure, dated, at typical load.

Load matters as much as tune. Boilers are least efficient at low load, where standing losses dominate, and worst of all when cycling on and off against a small demand, each purge cycle throwing heated air up the stack. A boiler log showing hours and firing pattern belongs in every assessment.

## Blowdown: necessary, but meterable

Boiler water concentrates dissolved solids as steam leaves, and the concentrate must be periodically **blown down** to drain to protect the boiler. Blowdown is hot boiler water at full pressure, so every kilogram discarded carries its full sensible heat. The assessment questions are: how much (as a percentage of steam make), controlled by what (a TDS controller, or a manually-opened valve and habit), and with any heat recovery (a flash vessel and heat exchanger on the blowdown line are standard kit on well-run plants).

<WorkedExample title="what tighter blowdown control is worth">
  <Given>
    - A 4 t/h boiler at 7 bar g blows down 10% of its steam make; TDS control would achieve 5%
    - Boiler water sensible heat ≈ 721 kJ/kg; feedwater ≈ 335 kJ/kg
    - Boiler 85% efficient; 6,000 h/yr; gas £0.06/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual saving from halving the blowdown rate.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Blowdown flow reduced by 0.05 × 4,000 = 200 kg/h. Net heat in each kilogram discarded
    = 721 − 335 = 386 kJ, so heat saved ≈ 200 × 386 ÷ 3,600 ≈ 21.4 kW.

    As fuel: 21.4 ÷ 0.85 ≈ 25.2 kW of gas × 6,000 h ≈ 151,000 kWh, about **£9,100 a
    year**, before counting the water and treatment chemicals saved with it. An automatic
    TDS control system typically costs a fraction of that; this is one of the boiler
    house's most reliable retrofits.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Building the loss inventory

The assessment's product is a one-page energy balance: fuel in; steam out (metered, or inferred from feedwater make-up); and the losses attributed line by line: flue (from the analyser), blowdown (from TDS figures), distribution radiation (from the [insulation survey](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-distribution)), trap losses (from the [trap survey](/courses/steam-and-condensate/trap-failure)), and unreturned condensate (from the [return-rate measurement](/courses/steam-and-condensate/condensate-return)). Each line gets a £ figure at the site's [cost per tonne](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-basics), and the inventory becomes the ranked project list, exactly as an [audit](/courses/energy-audits/identify-opportunities) would produce.

<Callout variant="key" title="Meter steam, or you are assessing blind">
The single most valuable instrument on a steam system is a steam (or feedwater) flow meter. Without one, efficiency is a guess between two bills; with one, the site has a kWh-per-tonne figure to trend, exactly like [specific energy on compressed air](/courses/compressed-air/pressure-flow), and every project's saving becomes provable. If the assessment finds no metering, metering is recommendation one, because it converts every later recommendation from assertion to measurement.
</Callout>
