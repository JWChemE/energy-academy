You've learned how steam is generated, distributed, trapped and recovered, and where it wastes energy. This capstone puts you on site as the auditor, but with a twist that matters for steam systems: **the panel gives you raw readings, and you have to do the engineering calculation yourself** before you can diagnose anything.

Nobody hands you "the condensate return is 60%" or "you're losing 13% to flash." You get the meter totals, the temperatures, the pressures and the dimensions, and you work the figure out, exactly as you would standing in a real plant room.

## How the cases work

Each of the eight call-outs is a five-step loop:

1. **Investigate**: read the brief and the raw site readings.
2. **Calculate**: compute the figure that actually matters (return %, flash %, trap loss, heat loss, dryness fraction…). The **reference data is on hand**: steam tables, trap-loss and pipe-loss figures, but the *method* is yours to recall. Stuck? The **"I'm stuck"** button gives you the formula, then a numeric nudge, but leaning on it means you won't get a clean pass until you come back and do the maths unaided.
3. **Diagnose**: use your numbers and the evidence to pick the root cause(s).
4. **Prescribe**: build an ordered work plan (safety → no-cost → remedial → capital).
5. **Verify**: see your score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, and read the debrief.

## The calculations you'll practise

- **Condensate return %** from feedwater and make-up meters, and the £/yr the lost heat costs.
- **Flash steam %** from the steam-table enthalpies, and the recoverable kW.
- **Steam-trap loss** in kg/h and £/yr from orifice size and pressure.
- **Pipe heat loss** from bare-vs-insulated figures, and the saving from re-lagging.
- **Distribution pressure** trade-offs: how flash loss changes with pressure.
- **Stall**: reading saturation temperatures to see when a coil can't drain.
- **Dryness fraction** and the heat penalty of wet steam.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Work it out, don't read it off">
The whole point of this capstone is the calculation. A figure you derived yourself, and can defend, is worth far more than one you were handed. If you take a hint, the tool won't mark it as a clean pass: that's deliberate. Come back, do it unaided, and the pass means you can actually do it on site.

## Differential thinking still applies

As in the boiler diagnostics, the same symptom can have different causes. A hot stream of condensate being dumped might be a genuine contamination problem, or someone being over-cautious with perfectly clean condensate (check the conductivity). A waterlogged coil might be a failed trap, or a stall that no new trap will fix. A wet-steam complaint might be poor separation, or carryover from a boiler running high on TDS, which ties straight back to your boiler water-treatment work.

## After you finish

- Which **single reading** was the decisive clue in each case?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on a blank sheet, on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, what would you put in the report, and how would you justify any capital (flash recovery, pumped return, separators)?
