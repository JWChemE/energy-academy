Steam loses heat as it travels through pipes. Insulation is essential, and the economic thickness is where added insulation cost equals heat saved annually.

## Heat loss from pipes

Uninsulated pipe at, say, 180°C in a 20°C room loses a lot of heat (roughly proportional to the temperature difference and pipe diameter).

**Insulation thickness** (mm of foam, mineral wool, etc.) reduces loss. Thicker = less loss, but at diminishing returns and increasing cost.

Economic thickness is where total cost (insulation cost amortised + energy cost of loss) is minimised.

Rule of thumb: insulation thickness (mm) ≈ pipe diameter (mm) / 2, for new industrial steam lines. For existing, it's usually cost-effective to add 50–100 mm of foam.

## Pipe sizing

Pipes must be large enough to deliver steam without excessive pressure drop (which causes flash losses) and small enough to avoid excessive heat loss and cost.

**Pressure drop** is critical: a 10 bar steam line that loses 1 bar of pressure to friction is now at 9 bar — some of the high-temp energy is wasted. Modern sizing targets 0.3–0.5 bar drop per 30 m of pipe for steam.

**Velocity** of steam in pipes should be around 20–30 m/s (too slow = larger, more expensive pipe; too fast = pressure drop and noise).

## Condensate return

Returning hot condensate (instead of letting it run to waste) saves energy:
- Hot condensate is 80–90°C, vs. fresh cold water at 10–15°C
- Reducing cold feed water means less fuel needed to re-boil
- Flash steam from the condensate can be captured
