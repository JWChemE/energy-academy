A **steam trap** is an automatic valve that lets condensate (water) and air escape, while blocking steam. Proper trap selection and operation are crucial.

## Thermostatic traps

Work on temperature. **Filled with a wax or liquid that expands when warm, pushes a piston, opens the valve.**

- **Advantage**: Simple, respond well to cold start-ups (let air and cold condensate through immediately)
- **Disadvantage**: Can be damaged by thermal shock (sudden cold condensate following hot steam); can become sluggish

Good for: moderate-pressure, moderate-load lines.

## Float traps

**A small float ball floats on liquid condensate, its buoyancy opening a valve.**

- **Advantage**: Respond quickly to condensate buildup; tolerate some steam/air carryover
- **Disadvantage**: The float can stick if condensate is dirty; require strainers upstream

Good for: high-load, fast-draining applications (main returns, heat exchangers).

## Inverted bucket traps

**An inverted bucket in a chamber of condensate; as condensate fills, it pulls the bucket down, opening a drain valve. When steam enters, the bucket rises, closing the valve.**

- **Advantage**: Very reliable, work at any pressure, tolerate large temperature swings
- **Disadvantage**: Require proper installation (orientation) and occasional maintenance

Good for: high-reliability, critical applications.

## Thermodynamic (disc) traps

**Use the principle that steam expands and cools as it passes through a small orifice. A disc valve oscillates, letting condensate through when cooled, sealing on steam.**

- **Advantage**: Very compact, no moving parts to stick
- **Disadvantage**: Sensitive to dirty steam; easy to damage during cleaning

Good for: high-temperature, compact spaces.

## Key rule

**Never use a steam trap larger than the load requires.** An oversized trap (e.g., a large inverted-bucket on a small radiator) will be sluggish and fail prematurely. Match trap size to actual condensate flow (kW load ÷ latent heat).
