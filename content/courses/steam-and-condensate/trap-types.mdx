Every point where steam gives up its heat produces condensate, and that condensate must leave immediately, without letting live steam escape with it. The device that performs this discrimination is the **steam trap**: an automatic valve that opens to water and air but closes to steam. A site may have dozens or hundreds of them, they are the most numerous active component in the system, and choosing the right type for each duty is what determines how well and how long they do it. This lesson covers the three families and the selection logic.

## Three ways to tell steam from water

Traps distinguish steam from condensate by exploiting one of three physical differences, and the choice of mechanism defines the family.

**Mechanical traps** use density: condensate is water, steam is vapour, and a float can tell the difference. The **ball float trap** opens a valve as condensate lifts the float, discharging continuously at steam temperature the moment condensate forms. That immediacy makes it the default choice for heat exchangers and process equipment, where condensate lying inside the plant would blank off heating surface. The **inverted bucket** is its rugged sibling: an open-bottomed bucket sinks in condensate (valve open) and floats on steam (valve shut). It tolerates waterhammer and dirt better, at the cost of some air-handling ability.

**Thermostatic traps** use temperature: condensate that has cooled a few degrees below saturation is provably not steam. A **balanced pressure** element (a capsule that boils and expands near steam temperature) or a **bimetallic** stack opens when contents cool. They hold condensate back until it sub-cools, which is ideal where you *want* the sensible heat used (tracing lines) and excellent at venting cold air on start-up, but wrong for process plant that must drain instantly.

**Thermodynamic traps** use velocity: flashing condensate and steam moving fast across a small disc create a pressure signature that snaps the disc shut on steam. The **TD trap** is compact, cheap, robust across a wide pressure range and indifferent to orientation, which makes it the workhorse for [steam main drain points](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-distribution); its weaknesses are poor air handling, dislike of very low pressure differentials, and an audible click-cycle as it works.

## Matching trap to duty

Selection follows the duty's priorities. **Process heat exchangers** (jackets, calorifiers, coils): ball float, for immediate discharge and best output. **Steam main drainage**: thermodynamic (or inverted bucket), for robustness outdoors and against waterhammer. **Tracing and low-priority lines**: thermostatic, to exploit condensate sub-cooling and save steam. **Anything with heavy air on start-up** (batch plant, autoclaves): float traps with integral thermostatic air vents, or added vent capacity.

Sizing then follows from the condensate load: the duty's heat rate divided by latent heat at the working pressure. A 100 kW heat exchanger at 3 bar g (latent heat ≈ 2,134 kJ/kg) produces 100 ÷ 2,134 × 3,600 ≈ **169 kg/h** of condensate; the trap is selected for that flow, times a start-up factor of two or three, at the actual pressure *differential* across it. Oversizing is not generosity: a grossly oversized trap cycles badly and fails early.

<Callout variant="key" title="Fit strainers, and record what you fit">
Two habits prevent most premature trap failures. First, a strainer immediately upstream of every trap (many float and TD failures are simply grit holding the valve off its seat). Second, an asset register: every trap tagged with its location, type, size, pressure and duty. The register turns the [trap survey in the next lesson](/courses/steam-and-condensate/trap-failure) from an annual scavenger hunt into a maintenance routine, and it is the first thing a good steam engineer asks to see.
</Callout>

Traps fail more often than any other steam component, and failed traps are money in both directions, which is exactly where the course goes next.
