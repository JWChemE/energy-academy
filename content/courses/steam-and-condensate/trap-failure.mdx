Steam traps are the most-failed maintenance item in steam systems. A building with 50 traps might have 20 failed ones within 5 years if not actively managed.

## Failure modes

**Stuck open** — condensate (and steam) escape continuously. Major waste. Detection: hissing, visible steam/water spray.

**Stuck closed** — condensate backs up, hammering (water banging), potential equipment damage. Detection: poor heating, pressure buildup, noise.

**Partial failure** — passing some steam, sluggish response. Hard to spot without measurement.

## Why they fail

- **Corrosion** — condensate is acidic (dissolved CO₂); corrodes internal parts
- **Water hammer** — rapid pressure swings cause fatigue
- **Thermal shock** — thermal cycling loosens components
- **Dirt/scale** — blocks orifices, sticks floats
- **Age** — mechanical parts wear out; typical life is 3–5 years in harsh conditions, 5–10 in mild

## Detection

**Infrared thermography** — quick scan of all traps; a failed (stuck-open) trap is usually hotter than expected.

**Listening** — hissing or steam sounds indicate stuck-open.

**Condensate sampling** — collecting condensate downstream of a trap and checking for steam (by observing boiling/pressure); a failed trap shows steam.

**Performance** — if an area fed by a trap is cold or slow to heat, the trap may be stuck closed.

## Economics

Cost to replace one trap: £500–1,500 (material + labor). Annual energy cost of a failed trap: £300–1,000 depending on steam pressure and load.

Payback: 1–3 years in most cases. **Trap maintenance programs (checking and replacing periodically) almost always have excellent ROI.**

Many facilities budget annual trap replacement: expect 10–15% failure rate per year (1–2 traps per 100).
