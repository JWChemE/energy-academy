Steam traps are wearing parts. Their internals cycle millions of times in dirty, hot, erosive service, and industry experience is blunt about the consequence: on a system without a survey programme, expect **10 to 20% of traps to have failed** at any moment. Each failure costs money in one of two directions, and the expensive direction is invisible without instruments. Trap management is therefore not a maintenance nicety; on most steam systems it is one of the top three energy line items, and this lesson covers the failure modes, the detection methods and the economics.

## Two ways to fail, two different bills

A trap **failed open** no longer closes against steam: live steam blows continuously through it into the condensate line. The heating still works, nothing alarms, and the only symptoms are a hot return line and a bigger gas bill, which is why failed-open traps survive for years.

<WorkedExample title="one failed-open trap, priced">
  <Given>
    - A trap with a 3 mm orifice fails open on a 7 bar g line (8 bar absolute)
    - Steam loss through an orifice ≈ 0.4 × d² × P(bar abs) kg/h, a standard approximation: 0.4 × 9 × 8 ≈ 29 kg/h
    - System live 6,000 h/yr; steam costs ≈ £48/tonne (from the [steam basics lesson](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-basics))
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual cost of leaving it.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Annual loss ≈ 29 × 6,000 ÷ 1,000 ≈ **174 tonnes of steam**, costing about
    **£8,300 a year**, through one component that costs a few hundred pounds to replace.
    Even if the trap is smaller or the pressure lower, the arithmetic rarely lands below
    four figures. Multiply by a 15% failure rate across a hundred-trap site and the case
    for a survey programme writes itself.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

A trap **failed closed** (or blocked) costs differently: condensate backs up into the plant it should be draining. Heat exchangers lose output as they waterlog, processes slow down, and the standing water invites corrosion and [waterhammer](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-distribution). The bill arrives as production and maintenance problems rather than fuel, and operators often "solve" it by cracking open a bypass valve, which then leaks steam permanently: a failed-closed trap converted by habit into a failed-open one.

## Finding failures: three signals

No single test condemns a trap reliably; surveys combine three. **Temperature** (infrared spot or camera): a working trap shows hot upstream and cooler downstream; both sides at steam temperature suggests blowing, both cold suggests blocked. Temperature alone misleads, because a working trap's discharge side runs hot with [flash steam](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-pressure). **Ultrasound**: the same instrument as [air leak surveying](/courses/compressed-air/leaks-detection) hears the difference between a trap's intermittent discharge and the continuous roar of blowing steam; this is the discriminating test. **Sight**: where discharge is visible, intermittent flash puffs are healthy; a continuous sharp jet is live steam.

Fixed monitoring (temperature/ultrasonic sensors piped to the [BMS or M&T system](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/exception-reporting)) exists for critical or inaccessible traps, turning annual surveys into continuous exception reporting; it is standard on large process sites and increasingly cheap.

## The programme, and its arithmetic

Best practice is unglamorous: an annual (six-monthly on high-pressure plant) survey of every trap on the [register](/courses/steam-and-condensate/trap-types), tagging each pass/fail; immediate replacement of failures, matched to duty rather than like-for-like if the original selection was wrong; and a running failure-rate KPI. Survey costs are typically a few pounds per trap; on a hundred-trap site finding the expected dozen failures at even £2,000 to £8,000 each, the programme's payback is measured in weeks. It is routinely the single best-returning line in a steam audit's [opportunity table](/courses/energy-audits/identify-opportunities).

<Callout variant="key" title="Unmanaged trap populations always drift to ~15% failed">
Traps fail at a few percent per year, so a population left unsurveyed accumulates failures until the losses are enormous and invisible. The steady state without management is 10 to 20% failed; the steady state with an annual survey is 3 to 5%. The difference on a mid-size site is tens of thousands of pounds a year, purchased with one instrument, one register, and two survey days annually.
</Callout>

Downstream of every healthy trap flows hot condensate, and what happens to it is the next lesson, and the largest single recovery in the course.
