When steam has given up its latent heat, what remains is condensate: distilled, chemically treated water at up to 100 °C, produced in exactly the quantity the boiler needs to feed. Every tonne poured down a drain must be replaced with cold mains water that has to be bought, treated, and heated from perhaps 10 °C, and it usually takes a drainage charge on the way out. Condensate return is consequently the largest single recovery available on most under-managed steam systems, and this lesson covers its value, its machinery and its pitfalls.

## What a tonne of condensate is worth

Condensate carries value three ways. **Heat**: at 85 °C it holds around 314 kJ/kg more than 10 °C make-up water, energy the boiler would otherwise buy as gas. **Water**: a tonne of make-up avoided, plus its disposal. **Chemistry**: condensate is already softened and treated; make-up water consumes softener capacity and chemicals, and its dissolved solids drive extra [blowdown](/courses/steam-and-condensate/efficiency-assessment), compounding the loss.

<WorkedExample title="raising the return rate">
  <Given>
    - A 4 t/h steam system, 6,000 h/yr, currently returns 40% of condensate; 70% is achievable
    - Condensate 85 °C (≈356 kJ/kg) versus make-up at 10 °C (≈42 kJ/kg); boiler 85% efficient; gas £0.06/kWh
    - Water plus treatment plus disposal ≈ £3/tonne avoided
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual value of the extra 30% returned.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Extra return = 0.30 × 4,000 kg/h × 6,000 h = 7,200 tonnes a year.

    Heat: 7,200 t × 314 MJ/t ÷ 0.85 ≈ 739,000 kWh of gas, worth ≈ **£44,300**.
    Water and treatment: 7,200 × £3 ≈ **£21,600**.

    Total ≈ **£66,000 a year**, for pumps, pipework and a receiver: a project that very
    commonly repays within a year. Steam systems are unusual in hiding savings this large
    behind this little glamour.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## The machinery of return

Where condensate sits above and near the boiler house, gravity and the trap's own pressure push it home. Everywhere else needs **pumping**: electric centrifugal sets where power is convenient, or mechanical **pressure-powered pumps** (driven by steam itself) where condensate is hot enough to make electric pumps cavitate. Hot condensate boils readily at a pump inlet, so receivers, static head and pump selection are a real engineering exercise rather than a catalogue purchase.

The destination is the **feedwater tank (hotwell)**, and it is also where the [flash steam lesson's](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-pressure) recovery lands: a flash vessel on the high-pressure returns feeds its low-pressure steam into the hotwell through a sparge, heating the feedwater for free. A well-run hotwell sits at 85 to 95 °C, which saves fuel directly and strips dissolved oxygen from the water, cutting corrosion and treatment dosing.

## Why return rates fall, and the one honest caution

Sites drift to low return rates for findable reasons: distant plant "temporarily" drained to gulley during a long-forgotten project; corroded return lines abandoned rather than repaired; receivers with failed pumps; new equipment installed without return pipework because the capital case ignored running cost. Walking the site against a condensate mass balance (steam metered out versus condensate metered or gauged back) locates the losses in an afternoon.

The caution: condensate from processes where steam contacts product, or where a heat exchanger leak could contaminate it (certain chemical duties), may be unreturnable or need conductivity monitoring before the hotwell. Food and brewery plants live with this rule daily, as the [brewery course](/sectors/breweries) shows. Contamination risk is a design constraint, not a reason to dump everything.

<Callout variant="key" title="Measure the return rate; it is the system's headline KPI">
Condensate return percentage is to a steam system what [specific energy is to compressed air](/courses/compressed-air/pressure-flow): one number that summarises stewardship. Meter or gauge it monthly, target above 80% where processes allow, and treat any fall as an [exception to investigate](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/exception-reporting), because a falling return rate is usually a new failure with a four-figure monthly cost.
</Callout>
