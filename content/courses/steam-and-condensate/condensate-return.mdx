Returning hot condensate to the boiler saves significant fuel. Adding flash-steam capture makes it even better.

## Why return condensate?

Fresh boiler feedwater is typically 10–15°C (from the cold mains or softener).
Returned condensate is 80–90°C (from the load, after it condensed).

Raising feedwater temp from 15°C to 85°C reduces the energy the boiler must add:
- From 15°C to saturation (say 180°C): needs 165 × specific heat ≈ 690 kJ/kg
- From 85°C to 180°C: needs 95 × specific heat ≈ 398 kJ/kg

**Savings: ~42% of the heat needed for that unit of steam.**

In a large system (say, 1,000 kg/h of steam), condensate return at 85°C vs 15°C saves roughly 430 kW — tens of thousands per year.

## Flash steam

When condensate at, say, 100°C returns through a restriction (pipe, valve), pressure drops, and some vaporises ("flashes") to steam at the lower pressure (say, atmospheric). About 5–15% by weight.

This flash steam can be captured in a **flash tank** (a simple receiver), separated from condensate, and sent to a low-pressure heating line (indirect radiators, process).

Savings: additional 5–15% energy from flash recovery, with minimal cost (just a tank and plumbing).

## Condensate polishing

Hot condensate is corrosive (contains dissolved oxygen and CO₂). A **deaerator** or **polishing unit** (small heater-degasser) removes dissolved gases, reducing boiler corrosion and extending boiler life.

Cost: ~5,000–15,000 GBP. Benefit: longer boiler life (10+ years) and lower maintenance.

## Return line pitfalls

- **Low return pressure** — if condensate line is not pressurised, it can cavitate (boil), causing noise and loss of efficiency. Solution: pressurised return, booster pump, or adequate elevation.
- **No strainer** — dirt in returned condensate clogs the boiler feedwater system. Always have a strainer upstream of the boiler.
- **Long uninsulated return lines** — if condensate must travel far, insulate it to retain heat.
