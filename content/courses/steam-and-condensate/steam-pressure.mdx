Operating pressure affects efficiency and losses significantly. Lower pressure reduces flash steam losses and boiler flue losses, but reduces heat-transfer rates (and may need larger equipment).

## Flash steam and losses

When steam at, say, 10 bar (180°C) is reduced to 1 bar (100°C) through a pressure-reducing valve, some of the pressure drop is lost as "flash steam" — about 10% of the flow vaporises at the lower pressure.

This flash steam carries energy that was meant to heat your load. It's often vented or escapes unused.

**Flash recovery** — capture this flash steam and use it for lower-pressure heating. A flash tank (atmospheric vessel) allows steam to separate from condensate; the steam goes to a low-pressure line, condensate to the return line.

## Optimising pressure

- **Operating at the lowest pressure that meets load requirements** reduces flash losses and boiler stack losses.
- Example: if your load needs 5 bar steam, don't generate at 10 bar just because the boiler can.
- Multi-pressure systems (high-pressure boiler feeding a pressure reducer to a secondary low-pressure header) can combine advantages: high-pressure for heavy users, flash recovery for light users.

## Seasonal variation

In winter, steam demand is high. In summer, much lower (mostly hot water). Operating at lower summer pressure saves energy.

Some facilities have manual valves or automatic regulators to drop pressure seasonally.
