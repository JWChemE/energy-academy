Steam is a powerful heat-transfer medium because of its enormous latent heat. Understanding steam properties is key to managing steam systems efficiently.

## Saturated vs superheated steam

**Saturated steam** — at a given pressure, it coexists with liquid water. At 1 bar (atmosphere), 100°C. At 10 bar, 180°C. Higher pressure = higher saturation temperature.

**Superheated steam** — heated further above saturation. 200°C at 1 bar is superheated. More energy, but less commonly used in buildings.

Most building steam systems use saturated steam (it's cheaper to generate and the high heat-transfer rate is sufficient).

## Latent heat

When water at 100°C becomes steam at 100°C (at 1 bar), an enormous amount of heat is absorbed: **2,257 kJ/kg**. This is latent heat.

When that steam condenses back to water, that heat is released. This is why steam is an excellent heating medium.

Total energy in steam = sensible heat (heating water) + latent heat. Example: steam at 10 bar, 180°C:
- Sensible: 180 × 4.19 = ~754 kJ/kg
- Latent: ~1,888 kJ/kg
- Total: ~2,642 kJ/kg

When this steam condenses, you recover ~1,888 kJ/kg (the latent part goes to the load you're heating; sensible heat comes out as hot condensate).

## Steam tables

Steam tables list properties at different pressures: temperature, density, enthalpy, entropy. Used for calculations.

Key insight: **all saturated steam at the same pressure has the same temperature.** At 10 bar, saturation temp is always ~180°C (whether dry or wet).

**Quality** — the fraction of steam that is vapor vs. liquid. Quality = 0.9 means 90% vapor, 10% liquid (wet steam). Quality = 1.0 is dry saturated steam; below that, you've got droplets.

Wet steam drops efficiency — the liquid particles don't transfer heat as well and can damage equipment.
