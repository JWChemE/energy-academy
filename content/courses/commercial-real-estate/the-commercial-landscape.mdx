Every other course on this platform assumes that whoever manages the energy also controls the building and pays the bills. Commercial real estate breaks that assumption. A multi-let office involves a landlord who owns the plant, a managing agent who operates it, and tenants who occupy the floors, pay most of the costs, and often hold their own electricity contracts. Before any of the engineering from Levels 1 and 2 can be applied here, you need to know who these parties are, which meters belong to whom, and how the service charge moves money between them, because in this sector the organisational map matters as much as the plant room.

## Who's who in a multi-let building

**The landlord** (often a fund, REIT or property company) owns the building, the central plant and the common parts. Its lease with each tenant defines what it must provide: heating, cooling, lifts, lit lobbies.

**The managing agent** runs the building day to day on the landlord's behalf: appointing maintenance contractors, operating the BMS, buying the landlord's energy and administering the service charge. When you audit a commercial building, the agent is usually your client contact.

**The tenants** occupy demised floors or suites under leases that typically run 5 to 15 years. On a standard full repairing and insuring (FRI) lease, each tenant maintains its own demise, usually holds its own electricity supply for it, and pays a share of the landlord's costs for everything shared.

**The occupiers' staff** are the people who actually flick the switches. In a building with twelve tenants there is no single "energy manager" with authority over the whole load, which is precisely why this sector needs its own course.

## Two kinds of supply, one building

Energy enters a multi-let building through two different doors, and confusing them wrecks any analysis:

- **Landlord supplies** feed the central plant and common parts: boilers, chillers, air handling, lifts, lobby and car-park lighting. In an air-conditioned office these can be half or more of the whole building's energy.
- **Tenant supplies** (or landlord sub-meters recharged to tenants) feed each demise: small power, tenant lighting, server cupboards, kitchenettes.

The [meter-data lesson](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/meter-data) taught you to start any analysis by mapping the meters. Here that map is also a map of *control*: the landlord can retune the chillers tomorrow, but cannot switch off a tenant's server room, and the tenant cannot fix an oversized central AHU however good its own housekeeping is.

## The service charge: where the money flows

The landlord's energy costs do not stay with the landlord. They are pooled with cleaning, security and maintenance into the **service charge** and apportioned between tenants, usually by floor area. Take a worked figure this course will keep returning to: an 8,500 m² air-conditioned office whose landlord supplies use 869,500 kWh of electricity and 468,660 kWh of gas a year. At £0.20/kWh and £0.06/kWh that is £202,020 a year of energy flowing through the service charge, about £29 for every square metre of lettable space, before a single tenant plugs anything in.

<Callout variant="key" title="The landlord spends it, the tenants pay it">
Service-charge recovery means the landlord's energy bill is, economically, the tenants' energy bill. That one fact drives most of this course: it explains why landlords historically under-invested in efficiency (they didn't keep the saving), why tenants scrutinise the service charge but can't fix the plant behind it, and why the industry invented green leases to bridge the gap. Whenever you evaluate a measure in this sector, ask not just "does it pay back" but "who funds it, and whose bill falls".
</Callout>

## Why the sector is worth your attention

Commercial buildings are where the UK's office-based economy keeps its energy waste. They are professionally managed, data-rich (half-hourly metering is near universal), and repeat the same faults across thousands of near-identical buildings, which means the diagnostics you learned in the [HVAC](/courses/hvac-systems/common-issues) and [controls](/courses/control-systems-and-bms/control-strategies) courses transfer wholesale.
