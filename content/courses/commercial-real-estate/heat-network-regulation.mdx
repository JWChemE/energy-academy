On 27 January 2026, a quiet revolution reached commercial property: Ofgem became the regulator of heat networks across Great Britain. Any landlord that generates heat centrally and supplies it to tenants, every communal boiler serving a multi-let building, every district scheme feeding an estate, is now operating in a regulated market with authorisation conditions, consumer-protection duties and an ombudsman behind it. For a sector used to treating the boiler house as private plumbing, this is a genuine change of legal category, and this lesson explains what the new regime requires and why it existed to begin with.

## Why heat networks needed a regulator

Around half a million UK homes and thousands of commercial tenants take heat from networks rather than their own boilers, and until 2026 they had none of the protections a gas or electricity customer takes for granted. A heat customer cannot switch supplier: the pipe in the wall is a natural monopoly. Historic complaints clustered exactly where you would expect: opaque tariffs, estimated bills, no redress route and reliability nobody had to answer for. The Energy Act 2023 created the framework, and the Heat Networks (Market Framework) Regulations 2025 gave Ofgem the job, deliberately mirroring the consumer protections of the wider energy market.

## What the regime requires

From 27 January 2026, operating or supplying heat on a network requires **authorisation**, with conditions that combine prescriptive rules and principles. The ones that matter most to a landlord or managing agent:

- **Fair treatment.** Ofgem's Standards of Conduct now apply: transparent pricing, clear contract terms, and specific regard for customers in vulnerable circumstances.
- **Accurate, metered billing.** Bills must be based on actual meter readings at least annually and carry minimum information (consumption, current charges, clear terms). This builds on the Heat Network (Metering and Billing) Regulations 2014, which already required meters where cost-effective.
- **Complaints and redress.** A defined complaints process is mandatory, with unresolved cases escalating to the Energy Ombudsman, a route heat customers simply did not have before.
- **A phased ramp-up.** Not everything switched on at once: obligations phase in through 2026, with the bulk of the regime in force and enforced by January 2027, and technical standards for network performance following behind.

The scope is functional, not corporate. A residential block's communal boiler, a landlord recharging heat in a multi-let office, and a town-scale district network are all "heat networks"; the regime distinguishes primarily between operators and suppliers, and a landlord can be both without ever having thought of itself as an energy company.

## The energy-efficiency angle

Regulation and efficiency meet in the same place: the network's losses. Distribution losses on a poorly designed or insulated network can be 20% or more of the heat generated, and under regulated, metered billing those losses become conspicuous, because the gap between fuel purchased and heat billed at meters is now a number someone computes annually. The methods you already have do the work here: a [mass and energy balance](/courses/mass-and-energy-balances/your-first-balance) across the network boundary quantifies the loss, [insulation economics](/courses/insulation-systems/economic-thickness) prices fixing the distribution pipework, and the [M&V discipline](/courses/measurement-and-verification/why-mv-matters) verifies the improvement. A landlord that treats the new metering data as a diagnostic asset rather than a billing chore will find the regime pays for its own compliance.

<WorkedExample title="what network losses look like in money">
  <Given>
    - A communal gas boiler house generates 900,000 kWh of heat a year; tenant heat meters record 720,000 kWh delivered
    - Gas at £0.06/kWh; boiler seasonal efficiency 85%
  </Given>
  <Find>The distribution loss and its annual fuel cost.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Loss = 900,000 − 720,000 = **180,000 kWh/yr**, a 20% distribution loss, typical of an
    uninsulated or poorly balanced network.

    Fuel behind that loss = 180,000 ÷ 0.85 ≈ 211,765 kWh of gas, costing 211,765 × £0.06 ≈
    **£12,706 a year**. Under the old regime this vanished into "the heating bill"; under
    metered, regulated billing it is a visible gap between generation and sales that someone
    has to either explain or fix.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

<Callout variant="key" title="If you sell heat, you are now an energy supplier">
The mental shift the 2026 regime demands is identity, not paperwork: a landlord recharging heat from central plant is a regulated heat supplier, with the fairness, billing-accuracy and redress duties that title carries. Managing agents need to know which buildings in their portfolio meet the definition, get the authorisations and processes in place through the 2026 phase-in, and treat heat metering as both a compliance requirement and the diagnostic dataset that finally makes network losses visible.
</Callout>
