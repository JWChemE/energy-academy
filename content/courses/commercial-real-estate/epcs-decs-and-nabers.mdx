A commercial building in the UK can hold three different energy labels at once, and they can flatly disagree with each other. An office can carry an EPC B, a mid-table benchmark position, and an operational performance that would embarrass everyone if it were published. Understanding why starts with one distinction: some ratings assess the *asset* (what the building is), others assess the *operation* (how it actually runs), and the gap between the two is one of the defining findings of modern building science. If you advise on commercial property, you must be able to say which rating answers which question.

## EPCs: the asset rating

An **Energy Performance Certificate** rates the building as a physical asset: its fabric, glazing, plant and lighting, as modelled under standard assumptions of occupancy and weather. Non-domestic EPCs are produced with the SBEM methodology (or dynamic simulation for complex buildings), graded A+ to G, valid for ten years, and required at construction, sale or letting. Because the occupant's behaviour is deliberately excluded, an EPC is a fair way to compare what two buildings *are*, and it is the metric UK law regulates through [MEES](/courses/commercial-real-estate/mees-minimum-standards), which is what gives the EPC its teeth.

What an EPC is not is a prediction of the energy bill. It models standardised use, not your tenants' hours, server rooms or overridden schedules.

## DECs: the operational rating almost nobody sees

A **Display Energy Certificate** does the opposite: it grades a building A to G on its *measured* annual energy against a benchmark for its category, with the certificate displayed in the entrance. DECs are mandatory only for buildings over 250 m² occupied by public authorities and frequently visited by the public, annually above 1,000 m². The private commercial sector was never brought into scope, an omission energy professionals have lamented for years, because a public operational label is exactly the pressure that improves behaviour rather than just fabric.

## The performance gap

Put an asset rating and an operational measurement side by side and you meet the **performance gap**: buildings routinely use two to three times the energy their design-stage assessments imply. The causes are precisely the ones this platform keeps teaching: plant never [commissioned properly](/courses/commissioning/what-is-commissioning), controls [drifting](/courses/commissioning/drift-and-degradation), schedules overridden, and the design assumptions (occupancy, hours, small power) bearing no resemblance to real life. The gap is why a portfolio of impressive EPCs can still have dreadful bills, and why the sector's centre of gravity is shifting from modelled ratings to measured ones.

## NABERS UK: measuring what matters

**NABERS UK**, launched in 2020 and administered by BRE under licence from the Australian scheme that pioneered it, rates offices from 1 to 6 stars on *measured* energy, separately for the landlord's services ("base building") and whole building. Two features make it powerful. The base-building rating isolates exactly what the landlord controls, solving the attribution problem that muddies whole-building comparisons in multi-let stock. And its **Design for Performance** route makes developers commit to a target star rating that is verified against metered data after completion, replacing "designed to be efficient" with "measured to be efficient". In Australia, three decades of public NABERS ratings demonstrably transformed the office market; the UK scheme is young but growing through institutional landlords, and increasingly appears in agents' particulars alongside the EPC.

<Callout variant="key" title="Ask which question each rating answers">
An EPC answers "what could this building do?" (asset quality, and MEES compliance). A DEC or NABERS rating answers "what does this building actually do?" (operational performance). A benchmark answers "compared with whom?". They are complements, not competitors: a good EPC with a poor NABERS score means capable fabric run badly, which is a controls and management problem; the reverse means diligent operation of a poor asset, which is a retrofit case. The combination diagnoses; either alone can mislead.
</Callout>

## Where UK policy is heading

Government has consulted on EPC reform (including better metrics and shorter validity) and, separately, on a performance-based rating framework for large commercial buildings explicitly modelled on NABERS. Timelines have slipped repeatedly, so treat specifics with dated caution, but the direction is consistent: more weight on measured, in-use performance, with the EPC retained as the regulatory floor. For a landlord, the practical reading as of mid-2026 is simple: MEES makes the EPC unavoidable, and investor and tenant expectations are making measured ratings the differentiator.
