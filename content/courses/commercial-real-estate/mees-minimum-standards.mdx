Most energy regulation asks companies to report; MEES tells landlords they may not let the building at all. The Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards are the sharpest instrument in UK buildings policy because they attach energy performance directly to the thing a landlord cannot do without: rental income. Since April 2023 it has been unlawful to continue letting a non-domestic property in England and Wales below EPC band E, and in June 2026 the government confirmed where the floor goes next. This lesson covers what applies now, what was just announced, and what a prudent landlord does about a regulation whose trajectory matters more than its current level.

## The rules in force today

Under the Energy Efficiency (Private Rented Property) Regulations 2015, a landlord of non-domestic property in England and Wales may not grant a new lease, and since **1 April 2023** may not *continue* to let, a building with an EPC rating of F or G, unless a valid exemption is registered. Enforcement sits with local authorities, and penalties scale with rateable value up to **£150,000 per property**, plus publication of the breach. Exemptions exist but are narrow, last five years, and must be entered on the public PRS Exemptions Register: the main ones are the "7-year payback" test (the improvements don't pay for themselves within seven years), consent refused by a tenant or third party, and evidence that works would devalue the property. An exemption is not a pass; it is a public statement that the building is substandard, renewed only with fresh evidence.

## What changed in June 2026

The long-trailed proposal was EPC C by 2027 and B by 2030 for all non-domestic lettings. On **18 June 2026**, DESNZ published its interim response to the consultations and softened both the level and the scope:

- The interim **EPC C milestone for 2027 is dropped**.
- Buildings **over 1,000 m²** will be required to reach **EPC B by 2031**, where cost-effective.
- Buildings **at or below 1,000 m²** stay at the existing EPC E floor, with no new deadline yet set.

The response is explicitly interim: enforcement design is still open, and the change needs secondary legislation before it binds. Treat the dates as the current planning basis, not as settled law, and check GOV.UK before relying on them in advice.

    when the letting is at risk.

## What a prudent landlord does now

The 2031 horizon sounds distant; lease and plant cycles make it close. The playbook that follows from this module so far: know every EPC in the portfolio, including when each was modelled and under which SBEM version, because many pre-2020 certificates are pessimistic and a re-assessment after minor works is sometimes the cheapest band improvement available. Sequence works around **lease events and plant end-of-life**, since a chiller replaced like-for-like in 2027 is a stranded opportunity when the B deadline lands in 2031 (the [retrofit lesson](/courses/commercial-real-estate/retrofit-and-plant-replacement) develops this). And treat the sub-1,000 m² carve-out with care: smaller buildings escaped this round, but the direction of policy is one-way, and a refurbishment that ignores it invites doing the work twice.

<Callout variant="key" title="Regulate to the trajectory, not the floor">
MEES has moved once (E in 2018/2023) and has now signalled its next step (B by 2031 for larger buildings). Any capital plan that targets only today's minimum is planning to be non-compliant later, at whatever the works cost in a rush, in an occupied building. The discipline this platform teaches for [regulation generally](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/future-policy) applies doubly here: comply with the floor, plan for the trajectory, and let every natural replacement move the building up a band.
</Callout>
