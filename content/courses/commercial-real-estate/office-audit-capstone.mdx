## How the stages work

This capstone follows the same staged format as the [Energy Audits course's own capstone](/courses/energy-audits/field-audit-capstone), because an office audit *is* an energy audit: this course has given you everything sector-specific on top of that general method:

1. **Plan & Scope**: choose the audit level, draw the boundary where the lease draws it (tenant supplies in or out?), and plan access, escorts and the asbestos register before you walk a 1990s building.
2. **On-Site**: the plant-room observations that matter in a multi-let office, plus twelve months of utility data with one figure missing for you to derive, exactly as billing gaps happen in real life.
3. **Analyse & Normalise**: build the building's gas and electricity models (base loads plus degree-day slopes, the [energy-signature method](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/energy-signatures) in action), then place the building against the REEB benchmarks from [earlier in this course](/courses/commercial-real-estate/kwh-per-m2-benchmarks).
4. **Opportunities**: quantify the out-of-hours saving and the weather-compensation gain, then decide what belongs in the recommendations and what would breach the leases that pay for everything.
5. **Report & M&V**: what the report owes its two audiences (the landlord who commissions it and the tenants who fund it through the service charge), and how the savings get verified rather than assumed.

<Callout variant="key" title="The boundary and the benchmark are the sector skills">
The arithmetic in this capstone is deliberately familiar: base loads, degree-day coefficients, demand × hours × price. What makes it a *commercial real estate* audit is stage 1's boundary decision (audit what the client controls; engage tenants for the rest) and stage 3's honest benchmark placement (157 kWh/m² sounds fine until good practice says 119). Get those two right and the findings write themselves; get them wrong and no amount of correct arithmetic rescues the report.
</Callout>

## What "audit-ready" looks like here

A strong run through this capstone means every stage's answers hold up on their own: the boundary defensible to both landlord and tenants, the hazards genuinely office-specific, the models exact (they reconcile against the meter to the kilowatt-hour), the opportunities correctly separated from the lease obligations no energy measure may cross, and a report grounded in a normalised baseline rather than a raw year-on-year bill comparison. That's the same bar the [Energy Audits capstone](/courses/energy-audits/field-audit-capstone) sets: this is that standard, applied to the landlord-tenant world this course has taught you to navigate.
