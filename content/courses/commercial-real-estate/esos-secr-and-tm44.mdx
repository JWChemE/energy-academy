Beyond MEES, a property business faces a second layer of energy regulation: schemes that require assessing, reporting and inspecting rather than hitting a standard. ESOS audits, SECR disclosures and TM44 air-conditioning inspections all exist elsewhere on this platform as general obligations; what this lesson adds is the property twist, because in a multi-let building the recurring question for every scheme is the same one the [split incentive](/courses/commercial-real-estate/the-split-incentive) taught you to ask: whose energy is it, the landlord's or the tenant's?

## ESOS: whose consumption gets audited?

The [Energy Savings Opportunity Scheme](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/esos) requires large undertakings (broadly, over 250 employees, or turnover above £44m with a balance sheet above £38m) to audit their energy every four years; Phase 4 assessments fall due by December 2027. The property nuance is that responsibility follows **who buys the energy**. A landlord's ESOS covers the supplies it purchases: central plant and common parts across the portfolio. A tenant's ESOS covers its own supplies in its demises. Consumption a landlord recharges to tenants still counts as the landlord's if the landlord holds the contract. For a fund with an appointed managing agent, the obligation sits with the corporate group that qualifies, not the agent, and one of the commonest ESOS failures in property is simply nobody having assembled the portfolio's supply list. Treat an ESOS deadline as a free forcing function: the data-gathering it compels is exactly the meter map this course's [metering lesson](/courses/commercial-real-estate/metering-and-tenant-billing) wanted anyway.

## SECR: the disclosure that investors read

[Streamlined Energy and Carbon Reporting](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/secr) puts energy use, emissions and an intensity metric into the annual reports of quoted companies and large unquoted ones. For property companies the wrinkle is again attribution: landlord-procured energy appears in the landlord's SECR, and tenant-procured energy in the tenants', so a REIT's disclosed footprint can shrink simply because tenants switched to direct contracts, with no building running any better. Sophisticated investors know this, which is why voluntary frameworks (GRESB, net-zero commitments) push landlords to report whole-building intensity anyway, and why the [operational ratings](/courses/commercial-real-estate/epcs-decs-and-nabers) that measure the base building separately are gaining ground. When you present SECR figures for a property business, always say which side of the lease boundary they cover.

## TM44: the air-conditioning inspection cycle

Any building with air conditioning whose effective rated output exceeds **12 kW** must have it inspected by an accredited assessor at least every **five years**, with the report lodged on the national register. The regime (from the Energy Performance of Buildings regulations, with CIBSE TM44 as the method) is the most ignored obligation in commercial property: compliance has historically been poor because the penalty is modest and no transaction forces it the way a sale forces an EPC. That is a mistake worth correcting, and not only for compliance. A competent TM44 inspection is a subsidised mini-audit of the largest electrical load in the building: oversized plant, dead economisers, fouled coils and absurd setpoints all surface in a decent report. Alongside TM44 sit the F-gas leak-check obligations on the same plant, scaling with refrigerant charge, familiar from the [refrigeration course](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/maintenance-and-faults).

<WorkedExample title="reading a portfolio's obligations at a glance">
  <Given>
    - A property company: 300 employees, 15 multi-let offices, landlord supplies on all
    - Each office has central chillers well above 12 kW output; tenants hold their own electricity contracts for demises
  </Given>
  <Find>Which schemes bite, and on what.</Find>
  <Solution>
    ESOS: qualifies on headcount; must audit the landlord-procured energy across all 15 buildings
    by the Phase 4 deadline (December 2027). Tenants' own supplies are the tenants' problem.

    SECR: if it meets the large-company tests, its annual report discloses the same
    landlord-side energy and emissions, with an intensity metric (kWh/m² is the natural one).

    TM44: every office needs a valid air-conditioning inspection report on a five-year cycle,
    15 reports to track, plus F-gas leak checks on each chiller. Three schemes, one underlying
    dataset: the portfolio meter map.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

<Callout variant="key" title="One dataset serves every scheme">
ESOS wants four-yearly audits, SECR wants annual disclosure, TM44 wants five-yearly inspections, and MEES wants EPCs. Run them as four separate scrambles and each is a cost; run them off one maintained portfolio dataset (supplies, meters, floor areas, plant registers, certificates with expiry dates) and each becomes a report from a system you already keep. The property businesses that find compliance cheap are the ones that treat it as a data-management problem, not a deadline problem.
</Callout>
