If commercial buildings are so data-rich and professionally managed, why are so many of them energy-inefficient? The honest answer is not technical at all. It is that for decades, the person who could fund an improvement was not the person whose bill would fall. Economists call it the split incentive, the property industry calls it the landlord-tenant problem, and it is the single most important idea in this course: until you can answer "who pays, and who saves?" for a measure, you cannot predict whether it will happen, however good its payback looks on paper.

## The problem, in both directions

The split cuts both ways depending on whose meter a load sits behind.

**Landlord invests, tenant saves.** Suppose the lighting in a tenant's demise is the tenant's own supply. If the landlord funded an LED retrofit there, every saved kilowatt-hour lands on the tenant's bill. The landlord gets nothing back, so the landlord doesn't invest, even for a project with a three-year payback that both parties would love to see happen.

**Tenant invests, landlord's asset gains.** Now suppose a tenant with six years left on its lease considers funding better zone controls. Much of the benefit outlives the lease and improves a building the tenant will hand back. Tenants rationally decline to improve someone else's asset.

**Everyone pays, nobody acts.** Central plant sits in the middle: the landlord funds it, the service charge socialises the running cost across all tenants, and any one tenant's careful behaviour changes its own share barely at all. Shared costs blunt everyone's incentive, which is one reason central plant drifts worst of all.

<WorkedExample title="a good project that dies at the lease boundary">
  <Given>
    - LED retrofit across a tenant's 2,000 m² demise, quoted at £60,000
    - It would save 75,000 kWh/yr on the tenant's own electricity supply, worth £15,000/yr at £0.20/kWh
    - The tenant has 3 years left on its lease; the landlord owns the fittings afterwards
  </Given>
  <Find>Why neither party funds a 4-year-payback project.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Simple payback = 60,000 ÷ 15,000 = **4 years**. Perfectly fundable by the standards of the
    [investment-case course](/courses/economic-analysis/simple-payback-revisited).

    For the tenant: only 3 years of savings (£45,000) before lease expiry, so the project loses
    £15,000. For the landlord: it funds £60,000 and receives £0, because the savings land on the
    tenant's meter. The project dies, not because the engineering or the economics failed, but
    because the lease boundary cuts the cash flows in the wrong place.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Green leases: rewiring the incentives

The industry's fix is contractual. A **green lease** adds clauses to the standard commercial lease that make efficiency investable:

- **Cost-recovery clauses** let a landlord recover the cost of an improvement through the service charge *where it demonstrably reduces tenants' total outgoings*, converting "landlord pays, tenant saves" into "tenant repays out of genuine savings".
- **Data-sharing obligations** require both parties to share consumption data, without which whole-building performance can be neither measured nor rated.
- **Standards and cooperation clauses** commit both sides to maintain an EPC band, to consider efficiency at every plant replacement, and to a forum (often quarterly) where building performance is actually discussed.

The Better Buildings Partnership's Green Lease Toolkit, refreshed in 2024, is the UK reference wording, and clauses that were novelties a decade ago are becoming standard in institutional leases.

<Callout variant="key" title="Follow the money before you size the plant">
In owner-occupied buildings, an energy assessment starts with the engineering. In commercial real estate it starts with the lease: whose meter, whose capital, whose saving, how long left on the term. A measure with a 2-year payback and the wrong incentive structure will lose to a 6-year measure that green-lease clauses make recoverable. Learn to read the contractual plumbing first, and the engineering second.
</Callout>

## Where the split is closing

Three forces are squeezing the split incentive. Regulation is making inefficiency the landlord's legal problem regardless of whose bill it is: [MEES](/courses/commercial-real-estate/mees-minimum-standards) can make a poor building unlettable, which converts efficiency into asset value the landlord *does* keep. Investor pressure (net-zero commitments, ESG reporting) does the same through valuations. And operational ratings such as [NABERS UK](/courses/commercial-real-estate/epcs-decs-and-nabers) make whole-building performance visible, so a well-run building can prove it and charge for it. The next module covers those measurement systems in detail.
