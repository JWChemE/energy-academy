Controls fixes harvest the waste; sooner or later the plant itself must change, and in commercial property *when* matters as much as *what*. A chiller lasts 20 years, a boiler 25, a lighting installation 15, and a lease runs 10: retrofit planning in this sector is the art of landing efficiency work on the natural rhythm of leases, voids and plant end-of-life, so the marginal cost of doing better approaches zero. This lesson covers the three big physical opportunities in a typical office (lighting, cooling plant, and the heating conversion question) and the sequencing logic that makes them fundable.

## Sequencing: the lease is the calendar

Three moments make works cheap. **Plant end-of-life**: when the chiller must be replaced anyway, the efficient option costs only the increment, and the [whole-life-cost method](/courses/economic-analysis/total-cost-of-ownership) applies to that increment, not the whole machine. **Voids and refurbishments**: an empty floor is the only cheap time to touch ceilings, distribution and controls zoning. **Lease events**: renewals and re-gears are when [green-lease clauses](/courses/commercial-real-estate/the-split-incentive) enter the contract and when incoming tenants state their fit-out plans, which decide what the central plant must actually serve. A portfolio retrofit plan is therefore a merged timeline of EPC expiries, [MEES deadlines](/courses/commercial-real-estate/mees-minimum-standards), plant ages and lease events, with measures attached to the moments they exploit. The anti-pattern is the panic retrofit: like-for-like replacement after a failure, procured in a week, locking in 1990s efficiency until the 2040s.

## Lighting: still the easy win where it remains

Where original fluorescent installations survive in common parts and older demises, [LED retrofit](/courses/lighting/led-retrofit) remains the most reliable project in buildings: 50 to 70% lighting energy reduction, paybacks commonly 2 to 4 years, minimal disruption, and an EPC improvement that counts toward the MEES trajectory. In multi-let stock the split matters as usual: common-parts lighting is the landlord's (service-charge funded, all tenants benefit), demise lighting is the tenant's, which is exactly the project the split-incentive worked example showed dying at the lease boundary and green-lease cost-recovery clauses reviving.

## Cooling plant: buy efficiency at replacement

Chillers illustrate the end-of-life increment logic perfectly, because efficiency between generations differs enormously and the machine runs for two decades.

<WorkedExample title="the chiller increment">
  <Given>
    - An office's chillers deliver about 300,000 kWh of cooling a year
    - The failing 1990s plant achieves a seasonal COP of about 3.0; a modern replacement with variable-speed compressors achieves about 5.0
    - Electricity £0.20/kWh; the efficient option costs £40,000 more than like-for-like
  </Given>
  <Find>The saving from choosing the efficient machine, and the payback on the increment.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Electricity at COP 3.0 = 300,000 ÷ 3.0 = 100,000 kWh/yr. At COP 5.0 = 300,000 ÷ 5.0 =
    60,000 kWh/yr. Saving = **40,000 kWh, £8,000 a year**.

    Payback on the increment = 40,000 ÷ 8,000 = **5 years** against a 20-year life: the
    efficient chiller earns its premium four times over. Framed as "£40,000 extra" it fights
    for capital; framed as the increment analysis the [investment-case course](/courses/economic-analysis/simple-payback-revisited)
    teaches, it is close to unarguable.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

The same replacement moment is the time to fix the system around the machine: variable-speed pumping, [free cooling](/courses/hvac-systems/free-cooling) where the load profile suits, and heat recovery to any simultaneous heating demand.

## The heating question: heat pumps in offices

Gas boilers are the next end-of-life wave, and the natural successor is the [heat pump](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/heating-modes). Be honest about both sides of the case at current prices. Take a building using 468,660 kWh of gas a year for heating and hot water: at 85% boiler efficiency that is roughly 398,000 kWh of delivered heat. A heat pump at a seasonal COP of 3.0 delivers it for about 132,800 kWh of electricity. At £0.20/kWh versus £0.06/kWh, the running cost is £26,557 against £28,120: a saving of only around £1,500 a year, because electricity's price premium (3.3×) nearly cancels the efficiency gain (3.5×). The carbon story is different: roughly 86 tonnes CO₂e/yr from the gas falls to about 27 tonnes on grid electricity, a two-thirds cut that keeps improving as the grid decarbonises. Heat-pump conversions in offices are therefore currently driven by MEES trajectory, net-zero commitments and asset positioning more than by the energy bill, and they usually ride along with a refurbishment that first *reduces* the heat demand (fabric, controls, lower flow temperatures) so the plant can be smaller and cheaper.

<Callout variant="key" title="Never replace like-for-like without running the increment">
Every plant replacement is a 15-to-25-year decision made under time pressure, and the default outcome is a copy of whatever failed. The discipline that prevents it costs one afternoon: run the increment analysis (efficient option minus default option, against the saving), check the MEES trajectory the building must meet during the new plant's life, and check the lease calendar for a smarter moment. The buildings that hit EPC B affordably by 2031 will be the ones that started doing this at every natural replacement from now on.
</Callout>
