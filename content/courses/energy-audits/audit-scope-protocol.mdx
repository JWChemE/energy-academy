Every disappointing audit shares the same post-mortem: the scope was never pinned down. The client expected the warehouse included; the auditor priced for the offices. The report arrived without the sub-metered analysis finance wanted, or with three weeks of logging the budget never covered. Scope is where an audit succeeds or fails before it begins, and protocols exist so that two competent auditors, given the same site, would produce comparable work. This lesson covers how to define scope precisely and which protocol to follow.

## The five scope decisions

Write these down and get them agreed before any site visit:

- **Boundaries.** Which buildings, which meters, which activities? Is the vehicle fleet in scope? Outdoor lighting? A tenant's floor? The clean answer is a list of meter points and site areas, because meters are unambiguous where descriptions are not.
- **End uses.** All energy, or named systems? "Heating, ventilation, compressed air and lighting" is a scope; "the main energy uses" is a dispute waiting to happen.
- **Depth.** Walk-through, detailed or investment-grade, per the [previous lesson](/courses/energy-audits/why-audit). Depth drives cost more than site size does.
- **Data period.** Twelve months of consumption data is the minimum that captures all seasons; anything shorter needs a stated justification and makes the [baseline](/courses/energy-audits/normalise-baseline) weaker.
- **Deliverables.** The ranked opportunities table, the baseline methodology, an implementation plan, a presentation to the board: name what the client receives, and in what form.

Scope creep is handled the way all professionals handle it: additions are welcome, and they change the price and the programme, in writing, at the time.

## Protocols: borrowed credibility

An audit that follows a recognised protocol inherits its credibility. Three matter in UK practice.

**ISO 50002** is the international standard for energy audits. It defines the process (planning, data collection, site work, analysis, reporting) and the professional requirements, and it is the natural reference for a detailed audit. Its European sibling **EN 16247** is broadly parallel and is the family of standards named in ESOS guidance.

**ESOS compliance** adds specific rules on top: audits must cover at least 90% of total consumption, be overseen by a registered lead assessor, sample sites defensibly across an estate, and land within the scheme's four-year cycle. The [ESOS lesson](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/esos) covers the scheme itself.

**IPMVP** is not an audit protocol but a measurement and verification one: it governs how savings are *proven* after a project. It enters audit scope when the client intends performance contracting, because the audit's baseline must then be built to IPMVP's rules from the start. The [M&V course](/courses/measurement-and-verification) goes deeper.

<Callout variant="key" title="Scope follows the money, not the floor plan">
The 80/20 rule dominates audit planning: on most sites, three or four systems account for the great majority of spend. Scope the depth accordingly. Two days of measurement on the compressed air system that costs £100,000 a year beats two hours on each of twenty systems that cost £2,000 each. Before fixing scope, get the annual consumption by meter and let the money allocate the effort.
</Callout>

## A one-page scope template

In practice a single agreed page prevents most disputes: site and boundaries (as a meter list), systems in scope, depth level and standard followed, data period, site access dates, measurement plan (what gets logged, for how long), savings estimation approach, deliverables and date, price, and the named client contact. Ten minutes to complete; weeks of ambiguity avoided.

With scope agreed, the work begins where every audit should: walking the site with your eyes open, which is the next lesson.
