An audit's scope defines what you're investigating and to what depth. Following a protocol ensures consistency and credibility.

## Defining scope

**System boundaries:** Will you audit just the main building, or include warehouses, outdoor lighting, vehicle fleet? Define this clearly.

**End uses:** Do you care about all energy, or just heating/cooling? Manufacturing and plug loads? Hot water?

**Depth:** Walk-through (quick), detailed (moderate), investment-grade (rigorous).

**Time period:** Audit consumption over what period? A year captures all seasons. A month is risky.

## Audit standards & protocols

**ISO 50002** — Energy audits: principles and requirements. Defines terms, process, and professionalism.

**ESOS protocol** — If auditing for ESOS compliance, follow the standard rules (all significant systems, specific format).

**IPMVP (International Performance Measurement & Verification Protocol)** — If you're proving savings after a retrofit, IPMVP defines how to establish baseline and measure actual performance.

**Local standards** — Some regions have specific requirements (e.g., EU EED directive).

## Scope creep

Define scope up front in writing. If a client asks to add a 3rd building mid-audit, that's a change in scope — time and budget adjustment.

## Checklist for defining scope

- Systems to include (HVAC, lighting, process, hot water, etc.)
- Buildings and areas
- Depth level (walk-through / detailed / investment-grade)
- Measurement points and frequency
- Baseline calculation method (utility bills, sub-meters, regression)
- Savings estimation approach (comparison, engineering calculation, supplier data)
- Report deliverables (executive summary, detailed findings, ranked table, financials)
