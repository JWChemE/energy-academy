Every number in an audit report is a comparison in disguise. "The site uses too much gas" means: more than the weather justifies. "The lighting is inefficient" means: more watts per square metre than good practice needs. Before any opportunity can be quantified, the audit must establish the reference points those comparisons stand on: a normalised baseline of what the site should consume given its conditions, and benchmarks that say how similar sites perform. This lesson builds both.

## The baseline: the site compared with itself

The machinery here is exactly the machinery of the [monitoring and targeting course](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/establishing-baseline), applied at audit speed. Take twelve months of consumption, regress the weather-dependent meters against [degree days](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/normalisation), and read off the base load (the intercept) and the weather sensitivity (the slope). The fit itself is diagnostic before any comparison is made: a strong fit says the building responds to weather consistently; a weak one says something else drives consumption, and finding that something becomes an audit task.

For an audit, the baseline serves three specific jobs. It converts the walk-through's suspicions into sized questions (a 40 kW electrical base load must be accounted for appliance by appliance). It provides the "before" against which every proposed measure's saving is estimated. And it becomes the yardstick the client uses after implementation, which is why its assumptions must be documented well enough for a stranger to reproduce, the standard the [M&V discipline](/courses/measurement-and-verification) formalises.

## Benchmarks: the site compared with its peers

Normalisation answers "is consumption consistent with the weather?"; benchmarking answers "is it consistent with good practice?". The standard currency is **energy intensity**: kWh per square metre per year for buildings, kWh per unit of output for industry. Published references (CIBSE's benchmarking guidance, sector bodies, the [platform's sector courses](/sectors)) tabulate typical and good-practice figures by building type.

<WorkedExample title="benchmarking an office">
  <Given>
    - A 4,000 m² office consumes 600,000 kWh of electricity a year
    - Published typical practice for its type is around 95 kWh/m²; good practice is lower still
    - Electricity £0.20/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The site's intensity and what the gap is worth.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Intensity = 600,000 ÷ 4,000 = **150 kWh/m²**, roughly 58% above typical practice.

    Closing just the gap to typical is worth (150 − 95) × 4,000 = 220,000 kWh, about
    **£44,000 a year**. The benchmark does not say which measures deliver it; it says the
    prize is large and the audit should dig hard. A site near good practice warrants a
    different, lighter audit than one 58% above typical, which is why benchmarking belongs
    early in the analysis.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

Benchmark honestly: match building type, hours and function; state the source and year; and treat benchmarks as compasses rather than verdicts. A data centre in the basement can put a legitimate 40 points on an office's intensity, which is exactly the kind of context the [interviews](/courses/energy-audits/interview-stakeholders) supply.

<Callout variant="key" title="No baseline, no believable savings">
Every savings claim in the final report is the difference between two numbers, and the baseline is always one of them. A weak baseline silently corrupts every recommendation built on it, and it is the first thing a sceptical finance director, or a performance contractor's engineer, will attack. Spend the analysis time here; the opportunities lesson that follows is only as strong as this foundation.
</Callout>

## Handling change during the data period

If the site changed materially inside the twelve months (an extension, a new line, COVID-era occupancy), either model the change explicitly with a second driver, or baseline on the clean period and say so. Never average across a known discontinuity: a baseline that straddles a change describes neither the before nor the after, and recommendations priced against it inherit the confusion.

With the reference points established, the audit's centrepiece follows: converting everything gathered so far into a ranked list of quantified opportunities.
