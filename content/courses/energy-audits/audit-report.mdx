An audit is judged by what gets implemented, and implementation is decided by people who will read three pages of your report at most. The report is therefore not a record of work done; it is a persuasion document with an evidence appendix. This final lesson covers the structure that works, the writing habits that make numbers land, and the follow-through that separates audits that changed a site from audits that furnished a shelf.

## Structure: three documents in one binding

**The executive summary (one to two pages)** is the only part the decision-makers are guaranteed to read, so it must stand entirely alone: what the site spends, how it compares with its peers, the total identified saving, the three biggest opportunities with their paybacks, and a one-line recommended next step. Write it last, and write it in money. "The site spends £250,000 a year on energy, roughly 30% more than typical for its type. This audit identifies £37,500 a year of savings, £18,000 of it from measures repaying within one year."

**The findings and opportunities section (five to fifteen pages)** carries the ranked table from the [previous lesson](/courses/energy-audits/identify-opportunities), then one short entry per measure: what was found (with the photograph), what is proposed, the saving with its method and confidence, the cost basis, and the named owner and constraint from the [interviews](/courses/energy-audits/interview-stakeholders). One page per major measure is a good discipline.

**The appendix** holds everything a technical reviewer needs to reproduce the work: the [baseline regression](/courses/energy-audits/normalise-baseline) and its statistics, logging charts, assumptions and prices used, instrument list, and the scope agreement. Rigour lives here so the front of the report can breathe.

## Writing habits that earn trust

Report in pounds first, kilowatt hours second, carbon alongside. Attach its evidence to every claim ("logging over two weeks showed…", "based on the enclosed regression…"). State confidence honestly, measure by measure. Quantify the do-nothing case, because "current waste: £12,400 a year" reframes inaction as a decision with a price. And keep the tone of a professional adviser rather than a salesman: the conservative-numbers principle from the opportunities lesson applies double in print.

One structural habit repays itself every time: phase the recommendations into a programme (quick wins now, funded projects this budget year, capital measures next cycle) rather than presenting a flat list. Boards approve programmes; lists get "noted".

<Callout variant="key" title="The report's real product is a decision">
Every element above exists to make one meeting go well: the one where someone with budget authority says yes to phase one. Write for that meeting. If the executive summary cannot be presented aloud in five minutes, shorten it. If a recommendation cannot survive the question "how sure are you?", downgrade it or gather more data before publishing. A modest report that triggers action beats an impressive one that triggers a filing decision.
</Callout>

## After the report

The audits that compound in value share three follow-through habits. Present the report in person and leave the meeting with owners and dates against phase one. Hand the baseline to whoever runs the site's [monitoring and targeting](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/why-mt), so savings are tracked against it from day one. And book a six-month review: implementation stalls are normal, and the review meeting is where they un-stall. For measures large enough to matter financially, formal verification against the baseline follows the [M&V discipline](/courses/measurement-and-verification).

That completes the audit process: scope, walk-through, measurement, interviews, baseline, opportunities, report.
