The audit report is your deliverable. It must be clear to non-technical people (finance, executives) while being rigorous.

## Structure

**Executive summary (1–2 pages):**
- Building overview (size, use, occupancy)
- Current consumption (kWh, cost, intensity vs. benchmark)
- Key findings (biggest waste, top 3 opportunities)
- Total potential savings (if all opportunities are implemented)
- Recommendation (start with quick wins)

**Findings (3–5 pages):**
- Baseline methodology and result
- Energy signature (if measurement-based)
- System assessment (heating, cooling, lighting, process)
- Specific inefficiencies and their impacts (e.g., "Failed trap 12: estimated 500 kWh/year loss")

**Opportunities table (1 page):**
- Ranked by payback
- Cost, energy, financial, CO₂ for each
- Simple payback and NPV (if desired)

**Detailed analysis (appendix):**
- Calculations, assumptions, data sources
- Photos, instrument readings
- References (standards, benchmarks)

**Implementation roadmap (1 page):**
- Phasing (Phase 1: quick wins 0–6 months, Phase 2: medium 6–18 months, Phase 3: long-term 2+ years)
- Estimated timeline and team/contractor needs
- Risk and contingencies

## Tone

- **For executives:** Focus on cost and ROI. "This retrofit saves £50k/year for a £150k investment; payback is 3 years."
- **For operators:** Explain how to implement. "Replace failed traps with new ones rated for 5 bar; they should last 5–10 years."
- **For engineers:** Be detailed. "Calculations based on ISO 50002; baseline R² = 0.92; savings confidence interval ±10%."

## Typical length

- Walk-through audit report: 10–20 pages
- Detailed audit: 30–50 pages
- Investment-grade: 50–100 pages (with extensive appendices)

## Follow-up

Submit report, present to client, agree on priorities, set timeline for implementation review.
