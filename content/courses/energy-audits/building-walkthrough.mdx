The walk-through is the part of an audit no instrument can replace: a systematic tour of the site with a torch, a camera, a notebook and a practised eye. Its purpose is not to quantify anything yet. It is to build the list of suspicions that measurement will later confirm or dismiss, and to see the building as it actually operates rather than as its drawings claim. Experienced auditors find a large share of their eventual recommendations in this one day, and this lesson is the method behind that.

## Preparation doubles the value of the day

Before arriving, get the last twelve months of bills and, ideally, half-hourly data; a floor plan marked with plant locations; the maintenance log if one exists; and a named escort who knows where the keys are. Study the consumption data first: if the [half-hourly profile](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/meter-data) shows a heavy overnight load, the walk-through's job is to go and find it. Arriving with questions turns a tour into an investigation.

Timing matters more than most auditors admit. A site at 10 a.m. on a Tuesday shows you its best behaviour. The waste performs after hours, so where the data justifies it, walk the site at night or at the weekend too. What is running that should not be is the single most profitable question in auditing.

<WorkedExample title="the after-hours walkround, priced">
  <Given>
    - An evening walkround finds an 11 kW air handling unit running continuously
    - The area it serves is occupied 12 hours a day, 5 days a week (3,120 h/yr)
    - Electricity £0.20/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The cost of the excess running, and the fix.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Running hours: 8,760 a year against 3,120 needed, so 5,640 excess hours.

    Excess energy = 5,640 × 11 = 62,040 kWh, costing **£12,408 a year**. The fix is a time
    schedule in the building management system, which likely exists already and costs
    nothing to use. Findings of exactly this shape, plant serving nobody, are why
    walk-throughs at unsociable hours pay for themselves many times over.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## A route that misses nothing

Walk the energy, not the corridors: follow each utility from where it enters to where it is used.

**Plant rooms first.** Note boiler and chiller age, nameplate ratings and visible condition; whether pipework and valves are insulated (bare flanges and valves are standard finds); what is running versus what the day's weather justifies; and the setpoints actually dialled in, not the ones in the O&M manual. Photograph every nameplate.

**Then distribution.** Ducts and pipes: insulation condition, obvious leaks, dampers wedged with bits of wood (a genuine classic), abandoned runs still live.

**Then the spaces.** Lighting type and whether it is on in empty rooms; local heaters fighting the air conditioning; blinds down with lights on; windows open above radiators, which usually signals control problems rather than user carelessness.

**Then the ends.** Compressed air drops hissing at the fittings, extract fans running into closed dampers, refrigeration condensers blocked with fluff, hot water at 70 °C where 60 °C would do.

Throughout, talk to whoever you meet; the [interviewing lesson](/courses/energy-audits/interview-stakeholders) formalises this, but the operator's aside ("that chiller has run flat out since the controls chap left") is walk-through gold.

<Callout variant="key" title="Record everything; conclude nothing yet">
The walk-through's discipline is separation of observation from judgement. Photograph, note, and list; do not diagnose on the spot or promise savings to whoever is escorting you. Half of the walk-through's suspicions dissolve under measurement, and the other half get bigger. The output is a prioritised list of hypotheses for the logging plan, which is exactly what the next lesson takes up.
</Callout>
