Meters record what a building does; only people know why. The maintenance engineer knows which chiller has run flat out since a control failure two winters ago. The production supervisor knows the afternoon shift props the loading bay doors open. The finance manager knows which projects died in committee and what killed them. An audit that skips these conversations delivers technically correct recommendations that fail for reasons everyone on site could have named. Interviewing is not a soft extra; it is a data-collection method with its own craft.

## Who to talk to, and what each knows

Four conversations cover most sites. The **facilities or maintenance lead** holds the operational truth: what is broken, what is bodged, what runs continuously because nobody trusts the controls, and the real story behind every setpoint. The **operations manager** owns the patterns: actual working hours, seasonal rhythms, planned changes to shifts or production that would invalidate a naive baseline. The **finance or energy manager** knows the budget cycle, the hurdle rate, the appetite for capital, and the history of past proposals. And the **occupants**, sampled informally, reveal the comfort problems that drive waste: the cold corner that spawned four electric heaters, the meeting room nobody can cool.

Ask open questions and let silence work. "Talk me through a typical Monday for the heating" yields more than "is the heating scheduled?". The most valuable single question, asked of everyone: **"what would you fix first?"** People who live with a building have usually diagnosed it already; nobody has asked them.

## What interviews find that instruments cannot

Three categories of finding come almost exclusively from conversation. **Explanations**: the logger shows the load; the engineer explains that the standby pump runs continuously because its auto-changeover failed in 2023. Without the explanation, the audit recommends investigating what a conversation just solved. **Constraints**: the compressor cannot be scheduled off at weekends because one process needs air on Sunday nights; the correct recommendation becomes a small dedicated compressor, not a timer. **History**: LED lighting was proposed twice and refused because the landlord owns the fittings; the recommendation must address the lease, not the lumens.

A worked illustration of the category: on one common pattern, an auditor's logging shows simultaneous heating and cooling in adjacent zones, worth perhaps £3,000 a year in fighting plant. The instruments prove it is happening. Only the interview reveals why: two departments with independent thermostats and a long-running dispute. The £3,000 fix is a management conversation and a locked setpoint policy, not new plant, and no instrument would ever have written that recommendation.

<Callout variant="key" title="Every recommendation has an owner and an objection; find both early">
For each measure the audit will propose, someone on site will have to approve it, operate it, and live with it, and someone probably has a reason it "won't work here". The interviews are where you meet both. A recommendation drafted with its future owner, and written to answer the known objection, is several times more likely to be implemented, and implementation is the only metric an audit is ultimately judged on.
</Callout>

## Practical craft

Book interviews in advance rather than ambushing people mid-shift, and hold them early in the audit so they can direct the [measurement plan](/courses/energy-audits/measurement-and-logging). Take verbatim notes: "the boiler house is a sauna even in June" is evidence with a location attached. Promise anonymity where the culture needs it; the person who tells you the night shift's habits should not be identifiable in the report. And close each interview by testing your emerging findings ("if we proposed scheduling the AHUs, what would break?"), which converts your interviewees from subjects into co-authors, with all the buy-in that implies.

With observations, measurements and testimony collected, the next lesson assembles them into the analytical core of the audit: a normalised baseline that says what the site *should* use.
