Measurement and data collection are the heart of an audit. Without good data, your analysis is guessing.

## What to measure

**Mandatory:**
- Utility consumption (electricity kWh, gas m³, oil litres)
- Utility cost/rate
- Weather (outdoor temperature for baseline)

**Highly recommended:**
- Sub-metering on major loads (HVAC, lighting, process)
- Thermal power (boiler output, chiller output) if not sub-metered
- Equipment runtime (is the system on all night?)

**Optional (for detailed audits):**
- Indoor temperature and humidity (comfort, control quality)
- Equipment voltage/current (power quality, efficiency)
- Equipment runtime logs from BMS if available

## Measurement tools

- **Power meter** (handheld clamp-on): ~£50–300, measures AC current/voltage/power
- **Data logger** (wireless, sub-meter): £200–1,000, logs kWh every 15 min–hourly, uploads to cloud
- **Thermometer** (non-contact infrared): £20–100, surface temperature
- **Anemometer**: ~£100, air velocity in ducts

For a detailed audit, invest in 2–4 data loggers on major systems.

## Frequency

- **Monthly (or daily from smart meter)**: minimum for trending
- **Hourly or 30-min intervals**: much better; reveals daily/weekly patterns
- **Real-time**: excellent for spotting anomalies

## Logging duration

- **Baseline:** 12 months (captures all seasons and weekly/seasonal patterns)
- **Proof of savings:** at least 3–12 months post-retrofit (to account for seasonal variation)

## Accuracy

- Data loggers: ±1–2% accuracy (good enough)
- Utility bills: ±0.5% (very accurate, but monthly resolution)
- Portable meters: ±2–5% (acceptable for spot checks)

## Quality checks

Before analysis:
- Verify cumulative meter readings against utility bills
- Check for missing or obviously erroneous data (zero readings, huge spikes)
- Interpolate small gaps (1–2 days) rather than losing the whole month
