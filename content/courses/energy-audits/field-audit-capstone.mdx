You've learned the parts of an audit separately: why we audit, how to scope it, what to look for on site, how to normalise data, and how to write it up. This capstone joins them into one job. You take a single real client, **Frostfield Foods**, from the first phone call to the final report, and you're scored on the whole chain: not just the arithmetic.

The goal is simple: by the end you should be confident to **plan, set up, organise and execute** an energy audit, and defend every number in it.

A client doesn't lose faith because one calculation was slightly off. ## How it works

The simulator walks five stages, mirroring a real engagement:

1. **Plan & scope**: pick the right level of audit for the client's goal and budget, decide what to request before you arrive, and sequence the job.
2. **On-site**: separate genuine findings from red herrings, choose the right instrument for each measurement, and ask the operator the questions the meters can't answer.
3. **Analyse & normalise**: the data work. Degree-days, a regression baseline, weather-normalised comparison, energy intensity, and the production-normalisation trap that catches most people.
4. **Opportunities**: quantify each measure (kWh, £, payback, CO₂) and prioritise them.
5. **Report & M&V**: what belongs in a credible, decision-ready report, and the pitfalls that get savings claims torn apart.

The calculations use the same engine as your other capstones: type your answer and check it, or hit **"I'm stuck"** for the method then a numeric nudge. Using help means it won't count as a clean pass, retry unaided to score full marks.

## The data work, in context

Stage 3 is the heart of it, and it's built on what you learned in [Normalise Data & Establish Baseline](/courses/energy-audits/normalise-baseline):

- **Degree-days** turn "it was a cold month" into a number you can divide by.
- The **regression slope** (kWh per HDD) is the building's heating sensitivity; the **intercept** is its weather-independent base load.
- **Weather-normalising** a month lets you tell a real saving from a mild winter: the Frostfield controls tweak looks like a win until you normalise it.
- **Specific energy consumption (kWh/tonne)** is the trap: Frostfield's electricity bill rose only a little, but once you divide by the lower production, efficiency clearly got *worse*. Pick the right normaliser for the site, for a factory that's usually production, not floor area.

## After you finish

For each stage, ask the questions a lead auditor would:

- Did I scope to what the client needs to *decide*, or over- or under-cook it?
- On site, did I chase the bankable findings, or get distracted?
- Could I defend every saving with a normalised baseline and stated assumptions?
- Did I sequence the recommendations so the quick win funds the capital projects?
