An **energy audit** is a systematic investigation of how a building or facility uses energy. It identifies inefficiencies and quantifies the business case for improvements.

## Why audit?

1. **Invisible waste** — You can't see energy being wasted. An audit reveals it with data.
2. **Prioritisation** — Not all improvements are equal. An audit ranks by cost-effectiveness (payback, NPV, CO₂).
3. **Investment proof** — Before spending £50k on a project, audit-backed analysis proves the return.
4. **Compliance** — ESOS (large undertakings) and SECR (large organisations) require audits and energy-reduction reporting.

## Types of audit

**Walk-through audit** (4–8 hours): Quick visual inspection. Identifies obvious waste (lights always on, poor insulation, old equipment). Cost: £500–2,000. Good for: getting started, screening.

**Detailed audit** (1–2 weeks): More measurement and analysis. Submeters on major systems, baseline calculation, quantified recommendations. Cost: £5,000–15,000. Good for: mid-size buildings, justified projects.

**Investment-grade audit** (2–4 weeks): Rigorous; justifies large capital (say, £100k+). Multiple measurement points, statistical confidence, defensible savings estimates. Cost: £15,000–50,000+. Good for: major retrofits, financing, performance contracts.

## Who needs an audit?

- Any building consuming significant energy (offices, factories, hospitals, etc.)
- Buildings over 10 years old
- Organisations meeting ESOS or SECR thresholds
- Anyone planning energy improvements
- Organisations with net-zero or carbon-reduction targets

Most organisations start with a walk-through, then do detailed audits on priority areas.

## The audit outcome

A report showing:
- Current energy consumption (kWh, GJ) and cost
- Baseline (what consumption "should" be accounting for weather/occupancy)
- Identified opportunities ranked by payback (cost / annual savings)
- Estimated savings for each (kWh, cost, tonnes CO₂)
- Recommended implementation sequence
- Business case (cash flow, ROI, payback period)
