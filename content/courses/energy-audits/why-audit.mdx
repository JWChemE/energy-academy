An energy audit is a structured investigation of where a site's energy goes, what it costs, and which improvements would pay. It is the foundation activity of the whole profession: almost every successful efficiency project starts life as a line in an audit report. Done well, an audit replaces opinion ("the boilers are probably fine") with evidence ("boiler 2 runs at 78% efficiency and a £3,000 tune-up would recover £4,500 a year"). This lesson covers why audits earn their keep, the three depths of audit and when each is appropriate, and what a good one actually delivers.

## What an audit is for

Energy waste is invisible. Nobody can see the 20% of compressed air escaping through leaks, or feel that a building's heating runs three hours longer than its occupancy justifies. Waste declares itself only through data, and the audit is the exercise that goes looking.

Four motives justify the effort. The first is money: audits of sites that have never had one routinely identify savings of 10 to 20% of annual spend. The second is prioritisation: a site can only fund a few projects a year, and the audit ranks them so capital flows to the best returns first. The third is proof: a board asked to spend £50,000 wants evidence, and an audit provides the numbers an investment case is built on. The fourth is compliance: [ESOS Phase 4](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/esos) makes audits a legal requirement for large undertakings every four years.

  <Given>
    - A manufacturing site spends £250,000 a year on energy and has never been audited
    - A detailed audit costs £10,000

  </Given>
  <Find>The identified savings and the audit's effective payback.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Identified savings ≈ £250,000 × 0.15 = **£37,500 a year** across the ranked measures.

    The audit pays for itself in £10,000 ÷ £37,500 × 12 ≈ **3.2 months**, before a single
    project is even implemented. The projects themselves then carry their own paybacks.
    Few investments an organisation can make return faster than finding out where its
    energy goes.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## The three depths

Audits come in three levels of rigour, and choosing the right one matters more than choosing the auditor.

A **walk-through audit** takes a day or less: a systematic tour, the last year's bills, and a short list of obvious findings. It costs little and is the right screening tool for small sites or as the first pass across a large estate.

A **detailed audit** takes one to three weeks: measurement and logging on the major systems, a normalised baseline, and quantified recommendations with costs and paybacks. This is the standard product for a site with meaningful spend, and it is what this course teaches you to deliver.

An **investment-grade audit** exists to support large capital decisions and third-party financing. Its savings estimates carry stated confidence bounds, its baseline follows a formal protocol such as IPMVP, and its cost (often £15,000 upwards) is justified only when the project it supports is large enough to need bankable numbers.

<Callout variant="key" title="An audit is a ranked list, not a report">
The deliverable that matters is a table: every opportunity, each with its cost, annual saving, payback and carbon impact, ranked so the first item is the best use of the next pound. Everything else in the report exists to make that table credible. If an audit ends with observations rather than a ranked, quantified list, it was a site visit, not an audit.
</Callout>

## How this course is organised

The lessons that follow walk the audit process in delivery order: defining scope and choosing a protocol, the walk-through itself, measurement and logging, interviewing the people who run the building, normalising the baseline, turning findings into quantified opportunities, and writing the report that gets them funded. The [field audit capstone](/courses/energy-audits/field-audit-capstone) then puts you through a full simulated audit with real numbers.
