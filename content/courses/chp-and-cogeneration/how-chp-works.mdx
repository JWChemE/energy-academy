Combined heat and power (CHP), or cogeneration, generates electricity on site and captures the heat that would otherwise be wasted. By using one fuel input to produce two useful outputs, it reaches overall efficiencies that conventional separate supply cannot, but only when the recovered heat is actually used, which is the single condition that decides whether a CHP scheme is a triumph or an expensive mistake. This lesson covers the efficiency argument, how the heat is captured, and the heat-led rule that governs everything else in the course.

## The efficiency argument

A central power station converts only 35 to 50% of its fuel into electricity; the rest leaves as heat through the cooling towers, and a further 5 to 8% is lost in transmission before it reaches your site. Burning gas in your own boiler is 85 to 90% efficient at making heat, but produces no power. CHP sits between the two and captures both outputs from one fuel. A typical reciprocating-engine CHP unit delivers roughly:

- 35 to 42% of fuel energy as electricity
- 40 to 50% as recoverable heat
- Only 10 to 15% lost

That gives an **overall efficiency of 80 to 90%** from a single fuel input, far better than generating power centrally and heat locally as two separate processes. The saving is real fuel: producing the same electricity and heat separately would burn considerably more.

## How the heat is captured

In an engine-based CHP unit, useful heat is recovered from several streams at different grades:

- **Exhaust gas**, the hottest, at around 400 to 500 °C
- **Engine jacket cooling water**, at around 80 to 90 °C
- **Lubricating oil and intercooler circuits**, lower grade

These feed a heat-exchanger network producing hot water, typically at 70 to 90 °C, for space heating, domestic hot water, or process use, the same [heat-recovery](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/economics-and-matching) logic applied to a generator's waste streams.

## The key principle: heat-led operation

CHP only delivers its efficiency advantage when the recovered heat is used. If the heat is dumped to a radiator because nothing needs it, you are simply running an expensive, inefficient generator that would have been beaten by importing grid power. This is why CHP is normally sized and operated to follow the *heat* demand, not the electrical demand, the defining design rule explored in the [heat-demand lesson](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/heat-demand).

<Callout variant="key" title="No heat sink, no CHP">
The one question that decides a CHP scheme is not "how efficient is the unit?" but "is there a genuine, year-round demand for the heat it produces?" A unit whose heat is used runs at 85%+ overall efficiency and beats separate supply comfortably; the identical unit dumping half its heat runs closer to 55% and loses money. Everything else in this course, sizing, integration, economics, ultimately serves that one requirement: keep the recovered heat useful for as many hours as possible.
</Callout>

Good CHP hosts therefore have a large, steady, year-round heat demand running alongside continuous electrical use: hospitals, leisure centres with pools, hotels, universities, district heating schemes, and many industrial processes. The more hours a year the heat is genuinely needed, the stronger the case, which the next lesson turns into measurable metrics.
