Combined heat and power (CHP), or cogeneration, generates electricity on site and captures the heat that would otherwise be wasted. By using one fuel input to produce two useful outputs, it reaches efficiencies that conventional separate supply cannot.

## The efficiency argument

A central power station converts only 35-50% of its fuel into electricity; the rest leaves as heat up the cooling towers, and a further 5-8% is lost in transmission. Burning gas in your own boiler is 85-90% efficient for heat, but produces no power.

CHP sits between the two. A typical reciprocating-engine CHP unit delivers:
- 35-42% of fuel energy as electricity
- 40-50% as recoverable heat
- Only 10-15% lost

That gives an **overall efficiency of 80-90%** from a single fuel input - far better than generating power centrally and heat locally as separate processes.

## How the heat is captured

In an engine-based CHP unit, useful heat is recovered from several streams:
- **Exhaust gas** (the hottest, around 400-500 C)
- **Engine jacket cooling water** (around 80-90 C)
- **Lubricating oil and intercooler circuits** (lower grade)

These feed a heat exchanger network producing hot water, typically at 70-90 C, for space heating, domestic hot water, or process use.

## The key principle: heat-led operation

CHP only delivers its efficiency advantage when the recovered heat is actually used. If the heat is dumped, you are simply running an expensive, inefficient generator. This is why CHP is normally sized and operated to follow the heat demand, not the electrical demand - the defining design rule explored later in this course.

## Where CHP fits

Good CHP hosts have a large, steady, year-round heat demand running alongside continuous electrical use: hospitals, leisure centres with pools, hotels, universities, district heating schemes, and many industrial processes. The more hours per year the heat is genuinely needed, the stronger the case.
