CHP has historically been promoted as a low-carbon technology, but whether it actually cuts emissions today depends entirely on what it displaces. As the grid decarbonises, the carbon case has shifted dramatically.

## The traditional carbon argument

CHP saves carbon by using fuel more efficiently than separate generation. When the grid was dominated by coal and gas (high carbon intensity), generating power on site at 80-90% overall efficiency clearly beat importing high-carbon grid electricity plus burning gas for heat.

## How grid decarbonisation changed everything

The carbon saving from CHP equals the grid emissions it displaces minus the emissions of the gas it burns. The key variable is the **grid carbon intensity**:

- In 2012 the UK grid averaged around 500 gCO2/kWh - gas CHP easily beat it.
- By the mid-2020s the grid fell below 200 gCO2/kWh and continues to drop as renewables grow.

A gas engine generating at 38% efficiency emits roughly 480-530 gCO2 per kWh of electricity (before heat credit). Once the grid is cleaner than this, the **electricity** from gas CHP is more carbon-intensive than the grid it replaces. The heat credit still helps, but the overall carbon advantage shrinks and can vanish.

## Counting it properly

A fair carbon assessment compares:
- **With CHP:** emissions from all the gas burned
- **Without CHP:** grid emissions for the same electricity + boiler emissions for the same heat

Crucially, use **marginal** or forward-looking grid intensity over the asset's 15-year life, not today's average. A unit installed now will spend most of its life competing against an ever-cleaner grid.

## Where gas CHP still cuts carbon

- High-grade-heat industrial processes that would otherwise burn gas directly anyway
- Sites displacing oil or coal heat
- Applications with very high heat utilisation and limited electrification options

## The renewable route

The carbon future of CHP lies in its fuel. Running engines on **biogas** (from anaerobic digestion) or **biomethane** transforms the assessment - the recovered energy is renewable, and such schemes can be genuinely low-carbon or near-neutral. **Hydrogen-ready** engines offer another path as low-carbon hydrogen emerges.

## The verdict

Treat gas CHP as a transitional technology. Assess each scheme against the decarbonising grid over its whole life, value the heat displacement honestly, and look hard at renewable fuels. A scheme that stacks up on cost may no longer stack up on carbon - and credible net-zero planning must test both.
