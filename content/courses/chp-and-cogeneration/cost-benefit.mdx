A CHP scheme is a major capital investment that earns its return over many years. The financial case rests on one central idea: the value of the energy it produces versus the cost of the fuel it burns.

## The spark spread

The heart of CHP economics is the **spark spread** - the gap between what the electricity is worth and what the gas costs to make it.

For each kWh of electricity generated at, say, 38% efficiency, the unit burns 1/0.38 = 2.6 kWh of gas. But that gas also yields useful heat that displaces boiler fuel. The net saving per operating hour is:

(electricity value displaced) + (heat value displaced) - (fuel cost) - (maintenance cost)

A wide spark spread - expensive grid electricity, cheap gas - makes CHP highly profitable. A narrow spread can make even a technically excellent unit lose money.

## A worked example

A 200 kWe / 300 kWth gas engine:
- Runs 7,000 hours/year
- Generates 1,400 MWh electricity, displacing grid power at 0.20 GBP/kWh = 280,000 GBP
- Recovers 2,100 MWh heat, displacing gas heat (at 85% boiler efficiency) worth around 124,000 GBP
- Burns gas: total fuel around 3,680 MWh at 0.06 GBP/kWh = 221,000 GBP
- Maintenance at around 0.012 GBP/kWhe = 17,000 GBP

Net annual benefit: roughly 166,000 GBP. Against a capital cost of around 300,000-400,000 GBP, that is a payback of about 2-2.5 years.

## Capital and lifecycle costs

Beyond the unit itself, budget for:
- Installation, hydraulic and electrical connection, acoustic enclosure
- The thermal store and controls
- Grid-connection (G99) protection and any export agreement
- Ongoing maintenance contracts (often per-kWh "operations and maintenance" deals)
- Major overhauls at intervals (engine top-end and full rebuilds over the life)

A whole-life view over 10-15 years, using NPV and IRR rather than simple payback, gives the honest picture - the techniques from the Economic Analysis course apply directly.

## What kills the case

- **Too few run hours** (heat not needed enough of the year)
- **A narrow spark spread** (cheap grid power or expensive gas)
- **Dumped heat** (poor sizing or integration)
- **Falling grid prices** over the asset life

Sensitivity analysis on energy prices and run hours is essential, because the result swings widely with both.
