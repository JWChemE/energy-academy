Sizing CHP well demands a real understanding of how a site uses both heat and power across the day and the year. Annual totals hide the patterns that make or break a scheme: two buildings with identical yearly energy can suit CHP completely differently depending on when they use it. This lesson covers why profiles beat totals, how to build them, why coincidence matters, and how absorption cooling can rescue a summer-idle scheme.

## Why profiles, not totals

One building with steady, round-the-clock demand is an ideal CHP host; another with sharp daytime peaks and idle nights is a poor one, even with the same annual consumption. Only the **load profile**, demand plotted against time, reveals which you have. To build one, gather half-hourly data for both heat and electricity: electricity from [half-hourly meter data](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/meter-data), heat from boiler-house heat meters or inferred from gas use and boiler efficiency. Then plot daily profiles for representative days (summer weekday, winter weekday, weekend) and the annual [load-duration curves](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/heat-demand), looking for the size and duration of the base load, how well heat and electricity demand coincide, and the seasonal swing.

## Coincidence matters

CHP produces heat and power together, at the same time, so its value is highest when the site needs both simultaneously. A site that needs heat at night but power by day gets less benefit unless a [thermal store](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/system-integration) or grid export bridges the gap. Overlaying the two profiles shows how often generation and demand actually coincide, which is what determines the real self-consumption of both outputs.

## The summer problem, and absorption cooling

Heat demand typically collapses in summer while electrical demand stays flat or rises with cooling. A heat-led unit therefore runs hard in winter and may shut down in summer, unless there is a summer heat sink. Finding a year-round heat use is often what lifts a marginal scheme into a viable one, and one option turns the problem into the solution.

<Callout variant="key" title="Absorption cooling turns summer heat into a load">
An **absorption chiller** uses CHP heat to drive cooling, converting summer's surplus heat into useful air-conditioning. This gives a heat-led unit a year-round heat sink, dramatically increasing its run hours, and it is the basis of **trigeneration** (combined cooling, heat and power). On a site with significant summer cooling demand, adding absorption cooling can be the difference between a CHP unit that shuts down for a quarter of the year and one that runs profitably through it. Where the summer heat sink is a pool, a process load or absorption cooling, the scheme's run hours (and its whole economic case) transform.
</Callout>

## From profile to specification

With profiles in hand, the specification falls out in order: the base-load heat demand sets the CHP thermal size, the prime mover's [power-to-heat ratio](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/efficiency-metrics) then fixes its electrical output, and the load-duration curve confirms the run hours that underpin the [financial case](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/cost-benefit). Skip the profile work and the sizing is guesswork, which is why disappointing CHP schemes so often trace back to a sizing done on annual totals rather than half-hourly patterns.
