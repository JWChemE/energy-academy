Sizing CHP well demands a real understanding of how a site uses both heat and power across the day and year. Annual totals hide the patterns that make or break a scheme.

## Why profiles, not totals

Two buildings can use identical annual energy yet suit CHP very differently. One with steady, round-the-clock demand is ideal; one with sharp daytime peaks and idle nights is poor. Only the **load profile** - demand plotted against time - reveals which you have.

## Building the profiles

Gather half-hourly data for both heat and electricity:
- **Electricity:** from half-hourly meter data, readily available for larger sites.
- **Heat:** from boiler-house heat meters, or inferred from gas consumption and boiler efficiency.

Plot daily profiles for representative days (summer weekday, winter weekday, weekend) and the annual load-duration curves. Look for:
- The size and duration of the base load
- How well heat and electricity demand coincide in time
- Seasonal swing between summer and winter

## Coincidence matters

CHP produces heat and power together, at the same time. The value is highest when the site needs both simultaneously. A site that needs heat at night but power by day gets less benefit unless a thermal store or grid export bridges the gap. Overlaying the two profiles shows how often generation and demand actually coincide.

## Seasonal swing

Heat demand typically collapses in summer while electrical demand stays flat or rises (cooling). A heat-led unit therefore runs hard in winter and may shut down in summer - unless there is a summer heat sink such as domestic hot water, a pool, an absorption chiller, or a process load. Identifying a year-round heat use is often what lifts a marginal scheme into a viable one.

## Absorption cooling: turning summer heat into a load

An **absorption chiller** uses CHP heat to drive cooling. This converts summer's surplus heat into useful air-conditioning, giving the unit a year-round heat sink and dramatically increasing run hours - the basis of "trigeneration" (combined cooling, heat, and power).

## From profile to specification

With profiles in hand, the base-load heat demand sets the CHP thermal size; the prime mover's power-to-heat ratio then determines its electrical output; and the load-duration curve confirms the run hours that underpin the financial case in the next module.
