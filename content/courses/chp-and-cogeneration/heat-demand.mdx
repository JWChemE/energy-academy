The golden rule of CHP sizing: size to the heat demand, not the electrical demand. Get this wrong and the most efficient technology becomes a money pit.

## Why heat leads

CHP earns its efficiency only when its heat is used. If you size a unit to meet peak electrical demand, it will often produce more heat than the building needs, and that surplus heat is dumped through a radiator - turning the unit into an inefficient, expensive generator. Sizing to heat ensures (almost) every unit of recovered heat displaces real boiler fuel.

Any electrical shortfall is simply topped up from the grid; any small electrical surplus can be exported. Electricity is easy to trade in both directions. Heat is not - dumped heat is pure waste.

## Reading the heat load-duration curve

The essential tool is the **annual heat load-duration curve**: the site's hourly heat demand sorted from highest to lowest across the 8,760 hours of the year. It reveals:
- The peak demand (met by boilers, not CHP)
- The **base load** - the demand present for most of the year
- How many hours each level of demand persists

CHP is sized to the base load, where demand is continuous, so the unit runs flat-out for thousands of hours.

## The base-load sweet spot

A common rule of thumb sizes the CHP unit to roughly the heat demand that is present for at least 4,500-5,000 hours per year. This typically corresponds to a unit covering 10-20% of peak heat capacity but supplying 40-60% of annual heat energy, because it runs almost constantly while boilers handle the peaks.

Oversizing is the classic mistake: a larger unit produces surplus heat in spring and autumn, forcing it to modulate down or dump heat, slashing run hours and ruining the payback.

## Worked illustration

A leisure centre has a 1,000 kW peak heat demand but a steady 200 kW base load (pool heating, hot water) present nearly 24/7. A 200 kWth CHP unit runs around the clock, achieving 7,000+ run hours and high heat utilisation. A 600 kW unit would meet more peak demand but spend much of the year dumping heat - a worse investment despite the bigger nameplate.

## Thermal store as a buffer

A hot-water buffer store smooths short-term mismatches: the CHP charges the store when heat demand dips, then runs steadily rather than stopping and starting. This lifts run hours and lets a single well-sized unit ride through demand fluctuations.
