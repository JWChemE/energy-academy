The golden rule of CHP sizing is one sentence: size to the heat demand, not the electrical demand. Get this wrong and the most efficient technology becomes a money pit, because a unit sized for peak electricity produces surplus heat it has to dump. This lesson explains why heat leads, how to read the heat load-duration curve, where the base-load sweet spot sits, and how a thermal store buffers the mismatch.

## Why heat leads

CHP earns its efficiency only when its heat is used. Size a unit to meet peak *electrical* demand and it will often produce more heat than the building needs, and that surplus is dumped through a radiator, turning the unit into the inefficient, expensive generator the [first lesson](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/how-chp-works) warned about. Size to *heat*, and almost every unit of recovered heat displaces real boiler fuel. Any electrical shortfall is simply topped up from the grid, and any small surplus can be exported, because electricity is easy to trade in both directions. Heat is not: dumped heat is pure waste.

## Reading the heat load-duration curve

The essential tool is the **annual heat load-duration curve**: the site's hourly heat demand sorted from highest to lowest across the 8,760 hours of the year. It reveals the peak demand (met by boilers, not CHP), the **base load** present for most of the year, and how many hours each level of demand persists. CHP is sized to the base load, where demand is continuous, so the unit runs flat out for thousands of hours rather than cycling.

## The base-load sweet spot

A common rule of thumb sizes the CHP unit to roughly the heat demand present for at least 4,500 to 5,000 hours a year. This typically corresponds to a unit covering only 10 to 20% of peak heat capacity but supplying 40 to 60% of annual heat *energy*, because it runs almost constantly while boilers handle the short peaks.

<WorkedExample title="sizing to base load, not peak">
  <Given>
    - A leisure centre with a 1,000 kW peak heat demand
    - A steady 200 kW base load (pool heating, hot water) present nearly 24/7
  </Given>
  <Find>Why a 200 kWth unit beats a 600 kWth one.</Find>
  <Solution>
    A 200 kWth CHP unit runs around the clock against the base load, achieving 7,000+ run
    hours and high heat utilisation, so almost all its heat is used. A 600 kWth unit would
    meet more of the peak, but through spring and autumn it would produce far more heat
    than the building wants, forcing it to modulate down or dump heat, slashing its run
    hours and ruining the payback. The smaller unit is the better investment *despite* the
    smaller nameplate, because run hours and heat utilisation, not capacity, drive the
    return. Oversizing is the classic CHP mistake.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

<Callout variant="key" title="A thermal store buys run hours">
A hot-water buffer store is the single most valuable addition to a CHP scheme, because it smooths short-term mismatches: the unit charges the store when demand dips, then runs steadily instead of stopping and starting. Stop-start cycling wrecks engine life and efficiency, so the store lets a single well-sized unit ride through demand fluctuations and lifts its annual run hours, exactly the [thermal-storage](/courses/thermal-energy-storage/water-tanks) buffering principle applied to CHP. Size the unit to the base load, then add a store to protect its run hours; the two decisions together make or break the scheme.
</Callout>
