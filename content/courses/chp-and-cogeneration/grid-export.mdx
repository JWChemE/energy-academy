Most building-scale CHP is designed to use all its power on site, but understanding grid connection and export is essential - both to stay legal and to capture value where it exists.

## Why on-site use beats export

A unit of electricity used on site displaces imported power at the **retail price** (perhaps 0.15-0.25 GBP/kWh including network and policy costs). The same unit exported earns only the **wholesale price** (often 0.05-0.10 GBP/kWh). The value of self-consumption is two to four times that of export.

This is why building CHP is normally sized to the base electrical load - so generation is always fully absorbed - rather than to peak demand, which would create surplus to export at low value.

## Connection requirements

Any generator running in parallel with the grid must meet the network operator's requirements:
- **G99 protection** (UK) automatically disconnects the unit if the grid fails or goes out of limits, preventing dangerous "islanding" that could energise supposedly-dead lines.
- A **connection agreement** with the Distribution Network Operator (DNO) sets the maximum export capacity.
- Larger schemes may need network reinforcement, which can be costly and slow.

Engaging the DNO early is essential - connection terms can change a project's viability.

## Metering and revenue

Where export is worthwhile, an **export meter** records power sent to the grid. Revenue routes include:
- A **Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)** with a supplier who buys the exported power
- The Smart Export Guarantee or equivalent for smaller units
- Selling flexibility or balancing services for larger, controllable plant

## Operating to the market

CHP with a thermal store can shift its running to the most valuable hours - generating hard when grid prices and demand charges peak, easing off when they fall, while the store keeps heat supply steady. On half-hourly tariffs this load-shifting can add materially to the return, turning the unit into a flexible asset rather than a baseload machine.

## The bigger picture

As the grid decarbonises and wholesale prices grow more volatile, the value of controllable on-site generation that can respond to price signals is rising. CHP that can flex - especially on biogas or hydrogen - has a role beyond simple energy cost savings.
