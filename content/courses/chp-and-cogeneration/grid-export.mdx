Most building-scale CHP is designed to use all its power on site, but understanding grid connection and export is essential both to stay legal and to capture value where it exists. The core insight is simple: a unit of electricity is worth far more used on site than exported, and that fact shapes how CHP is sized and operated. This lesson covers why self-consumption wins, the connection requirements, export revenue routes, and operating to the market.

## Why on-site use beats export

A unit of electricity used on site displaces imported power at the **retail price**, perhaps £0.15 to £0.25/kWh including network and policy costs. The same unit exported earns only the **wholesale price**, often £0.05 to £0.10/kWh. Self-consumption is worth two to four times as much as export, which is why building CHP is normally sized to the base *electrical* load, so that generation is always fully absorbed on site, rather than to peak demand, which would create surplus to export at low value. This mirrors the same self-consumption logic that governs [on-site solar](/courses/renewable-energy/pv-sizing).

## Connection requirements

Any generator running in parallel with the grid must meet the network operator's requirements:

- **G99 protection** automatically disconnects the unit if the grid fails or goes out of limits, preventing dangerous "islanding" that could energise supposedly dead lines and endanger engineers.
- A **connection agreement** with the Distribution Network Operator (DNO) sets the maximum export capacity.
- Larger schemes may need network reinforcement, which can be costly and slow.

Engaging the DNO early is essential, because connection terms can change a project's viability before a single unit is generated.

## Metering and revenue

Where export is worthwhile, an **export meter** records power sent to the grid, and revenue routes include a **Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)** with a supplier who buys the exported power, the Smart Export Guarantee or equivalent for smaller units, and, for larger controllable plant, selling flexibility or balancing services.

<Callout variant="key" title="A CHP with a store is a flexible asset, not just a baseload machine">
CHP paired with a [thermal store](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/system-integration) can shift its running to the most valuable hours: generating hard when grid prices and [demand charges](/courses/motors-and-drives/demand-management) peak, easing off when they fall, while the store keeps the heat supply steady. On half-hourly tariffs this load-shifting can add materially to the return, turning the unit from a machine that simply runs into a controllable asset that responds to price. As the grid decarbonises and wholesale prices grow more volatile, the value of dispatchable, price-responsive on-site generation is rising, especially for CHP that can flex on [biogas or hydrogen](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/carbon-assessment).
</Callout>

The value of flexibility points to CHP's future, but its present is judged on cost and carbon, the subjects of the final two lessons.
