You've learned how CHP turns one fuel into two useful outputs, why it must be sized and run to the heat demand, and how its economics and carbon case really work. This capstone puts you on site as the auditor — not to admire a humming engine, but to work out whether it's actually earning its keep, and to fix it when it isn't.

The hard truth about CHP is that a unit can look perfectly healthy — high nameplate efficiency, running smoothly — and still be a poor investment. The faults here are rarely a broken machine; they're dumped heat, the wrong size, a closed spark spread, low-value export, or a carbon case that the grid has quietly overtaken.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate** — read the brief and the panel: fuel in, electricity out, heat recovered and used, temperatures, prices, run hours.
2. **Calculate** — work out the figure that decides it: the *real* overall efficiency once dumped heat is excluded, the net £/hour of the spark spread, the value given away on export, the lost heat recovery, or the carbon balance. The **reference data is on hand**; the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose** — pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe** — build an ordered work plan (operational fixes first, then capital).
5. **Verify** — score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="Heat used, not heat made">
Almost every CHP fault traces back to one principle: **the efficiency only exists when the heat is genuinely used.** Dumped heat, a unit that can only run in winter, one that short-cycles for want of a buffer — all of them are heat-utilisation failures wearing different clothes. Size to the heat base load, run heat-led, give it a year-round heat sink, and only then worry about the electrical and carbon sides.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **Heat utilisation** — spotting the dump radiator and proving the "86% efficient" unit is really at 60%.
- **Economics** — the spark spread, and why a technically excellent unit can still lose money when gas rises and grid power falls.
- **Export value** — why a unit sized to the electrical peak gives away value every hour, and why "export more" is the wrong answer.
- **Sizing & run hours** — seasonal heat demand, capacity factor, and the absorption-chiller rescue (trigeneration).
- **Maintenance** — telling a fouled heat exchanger (thermal side) from a worn engine (electrical side) by which efficiency has dropped.
- **Carbon** — running the with/without comparison against a decarbonising grid, and why gas CHP is now a transitional technology.

## Differential thinking

The same symptom rarely has one cause. Boilers running more than they should might be a fouled heat exchanger — or a worn engine — or heat being dumped: the decisive clue is *which* efficiency has moved and *where the heat is going* (the exhaust temperature, the dump radiator, the electrical efficiency). A unit that won't pay back might be the wrong size, short-cycling, or simply hosted somewhere with no summer heat. Read the whole picture before you commit.

## After you finish

- Which **single reading** decided each case — the exhaust temperature, the dump radiator, the electrical efficiency, the spark spread?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, is it a no-cost operational change (run heat-led, operate to the spread), a maintenance job (service the heat exchanger, overhaul the engine), or a capital project (thermal store, absorption chiller, fuel switch) that has to earn its payback?
