To judge whether a CHP scheme is worthwhile, you have to measure its performance properly, because a nameplate efficiency means nothing if half the heat is dumped. A handful of metrics separate a genuinely efficient installation from an expensive generator, and understanding them is what lets you challenge an optimistic vendor projection. This lesson covers the efficiencies, the power-to-heat ratio, primary energy saving, utilisation, and the spark spread that ultimately decides whether the unit runs.

## Electrical, thermal and overall efficiency

- **Electrical efficiency** = electrical output ÷ fuel energy input, typically 35 to 42% for a gas engine.
- **Thermal efficiency** = *useful* heat recovered ÷ fuel input, typically 40 to 50%.
- **Overall efficiency** = (electricity + useful heat) ÷ fuel input, 80 to 90% for good schemes.

The crucial word is *useful*. Heat that is generated but dumped does not count, so a unit advertising 85% overall efficiency that only finds a use for half its heat is really operating far lower. Insisting on *useful* heat in every efficiency figure is the first defence against a flattering business case.

## Power-to-heat ratio

This ratio (electrical output divided by heat output) describes the "shape" of a unit's output. A gas engine sits around 0.8 to 1.0; a gas turbine lower. Matching this ratio to the site's own electricity-to-heat demand ratio is central to good [sizing](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/heat-demand): a mismatch means either wasted heat or imported power, whichever the unit produces in surplus.

## Primary energy saving

Because CHP produces two outputs, the fair comparison is against producing the same electricity and heat separately. **Primary Energy Saving (PES)** quantifies the fuel saved versus separate generation. "High-efficiency CHP" status under the [CHPQA scheme](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/combined-heat-and-power) requires a PES above 10% (any saving for micro-CHP). PES is the honest measure of whether cogeneration is actually saving primary energy rather than just relocating it.

## Utilisation and run hours

A CHP unit only earns its capital when running with its heat used, tracked by two figures: **annual run hours** (good schemes exceed 4,500 to 8,000 hours a year) and **heat utilisation** (the fraction of recovered heat actually used). A unit that runs only in winter, or whose heat is frequently rejected, will never repay its cost regardless of nameplate efficiency, which is why the [load-profile analysis](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/load-profile) matters so much.

<Callout variant="key" title="The spark spread decides whether it runs at all">
The economics hinge on the **spark spread**: the difference between the value of the electricity displaced and the cost of the gas burned to make it. A wide spread (expensive grid power, cheap gas) makes CHP highly profitable; a narrow or negative spread can make even a technically excellent unit uneconomic to run. Because grid and gas prices move independently, the spark spread is volatile, and a scheme that stacked up when it was specified can become uneconomic if the spread narrows. Model it across a realistic range of future prices, not just today's, before committing, since this single number often decides hour by hour whether the unit should be running.
</Callout>
