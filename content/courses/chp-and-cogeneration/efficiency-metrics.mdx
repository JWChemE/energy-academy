To judge whether a CHP scheme is worthwhile, you need to measure its performance properly. A handful of metrics separate a genuinely efficient installation from an expensive generator.

## Electrical and thermal efficiency

- **Electrical efficiency** = electrical output / fuel energy input. Typically 35-42% for a gas engine.
- **Thermal efficiency** = useful heat recovered / fuel energy input. Typically 40-50%.
- **Overall efficiency** = (electricity + useful heat) / fuel input. Good schemes reach 80-90%.

The crucial word is *useful*. Heat that is generated but dumped does not count. A unit advertising 85% overall efficiency that only finds a use for half its heat is really operating far lower.

## Power-to-heat ratio

This ratio (electrical output divided by heat output) describes the "shape" of a unit's output. A gas engine sits around 0.8-1.0; a gas turbine lower. Matching this ratio to the site's own electricity-to-heat demand ratio is central to good sizing - a mismatch means either wasted heat or imported power.

## Primary energy savings

Because CHP produces two outputs, the fair comparison is against producing the same electricity and heat separately. **Primary Energy Saving (PES)** quantifies the fuel saved versus separate generation. EU "high-efficiency CHP" status requires a PES above 10% (or any saving for micro-CHP). PES is the honest measure of whether cogeneration is actually saving energy.

## Utilisation and run hours

A CHP unit only earns its capital when running with its heat used. Two figures track this:
- **Annual run hours** - good schemes exceed 4,500-8,000 hours/year.
- **Heat utilisation** - the fraction of recovered heat actually used.

A unit that runs only in winter, or whose heat is frequently rejected, will never repay its cost regardless of nameplate efficiency.

## Spark spread

The economics hinge on the **spark spread** - the difference between the value of electricity displaced and the cost of the gas burned to make it. A wide spread (expensive grid power, cheap gas) makes CHP highly profitable; a narrow or negative spread can make it uneconomic even when technically efficient. This single number often decides whether a scheme runs.
