CHP is a principle, not a single machine. Several prime-mover technologies turn fuel into electricity and recoverable heat, each suited to a different scale, heat grade and application, and the choice follows the site rather than a league table. This lesson covers the main prime movers, the fuels they can run on, and how to match technology to duty.

## Reciprocating gas engines

The workhorse of small and medium CHP: a gas-fired internal combustion engine drives an alternator, and heat is recovered from the exhaust, jacket and oil circuits.

- Size: 5 kWe to several MWe
- Electrical efficiency: 35 to 42%
- Best for: buildings and sites with steady hot-water heat demand
- Strengths: high power efficiency, good part-load behaviour, fast to start
- Weaknesses: regular maintenance (oil changes, top-end overhauls), some noise

Most building-scale schemes use these, which is why the [economics](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/cost-benefit) and [integration](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/system-integration) lessons focus on them.

## Gas turbines

A gas turbine drives a generator, and its hot exhaust (450 to 550 °C) raises steam or high-grade hot water.

- Size: roughly 1 MWe upward
- Electrical efficiency: 25 to 40%, rising with size
- Best for: large industrial sites needing high-grade heat or [steam](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-basics)
- Strengths: large heat output, high-grade exhaust, compact for its capacity
- Weaknesses: poorer part-load efficiency, less attractive at small scale

## Micro-CHP, Stirling engines and fuel cells

**Micro-CHP** units (often 1 kWe, sometimes external-combustion Stirling engines) suit homes and small premises with constant hot-water demand: quiet and low-maintenance, but low electrical output relative to heat. **Fuel cells** convert fuel to electricity electrochemically with heat as a by-product, reaching electrical efficiencies of 50 to 60% with low emissions and no noise; they remain expensive with limited stack life, but are maturing fast.

## Fuels: where the carbon case lives

Most CHP runs on natural gas, but units can use **biogas** (from anaerobic digestion or landfill), **biomethane**, LPG, biomass (via steam cycles), or hydrogen blends. The fuel, not the prime mover, determines the [carbon outcome](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/carbon-assessment): running an engine on biogas transforms its carbon case, because the recovered energy comes from a renewable source rather than fossil gas.

<Callout variant="key" title="Match the prime mover to scale and heat grade">
The selection rule is straightforward once the site is understood: reciprocating engines for most building-scale schemes needing hot water, gas turbines where large or high-grade (steam) heat is required, micro-CHP for small constant loads, and fuel cells where emissions and power efficiency justify the premium. The [power-to-heat ratio](/courses/chp-and-cogeneration/efficiency-metrics) of the chosen machine must then match the site's own electricity-to-heat demand, because the best prime mover for the wrong load shape still wastes heat or imports power.
</Callout>

The technology is only as good as its fit to the site's demand, which the next lessons analyse: the heat demand that sets the size, and the load profiles that confirm the run hours.
