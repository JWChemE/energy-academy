CHP is a principle, not a single machine. Several prime-mover technologies turn fuel into electricity and recoverable heat, each suited to a different scale and application.

## Reciprocating gas engines

The workhorse of small and medium CHP. A gas-fired internal combustion engine drives an alternator; heat is recovered from exhaust, jacket, and oil circuits.

- Size: 5 kWe to several MWe
- Electrical efficiency: 35-42%
- Best for: buildings and sites with steady heat demand
- Strengths: high power efficiency, good part-load behaviour, fast to start
- Weaknesses: regular maintenance (oil changes, top-end overhauls), some noise

## Gas turbines

A gas turbine drives a generator, and its hot exhaust (450-550 C) raises steam or hot water.

- Size: roughly 1 MWe upward
- Electrical efficiency: 25-40% (rising with size)
- Best for: large industrial sites needing high-grade heat or steam
- Strengths: large heat output, high-grade exhaust, compact for its capacity
- Weaknesses: poorer part-load efficiency, less attractive at small scale

## Micro-CHP and Stirling engines

Domestic and small-commercial units, often 1 kWe, sometimes using an external-combustion Stirling engine.

- Best for: homes and small premises with constant hot-water demand
- Strengths: quiet, low maintenance
- Weaknesses: low electrical output relative to heat

## Fuel cells

An emerging option that converts fuel to electricity electrochemically, with heat as a by-product.

- Electrical efficiency: up to 50-60%
- Strengths: very high power efficiency, low emissions, quiet
- Weaknesses: high capital cost, shorter stack life - still maturing

## Fuels

Most CHP runs on natural gas, but units can use biogas (from anaerobic digestion or landfill), LPG, biomass (via steam cycles), or hydrogen blends. Running CHP on biogas transforms its carbon case, since the recovered energy comes from a renewable source.

## Choosing

Match the prime mover to scale and heat grade: reciprocating engines for most building-scale schemes needing hot water; gas turbines where large or high-grade (steam) heat is required; micro-CHP for small constant loads; fuel cells where emissions and power efficiency justify the premium.
