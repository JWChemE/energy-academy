Many facilities have **oversized systems** — undersized loads with equipment designed for much higher capacity. Optimising system-wide saves significantly.

## Oversized systems

Common in buildings retrofitted down (shrunk occupancy, process changes) or conservatively designed:
- HVAC designed for 500-person building, now 200 occupants
- Pump sized for 100 bar pressure; actual demand is 60 bar
- Compressor capable of 10 bar; facility only needs 6 bar

**Cost:** 15–25% more energy to run oversized equipment.

## Practical retrofit strategies

**Impeller trimming:** On centrifugal pumps/fans, slightly smaller impeller reduces output but improves efficiency
- Cost: £500–2,000
- Saving: 5–15% energy reduction for partial-flow applications

**Demand-based controls:** Instead of running at fixed setpoint, reduce capacity when demand drops
- Boiler lead/lag: Fire largest boiler first; add smaller boilers only if needed
- Compressor unload: Let compressor unload (not discharge to tank) when pressure is high enough

**Variable-speed drives:** Instead of fixed-speed motor + valve/damper, add VFD to match load
- Cost: £5,000–15,000
- Saving: 30–50% for variable-load equipment
- Payback: 2–5 years

**Retirement / right-sizing:** If a piece of equipment is oversized and rarely needed, consider removing it
- Cost: labour only
- Saving: eliminates standby losses, improves overall efficiency

## System audit for oversizing

Check if:
- Equipment runs cool (motor, pump outlet)
- Setpoint is below normal (thermostat at 18°C instead of 21°C)
- Throttling losses (dampers, valves, vents always partly open)
- Intermittent duty (equipment runs a few hours/month but is sized for 24/7)

These are red flags for oversizing.
