VFDs are most effective on **pumps and fans with variable load.** Industrial applications (compressors, presses) may have less benefit.

## Pumps

**Heating/cooling loops:** Thermostats reduce demand in mild weather. With VFD, pump speed drops, saving energy.
- Typical saving: 30–60% (mild seasons) to minimal (peak heating/cooling)
- Annual average: 30–40% saving

**Chilled water:** Cold days → less cooling needed → pump slows
- Typical saving: 20–40%

**Booster/pressure pumps:** Pressure relief reduces flow in high-pressure situations. VFD lets motor back off instead.

## Fans

**HVAC with economiser:** Cold days → outdoor air is sufficient → chiller off, fan remains at 100% (waste). VFD scales fan to actual load.
- Typical saving: 30–50% in temperate climates

**Dust collection, exhaust fans:** Ductwork resistance drops if dampers close. VFD matches motor to actual resistance.

## Compressors

**Fixed-displacement compressor with pressure relief:** Motor runs at full speed; pressure relief vents excess (waste heat).

**VFD on compressor:** Motor speed varies with demand.
- Saving: 15–30% (less dramatic than pump/fan because of harder load)

## Where NOT to use VFD

- **Constant-load equipment:** Motor always runs at full capacity. VFD doesn't help.
- **Full-speed critical:** Some motors must run at exact speed (printing presses, synchronous equipment) — VFD adds cost without benefit
- **High-inertia loads:** Heavy flywheels take time to accelerate; soft-starter may be better

## Retrofit vs. new installation

**Retrofit:** VFD on existing motor/pump system. Cost higher (retrofitting control, testing), but payback still good on high-run equipment.

**New installation:** VFD often mandatory in modern building codes for HVAC/pump systems. Cost is lower because wiring is already sized appropriately.
