A **variable-frequency drive (VFD)** changes motor speed to match load, not just on/off or with dampers.

## The cube law

For pumps and fans, **power ∝ speed³**

If a pump at full speed (100%) uses 100 kW, then:
- 80% speed: 80³ / 100³ = 51.2% power = 51 kW
- 60% speed: 60³ / 100³ = 21.6% power = 22 kW
- 50% speed: 50³ / 100³ = 12.5% power = 13 kW

**At half speed, power is 1/8.** This is why VFDs save so much energy.

## Old method: dampers and valves

Conventional approach: Run motor at full speed, close a damper or valve to reduce flow.

**Problem:** Motor still draws 80–90% power even at 20% flow. The damper/valve wastes the excess energy as heat.

**Example:** 100 kW motor at 20% flow with damper still draws 85 kW (15 kW lost in the damper), delivers only 13 kW to the load.

## VFD method

VFD slows the motor to match the required flow/pressure. At 20% flow, motor runs at ~50% speed, drawing 13 kW instead of 85 kW.

**Energy saving: 72 kW (85% reduction!)**

## When VFDs save the most

- **Pumps:** heating/cooling loops with variable load (warm days, reduced occupancy)
- **Fans:** HVAC with dampers (economiser), or varying demand
- **Compressors:** pressure-regulated (discharge to tank), with variable demand

**Hours of operation matter:** VFD on a fan running 16 h/day pays for itself faster than one on a fan running 4 h/day.

## Typical payback

- Large pump/fan, 5,000+ h/year: 2–4 years
- Medium load, 3,000 h/year: 4–6 years
- Small, low-hours: 8+ years (may not be justified)

A 30 kW pump on 16 h/day can save £10k+/year with a VFD at £15k cost = 1.5-year payback.
