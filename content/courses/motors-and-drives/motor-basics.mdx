An **electric motor** converts electrical energy into mechanical motion. Understanding motor specifications and efficiency is key to energy management.

## How motors work (simplified)

AC induction motor: current flows through the stator (coils), creating a rotating magnetic field. This induces current in the rotor (cage), creating force that spins the shaft.

Key parameters:
- **Power (kW):** mechanical output power
- **Voltage (V):** 230V single-phase, 400V three-phase (UK)
- **Speed (rpm):** 1,450 or 2,900 rpm (at 50 Hz)
- **Power factor:** typically 0.8–0.9 (lags voltage)
- **Efficiency (%):** 85–96% depending on age and size

## Motor nameplate

The nameplate lists:
- **Rated power (kW):** continuous output the motor is designed for
- **Rated voltage & current**
- **Rated speed (rpm):** synchronous speed minus slip
- **Service factor:** can briefly run above rated power (usually 1.15, meaning 115% is safe)
- **Efficiency class (IE):** IE2, IE3, IE4, etc.

**Important:** Rated power is output. Input power = output / efficiency. A 10 kW motor at 90% efficiency draws ~11.1 kW from the wall.

## Slip and losses

**Slip** = (synchronous speed − actual speed) / synchronous speed

At 50 Hz, a "four-pole" motor has synchronous speed 1,500 rpm. With 3% slip, it runs at 1,455 rpm.

Slip causes losses in the rotor as heat. Higher slip = higher losses = lower efficiency.

## Oversizing problem

A 50 kW motor running a 25 kW load is oversized. It:
- Draws more reactive power (poor power factor)
- Has poor efficiency at part load (losses don't scale down with load)
- Costs more upfront and draws more current

Result: Same 10–15% energy waste at half load. Matching motor size to actual load is crucial.

## Motor age and efficiency

- **Before 1990:** ~85% efficiency, no standard
- **2000s (IE2):** 87–91% efficiency, improving
- **2010s (IE3):** 90–95% efficiency, good
- **2020s (IE4):** 93–97% efficiency, best-in-class

Upgrading an old motor to IE3/IE4 saves 2–4% energy, paying back in 3–8 years for motors running > 5,000 h/year.
