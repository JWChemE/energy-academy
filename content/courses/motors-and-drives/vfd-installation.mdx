A variable-speed drive is power electronics, not a motor accessory, and installing one changes the electrical character of everything around it. Most disappointing drive projects fail not on the energy arithmetic but on installation details: harmonics upsetting neighbours, cable-induced voltage spikes eating motor insulation, bearings pitted by stray currents, or commissioning that left the promised control strategy switched off. This lesson is the checklist that protects the business case.

## What a drive does to the supply: harmonics

A VFD rectifies the incoming AC ([as the electrical course described](/courses/electrical-science/ac-vs-dc)), and rectifiers draw current in pulses rather than smooth sine waves. The distortion, **harmonics**, flows back into the site's supply, where it heats transformers and cables, disturbs sensitive equipment, and in bulk can breach the limits distribution operators enforce (G5/5 in the UK). One drive on a healthy supply is rarely a problem; a plant room full of them needs assessment. Mitigation is a menu with rising cost: line reactors (standard, cheap, fit by default), passive filters, low-harmonic or active-front-end drives for the heavy cases. Ask the supplier for a harmonic estimate against G5/5 as part of any multi-drive project.

## What a drive does to the motor: cables, insulation, bearings

The drive's output is not a sine wave but millions of sharp voltage pulses. Three consequences follow. **Cable length matters**: long motor cables let pulse reflections stack into voltage spikes at the motor terminals; stay within the drive maker's cable limit or fit an output filter. **Insulation matters**: modern motors rated for inverter duty (reinforced insulation) shrug this off; elderly motors may not, which belongs in the [rewind-or-replace decision](/courses/motors-and-drives/motor-selection). **Bearings matter**: pulse-induced stray currents can arc through bearing lubricant, machining tiny fluting into the races until the bearing screams; the standard defences (insulated non-drive-end bearings above ~75 kW, shaft grounding rings, correctly bonded screened cable) cost little at specification time and a rebuild later.

Screened (shielded) motor cable, glanded correctly at both ends, is non-negotiable: it contains the electromagnetic interference that otherwise finds the building's control wiring.

## The honest energy accounting

A drive consumes 2 to 4% of throughput in its own losses, always, at every load. On a variable load this vanishes inside cube-law savings. On a load that runs at full speed, it is a pure new cost.

<WorkedExample title="the drive that should not have been fitted">
  <Given>
    - A 90 kW motor drives a process fan that genuinely needs full output whenever it runs
    - A drive is fitted anyway ("for flexibility"); drive losses ≈ 3%
    - 6,000 h/yr; electricity £0.20/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual cost of the unnecessary drive.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Losses ≈ 0.03 × 90 × 6,000 ≈ 16,200 kWh: about **£3,240 a year**, forever, plus the
    capital. The [applications lesson's](/courses/motors-and-drives/vfd-applications)
    load-profile discipline exists precisely to prevent this purchase. Where full-speed
    running dominates but a drive is justified for other reasons, specify one with a
    **bypass contactor** so the motor runs direct-on-line at full speed.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Commissioning: where savings are switched on

The drive saves nothing until its control strategy is configured: the feedback signal (pressure, temperature, flow) connected and scaled, the control curve set (a pump on a pressure signal wants proportional control with a sensible minimum speed, not a fixed 45 Hz someone chose on day one), ramps tuned, and **minimum speed** set with care (pumps need flow for cooling and to avoid dead-heading; motors on drives at very low speed lose their cooling fan's effect). Insist commissioning ends with a witnessed demonstration: the drive turning down as demand falls, and the [logged power data](/courses/energy-audits/measurement-and-logging) for the first weeks against the pre-installation baseline. That comparison is the project's proof, per the [M&V discipline](/courses/measurement-and-verification).

Every failure mode in this lesson is cheap at specification time and expensive afterwards: reactors and screened cable in the quote, inverter-rated motor or bearing protection named, harmonic assessment for multi-drive projects, bypass where full-speed hours dominate, commissioning with witnessed turndown and data. </Callout>
