Installing a VFD requires electrical knowledge. Key considerations:

## Electrical requirements

**Power supply:** VFD must match supply voltage (230V single-phase, 400V three-phase). Most industrial installations are three-phase.

**Cable sizing:** VFD output is pulse-width modulated (PWM) — very high frequency switching. Standard cables can cause:
- Motor winding damage (voltage spikes)
- Electromagnetic interference (EMI)

Solution: **Shielded motor cable** and **reactor** (inductor) on VFD output to smooth waveform.

Cost: adds ~20% to VFD cost, but essential.

## Harmonic distortion

VFD switching at ~16 kHz creates harmonics that can distort the voltage waveform, affecting:
- Transformer heating (losses, shortened life)
- Other equipment interference (sensitive controls, PLCs)
- Utility penalties (if harmonics exceed allowed limits)

**Solution:** Harmonic filter (active or passive) on VFD input. Cost: ~5–10% of VFD cost.

## Motor heating

VFD's switching creates additional losses in the motor (core losses, cooling losses). Motor runs hotter than with mains supply.

**Impact:** Motor may overheat if:
- Motor is already running hot
- Ambient temperature is high
- Motor cooling fan is blocked

**Solution:** Thermal sensor or slightly lower rated load. Well-ventilated motors are less affected.

## Installation cost

- VFD unit: 2,000–10,000 GBP (depending on power)
- Cable/reactor/filter: 1,000–3,000 GBP
- Labour/commissioning: 1,000–2,000 GBP
- **Total: 4,000–15,000 GBP for typical pump/fan installation**

Payback on high-run motors (>5,000 h/year): 2–4 years.

## Commissioning

VFD must be tuned to the motor and load:
- Acceleration ramps (how fast motor speeds up)
- Deceleration ramps
- Load sensing (is pressure/flow feedback available?)
- Safety interlocks (what happens if VFD fails?)

Poor commissioning leads to poor performance. Budget for a qualified technician.
