Two decisions shape a motor's lifetime cost more than its efficiency class: whether it is the right size for its load, and what happens on the day it fails. Both are usually made badly, for understandable reasons: oversizing feels safe, and failure-day decisions get made at speed with production waiting. This lesson gives both decisions their proper arithmetic.

## The oversizing problem

Motors are efficient across a surprisingly wide band, typically from about 60% up to full load, but below half load efficiency starts sliding, and below a quarter load it falls off steeply while [power factor collapses](/courses/electrical-science/power-factor) with it. Oversizing happens innocently: a designer adds margin, the next engineer adds margin to the margin, the pump gets trimmed years later, and the site ends up with a 30 kW motor loafing along at 8 kW, deep in the inefficient zone, dragging the site's power factor down as it goes.

Finding these machines costs a clamp meter and an afternoon: measure running current against nameplate full-load current, per the [three-phase lesson](/courses/electrical-science/three-phase-power). A motor drawing under 40% of rated current is a candidate; under 25% is a finding. The remedy is rarely "replace immediately" (a running motor's sunk cost is sunk) but "replace right-sized when it next fails", recorded in the motor list so the failure-day decision is pre-made, or, where the load genuinely varies, a [drive](/courses/motors-and-drives/why-vfds) that lets one machine serve the whole range well.

## Rewind or replace: the failure-day decision

A failed motor can usually be rewound (new stator windings in the old frame) for roughly half the cost of a new machine, and same-day if the rewinder is local. The catches: a rewind preserves the old motor's efficiency class at best, and a poorly-controlled bake-and-strip process can cost half a point to two points of efficiency; a good rewinder working to the EASA/AEMT good-practice standard loses little or nothing, so the rewinder's process matters as much as the choice.

<WorkedExample title="rewind vs replace on a 55 kW motor">
  <Given>
    - A 55 kW IE2-era motor (93%) fails; it runs 6,000 h/yr at good load; electricity £0.20/kWh
    - Rewind: ≈ £2,000, efficiency at best preserved, assume 1 point lost → 92%
    - New IE4: ≈ £4,500 at 95.5% (indicative for this size)
  </Given>
  <Find>The running-cost difference and effective payback of replacing.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Input at 92%: 55 ÷ 0.92 = 59.8 kW. At 95.5%: 57.6 kW. Difference ≈ 2.2 kW ×
    6,000 h ≈ 13,200 kWh ≈ **£2,640 a year**.

    The replacement premium over rewinding (£2,500) repays in **under a year**, then
    pays every year for two decades. At 1,500 running hours the same premium takes
    nearly four years, and the rewind wins. The rule that falls out: **high-hours
    motors are replaced with best-in-class; low-hours motors are rewound**, with the
    threshold around 2,000 to 3,000 h/yr at today's prices.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Duty details that bite

Three selection factors beyond size and class earn a check. **Duty cycle**: frequent starting heats motors (starting current is several times full-load current, per the [demand lesson](/courses/motors-and-drives/demand-management)), so high-start-frequency duties need motors and control gear rated for it. **Environment**: ambient temperature, dust and washdown ratings (IP class) decide real-world life; remember the 10 K insulation rule from [motor basics](/courses/motors-and-drives/motor-basics). **Drive compatibility**: motors destined for VFD service want inverter-rated insulation and, above ~75 kW, insulated bearings, a detail that belongs to the [installation lesson](/courses/motors-and-drives/vfd-installation) but is cheapest specified at purchase.

<Callout variant="key" title="Pre-make the failure-day decision">
Motor economics are decided in the worst possible meeting: an unplanned stop, a rewinder on the phone, and a spares list from 1998. The fix is the motor policy from the [previous lesson](/courses/motors-and-drives/efficiency-classes) extended with one column: for every significant motor, the pre-agreed action on failure (rewind to EASA standard / replace with specified IE4 model / replace right-sized to measured load). Ten minutes per motor now converts every future failure into an upgrade opportunity instead of a like-for-like reflex.
</Callout>
