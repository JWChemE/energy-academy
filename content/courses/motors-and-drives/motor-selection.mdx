**Oversized motors are one of the largest hidden energy wastes.** Matching motor size to actual load can save 10–20% on motor energy.

## Why oversizing happens

1. **Safety margin:** "We might need more power someday," so engineers size up
2. **Standard sizes:** Motors come in discrete sizes (11 kW, 15 kW, 22 kW), not custom
3. **Part-load operation:** Many motors run well below nameplate (pump 50% of capacity, fan on damper)
4. **Old equipment:** Original load may have been higher; load never came down

## Cost of oversizing

**Scenario:** Pump needs 10 kW, but installed motor is 15 kW (50% oversized)

- Motor cost: 15 kW motor ~25% more than 10 kW
- Energy cost: At 50% load, IE3 motor efficiency drops to ~85% vs 94% at rated load
- Example: 10 kW demand at 85% efficiency = 11.8 kW input vs 10.6 kW for right-sized motor at 94%

**Annual cost of oversizing:** 1.2 kW × 7,500 h × £0.08 = £720/year wasted

**10-year cost:** £7,200 to run an oversized motor

## Solutions

1. **Right-size on commissioning:** Measure actual load, select motor accordingly
2. **Variable-speed drive (VFD):** If load varies, VFD lets one motor run efficiently at any load
3. **Two smaller motors:** For intermittent operations, run only what's needed
4. **Demand control:** Reduce peak load through scheduling or load management

## Checking if a motor is oversized

- Motor runs cool (temperature < 80°C under full load) → likely oversized
- Power factor < 0.8 (poor) → likely oversized or running light
- Motor sounds like it's "coasting" or not working hard → likely oversized

Measure actual current (should be close to nameplate current under typical load). If much lower, motor is oversized.
