You've learned how motors work, where they lose energy, why efficiency class and sizing matter, what variable-speed drives do, and how power factor and demand drive the bill. This capstone puts you on the shop floor as the auditor — and like real life, the answer is often a calculation away, and sometimes the right move is to do *nothing yet*.

Some of these call-outs you'll crack with a quick sum — a load factor, an affinity-law saving, a power-factor correction, a payback. Others you reason out from the symptoms. And a couple are **financial judgments**: rewind or replace, upgrade now or wait for failure. Read the raw panel, work out what the prize is worth, then diagnose and prescribe.

## How the cases work

Each is a five-step loop:

1. **Investigate** — read the brief and the raw panel readings.
2. **Calculate** — work out the figure that matters (load factor, fan power at part speed, capacitor kVAr, annual saving, payback). The **reference data is on hand** — efficiency tables, the affinity laws, power-factor trig, belt and imbalance figures — but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose** — pick the root cause(s) from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe** — build an ordered work plan (no-cost first, then capital).
5. **Verify** — score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

## What you'll practise

- **Sizing** — reading a motor's true load factor from measured power, and the cost of running a big motor on a small job.
- **Variable-speed drives** — the affinity laws, where slowing a throttled fan to 70% speed cuts power to a third.
- **Repair vs replace** — the efficiency penalty of repeated rewinds, and the payback on a new motor.
- **Power factor** — turning a poor power factor into kVA and the capacitors needed to fix it.
- **Idle running, belt losses, and efficiency upgrades** — and a hot, tripping motor where the supply, not the motor, is to blame.

<Callout variant="key" title="The most expensive motor is the one you didn't need to run">
Three numbers do most of the work in this room: power follows the **cube of speed** (so slowing a throttled fan or pump is the biggest prize); a motor's efficiency and power factor both **collapse at low load** (so oversizing quietly costs you twice); and **running hours decide every replacement**. A premium motor on long hours pays back fast — but scrapping a healthy motor that barely runs is just waste with a green label on it. Do the sum before you spend.
</Callout>

## Restraint is a valid answer

Not every case ends in a purchase order. One of them tempts you to scrap a perfectly good motor for a shiny efficiency class — and the payback says wait, and specify the upgrade when it next fails. Knowing when *not* to invest is as much a part of energy management as spotting the savings.

## After you finish

- Which **single number** decided each case?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, what goes in the report — and is it a no-cost control, a low-cost retrofit, or a capital project that has to earn its payback?
