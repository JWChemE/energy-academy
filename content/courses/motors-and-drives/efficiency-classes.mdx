Motor efficiency is standardised into internationally agreed bands, the IE classes, and UK law now sets a floor under them: most new three-phase motors must be at least IE3, and the mid-range sizes IE4. For an energy manager the classes turn efficiency from a brochure claim into a comparable, regulated number, and the interesting question becomes economic: when is a class above the legal floor worth paying for? The arithmetic in this lesson answers it.

## The classes and the law

The IEC scale runs from IE1 ("standard", long obsolete) through **IE2** (high), **IE3** (premium), **IE4** (super-premium) to the emerging IE5. Each step trims losses meaningfully: for a 15 kW 4-pole motor, indicative full-load efficiencies run roughly 90.6% (IE2), 92.1% (IE3), 93.6% (IE4), with the gaps larger for small motors and smaller for very large ones.

UK ecodesign rules (assimilated from EU Regulation 2019/1781; checked July 2026) set the floor: since July 2021, most three-phase motors from 0.75 kW to 1,000 kW placed on the market must meet **IE3**; since July 2023, motors from 75 to 200 kW must meet **IE4**. The regulations bind what can be *sold*, not what already runs, so the installed base remains full of IE1/IE2 machines whose replacement economics are exactly this lesson's subject.

## What a class step is worth

Efficiency differences look small (a point or two) until multiplied by the hours and the [six-week fact](/courses/motors-and-drives/motor-basics).

<WorkedExample title="IE2 to IE4 on a working motor">
  <Given>
    - A 15 kW motor running 4,000 h/yr at full load; electricity £0.20/kWh
    - IE2 efficiency 90.6%; IE4 efficiency 93.6%; IE4 premium over a like-for-like IE2 replacement ≈ £500
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual saving and payback of choosing IE4.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Input power: 15 ÷ 0.906 = 16.56 kW versus 15 ÷ 0.936 = 16.03 kW: a difference of
    0.53 kW. Annual saving ≈ 0.53 × 4,000 ≈ 2,120 kWh ≈ **£425 a year**.

    Payback on the premium ≈ 500 ÷ 425 ≈ **1.2 years**, then £425 a year for a 15-to-20
    year machine life. At 2,000 running hours the payback stretches to ~2.4 years; at
    8,000 it drops to ~7 months. Hours decide, which is why the motor list from the
    previous lesson matters: it tells you which machines earn the premium.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Policy, not purchase-by-purchase heroics

The gains compound only if they happen by default, so the practical instrument is a **motor purchase policy**: minimum IE4 for anything above a threshold of running hours (say 2,000 h/yr), decided once, applied by whoever raises the order, including at distress-purchase speed when a motor has failed and production is waiting. Failure-day decisions default to "same again, fastest delivery" unless the policy has already made the choice. The policy should also cover the failure decision itself, rewind versus replace, which the [next lesson](/courses/motors-and-drives/motor-selection) treats properly, and note that class labels assume full-load operation: a grossly oversized IE4 motor loafing at 30% load surrenders its advantage, which is the other half of that lesson.

<Callout variant="key" title="Efficiency classes only pay at the hours you actually run">
The IE arithmetic is linear in running hours, so one number from the motor list sorts the estate: machines above ~4,000 h/yr justify best-in-class on replacement, machines below ~1,000 h rarely repay any premium, and the band between takes the [payback arithmetic](/courses/economic-analysis) case by case. Classify once, write it into the policy, and the savings arrive without further meetings.
</Callout>
