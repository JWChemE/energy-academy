Motor efficiency is standardised in **IE classes.** Choosing the right class depends on motor size, hours of operation, and energy costs.

## IE classes (EU standard, UK aligned)

**IE1 (Standard Efficiency):** ~85%, being phased out

**IE2 (High Efficiency):** 87-92%, common in new installations

**IE3 (Premium Efficiency):** 91-96%, mandatory for many applications since 2017

**IE4 (Super Premium Efficiency):** 94-97%, emerging, highest cost

## Energy cost over lifetime

For a 15 kW motor, 7,500 h/year, £0.08/kWh:

- IE2 (90% efficiency): 7,500 h / 0.90 × 15 kW × £0.08 = £10k/year energy
- IE3 (94% efficiency): 7,500 h / 0.94 × 15 kW × £0.08 = £9.6k/year energy
- **Annual savings: £400/year**
- **10-year savings: £4,000**

If IE3 motor costs £500 more than IE2, payback is just 1.25 years. Over 15 years, IE3 saves £6,000.

## Where efficiency drops most

Efficiency varies with load and speed. IE3 is best over the full operating range.

Oversized motors (25 kW motor on 15 kW load) lose this advantage — the "high efficiency" range is narrow.

## Selection rule

- **High hours (over 5,000 h/year):** IE3 mandatory (payback under 2 years in most cases)
- **Medium hours (2,000–5,000 h/year):** IE3 preferred (payback 2–5 years)
- **Low hours (under 2,000 h/year):** IE2 acceptable (payback over 5 years)
- **Always:** Match motor size to load (see oversizing section)

## Maintenance impact

Well-maintained motors retain efficiency. Dirty (dust, cooling fins blocked), corroded, or hot motors lose efficiency. Annual maintenance is a free efficiency gain.
