**Demand charges** are based on peak power draw, not total energy. Managing peaks can save thousands per year.

## How it works

Utility measures demand in 30-minute intervals (or 15-minute in some areas). **The highest peak in the month sets your demand charge for the entire month.**

Example:
- Your facility typically draws 80 kW
- At 3 PM on a Tuesday, two large motors start simultaneously → 110 kW peak
- **You're billed for 110 kW demand for the whole month** (£1,000+ extra cost)

## Demand vs. energy

- **Energy (kWh):** all the electricity you use (kW × hours)
- **Demand (kW or kVA):** your peak power draw

A facility might reduce energy by 10% but reduce demand by 30% through better scheduling — the demand savings are worth more.

## Strategies to reduce demand

**Load shifting:** Stagger equipment start-ups so they don't all turn on together
- Boilers: Fire one at a time, not all simultaneously
- HVAC: Stagger compressor starts
- Production: Schedule heavy equipment use outside peak hours

**Demand limiting:** Install a demand controller that prevents loads from exceeding a target (e.g., 100 kW max)
- Cost: £2,000–5,000
- Savings: Often £5,000–20,000/year
- Payback: < 1 year

**Soft starters:** Large motors draw 6–7× inrush current when hard-starting. Soft starters ramp voltage gradually, reducing inrush.
- Cost: £1,000–3,000
- Savings: Reduced demand charge, less voltage sag affecting other equipment
- Payback: 1–3 years

**Thermal storage / batteries:** Charge during off-peak, discharge during peak
- More expensive but highly effective for large loads

## Measurement

Install a demand meter or data logger to see your real-time and peak demand. Often you'll find:
- Unexpected peaks at specific times
- Simultaneous start-ups you didn't realise
- Opportunities for easy load shifting
