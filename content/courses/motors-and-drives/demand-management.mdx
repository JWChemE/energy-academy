Motors do not just consume energy; they shape a site's electrical peaks, and peaks carry their own charges. A big motor starting direct-on-line briefly draws several times its running current, and a shift's worth of machines starting together can set the half-hourly maximum demand that prices the whole month. This lesson connects motor starting behaviour to the demand and capacity charges on the bill, and covers the levers: staggering, soft starting, and drives.

## What starting really draws

An induction motor started **direct-on-line (DOL)** draws its locked-rotor current, typically **6 to 8 times full-load current**, until it accelerates. A 30 kW motor whose full-load current is about 57 A can momentarily pull around 400 A. The draw is brief (seconds), but it stresses the local network: voltage dips that flicker lights and trip sensitive kit, heating in cables and the motor itself, and mechanical shock through couplings and belts. Frequent DOL starting is also a maintenance bill: motors have limits on starts per hour precisely because each start deposits heat.

**Star-delta** starters (the traditional compromise) cut starting current to roughly a third at the cost of reduced starting torque and a characteristic changeover thump. **Soft starters** ramp the voltage electronically for a smooth, adjustable start at modest cost, then usually bypass themselves for efficient running. **VFDs** start most gently of all, drawing little more than full-load current while accelerating on a controlled ramp, one of the secondary benefits the [applications lesson](/courses/motors-and-drives/vfd-applications) listed.

## Starting behaviour meets the bill

The [electrical science course](/courses/electrical-science/demand-and-power-factor) covered how larger sites pay for capacity (kVA) and maximum demand measured over half-hour windows. A single motor's seconds-long start barely moves a half-hour average; what sets the peaks is **coincidence**: many machines running (and starting) simultaneously, classically at shift start when everything from compressors to conveyors wakes at once.

The lever is scheduling. Staggering large starts across even twenty minutes, by simple time-delay relays, a BMS sequence, or a written start-up procedure, can shave the morning peak, and where the site pays maximum-demand or agreed-capacity charges, every kVA shaved is money at the [rates that lesson worked through](/courses/electrical-science/demand-and-power-factor). The same logic governs which loads run *during* the site's peak window: a motor-driven process that can shift an hour (grinding, pumping to storage, charging) moves its contribution out of the expensive half-hours entirely, and time-of-use network charges reward the same shift.

The [half-hourly data](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/meter-data) makes the whole exercise empirical: the site's peak half-hours are visible by inspection, and the motor list from [this course's first lesson](/courses/motors-and-drives/motor-basics) usually explains them line by line.

<Callout variant="key" title="Peaks are appointments, not accidents">
A site's maximum demand almost always happens at a predictable, explainable moment: the cold Monday 8 a.m. start, the afternoon when the compressor test coincides with the ovens. Find it in the half-hourly data, name the machines behind it, and ask which of them genuinely needed to be simultaneous. Rescheduling costs procedure, not capital, and on capacity-charged supplies it is among the cheapest money in this course. It also frees headroom that can defer a supply upgrade, which is real capital avoided.
</Callout>

One caution to keep claims honest: reducing *peaks* saves demand-related charges; it rarely saves many kWh (the shifted machine still runs). Present the two savings separately, exactly as the bill charges them, and the case will survive the finance director's scrutiny.
