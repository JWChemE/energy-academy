Modern BMS systems do far more than just control. Data analytics, alarming, and scheduling are powerful tools for energy management.

## Data trending

Collect time-series data (kWh, temperature, runtime) and plot over time (daily, weekly, monthly).

Trends reveal: equipment cycling (are pumps running 24/7?), seasonal changes (cooling season vs heating), anomalies (sudden spikes).

Example: BMS plot of chiller kW over 4 weeks shows it running 150 kW overnight when building is empty. Opportunity: occupancy-based scheduling.

## Alarming

Flag when a variable exceeds a threshold or doesn't behave as expected.

Example alarms:
- High: Chiller running but not producing cold (sensor or equipment failure)
- Low: Zone temperature below 18°C (heating failure or setpoint error)
- Rate-of-change: Supply air temperature rising 2°C in 1 minute (actuator stuck, control failure)

**Alarm fatigue:** Too many alarms → operators ignore real problems. Thresholds should be meaningful.

## Scheduling

Automatically adjust setpoints based on time:
- Weekdays: 8 AM–6 PM occupied, setpoint 21°C. Evenings/weekends: unoccupied, setpoint 16°C.
- Summer: cooling setpoint 24°C. Winter: heating setpoint 19°C.

Scheduling saves significant energy by not conditioning unoccupied spaces.

## Reporting

BMS can generate reports: energy consumption by system, cost, efficiency metrics, seasonal analysis.

Useful for energy audits, utility benchmarking, identifying efficiency projects.
