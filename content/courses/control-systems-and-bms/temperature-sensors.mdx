Temperature sensors are the eyes of the control system. Accuracy, response time, and placement determine control quality.

## RTD (Resistance Temperature Detector)

**Principle:** Resistance of a metal (usually platinum) changes with temperature.

Common types: Pt100 (0°C = 100Ω), Pt1000 (0°C = 1,000Ω).

Pros: Accurate (±0.5–1°C typical), linear, long-term stable, not affected by vibration.
Cons: Needs 4-wire wiring for accuracy, slower response than thermocouple.

Best for: HVAC, building automation, most comfort control.

## Thermistor (NTC = Negative Temperature Coefficient)

**Principle:** Resistance drops as temperature rises (opposite of RTD).

Pros: Cheap, fast response, small.
Cons: Non-linear, less accurate, drifts over time.

Best for: Simple applications, low-cost devices.

## Thermocouple

**Principle:** Two different metals at different temperatures produce voltage (Seebeck effect).

Pros: Fast response (seconds), works at very high temp (up to 1800°C), simple.
Cons: Low output voltage (needs amplification), non-linear, reference junction errors.

Best for: Industrial high-temperature (furnaces, ovens).

## Placement errors

**Window-mounted sensor:** Affected by direct sun, cold from outside. Reading unrepresentative.

**Sensor in stagnant air:** Doesn't respond to room conditions; control lags.

**Ceiling-mounted sensor:** Reads higher temp (warm air rises).

**Best practice:** Mount in active space, away from drafts, direct sun, and radiators. Wall height (1.2–1.5 m), centrally located.

## Calibration drift

Sensors drift over time (1–2% per year typical). Recalibrate annually or every 2 years for critical systems.
