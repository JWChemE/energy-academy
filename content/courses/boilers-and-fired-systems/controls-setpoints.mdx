A boiler doesn't run in isolation. It responds to demand from the building, heating
water for radiators, heating a swimming pool, generating steam for a process. How
that demand is sensed and how the boiler responds defines whether it runs efficiently
or wastes energy.

## Demand signals: temperature setpoint

The simplest control: a **temperature sensor** in the boiler outlet water, with a
setpoint (e.g., 70 °C). The burner modulates or cycles to keep the water at that
temperature.

- If water temperature drops below setpoint, the burner fires
- If it reaches setpoint, the burner throttles back or shuts off

This is **reactive control**: the boiler responds to what's happening now.

## The problem: constant temperature is wasteful

Running boiler outlet water at 70 °C all year is inefficient. In winter, when demand
is high, you need that heat. In summer (or mild weather), a building needs only 40–50
°C water for low-load heating. Running at 70 °C anyway wastes energy:
- Distribution losses are higher (higher ΔT, more heat lost from pipes)
- The boiler runs frequently even when demand is small
- If you have a condensing boiler, it won't condense (return water is too warm)

## Weather compensation: follow the load

**Weather compensation** adjusts the boiler setpoint based on **outdoor air
temperature**.

When it's cold outside:
- Building heat loss is high
- You set the boiler to 70 °C (high output)

When it's mild (say, 15 °C):
- Building heat loss is lower
- The controller lowers the setpoint to 50 °C

The relationship is called a **heating curve**. Modern controls have a slope and
offset you can tune.

<Callout variant="key" title="Weather compensation saves 5–10% on heating energy">
If your building heats for 5,000 degree-days per year, weather compensation
typically saves 1–2 percentage points of total energy. It's especially valuable
with condensing boilers, which need cold return water to function.
</Callout>

## Sequencing: multi-boiler plants

If you have two or more boilers, how do they work together?

**Lead/lag (cascade) sequencing:**
- Boiler 1 handles the load first (is the "lead" boiler)
- When demand exceeds Boiler 1's capacity, Boiler 2 fires (the "lag")
- As demand drops, Boiler 2 shuts down before Boiler 1 modulates down

**Benefit**: both boilers share load, which keeps each running closer to optimal
efficiency. You avoid one boiler at very low part-load while another idles.

**Parallel sequencing** (less common):
- Both boilers fire together at part load, each doing half the work
- More complex but can optimize for efficiency

The choice depends on the boiler type and the range of loads. Typically:
- **Best practice**: lead/lag with both boilers sized to ~60% of maximum demand
  (ensures good part-load efficiency)
- **Common mistake**: boilers badly oversized, so they run at 20–30% load and
  cycling dominates efficiency loss

## Setpoint problems and drift

Temperature setpoints sometimes **creep higher** over time:

- Operators raise the setpoint thinking it will speed up heating (it doesn't,
  much: it just wastes energy)
- An old controller's thermostat fails and is replaced with a wrongly-calibrated
  one
- The weather compensation is disabled because "it wasn't working properly" (maybe
  it just needed tuning)

A boiler running at 75 °C instead of 65 °C wastes roughly **5–10% more energy**.
Over a year, that's thousands of pounds.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Check setpoints quarterly">
If your boiler setpoint has drifted up in the last few years, that's lost savings.
Resetting it can recover 5–10% of heating energy with zero capital cost.
</Callout>

## Demand-side control: load balancing

The best control is one that doesn't waste fuel in the first place. That starts with
**balancing the heating distribution system** so demand is met without overshooting.

- **Oversized radiators** in one room → water is hotter than needed → boiler
  setpoint must be high
- **Underbalanced valve** → some zones get cold water, others get scalding hot

**Balancing the system** (using differential-pressure regulators and manual balancing
valves) ensures every zone gets the right flow for its demand, reducing the
temperature needed at the boiler.

## Practical tuning checklist

- **Check the setpoint**: is it set correctly for the season?
- **Enable weather compensation** if available (and tune it to your building)
- **Check for setpoint drift**: if it's crept up over a year, reset it
- **Review multi-boiler sequencing**: is lead/lag set up correctly?
- **Balance the heating system**: are some zones hot while others are cold?

A well-controlled boiler plant runs at the lowest possible temperature to meet
demand, adjusts to weather, and sequences multiple boilers efficiently. These are
all software/tuning changes: no capital cost, but significant savings.
