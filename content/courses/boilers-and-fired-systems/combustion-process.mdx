A boiler is a heat-exchange system that converts chemical energy in fuel into
heat in water or steam. Understanding combustion, the chemical reaction that releases that energy, is the key to understanding where boiler efficiency is lost
and how to recover it.

## The combustion reaction

Combustion happens when fuel, oxygen, and heat come together. The simplest case
is complete combustion of natural gas (methane):

> CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + **energy (heat)**

For every molecule of methane, you need exactly 2 molecules of oxygen. In reality,
air is only about 21% oxygen and 79% nitrogen, so you need about 10 volumes of air
for every 1 of methane: the **stoichiometric air requirement** or **theoretical
air**.

<Callout variant="key" title="The stoichiometric ratio is the starting point">
Stoichiometric combustion is the theoretical ideal: all the fuel burns completely
with just enough oxygen, producing only CO₂ and H₂O. In practice, you can never
achieve this perfectly: you need *more* air than the stoichiometric amount to
ensure complete combustion.
</Callout>

## Excess air: the practical necessity

If you starve a burner of air, you get incomplete combustion: some fuel turns into
carbon monoxide (CO), soot, and unburned hydrocarbons. Those are energy losses, and
CO is poisonous. So every boiler runs with **excess air**: more oxygen than the
stoichiometric minimum.

Typical boiler plants run with **10–20% excess air**. That extra air, the nitrogen in it especially, goes up the flue, carrying sensible heat with it. This is where
one of the biggest boiler losses lives.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Too much excess air is expensive">
Every 1% increase in excess air above stoichiometric raises flue-gas temperature
by about 0.5 °C and cuts boiler efficiency by about 0.1%. Running at 40% excess air
instead of 15% can cost 2.5% of efficiency. But too little and you risk incomplete
combustion and CO.
</Callout>

## Measuring combustion quality: O₂ and CO

Two measurements tell you instantly whether a boiler is burning well:

| Parameter | What it means | Typical target |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Oxygen (O₂) in flue gas** | How much unburned oxygen left the burner: a proxy for excess air | 3–5% |
| **Carbon monoxide (CO)** | Incomplete combustion: a sign of fuel waste and a toxicity risk | below 100 ppm (ideally below 50) |

A boiler running at 8% O₂ with below 50 ppm CO is burning cleanly. Running at 12% O₂
wastes energy, and >200 ppm CO signals that the fuel isn't completely oxidising.

<Callout variant="tip">
Flue-gas analysers are cheap and portable. If you work on boiler plants, owning one
is essential: you can spot drift in 10 minutes and quantify the cost of fixing it.
</Callout>

## The energy balance

Not all the energy in the fuel becomes useful heat in the boiler water. Energy is
lost:

1. **Up the flue** (sensible heat in hot gases, water vapour, etc.), typically 10–15%
   of input.
2. **Through the casing** (radiation and convection), typically 1–3%.
3. **Blowdown** (removing mineral-heavy water to prevent scaling), varies by water quality.
4. **Incomplete combustion** (CO, soot): usually small, but can grow if tuning drifts.

A modern efficient boiler: ~90% efficient. An old or poorly maintained one: 75–80%.
That 10–15 percentage point gap is real money every year.

## Condensing boilers: capturing latent heat

When you cool the flue gas enough, the water vapour in it condenses, releasing its
latent heat (around 2450 kJ/kg). A **condensing boiler** has an extra heat exchanger
in the flue that cools gases below the dew point (~55 °C for natural gas).

This can recover an extra 10% of fuel energy, pushing efficiency toward 105% on a
fuel basis (though the conventional definition, which ignores latent heat, would call
it ~95%).

The catch: you must handle the condensate (acidic; it needs neutralising in some
cases) and run relatively cool return water (below 50–55 °C) for condensation to
happen. Older heating systems designed for 80 °C flow struggle to use condensing
boilers efficiently.

<Callout variant="key" title="Condensing boilers need low-temperature returns">
If your system runs return water above 55 °C, a condensing boiler won't condense
and you lose the efficiency gain. This is why boiler retrofit should start with
system diagnostics, not just a like-for-like swap.
</Callout>
