Not all boilers are the same. The way a boiler is designed, how the flame contacts the water and how the hot gases flow around it, affects its efficiency, size,
maintenance, and suitable applications.

## The main types

### Firetube boilers

The hot combustion gases flow **inside tubes** that are surrounded by water. This
is the design you see in many old industrial plants.

**Pros:**
- Robust and forgiving of poor water treatment (boilers are less fussy)
- Lower capital cost
- Compact footprint

**Cons:**
- Lower efficiency (typically 80–85%) because gas contact time is short
- Harder to retrofit (the layout doesn't lend itself to economisers or air
  preheaters)
- Slower to respond to load changes
- More prone to fouling because of the gas-side design

A 30-year-old 500 kW firetube is expensive to run but often cheap to buy used.

### Watertube boilers

Hot gases flow **around tubes** carrying water, so the water is inside the tubes. This
allows much more flexible tube arrangements and higher steam/water velocities.

**Pros:**
- Higher efficiency (typically 85–90%)
- Better load response (water is in smaller tubes, quicker transient)
- Flexible design for high pressure
- Easier to add economisers or air preheaters

**Cons:**
- More sensitive to poor water treatment (scale inside the tubes damages them)
- Higher capital cost
- Requires regular chemical treatment and monitoring

Most modern commercial and industrial boilers are watertube.

### Condensing boilers

A newer type with an extra low-temperature heat exchanger that cools flue gas below
the dew point of water vapour, capturing latent heat.

**Pros:**
- Very high efficiency (90–98% on NCV basis, or 110%+ on gross calorific basis if
  you count the latent heat)
- Compact compared to what you'd need for equivalent output with traditional design
- Ideal for modern, low-temperature heating systems

**Cons:**
- Requires return water below ~55 °C to fully condense
- Needs condensate handling (drain, possible pH neutralisation)
- Higher capital cost than traditional boilers (though gap is closing)
- Not a retrofit into old systems designed for 80 °C returns

<Callout variant="key" title="Condensing boilers need a low-temperature system">
Installing a condensing boiler into a system with 70 °C return water is wasting
your investment. The boiler becomes a non-condensing boiler running expensive fuel
through expensive equipment.
</Callout>

## Boiler capacity and turndown

**Boiler capacity** is the maximum heat output (kW). **Turndown** is the ratio of
maximum to minimum load: how far down you can modulate the burner while still
burning cleanly.

- **Fixed-rate burners** (on/off only): no turndown. The boiler runs at full power
  or is off. Efficient only if load is stable; inefficient in buildings where demand
  varies minute-to-minute.
- **Modulating burners**: 4:1 to 10:1 turndown typical. The burner throttles to match
  demand. Much better for variable-load systems.

Oversized boilers (capacity >> typical load) are common and expensive. A 500 kW boiler
running average load of 150 kW spends most of its time at 30% capacity, where:
- Cycling losses mount (burner starts/stops repeatedly)
- Casing loss becomes a larger % of useful output
- Efficiency suffers

<Callout variant="tip">
If you find an oversized boiler running low load, either fit a smaller boiler or
fit a modulating burner to reduce cycling losses. Sizing to actual load is often
cheaper than you think.
</Callout>

## Cast iron vs steel

**Cast-iron sectional boilers** are made of bolted cast-iron sections. They're common
in older systems, especially in the UK.

- **Pros:** robust, tolerant of corrosion and poor water treatment, easy to replace
  sections
- **Cons:** slow to heat up, lower efficiency, harder to retrofit equipment

**Steel boilers** are welded. Most new boilers are steel.

- **Pros:** more compact, faster to respond, higher efficiency potential
- **Cons:** more sensitive to corrosion and requires better water treatment

In energy terms, the direction of travel is clear: modern steel boilers with
modulating burners and condensing heat recovery are 10–15 percentage points more
efficient than cast-iron sectional boilers from the 1990s. If you're managing an
old site, this often represents the single biggest lever for energy saving.

## Fuel flexibility

Most boilers burn **natural gas** (the standard in the UK) or **oil** (common in
rural sites without mains gas). Some can burn both, switching between them.

- Dual-fuel boilers cost more but provide security of supply
- Oil burners need more maintenance (nozzle cleaning, filter changes)
- Biofuel (biomass pellets) is increasingly available but requires a different
  boiler type

The fuel choice often drives the system choice, but for energy efficiency, **the
boiler type and its controls matter more than the fuel**.
