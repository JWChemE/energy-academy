Boiler efficiency is about **how much of the fuel energy ends up heating water**, not
lost up the flue or through the casing. There are two ways to think about it: the
energy lost and the efficiency number.

## The efficiency gap: input to output

An old or poorly tuned boiler might be only 80% efficient. That means **20% of the
fuel energy is wasted**. A modern efficient boiler might be 92%. The difference:
**12 percentage points**. On a 500 kW boiler running 4,000 hours a year, that's:

- 500 kW × 4,000 h ÷ 0.80 = **2,500,000 kWh/year** at 80% efficiency
- 500 kW × 4,000 h ÷ 0.92 = **2,174,000 kWh/year** at 92% efficiency
- **Difference: 326,000 kWh/year**: about £40,000 at today's gas prices

That is the size of the prize in boiler efficiency.

## How boiler efficiency is measured

The standard test (BS 3958 / EN 303) runs a boiler under fixed conditions and
measures:

- **Fuel input** (calorific value of the gas burned)
- **Useful heat output** (temperature rise of the water and flow rate)
- **Losses** (temperature of flue gas, radiation/convection from the casing)

The efficiency is simply:

> **Efficiency = Useful heat output ÷ Fuel input**

This is called the **net calorific value (NCV)** or **lower heating value (LHV)**
basis, because it doesn't count the latent heat in water vapour that condenses in
the boiler. Condensing boilers, which do capture that heat, report higher
efficiencies (sometimes >100% on the NCV basis, which can seem odd until you
understand the maths).

## Why boilers lose efficiency in the field

The nameplate efficiency is measured in a lab at steady state. In the real world,
boilers lose efficiency through:

1. **Fouling**: soot and scale on heating surfaces insulate the water from heat.
2. **Excess air drift**: burners gradually run richer (more fuel relative to air),
   starving the system of oxygen or running too lean.
3. **Burner degradation**: worn nozzles and electrodes misalign the fuel spray.
4. **Control hunting**: modulating burners that cycle on/off instead of running
   smoothly waste energy in transients.
5. **Low-load operation**: if the boiler is oversized and runs at 20% of capacity
   most of the time, efficiency drops because of standing losses (casing loss as a
   % of useful output).

<Callout variant="key" title="Nameplate efficiency is not field efficiency">
A 92%-efficient boiler in the factory might be 87% in the field after a year of
fouling and drift. This is why regular burner tuning is so valuable.
</Callout>

## Testing boiler efficiency on-site

A portable flue-gas analyser (£1,000–3,000) measures:

- **Flue-gas temperature** → gives you an estimate of sensible loss up the flue
- **O₂** → tells you excess air
- **CO** → tells you if combustion is complete

From these, you can calculate a **field efficiency** that's often surprisingly
different from the nameplate:

> **Estimated efficiency ≈ 100% - (flue loss%) - (casing loss%) - (other loss%)**

A boiler running at 120 °C flue gas with 8% O₂ might be running at 88%. Running at
160 °C with 12% O₂ might only be 82%. The difference is **20 year of lost savings**.

<Callout variant="tip">
Measure a boiler when cold (start of day) and when hot (steady state after 30 min).
The difference tells you whether the stack damper is sealing properly and whether
the casing is losing heat.
</Callout>

## Flue-gas temperature and the baseline

Higher flue-gas temperature is always a sign of lost energy. A well-tuned boiler
runs with:

- **Condensing boiler**: 45–55 °C (if return water is cold enough to condense)
- **Non-condensing boiler**: 140–160 °C

If a non-condensing boiler is running at 180 °C or higher, something is wrong:
fouling, excess air, or a failing heat exchanger.

Lowering flue-gas temperature by 10 °C (by cleaning, tuning air/fuel ratio, or
improving the heat exchanger) gains about **1% efficiency**. On a 500 kW boiler,
that's 5 kW of energy saved continuously, or 40 MWh/year.

## The economics of efficiency

Boiler efficiency improvements often pay back in **1–3 years**:

- **Cleaning the boiler**: £500–2,000 → ~0.5–1% efficiency gain
- **Burner tuning**: £800–1,500 → ~1–2% gain
- **Boiler replacement** (old to modern efficient): £10,000–25,000 → 10–15% gain over 15 years

A modern, efficient boiler is table stakes for energy management. If you're running
a 20-year-old boiler at 80% efficiency, replacement is not a nice-to-have: it's
often the single highest-return project on site.
