You need excess air to burn fuel completely. But more is not better. Too much excess
air wastes energy up the flue; too little leaves CO and incomplete combustion. The
art of boiler tuning is finding the sweet spot.

## The efficiency penalty of excess air

Nitrogen in air doesn't participate in combustion: it just heats up and leaves via
the flue, carrying sensible heat. The more excess air, the more nitrogen goes up the
flue.

The rule of thumb:

> **Every 1% of excess air above stoichiometric costs about 0.1% efficiency**

Practical example:
- **Stoichiometric (theoretical)**: 0% O₂ in flue gas (impossible to achieve)
- **10% excess air**: ~1.8% O₂ in flue gas, efficiency ~99% (never happens)
- **20% excess air**: ~3.6% O₂ in flue gas, efficiency ~98%
- **40% excess air**: ~7.2% O₂ in flue gas, efficiency ~96%
- **80% excess air**: ~14.4% O₂ in flue gas, efficiency ~92%

A boiler running at 80% excess air (a not-uncommon sight on old plants) is throwing
away **~6 percentage points of efficiency** compared to one running 20% excess. On a
500 kW boiler, that's **30 kW of continuous waste**, or roughly **250 MWh/year**.

<Callout variant="key" title="Measure, don't guess">
The only way to know excess air is to measure O₂ in the flue gas. A flue-gas
analyser gives you the number in seconds; estimation by visual inspection of flame
colour is guesswork.
</Callout>

## Why we run excess air anyway

If excess air wastes energy, why not run at stoichiometric? The answer: **incomplete
combustion is worse**.

At or near stoichiometric air, small variations in burner tuning lead to pockets of
fuel-rich mixture (not enough oxygen). That unburned fuel becomes:
- **Carbon monoxide (CO)**: poisonous and an energy loss
- **Smoke and soot**: dirty and fouls the boiler
- **Unburned hydrocarbons**: lost fuel

A small amount of CO (50 ppm) is acceptable and indicates good burner efficiency. A
lot of CO (>200 ppm) means the fuel is not burning and you're wasting money.

The **minimum safe excess air** is the point where CO stays low (below 50 ppm) under
typical operating conditions. For most burners, that's **10–20% excess air**, or
**2–4% O₂ in the flue gas**.

## Tuning the air/fuel ratio

A burner has two controls:

- **Fuel**: gas pressure or oil-pump setting
- **Air**: damper position on the air inlet

To tune a burner:

- **Start with gas/oil rate** fixed to the desired output (say, 80% of max)
- **Adjust air damper** until O₂ reads around 3%. CO should be below 50 ppm
- **Check flue temperature**: if it's unusually high (>170 °C for non-condensing),
  something is wrong (fouling, poor heat exchange)
- **Repeat at different loads**: test 100%, 75%, 50%, and 25% if the burner modulates

<Callout variant="warning" title="Tuning on O₂ alone can hide fouling">
If O₂ is good (3%) but flue temperature is high (180 °C), the boiler is dirty.
Clean it first, then retune the air/fuel ratio.
</Callout>

## Lambda and oxygen trim

Modern burners have **oxygen trim**: automatic sensors that adjust the air damper to
maintain a target O₂. This is called **lambda control** (lambda = air/fuel ratio).

- **Manual tuning**: you adjust the damper once and hope it doesn't drift
- **Automatic lambda trim**: the sensor actively adjusts damper to keep O₂ at a
  setpoint (typically 3%)

Lambda trim systems cost £1,500–3,000 to retrofit and can save **1–2% efficiency**
by preventing drift. Over a year on a 500 kW boiler, that's roughly **50 MWh** and
**£5,000–6,000**.

<Callout variant="tip">
If you manage a boiler burning >1,000 hours/year, oxygen trim pays for itself. It's
one of the fastest payback retrofits available.
</Callout>

## Practical tuning: a checklist

- **Get a flue-gas analyser** (or hire someone with one)
- **Clean the boiler first** (soot reduces efficiency)
- **Measure O₂, CO, and flue temperature** at full and part load
- **Adjust the air damper** to target 3% O₂ and below 50 ppm CO
- **Record the before/after** efficiency and fuel consumption
- **Follow up every 6–12 months**: drift happens

A well-tuned boiler stays tuned for a year or so, then drifts. A poorly tuned one
wastes 2–5% right from day one.
