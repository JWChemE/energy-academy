Once you've assessed a boiler plant, the question is: fix it, or replace it? And if
you replace, what with? The answer depends on age, efficiency, capital budget, and
your heating system's maturity.

## The retrofit ladder: improvements you can make without replacing

### Step 1: Cleaning and basic tuning (cost: 500–1500 GBP)

If the boiler is dirty, cleaning and retuning the burner often recover 1–2% efficiency.

- **Clean boiler tubes** (internal and external) to restore heat transfer
- **Tune air/fuel ratio**: measure O₂ and adjust to 3%
- **Check and replace gaskets/seals** to reduce air leaks
- **Bleed air from pipes** (steam systems)

**Payback:** often under 6 months. If any boiler is more than a year out of service, this
is worth doing.

### Step 2: Oxygen trim retrofit (1,500 GBP–3,000)

Install an **automatic oxygen sensor** that maintains the burner at the optimal
air/fuel ratio continuously.

- Prevents drift over time
- Gains typically 1–2% efficiency
- Works on burners that modulate (not on/off-only)

**Payback:** 1–2 years on a 300+ kW boiler. Strong candidate if the boiler is
otherwise sound.

### Step 3: Weather compensation control (2,000 GBP–4,000)

If the boiler has no outdoor sensor or automated setpoint control, add one.

- Lowers the setpoint on mild days
- Reduces distribution losses
- Typical savings: 1–3% of heating energy

**Payback:** 1–3 years.

### Step 4: Blowdown reduction (2,000 GBP–8,000)

Depending on water hardness:

- **Water softener** (3,000 GBP–8,000): reduces blowdown from 3% to 0.5%,
  saving 3,000 GBP–5,000/year. **Payback: 1–2 years.**
- **Blowdown heat recovery** (2,000 GBP–5,000): captures 80% of blowdown heat,
  saving 2,000 GBP–3,500/year. **Payback: 1–2 years.**

### Step 5: Burner or system upgrades (5,000 GBP–15,000)

- **Upgrade to a modulating burner** (on an old fixed-rate boiler): reduces cycling
  losses. Gains ~1–2%.
- **Add a second smaller boiler** (if space permits) for better part-load efficiency.
- **Upgrade distribution system** (balance, insulation, faster pump) to reduce
  setpoint needs.

**Payback:** 2–4 years typically.

At this point, you've exhausted most no/low-capital improvements. If the boiler is
still old (>20 years), replacement often becomes the better option.

## Boiler replacement: what are your options?

### Option 1: Like-for-like traditional boiler replacement

Replace the old boiler with a new one of the same type and size.

- **Cost:** 8,000 GBP–20,000 fitted (depending on size and complexity)
- **Efficiency gain:** typically 8–12 percentage points (old 80% → new 92%)
- **Payback:** often 5–8 years on heating energy alone
- **Pros:** simple, proven, fits existing systems
- **Cons:** doesn't take advantage of modern heating strategies (e.g., low-temperature
  operation)

### Option 2: Condensing boiler replacement

A modern condensing boiler is 95%+ efficient, capturing latent heat.

- **Cost:** 10,000 GBP–25,000 fitted (slightly more than traditional)
- **Efficiency gain:** 12–20 percentage points (old 80% → new 96%+)
- **BUT:** requires return water below 55 °C to fully condense (see caveat below)
- **Payback:** 4–6 years if your system can achieve low returns
- **Pros:** very high efficiency, compact, faster response
- **Cons:** needs system rebalancing or upgrading to run low-temperature

<Callout variant="warning" title="Condensing boiler caveat: return temperature must be low">
If your heating system is old (large radiators, 15–20 °C ΔT, high return temperature),
a condensing boiler will not condense and becomes just an expensive traditional
boiler. Upgrading to condensing often requires simultaneously upgrading the system
(better radiators, lower flow temperatures, or adding thermostatic radiator valves).

Total cost: boiler + system upgrade could be 15,000 GBP–30,000. Still often the right
move, but budget accordingly.
</Callout>

### Option 3: Heat pump replacement

A **air-source heat pump** or **ground-source heat pump** replaces the boiler entirely
and uses electricity (plus renewable energy from air or ground) instead of fuel.

- **Cost:** 12,000 GBP–35,000 fitted (depending on type and system upgrades)
- **Efficiency gain:** coefficient of performance 3–4, meaning 1 kW of electricity
  delivers 3–4 kW of heat. Carbon intensity depends on grid mix.
- **Payback:** 7–15 years (longer than boiler replacement, but lower lifetime
  carbon)
- **Pros:** future-proof, very low carbon (especially as grid decarbonises),
  technology improving fast
- **Cons:** higher capital, requires system rebalancing (low temperature), upfront
  noise/space concerns (especially ground-source)

### Option 4: Hybrid or multi-source

Some modern systems combine boiler + heat pump:
- **Heat pump** provides the bulk of heating on mild days (cheaper to run)
- **Boiler** covers peaks and cold snaps
- **Solar thermal** or **thermal storage** may be added for seasonal shifting

More complex, but increasingly common in new builds and major retrofits.

<Callout variant="key" title="Boiler replacement is often part of a building-wide strategy">
The best boiler replacement decision depends on the building: age, insulation,
radiator size, system sophistication, local grid carbon intensity, and whether you're
also improving fabric or controls.

Don't replace the boiler in isolation; think about the whole system.
</Callout>

## Decision tree

**Old boiler (under 85% efficient), >15 years old?**
→ Likely candidate for replacement. Explore condensing (if system can support it) or heat pump (if capital available and building suitable).

**Boiler 10–15 years old, 88–92% efficient, but controls are poor?**
→ Retrofit weather compensation and oxygen trim first. Replacement in 5–10 years.

**Boiler under 10 years old?**
→ Focus on tuning, controls, and system balancing. Replacement not yet justified
unless specific problem (failed heat exchanger, etc.).

**No capital budget right now?**
→ Cleaning, tuning, and weather compensation give 2–3% efficiency improvements for
under 5,000 GBP and buy you time.

## Procurement and installation

If you're replacing:

1. **Get a heating system designer** (not just an installer) to spec the right boiler
   and any system changes (new radiators, balancing, insulation, controls)
2. **Require a post-installation commissioning report**: make sure it's tuned and
   working as designed
3. **Arrange quarterly monitoring** (bill analysis, spot efficiency checks) for the
   first year to catch drift early
