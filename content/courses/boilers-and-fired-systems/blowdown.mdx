Blowdown is **purposely draining hot water from the boiler** to remove dissolved minerals
before they form scale. If you don't blow down, the boiler gets encrusted with hard
scale that insulates the water from heat and reduces efficiency. But blowdown has a
real energy cost, and many boilers blow down far more than necessary.

## Why minerals accumulate

Mains water contains dissolved salts — calcium, magnesium, bicarbonates. When water
is heated, some of these become insoluble and precipitate as scale on the inside of
tubes. Scale is:
- A thermal insulator (3 mm of scale can cut heat transfer by 20%)
- A mechanical nuisance (it breaks off, fouls pumps and controls)

The hotter the water and the harder the water (more minerals), the faster scale forms.

## How blowdown works

The simplest approach: **open a valve at the bottom of the boiler and let hot water
drain out**. Fresh water flows in from the feed, replacing it. The blowdown water
carries the dissolved minerals away.

**Continuous blowdown** — a small amount of water (say, 2–5% of boiler flow)
continuously drains. This is simple but wasteful because you're discarding a lot of
heat.

**Intermittent blowdown** — you manually open the drain when needed (maybe once a
week, 10 seconds per drain). This wastes less energy but requires attention.

## The energy cost of blowdown

Blowdown is expensive because **you're throwing away hot water and the energy it
took to heat it**.

Example: a 500 kW boiler running 4,000 hours/year with continuous blowdown at 3%:

- **Blowdown rate**: 500 kW ÷ (4.2 kJ/kg·K × ΔT) ≈ 100 L/hour (rough estimate,
  depends on system ΔT)
- **3% loss**: 3 L/hour × 4,000 h = 12,000 L/year of hot water at ~70 °C
- **Enthalpy loss**: 12,000 L × 70 K × 4.2 kJ/kg ≈ 3,500 MWh/year (!!)

Wait — that doesn't seem right. Let me recalculate: the energy loss is the *extra*
fuel needed to heat the incoming cold water.

Better calculation:
- Assume blowdown at 3% = 3 kW of hot water out, replaced by cold feed water
- To heat that from 10 °C to 70 °C, you need 3 kW × (70–10)/(ΔT of useful output)
- If useful ΔT is 15 K, that's **12 kW of extra fuel use** just for blowdown
- Over 4,000 hours: **48 MWh/year**, or roughly **£6,000/year** in gas

That's significant. **Cutting blowdown by half saves £3,000/year.**

<Callout variant="key" title="Blowdown is visible waste">
Every litre of hot water that goes down the drain had to be heated. Reducing blowdown
is one of the easiest efficiency wins — and it doesn't require capital spending.
</Callout>

## How much blowdown do you actually need?

The answer depends on **water hardness** (measured in ppm of dissolved solids) and
**boiler type**:

- **Soft water** (exceeding 200 ppm hardness): very little blowdown needed, maybe 0.5% continuous
- **Hard water** (>400 ppm): may need 3–5% continuous to prevent scaling

A **water analysis** costs £50–100 and tells you the exact mineral content. Many
facilities blow down based on habit ("we drain every morning") rather than necessity.

If you don't know the water hardness, measure it. Then:
- **Start with 1% continuous blowdown** and monitor for scale
- **If scale forms, increase gradually** (don't guess)
- **If no scale appears over a month, you may be overdoing it**

## Better strategies than continuous blowdown

### Softening the water

Install a **water softener** upstream of the boiler. It removes hardness (calcium,
magnesium) so less blowdown is needed.

- **Cost**: £3,000–8,000 fitted
- **Savings**: often reduces blowdown from 3% to 0.5%, saving £4,000+/year
- **Payback**: 1–2 years on a large boiler

### Intermittent blowdown

Skip continuous blowdown and open a valve by hand once or twice a week for a few
seconds. This:
- Wastes less energy (less frequent)
- Requires discipline and monitoring

Works well for smaller plants where someone checks the boiler anyway.

### Blowdown heat recovery

Some modern systems capture the energy from blowdown water before it goes to drain:
- Heat the incoming feed water, or
- Preheat the air (if it's hot enough)

This recovers maybe 80% of the heat in blowdown water.

- **Cost**: £2,000–5,000
- **Savings**: 80–90% of blowdown losses, so £2,000–3,500/year on a large boiler
- **Payback**: 1–2 years

<Callout variant="tip">
If you have a large boiler (>300 kW) and hard water, blowdown is costing you serious
money. A water softener or blowdown-heat-recovery system often pays back in under
two years.
</Callout>

## Practical action

1. **Check what you're blowing down**: is it continuous (valve always open) or
   intermittent (manual drain)?
2. **Get a water analysis** from your water supplier or a treatment company
3. **If continuous blowdown >1%**, consider softening or heat recovery
4. **If intermittent, check that blowdown actually happens regularly** — if the
   operator forgets, scale will form and ruin efficiency
