Incentives reinforce energy-conscious behaviour, but their design is subtle. The wrong incentives can backfire; the right ones - often non-financial - sustain behaviour and build culture. Understanding the types and their effects is key.

## Why incentives in behaviour change

Behaviour that is reinforced gets repeated (the change-model link). Incentives - rewards and recognition - provide that reinforcement, helping move new behaviours through trial into lasting habit. But incentives are double-edged: well-designed, they sustain and spread good behaviour; poorly designed, they can undermine the very motivation they aim to build. Their design demands care.

## Financial and non-financial

Incentives fall into two broad types (the leadership-incentives link):

**Financial incentives** - money-based rewards:
- Bonuses tied to energy performance
- Prizes and rewards in competitions
- Shared-savings schemes

**Non-financial incentives** - everything else:
- Recognition and praise
- Awards and celebration
- Status and visibility
- A sense of achievement and purpose

A common assumption is that financial incentives are the most powerful. In fact, for sustained behaviour change, non-financial incentives are often more effective and durable - a crucial and counterintuitive insight.

## The limits of financial incentives

Financial incentives have real limitations for behaviour change:
- **Conditional** - behaviour can become dependent on the payment, stopping when the money stops
- **Crowding out** - extrinsic rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation (people who acted from genuine care may come to act only for the reward, weakening the deeper motivation)
- **Cost** - financial incentives cost money, and at scale this adds up
- **Gaming** - rewarding a metric can tempt people to game it rather than genuinely change

This does not mean financial incentives have no place - they can be effective, especially for teams and clear targets - but relying on them alone is risky. The "crowding out" effect is particularly important: paying people to do what they would have done from genuine motivation can actually reduce their commitment.

## The power of recognition

**Recognition** - acknowledging and celebrating good behaviour - is often the most powerful and durable incentive (the leadership-incentives link):
- People deeply value being seen and appreciated
- It is cheap (or free) yet highly motivating
- It reinforces intrinsic motivation rather than undermining it
- It is visible, spreading social norms (others see what is valued - the psychology link)

Effective recognition is specific (naming the action and result), timely, visible, and sincere. A genuine, public acknowledgement of someone's contribution often motivates more, and more lastingly, than a cash reward - while costing almost nothing and reinforcing the deeper motivation that sustains behaviour.

## Intrinsic motivation

The deepest and most durable driver is **intrinsic motivation** - people acting because they genuinely care (the psychology link):
- Connected to values and purpose (the leadership link)
- Self-sustaining (it does not depend on external reward)
- Resilient (it persists when incentives are absent)

The most effective behaviour-change programmes tap intrinsic motivation - connecting energy behaviour to what people genuinely care about (cost, climate, pride, purpose) - rather than relying on extrinsic rewards. Incentives, especially recognition, are then used to reinforce and celebrate this intrinsic motivation, not to replace it. Behaviour rooted in genuine care outlasts behaviour bought with rewards.

## Team and competitive incentives

Incentives can harness social and competitive dynamics (the campaign link):
- **Team incentives** - rewarding collective achievement, building peer support and shared effort
- **Competitions** - inter-team or inter-site, tapping social motivation and fun (the campaign link)
- **League tables and recognition** - visible comparison driving engagement (social norms)

Team and competitive approaches often work well because they harness peer effects and make energy engaging, while avoiding some pitfalls of individual financial rewards. They build collective ownership and social reinforcement rather than individual transactions.

## Avoiding perverse incentives

Badly designed incentives can backfire (the leadership-incentives link):
- **Gaming** - rewarding a crude metric tempts manipulation rather than genuine change
- **Crowding out** - extrinsic rewards undermining intrinsic motivation
- **Unfairness** - incentives perceived as unfair breeding resentment
- **Division** - excessive individual competition damaging cooperation
- **Wrong behaviour** - rewarding the wrong thing driving unintended consequences

Designing incentives around the genuine goal, measured fairly (the M&V link), and watching for unintended effects, avoids these traps. The aim is to reinforce genuine, beneficial behaviour and the intrinsic motivation behind it - not to create a game or undermine the deeper commitment.

## Incentives within culture

The key principle is that incentives are a tool within a broader approach, not a substitute for genuine engagement (the culture link). The most effective approach:
- Builds **intrinsic motivation** (purpose, values - the foundation)
- Provides **capability** (knowledge and ease - the change-model link)
- Uses **recognition** to reinforce and celebrate good behaviour
- Uses **financial and competitive incentives** judiciously where they help
- Watches for **perverse effects**

Used to reinforce a culture built on genuine commitment and purpose, incentives - especially recognition - accelerate and sustain behaviour change. Used alone, as rewards in a culture that does not care, they buy only temporary, conditional compliance. The skill, as the leadership course also stresses, is using incentives to nurture genuine, lasting behaviour - so that, over time, the behaviour becomes habit and culture, outlasting any incentive.
