A central energy team cannot reach everyone or change behaviour everywhere. A network of energy champions - enthusiasts distributed throughout the organisation - extends the programme's reach and harnesses the power of peer influence.

## The reach problem

The energy manager and a small team cannot personally engage everyone in a large organisation, cannot be present in every department, and lack the local knowledge and peer relationships that drive behaviour. A central team pushing messages out has limited reach and impact. The solution is to distribute the effort through a network of champions - people embedded throughout the organisation who carry the energy message locally.

## What champions do

**Energy champions** are volunteers or nominated individuals across the organisation who promote energy awareness and action in their own areas:
- **Spreading the message** - communicating energy initiatives locally
- **Prompting behaviour** - encouraging energy-conscious actions among peers
- **Local knowledge** - identifying area-specific opportunities and barriers
- **Feedback** - channelling ideas and concerns back to the energy team
- **Leading by example** - modelling energy-conscious behaviour
- **Sustaining attention** - keeping energy present locally between central campaigns

Champions are the energy programme's presence on the ground - its eyes, ears, and voice throughout the organisation.

## The power of peer influence

Champions are effective because of **peer influence** (the social-norm psychology - the psychology link):
- People are influenced more by peers than by management messages
- A colleague encouraging energy-conscious behaviour is more persuasive than a corporate email
- Champions make energy-conscious behaviour visible and normal locally (social norms)
- Peer-to-peer communication is more credible and relatable

This is the key insight: behaviour change spreads more effectively peer-to-peer than top-down. Champions harness this, turning the programme from a central broadcast into a distributed, peer-led movement. A respected colleague who champions energy shifts local norms in a way no management directive can.

## Building a champion network

Establishing an effective network involves:
- **Recruiting** - finding enthusiasts (volunteers are best - intrinsically motivated), with coverage across areas
- **Training** - equipping champions with knowledge and skills (the capability link)
- **Supporting** - providing materials, information, and backing
- **Connecting** - linking champions into a network for mutual support and shared learning
- **Empowering** - giving them the remit and confidence to act
- **Recognising** - valuing and rewarding their contribution (the incentives link)

A champion network needs nurturing - champions who are recruited and then abandoned fade. Ongoing support, connection, empowerment, and recognition keep the network active and effective.

## Motivating champions

Champions are usually volunteers giving discretionary effort, so their motivation matters:
- **Purpose** - connecting their role to a cause they care about (intrinsic motivation - the psychology link)
- **Recognition** - valuing and celebrating their contribution (the incentives link)
- **Empowerment** - giving them real influence and ownership
- **Support** - making the role rewarding and not burdensome
- **Community** - the network itself, connecting like-minded people

Champions sustain their effort when the role is meaningful, supported, recognised, and enjoyable. Tapping intrinsic motivation - their genuine care about energy and climate - is what sustains volunteer champions over time, far more than any token reward.

## Champions and culture

The champion network is a key mechanism for building an energy culture (the leadership link):
- It distributes ownership of energy throughout the organisation (not just the energy team)
- It embeds energy-consciousness locally, in every area
- It harnesses peer influence to shift social norms
- It creates a grassroots movement rather than a top-down initiative

Over time, a strong champion network helps shift the organisation from one where energy is the energy manager's concern to one where it is owned and championed throughout - the cultural transformation that the leadership course describes. Champions are how the energy programme reaches into every corner and makes energy-consciousness a distributed, peer-led norm.

## The multiplier effect

The fundamental value of champions is the multiplier effect: they extend the energy manager's reach and impact far beyond what a central team could achieve alone. A network of engaged champions, each influencing their own area, multiplies the programme's reach, harnesses peer influence, provides local knowledge and feedback, and distributes ownership. Building and supporting this network is one of the highest-leverage activities in behaviour change - turning a small central team into a movement spread throughout the organisation. Combined with engaging campaigns, effective communication, incentives, and the habit-building of the following lessons, the champion network is central to delivering and sustaining behaviour change at scale.
