Behaviour change programmes consume effort and need to justify it. Measuring behaviour change - both the behaviours and the energy savings they produce - is essential to managing programmes, proving their value, and improving them.

## Why measure behaviour change

Measurement serves several purposes:
- **Managing** the programme - knowing what is working and what is not
- **Proving** value - demonstrating the savings to justify the effort and budget (the investment-case link)
- **Improving** - identifying what to reinforce and what to change
- **Sustaining** support - showing results to maintain leadership backing (the leadership link)

Without measurement, a behaviour programme is flying blind - unable to show its worth or to improve. But measuring behaviour change is genuinely harder than measuring a technical retrofit, which is part of why it is often neglected.

## What to measure

Behaviour change can be measured at several levels:

**Engagement metrics** - the inputs and participation:
- Awareness levels (surveys)
- Participation in campaigns and activities
- Champion network size and activity
- Communication reach and response

**Behaviour metrics** - the behaviours themselves:
- Observed behaviours (lights off, equipment switched off, windows closed)
- Self-reported behaviour (surveys)
- Proxy indicators (out-of-hours energy use as a sign of switch-off behaviour)

**Outcome metrics** - the energy and cost results:
- Energy savings attributable to behaviour
- Cost savings
- Carbon reductions

Measuring across these levels - from engagement through behaviour to energy outcomes - gives a full picture of whether the programme is working and where.

## The attribution challenge

The central difficulty in measuring behaviour change is **attribution** - separating behaviour-driven savings from everything else (the M&V link):
- Energy use changes for many reasons (weather, occupancy, production, technical measures)
- Behaviour change is just one factor, and often a modest one
- Isolating the behaviour effect from the noise is genuinely hard

This is the measurement and verification challenge applied to behaviour. The same disciplines help:
- **Normalising** for weather, occupancy, and production (the M&V link)
- **Baselines** against which to measure change
- **Controls** - comparing engaged areas with unengaged ones
- **Proxy indicators** - out-of-hours or weekend energy use isolates behaviour (equipment left on when no one is there is almost purely behavioural)

Out-of-hours energy use is a particularly useful proxy: it strips out occupancy-driven and process energy, leaving largely the behavioural "leftovers" that switch-off campaigns target.

## Proxy indicators

Because directly attributing savings is hard, **proxy indicators** are valuable:
- **Out-of-hours / baseload energy** - energy used when the building is empty, largely behavioural waste
- **Weekend / holiday consumption** - similar, isolating the behavioural baseline
- **Specific equipment monitoring** - tracking whether particular loads are switched off
- **Spot observations** - counting lights left on, equipment idle

These proxies sidestep the full attribution problem by focusing on energy use that is predominantly behavioural - giving a clearer read on whether switch-off and similar behaviours are improving.

## Engagement versus outcomes

A balanced measurement approach tracks both engagement and outcomes:
- **Engagement metrics** show whether people are participating and aware - leading indicators that the programme is reaching people
- **Outcome metrics** show whether energy is actually being saved - the ultimate test

Engagement without savings suggests the activity is not translating into behaviour; savings validate the engagement. Tracking both reveals not just whether savings are happening but why - whether the programme is engaging people and whether that engagement is producing results.

## Using measurement to improve

The point of measurement is action:
- **Reinforce** what works - successful campaigns, effective channels, engaged areas
- **Address** what does not - low engagement, areas without savings, fading behaviour
- **Adapt** the programme based on the evidence
- **Report** results to sustain support and recognise success (the leadership and incentives links)

Measurement turns behaviour change from a hopeful activity into a managed, improving programme - one that can demonstrate its value, learn from experience, and get better over time.

## Honest measurement

As throughout this academy, integrity matters (the M&V link). Behaviour-change savings are easy to overstate - tempting to claim weather-driven or coincidental savings as behavioural success. Honest measurement:
- Uses proper baselines and normalisation
- Attributes conservatively
- Acknowledges uncertainty
- Distinguishes behaviour from technical and external effects

Credible, honest measurement of behaviour change builds the credibility that sustains the programme and the energy function (the self-leadership link). Overstated behaviour claims, like overstated savings anywhere, eventually undermine trust. Measured honestly, behaviour change can demonstrate real, valuable, low-cost savings - justifying its place in the energy manager's toolkit and providing the evidence to keep improving it. The following lessons turn to delivering the change: designing campaigns, building champion networks, using incentives, and sustaining the new behaviours over time.
