Much energy use is determined not by equipment but by behaviour - the countless daily choices of the people who occupy buildings and operate processes. Changing that behaviour requires understanding the psychology that drives it.

## Behaviour matters

A large share of energy use is behavioural: lights left on, windows opened with the heating running, equipment left idle, thermostats nudged up. No amount of efficient equipment captures these losses if behaviour wastes the savings (the rebound effect). Conversely, engaged behaviour can deliver significant savings at little cost. Understanding and changing behaviour is therefore a core part of energy management - and it begins with how people actually make decisions.

## People are not rational calculators

A common but flawed assumption is that people use energy rationally - that giving them information and financial incentives will change behaviour. In reality, human behaviour is driven by far more than rational calculation:
- **Habit** - much behaviour is automatic, not consciously decided
- **Convenience** - people take the easy path
- **Social norms** - people do what others do
- **Cognitive biases** - systematic quirks in how people think and decide
- **Emotion and identity** - what people feel and who they think they are

Energy behaviour change that ignores this psychology and relies only on information and rational appeals largely fails. Effective change works with how people actually think and behave.

## The power of habit

Much energy behaviour is **habitual** - performed automatically, without conscious thought:
- Habits are triggered by context and repeated without decision
- They are resistant to information (knowing better does not break a habit)
- They form through repetition and reinforcement

This has a crucial implication: changing energy behaviour often means changing habits, which requires more than information - it requires disrupting old habits and building new ones through repetition, cues, and reinforcement (the next lesson on the change process). A poster telling people to switch off does not break the habit of leaving lights on.

## Social norms

People are powerfully influenced by what others do - **social norms**:
- People conform to the perceived normal behaviour of their group
- "Most of your colleagues switch off their monitors" is more effective than "switching off saves energy"
- Visible behaviour of peers shapes individual behaviour
- Norms can be harnessed to drive energy-conscious behaviour

Social norms are one of the most powerful behaviour-change levers (used in many successful programmes). Making energy-conscious behaviour visible and normal - and showing that peers do it - drives change more effectively than facts alone.

## Nudges and choice architecture

The insight that people are predictably influenced by context gives rise to **nudges** - small changes to the environment that steer behaviour without restricting choice:
- **Defaults** - setting the energy-efficient option as the default (people tend to stick with defaults), e.g. equipment defaulting to power-save
- **Salience** - making energy use visible and prominent (displays, feedback)
- **Ease** - making the efficient choice the easy choice
- **Prompts** - timely reminders at the moment of decision

Nudges work with human psychology rather than against it, steering behaviour through the design of the environment. They are often more effective - and cheaper - than information campaigns or financial incentives, because they address how people actually decide.

## Motivation: intrinsic and extrinsic

Understanding what motivates people is central:
- **Extrinsic motivation** - external rewards and punishments (money, rules)
- **Intrinsic motivation** - internal drivers (values, purpose, satisfaction, identity)

While extrinsic motivators (incentives) have a role (the incentives lesson), intrinsic motivation is often more powerful and durable. People who act on energy because they care about it - it aligns with their values and identity - sustain the behaviour far longer than those acting only for a reward. Connecting energy behaviour to purpose and values (the leadership and culture links) taps this deeper, more lasting motivation.

## The foundation for change

Understanding the psychology of energy behaviour - the roles of habit, social norms, nudges, biases, and motivation - is the foundation for effective behaviour change. It explains why information-only campaigns fail and reveals what actually works: disrupting habits, harnessing social norms, designing nudges, making efficient choices easy, and connecting to intrinsic motivation. The following lessons build on this foundation: the process of changing behaviour, designing engagement campaigns, and sustaining new habits over time. Behaviour change is not about telling people facts - it is about understanding and working with the psychology that drives what they actually do.
