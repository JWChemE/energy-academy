Engagement campaigns are the visible face of behaviour change - the activities, themes, and events that raise awareness and prompt action. Designing them well, around the psychology of how people change, is what makes them work.

## What campaigns do

An engagement campaign is a coordinated set of activities designed to raise awareness, build motivation, and prompt energy-conscious behaviour. Campaigns are how behaviour-change programmes reach people - turning the principles of psychology and the change journey into concrete activities people experience. Well-designed campaigns engage and move people; poorly designed ones (dull information dumps) wash over them.

## Designing around the change journey

Effective campaigns are built around how people actually change (the psychology and change-model links):
- **Awareness** - making the issue and the desired behaviour visible and salient
- **Motivation** - providing the compelling why, connecting to what people care about
- **Enabling** - making the behaviour easy, providing prompts and removing barriers
- **Reinforcement** - feedback and recognition that sustain the behaviour

A campaign that only informs (awareness) without motivating, enabling, and reinforcing will not change behaviour. Good campaign design addresses the whole journey.

## Themes and focus

Campaigns benefit from clear themes and focus:
- **Specific behaviours** - targeting particular actions (switch-off, heating, equipment) rather than vague "save energy"
- **Seasonal relevance** - heating behaviour in winter, cooling in summer, when the behaviour matters most
- **Memorable themes** - a campaign identity that engages and sticks
- **Achievable asks** - clear, specific, doable actions rather than overwhelming demands

A focused campaign around a specific, timely, achievable behaviour ("switch off this winter") is more effective than a diffuse, general appeal. People respond to clear, concrete asks they can act on.

## Making it engaging

Campaigns must compete for attention and engage people:
- **Emotion and story** - engaging hearts, not just informing minds (the communication link)
- **Visual and creative** - memorable, appealing materials
- **Interactive** - involving people (events, competitions, challenges)
- **Relevant** - connecting to what people care about (cost, climate, comfort, pride)
- **Positive** - inspiring and enabling, not hectoring or guilt-tripping

Dull, preachy, information-heavy campaigns fail. Engaging, creative, positive ones that involve people and connect to their motivations succeed. The campaign should make energy-conscious behaviour appealing and easy, not a chore imposed by management.

## Channels and reach

Campaigns use multiple channels to reach people (the communication link):
- **Digital** - email, intranet, screens, social channels
- **Physical** - posters, stickers, signage at the point of behaviour
- **Events** - launches, roadshows, energy days, competitions
- **Personal** - briefings, team meetings, champions (the next lesson)
- **Feedback** - displays showing energy use and progress

Different channels reach different people and reinforce the message. Point-of-behaviour prompts (a sticker by the light switch) are particularly effective - reaching people at the moment of the decision (the nudge link).

## Competitions and challenges

A popular and effective campaign format is competition:
- **Inter-team or inter-site competitions** - harnessing social motivation and fun
- **Challenges** - targets to achieve, milestones to reach
- **League tables** - visible comparison driving engagement (the social-norm link)
- **Recognition** - celebrating winners and participants (the incentives link)

Competitions tap social motivation, make energy engaging and fun, and harness peer effects. They are among the most effective campaign formats - turning energy saving into a shared, motivating challenge rather than a solitary chore.

## Feedback and visibility

Making energy use visible is powerful (the psychology link):
- **Energy displays** - showing real-time or regular consumption
- **Progress feedback** - showing savings achieved against targets
- **Comparison** - against peers, past performance, or benchmarks (social norms)

Feedback closes the loop, letting people see the effect of their behaviour - which both motivates and reinforces. Invisible energy is easy to waste; visible energy, with feedback on the impact of behaviour, prompts and sustains action.

## Sustaining and refreshing

Campaigns must be sustained and refreshed to avoid fatigue:
- **Ongoing** rather than one-off - behaviour change is a long journey (the change-model link)
- **Refreshed themes** - keeping it fresh to avoid people tuning out
- **Seasonal cycles** - aligning with when behaviours matter
- **Building on success** - reinforcing and extending what works

A single campaign creates a spike of attention that fades; a sustained programme of refreshed campaigns keeps energy in people's minds and progressively embeds behaviour. The goal is not a one-off event but an ongoing rhythm of engagement that, over time, shifts behaviour and builds culture (the leadership link).
