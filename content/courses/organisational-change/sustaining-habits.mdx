The hardest part of behaviour change is not starting new behaviours but sustaining them. Initial enthusiasm fades, old habits reassert themselves, and savings drift away. Sustaining change - turning behaviours into lasting habits - is where many programmes fail.

## The sustainability challenge

Behaviour change programmes often achieve initial success - a burst of awareness and action - only to see it fade as attention wanes and old habits return. This is the central challenge: not achieving change, but sustaining it. The energy savings from a behaviour campaign can evaporate within months without deliberate effort to embed and maintain the new behaviours. Understanding why change fades, and how to sustain it, is essential.

## Why behaviour reverts

New behaviours revert for understandable reasons (the psychology link):
- **Old habits are strong** - deeply ingrained automatic behaviours reassert themselves
- **Attention fades** - the campaign ends, the prompts disappear, energy slips from mind
- **Effort tires** - new behaviours that require conscious effort are abandoned for the easy default
- **Reinforcement stops** - without continued feedback and recognition, behaviour is not maintained
- **People change** - new staff arrive without the engagement, trained people leave

The **rebound effect** is a related phenomenon (the M&V link): after efficiency improvements, behaviour can drift toward using more (a more comfortable temperature, leaving efficient lights on longer), eroding the savings. Both reversion and rebound mean savings decay without sustained effort.

## Building genuine habits

The goal is to move behaviours from effortful, conscious actions into automatic **habits** that persist without ongoing effort (the change-model link):
- **Repetition** - habits form through consistent repetition until the behaviour becomes automatic
- **Cues** - linking the behaviour to consistent triggers (leaving a room cues switching off)
- **Ease** - making the behaviour easy enough to persist (the nudge link)
- **Consistency** - the same behaviour, repeated, until it requires no thought

Once a behaviour becomes a genuine habit, it sustains itself - performed automatically, resistant to fading. Reaching this point is the goal, and it requires sustained reinforcement through the repetition that builds the habit.

## Sustained reinforcement

Sustaining behaviour requires ongoing reinforcement, not a one-off push:
- **Continued communication** - keeping energy present (the communications link)
- **Ongoing feedback** - showing the continued impact of behaviour (the feedback link)
- **Refreshed campaigns** - maintaining attention without fatigue (the campaign link)
- **Continued recognition** - sustaining motivation (the incentives link)
- **Active champions** - maintaining local presence and peer influence (the champions link)

The programme must be ongoing, not a one-off event. This is why behaviour change is a long-term commitment, not a campaign - sustaining the reinforcement until behaviours become habits, and maintaining enough presence to keep habits alive and counter the constant pull of reversion.

## Embedding in systems and procedures

A powerful way to sustain behaviour is to embed it in systems and procedures, so it does not depend on memory and effort (the change-model link):
- **Defaults** - making the efficient option automatic (equipment defaulting to power-save - the nudge link)
- **Procedures** - building energy-conscious actions into standard operating procedures
- **Design** - designing environments so the efficient behaviour is the easy or only option
- **Automation** - where possible, removing the behavioural dependency entirely (controls, sensors - the controls link)

Embedding behaviour in systems is the most durable form of sustainability - it removes the reliance on people remembering and choosing, locking in the saving structurally. Where a behaviour can be replaced by a default, a procedure, or automation, the saving persists regardless of attention and effort. This is why behaviour change and technical measures work best together.

## Embedding in culture

The deepest form of sustainability is cultural (the leadership link):
- When energy-conscious behaviour becomes "how we do things here," it sustains itself
- New staff absorb the norms (social norms - the psychology link)
- The behaviour is owned collectively, not imposed
- It persists through individual changes

Building behaviour into the organisation's culture - through sustained engagement, champions, recognition, and leadership (the leadership and culture links) - is what makes it truly lasting. A culture where energy-consciousness is normal sustains behaviour far more durably than any campaign, because it no longer depends on the programme at all.

## Guarding against drift

Even embedded behaviours and habits need monitoring to guard against drift (the M&V and maintenance links):
- **Monitoring** - tracking whether behaviours and savings persist (the metrics link)
- **Detecting decay** - catching reversion and rebound early
- **Refreshing** - renewing engagement where behaviour is fading
- **Onboarding** - engaging new staff to maintain the culture

This connects behaviour change to the maintenance and verification disciplines: just as technical savings drift without maintenance, behavioural savings drift without sustained attention. Ongoing monitoring catches the decay, allowing the programme to refresh and reinforce where needed.

## The long game

Sustaining behaviour change is, ultimately, a long game (the leadership link). It requires:
- **Persistence** - ongoing effort, not a one-off campaign
- **Patience** - habits and culture form slowly
- **Embedding** - in habits, systems, procedures, and culture
- **Monitoring** - guarding against drift
- **Renewal** - refreshing engagement and onboarding new people

Done well - sustaining reinforcement until behaviours become habits, embedding them in systems and culture, and monitoring to guard against drift - behaviour change delivers lasting, low-cost savings and contributes to the cultural transformation that is the goal of energy leadership. The behaviours become simply how the organisation operates, sustained not by the programme but by habit and culture. This is the destination: not a successful campaign, but a permanently changed organisation - which is why behaviour change, though challenging, is among the most valuable and enduring contributions an energy manager can make.
