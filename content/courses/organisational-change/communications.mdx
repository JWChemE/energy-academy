Communication is the engine of engagement - how messages reach people, how feedback flows back, and how energy stays in people's minds. Effective, sustained, two-way communication is what keeps a behaviour-change programme alive.

## Communication as the lifeblood

Behaviour change depends on communication: people cannot act on what they do not hear, and behaviour fades without ongoing reinforcement. Communication is the lifeblood of engagement - carrying the messages that build awareness and motivation, the prompts that enable behaviour, the feedback that reinforces it, and the recognition that sustains it. Getting communication right is central to behaviour change.

## The channels

Effective programmes use a mix of channels to reach everyone (the campaign link):
- **Email and intranet** - direct, but easily ignored if overused
- **Digital screens and signage** - visible, repeated exposure
- **Posters and point-of-behaviour prompts** - reaching people at the moment of decision (the nudge link)
- **Meetings and briefings** - personal, allowing dialogue
- **Champions** - peer-to-peer communication (the next lesson)
- **Events** - high-engagement moments
- **Feedback displays** - showing energy use and progress

Different people respond to different channels, and repetition across channels reinforces the message. No single channel reaches everyone, so a mix is essential.

## Crafting the message

How messages are crafted determines their impact (the communication-skills link from the leadership course):
- **Clear and simple** - easy to understand and act on
- **Relevant** - connected to what the audience cares about (cost, climate, comfort, pride)
- **Specific** - clear, concrete asks ("switch off your monitor"), not vague appeals
- **Positive** - enabling and inspiring, not hectoring or guilt-inducing
- **Emotional and story-driven** - engaging hearts, not just informing minds
- **Visual** - memorable and appealing

The same principles that make any communication effective apply here: knowing the audience, framing in their terms, balancing logic and emotion, and being clear about the ask.

## Feedback: closing the loop

A vital and often-neglected element is **feedback** - showing people the effect of their behaviour:
- **Energy use displays** - real-time or regular consumption
- **Progress against targets** - savings achieved
- **Comparison** - against peers, past performance, benchmarks (social norms - the psychology link)
- **Recognition** - celebrating contributions and successes (the incentives link)

Feedback closes the loop between behaviour and outcome, which both motivates (people see their impact) and reinforces (the behaviour is validated). Without feedback, people act in a vacuum, never seeing whether their effort matters. Feedback is one of the most powerful behaviour-change tools - making the invisible visible and the abstract concrete.

## Two-way communication

The most effective communication is two-way, not just broadcast:
- **Listening** - inviting ideas, questions, and concerns
- **Involving** - engaging people in shaping the programme (the culture link)
- **Responding** - acting on feedback and showing it was heard
- **Dialogue** - conversation, not just announcement

Two-way communication builds ownership and engagement (people support what they help shape - the culture link), surfaces good ideas from the front line, and keeps the programme responsive. One-way broadcasting tells people what to do; two-way communication engages them as participants. The shift from broadcasting to dialogue is what turns passive recipients into active contributors.

## Sustaining communication

Behaviour change is a long journey (the change-model link), so communication must be sustained:
- **Regular** - keeping energy present in people's minds
- **Refreshed** - varying the message to avoid fatigue and tuning-out
- **Consistent** - reinforcing the core messages over time
- **Responsive** - adapting to results and feedback

A burst of communication creates a spike that fades; sustained, refreshed communication keeps energy salient and progressively embeds behaviour. The challenge is maintaining attention over the long term without the message going stale - hence the value of refreshed campaigns, seasonal themes, and varied channels (the campaign link).

## Celebrating success

A particularly important communication is sharing success:
- **Reporting savings and progress** - showing the programme works
- **Recognising contributions** - celebrating individuals and teams (the incentives link)
- **Telling stories** - making success vivid and relatable
- **Building momentum** - using success to inspire further effort

Communicating success serves multiple purposes: it reinforces behaviour (recognition), motivates continued effort (momentum), sustains leadership support (demonstrating value - the leadership link), and builds the sense of shared achievement that underpins culture. Celebrating success is not self-congratulation but a vital part of sustaining and growing the change.

## Communication and culture

Ultimately, sustained, engaging, two-way communication is part of building an energy culture (the leadership link). Over time, consistent communication - reinforcing the why, prompting behaviour, providing feedback, recognising success, and inviting participation - shifts energy from an occasional campaign topic to a normal, valued part of organisational life. Communication is not just how a programme delivers messages; it is how, over time, it changes what the organisation pays attention to, talks about, and values - the foundation of lasting cultural change.
