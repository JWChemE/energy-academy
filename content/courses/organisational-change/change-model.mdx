Behaviour change is a process, not an event. People move through stages from unawareness to ingrained habit, and effective programmes support each stage. Understanding this journey is key to designing change that lasts.

## Change as a journey

A single message rarely changes behaviour. Real, lasting behaviour change unfolds as a journey through stages - from not knowing or caring, through trying, to consistent action, and finally to automatic habit. Each stage needs different support, and skipping stages or pushing too fast fails. Understanding the journey lets a programme meet people where they are and move them forward.

## The stages of behaviour change

A simple, practical model of the journey:

**1. Awareness / unawareness.** People do not know about the issue, their role in it, or what to do. The first task is building awareness - of the energy waste, its impact, and the desired behaviour.

**2. Interest / contemplation.** People become aware and start to consider changing. They weigh the reasons and the effort. The task is building motivation - the compelling why (the leadership link).

**3. Trial / action.** People try the new behaviour. This is fragile - the behaviour is new, effortful, and easily abandoned. The task is enabling and supporting the trial - making it easy, removing barriers, providing prompts.

**4. Maintenance / habit.** The behaviour is repeated until it becomes routine and finally automatic. The task is reinforcement - feedback, recognition, and support until the behaviour becomes an ingrained habit (the sustaining-habits lesson).

Moving people along this journey - not just informing them once - is what produces lasting change.

## Matching intervention to stage

Different stages need different interventions:
- **Awareness** - communication, information, making the issue visible (the campaigns lesson)
- **Interest** - motivation, the compelling why, connecting to values
- **Trial** - enabling, making it easy, nudges, removing barriers, prompts (the psychology link)
- **Habit** - reinforcement, feedback, recognition, embedding (the incentives and sustaining links)

A common mistake is using only awareness-stage interventions (information) and expecting behaviour change - but information alone moves people from unaware to aware, not from aware to acting. Each stage needs its appropriate support; the journey needs all of them.

## Knowledge, motivation, ability

A useful lens on what behaviour change requires: for someone to perform a behaviour, they generally need three things:
- **Knowledge** - knowing what to do and why
- **Motivation** - wanting to do it
- **Ability** - being able to do it easily

A gap in any one blocks the behaviour:
- Knowledge without motivation - they know but do not care
- Motivation without ability - they want to but it is too hard
- Ability without knowledge - they could but do not know to

Effective behaviour change addresses all three: building knowledge (awareness), motivation (the why), and ability (making it easy - the nudge and barrier-removal links). Diagnosing which is missing reveals what an intervention needs to provide.

## Removing barriers

A crucial and often-overlooked principle: making the desired behaviour **easy**. People take the path of least resistance (the psychology link), so removing barriers is often more effective than adding motivation:
- If switching off is awkward, make it easy (accessible switches, defaults)
- If the efficient option is hidden, make it visible and convenient
- If procedures obstruct the behaviour, change them

Often the biggest lever is not persuading people harder but removing the friction that makes the wasteful behaviour easier than the efficient one. Addressing ability - the ease of the behaviour - frequently unlocks change that motivation alone could not.

## Measuring the change

To manage behaviour change, it must be measured (the next lesson on metrics):
- Tracking where people are on the journey (awareness, action, habit)
- Measuring the behaviours themselves (and the resulting energy savings)
- Identifying what is working and what is not
- Distinguishing behaviour-driven savings from technical ones (the M&V link)

Measurement reveals whether the change is happening and progressing, allowing the programme to adapt - reinforcing what works, addressing what does not.

## A staged approach

The practical implication is to design behaviour change as a staged journey:
1. Build awareness (communication)
2. Build motivation (the compelling why)
3. Enable trial (make it easy, prompt, remove barriers)
4. Reinforce into habit (feedback, recognition, embedding)
5. Measure and adapt throughout

This staged, psychology-informed approach - meeting people where they are and moving them forward, addressing knowledge, motivation, and ability - is what produces genuine, lasting behaviour change, rather than the fleeting effect of a one-off information campaign. The following lessons detail how to deliver it: designing campaigns, building champion networks, using incentives, and sustaining the change over time.
