Behaviour that is recognised and rewarded gets repeated; behaviour that is ignored fades. Incentives and recognition are the tools that reinforce energy-conscious action until it becomes habit - and they need not cost much.

## Why incentives matter

People respond to consequences. An energy-saving action that brings recognition, reward, or a sense of achievement gets reinforced; one that goes unnoticed competes poorly against the easier old habit. Incentives close the gap between knowing what to do and consistently doing it - turning one-off effort into sustained behaviour.

## Financial versus non-financial

**Financial incentives** - bonuses, shared savings, prizes - can motivate, but have limits:
- They can be effective for teams and measurable targets
- But they risk making behaviour conditional on payment (it stops when the money does), and can feel manipulative or create unhealthy competition

**Non-financial incentives** are often more powerful and durable:
- **Recognition:** public praise, awards, mention in newsletters - people value being seen to do well
- **Achievement:** visible progress toward a goal, league tables, milestones reached
- **Purpose:** connecting effort to a cause people believe in
- **Autonomy:** trusting people to find and own solutions

Research consistently shows that recognition and purpose often motivate more sustainably than cash, especially for discretionary effort.

## Recognition done well

Recognition is cheap and potent, but must be genuine:
- **Specific:** name the action and its result ("the warehouse team's switch-off routine cut 12% off lighting energy"), not vague praise
- **Timely:** soon after the behaviour, so the link is clear
- **Visible:** seen by peers, which both rewards the individual and signals to others what is valued
- **Sincere:** hollow or tokenistic recognition backfires

## Team and individual

Incentives can target individuals or teams:
- **Individual** recognition rewards personal initiative
- **Team** incentives (inter-department competitions, shared goals) harness peer support and make energy a collective effort

Team approaches often work well because they build social reinforcement - colleagues encouraging each other - and avoid the divisiveness of singling out individuals.

## Avoiding perverse incentives

Badly designed incentives can backfire:
- Rewarding energy reduction crudely can tempt people to game the measurement, or to cut energy in ways that harm the work
- Over-competition can damage cooperation
- Rewarding the wrong metric drives the wrong behaviour

Design incentives around the *real* goal, measured fairly (the link to M&V), and watch for unintended consequences. The aim is to reinforce genuine, beneficial behaviour - not to create a game.

## Integrating with culture

Incentives are not a substitute for culture - they are a tool within it. The most effective approach combines:
- A genuine purpose (the why)
- Capability (the knowledge to act)
- Reinforcement (recognition and reward)

Used to reinforce a culture built on commitment and purpose, incentives accelerate the shift to habitual energy-conscious behaviour. Used alone, as a bribe in a culture that does not care, they buy only temporary, conditional compliance. The skill is using recognition and reward to nurture the behaviours that genuine commitment produces - so that, over time, the behaviour outlasts the incentive.
