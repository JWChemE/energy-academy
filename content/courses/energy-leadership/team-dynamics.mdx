Energy management touches every part of an organisation, so the energy leader must work across boundaries - with operations, finance, procurement, IT, and more. Navigating these cross-functional relationships, and the silos that divide them, is essential to getting anything done.

## Energy crosses every boundary

Energy is not contained in one department - it flows through all of them:
- **Operations** runs the equipment that uses the energy
- **Finance** holds the budgets that fund efficiency and pays the bills
- **Procurement** buys the energy and the equipment
- **Facilities/maintenance** keeps the plant running efficiently
- **IT** runs energy-hungry data systems and the controls
- **HR and communications** shape the culture and behaviour
- **Senior management** sets the strategy and priorities

To deliver, the energy leader must work effectively with all of them - usually without authority over any.

## The problem of silos

Organisations divide into functional silos, each with its own goals, budgets, and incentives. Silos obstruct energy management because:
- Energy decisions span boundaries (procurement buys a cheap pump; operations pays for its inefficiency for 15 years)
- Budgets are siloed (the capital saving and the energy saving sit in different accounts)
- Incentives conflict (one department's target works against another's)
- Information does not flow across the divides

The classic example: split incentives, where the party who pays for an efficiency measure is not the party who benefits - so nobody acts. Recognising and bridging these silo effects is a core part of the role.

## Working across functions

Effective cross-functional working means:
- **Understanding each function's world** - their goals, pressures, language, and constraints (the stakeholder-mapping discipline)
- **Speaking their language** - framing energy in operations' reliability terms, finance's return terms, procurement's cost terms
- **Finding shared goals** - the overlap where energy efficiency also serves their objective (lower costs, less downtime, better compliance)
- **Building relationships** - the personal trust that lets you get things done across boundaries

## Aligning incentives

Where silos and split incentives block progress, the energy leader works to realign them:
- Make whole-life cost (not just capital cost) the basis for purchasing decisions, so procurement and operations pull together
- Bring the energy saving and the budget that funds it into the same frame
- Embed shared energy targets across departments so they cooperate rather than compete (the governance link)

Aligning incentives so that everyone gains from efficiency is often more powerful than any technical fix.

## Leading without managing

Much of the energy leader's cross-functional work is leading people who do not report to them:
- Influence, not instruction (the foundational skill of this course)
- Enrolling others in a shared goal
- Making it easy and rewarding for other functions to support energy
- Giving credit generously, so others want to be involved

This is leadership in its purest form - getting things done through people you do not control, by making them want to act.

## Building the energy team

Finally, energy management is increasingly a team effort spanning functions:
- A cross-functional energy group or committee, with representation from operations, finance, procurement, and more
- A network of energy champions across departments (the link to organisational change)
- Clear roles, shared goals, and regular collaboration

Building and leading this distributed team - formal and informal - multiplies the energy leader's reach far beyond what one person, or one department, could achieve. The work of energy management is, in the end, the work of getting a whole organisation to pull in the same direction.
