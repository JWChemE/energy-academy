Energy managers rarely control the budgets, the equipment, or the people whose decisions determine a building's energy use. Their power comes not from position but from influence - the ability to change what others choose to do without the authority to compel them.

## The authority gap

The classic frustration of the energy role: you can see the savings, you know the fixes, but the boiler belongs to facilities, the budget to finance, the behaviour to every occupant, and the strategy to the board. You are responsible for outcomes you cannot command. Closing this gap is the central leadership challenge of the job.

## The currencies of influence

People say yes when you offer something they value. Different stakeholders trade in different currencies:
- **Finance** responds to return on investment, risk reduction, and cash flow
- **Operations** responds to reliability, reduced downtime, and an easier life
- **The board** responds to reputation, compliance, and strategic fit
- **Staff** respond to comfort, recognition, and a sense of purpose

Influence means framing your proposal in the listener's currency, not your own. "It saves 80 tonnes of carbon" persuades a sustainability lead; "it cuts 40,000 GBP from running costs and reduces breakdown risk" persuades a finance director and an operations manager. Same project, different currency.

## Credibility: the foundation

You cannot influence without credibility, and credibility is earned:
- **Competence:** you know your subject and your numbers are right
- **Reliability:** you deliver what you promise, so people trust your next claim
- **Honesty:** you report failures as well as successes, so your successes are believed

Overstate a saving once and every future figure is doubted. A track record of accurate, verified results (the link to measurement and verification) is the single greatest source of influence an energy manager has.

## Reciprocity and coalitions

Influence is rarely a single act of persuasion - it is built over time through relationships:
- Help others with their problems, and they help with yours
- Find the people who gain from your proposals and enlist them as allies
- Build a coalition so that change is wanted by many, not pushed by one

A proposal backed by facilities, finance, and a respected operations manager is far harder to refuse than one championed by the energy manager alone.

## Small wins build momentum

Early, visible, low-risk successes earn the credibility and goodwill that bigger asks require. Start with quick wins that demonstrate competence and deliver real results; use them to build the trust and the coalition that unlock the larger, harder projects later. Influence compounds - each delivered success makes the next proposal easier.

## The mindset

Influencing without authority is not manipulation; it is the genuine work of understanding what others need, framing your goals in terms of their goals, earning trust through reliability, and patiently building the relationships and coalitions that turn a lone energy manager into the centre of a movement the whole organisation owns.
