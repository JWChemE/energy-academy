Energy management generates a great deal of data, but data alone changes nothing. The energy leader's skill is turning numbers into a message that moves people to act - through clear, compelling, well-targeted communication.

## Why communication is the bottleneck

You can have the best analysis in the building, but if you cannot make a finance director care, a board approve, or a workforce engage, the analysis stays on the shelf. Communication is where good energy management is so often won or lost - the translation of technical truth into human action.

## Know your audience

Effective communication starts where stakeholder mapping leaves off - with the listener:
- A **board** wants the headline, the risk, and the strategic fit, in two minutes, not the engineering
- A **finance director** wants the numbers, the assumptions, and the return
- An **operations team** wants to know what changes for them and why it will not cause trouble
- **Staff** want to know what is being asked of them, and why it matters

Pitch the same project at the right altitude and in the right currency for each audience. Drowning a board in technical detail, or fobbing off an engineer with vague vision, both fail.

## The power of narrative

Numbers inform, but stories persuade. People remember and act on narrative far more than on spreadsheets:
- Frame the **problem** vividly (the energy being wasted, the risk being run, the opportunity being missed)
- Show the **journey** (what the project does)
- Paint the **outcome** (the savings, the carbon cut, the reputation gained)

A story about how a similar building cut its bills by a third, with a face and a place attached, lands harder than a table of figures. Wrap the data in a narrative and it sticks.

## Presenting data well

When you do show data, make it work:
- **One message per chart** - what should they take away?
- **Visual and simple** - a clear graph beats a dense table
- **Context and comparison** - "30% below benchmark" means more than a raw number
- **Lead with the conclusion** - tell them what it means, then show the evidence

Avoid the common trap of presenting everything you measured; present only what drives the decision.

## Balancing logic and emotion

Persuasion needs both:
- **Logic** (the business case, the numbers, the risk) gives people permission to say yes
- **Emotion** (purpose, pride, urgency, the story) gives them the desire to

A purely rational pitch leaves people unmoved; a purely emotional one leaves them unconvinced. The energy leader weaves both - the solid case and the compelling reason to care.

## Listening as communication

Communication is not just broadcasting. The most influential energy leaders listen:
- Understand objections before answering them
- Let stakeholders shape the proposal so they own it
- Read the room and adapt

Listening builds the relationships and trust that make your messages land, and often reveals the very argument that will win a sceptic over.

## Clarity and the call to action

Finally, every communication should be clear about what happens next:
- What exactly are you asking for? (a decision, a budget, a behaviour)
- What is the single thing you want them to remember?
- What is the next step?

Vague communication produces vague outcomes. The energy leader closes with a clear, specific call to action - so the meeting ends not with nods, but with a decision and a commitment.
