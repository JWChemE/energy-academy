Change happens through people, and not all people matter equally to a given decision. Stakeholder mapping is the disciplined practice of identifying who affects your goals, understanding their position, and tailoring your approach to each.

## Who is a stakeholder

A stakeholder is anyone who can affect, or is affected by, your energy initiatives:
- **Decision-makers:** the board, finance directors, senior managers who approve budgets
- **Gatekeepers:** facilities, IT, procurement who control access and implementation
- **Influencers:** respected individuals whose opinion sways others
- **Affected parties:** occupants, staff, tenants whose behaviour and comfort are involved
- **External:** regulators, landlords, suppliers, the local community

Mapping them ensures you engage everyone who matters - and do not waste effort on those who do not.

## The power-interest grid

The classic tool plots stakeholders on two axes - their **power** to affect the outcome and their **interest** in it:

- **High power, high interest:** manage closely - these are your key players (engage deeply, involve them, win them over)
- **High power, low interest:** keep satisfied - powerful but disengaged (a finance director who could block funding); give them enough to keep them comfortable
- **Low power, high interest:** keep informed - enthusiasts and the affected (staff who care); they can become advocates and allies
- **Low power, low interest:** monitor - minimal effort

This grid focuses your energy where it changes outcomes, rather than treating everyone alike.

## Understanding positions

For the stakeholders who matter, dig deeper than their job title:
- **What do they want?** Their goals, pressures, and the currency they value
- **What do they fear?** Risk, disruption, cost, blame, loss of control
- **Where do they stand on your proposal?** Champion, supporter, neutral, sceptic, or blocker
- **What would move them?** The argument, evidence, or reassurance that shifts their position

A finance director may fear a risky, unproven spend; an operations manager may fear disruption to a critical process. Address the fear, and resistance often dissolves.

## Allies, blockers, and fence-sitters

Classify the key stakeholders by their stance and act accordingly:
- **Allies and champions:** enlist them actively - they carry your message into rooms you cannot enter
- **Blockers:** understand their objection and address it directly, or build a coalition strong enough to outweigh it
- **Fence-sitters:** the swing votes - often where your effort is best spent, because moving a neutral to a supporter shifts the balance

## Tailoring the message

The same project is pitched differently to each:
- To finance: return, risk, payback
- To operations: reliability, reduced downtime, simplicity
- To the board: reputation, compliance, strategy
- To staff: comfort, purpose, recognition

Stakeholder mapping tells you not just who to talk to, but what to say to each - the foundation of the targeted communication covered next.

## A living tool

Stakeholder maps change as projects evolve, people move, and positions shift. Revisit the map at each stage of a major initiative - new blockers emerge, allies gain or lose power, and yesterday's fence-sitter becomes today's champion. Treating it as a living tool, not a one-off exercise, keeps your influence strategy aligned with the real political landscape.
