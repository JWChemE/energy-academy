Technology and projects deliver one-off savings; culture delivers savings that persist and grow. An organisation where energy-conscious thinking is simply "how we do things" achieves far more than one relying on a lone energy manager pushing initiatives uphill.

## From compliance to commitment

There is a crucial difference between an organisation that *complies* with energy rules and one that is *committed* to energy performance:
- **Compliance:** people follow the policy because they have to; the moment attention lapses, old habits return
- **Commitment:** people act because they believe it matters; they find savings you never asked for

The goal of culture change is to move the organisation from grudging compliance to genuine commitment - where energy is owned by everyone, not policed by one.

## What an energy culture looks like

In an organisation with a strong energy culture:
- People switch things off without being told, and challenge waste when they see it
- Energy is considered routinely in decisions - purchasing, design, operation
- Good ideas come from the floor, not just from management
- Energy performance is a source of shared pride, not a chore

This does not happen by accident. It is built deliberately, over time.

## The ingredients of culture change

**Leadership commitment.** Culture follows the visible priorities of leaders. If senior managers talk about energy, ask about it, and act on it, others follow. If they ignore it, no campaign will compensate. Genuine, visible leadership support is the precondition for everything else (the link to embedding energy in governance).

**A compelling why.** People engage with purpose, not instructions. Connect energy to things they care about - cost savings that protect jobs, carbon cuts that matter to a warming world, comfort and pride in their workplace. The "why" turns a task into a cause.

**Involvement and ownership.** People support what they help create. Involve staff in setting targets, finding savings, and shaping initiatives, and they own the results. Impose change from above and they resist it.

**Knowledge and capability.** People cannot act on what they do not understand. Give them the awareness and skills to see waste and know what to do about it.

**Reinforcement.** New behaviours need reinforcement to become habits - feedback, recognition, and incentives (covered in the next lessons) that reward the behaviour until it becomes automatic.

## The role of the energy leader

In culture change, the energy manager shifts from *doer* to *enabler*:
- Less "I will find and fix the savings"
- More "I will help everyone find and fix savings"

This means equipping, motivating, and supporting others rather than carrying the whole load - building a movement rather than running a one-person campaign. It is a profound shift in role, and the key to scaling impact beyond what any individual could achieve.

## Patience and persistence

Culture changes slowly. It is built through consistent messages, repeated reinforcement, visible leadership, and accumulated small wins, over months and years. There is no single intervention that flips a culture; there is the patient, persistent work of making energy-conscious behaviour normal, valued, and rewarded - until one day it is simply who the organisation is.
