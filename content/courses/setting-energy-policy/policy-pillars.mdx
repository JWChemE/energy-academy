A well-structured energy policy rests on a few core pillars - the fundamental commitments around which everything else is organised. Understanding these pillars helps build a policy that is comprehensive without being bloated.

## The three core pillars

Most effective energy policies are built around three pillars:

**1. Operational efficiency** - committing to use energy efficiently in the organisation's own operations. This is the foundation: reducing waste, improving the efficiency of buildings and equipment, and continually improving energy performance. It draws on the whole technical curriculum.

**2. Renewable and low-carbon energy** - committing to source energy from renewable and low-carbon sources, on site (generation) and through procurement (the procurement course). This addresses not just how much energy is used, but where it comes from.

**3. Governance and continual improvement** - committing to manage energy systematically, with clear accountability, measurement, targets, and ongoing improvement (the ISO 50001 and governance links). This is the pillar that makes the other two endure.

Together, these pillars cover the essence of energy management: use less, source it cleaner, and manage it well.

## Efficiency first

The operational efficiency pillar embodies the "demand reduction first" principle that runs through energy management:
- Reduce waste before generating more
- Improve efficiency before procuring renewables
- It is usually cheaper to save a unit of energy than to generate it

A policy that commits to efficiency first ensures the organisation does not leap to expensive renewable generation while ignoring cheap savings - the sequencing that the net-zero roadmap course makes explicit.

## Clean energy

The renewable and low-carbon pillar addresses the carbon intensity of energy:
- On-site generation (solar PV, etc. - the renewables course)
- Renewable procurement (green tariffs, PPAs - the procurement course)
- Low-carbon heat (heat pumps, the electrification of heat)
- A pathway away from fossil fuels

This pillar connects the energy policy to the organisation's carbon and net-zero commitments - increasingly central as climate concern grows.

## Governance and improvement

The governance pillar is what turns aspiration into durable result:
- Clear roles and accountability (the governance link)
- Measurement and targets (the ISO 50001 and M&V links)
- Continual improvement, not one-off effort
- Regular review and renewal

Without this pillar, the other commitments fade when attention wanders. With it, energy management is embedded and self-sustaining.

## Tailoring the pillars

While these three pillars suit most organisations, the policy should reflect the specific organisation:
- A heavy industrial site might emphasise process efficiency
- A property company might emphasise building performance and tenant engagement
- A public body might emphasise leadership and exemplary practice
- A company with a large supply chain might add a scope 3 / value-chain pillar

The pillars provide a tested structure; the content within them is tailored to the organisation's energy profile, ambitions, and stakeholders.

## From pillars to commitments

Each pillar is expressed through specific commitments in the policy - statements of what the organisation will do. The efficiency pillar might commit to continual improvement in energy performance; the clean energy pillar to a renewable target; the governance pillar to clear accountability and regular review. These commitments, and the targets and accountability that give them teeth, are the subject of the next module. The pillars provide the framework; the commitments fill it with substance - producing a policy that is structured, comprehensive, and genuinely meaningful.
