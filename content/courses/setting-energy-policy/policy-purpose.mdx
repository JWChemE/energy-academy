An energy policy is the foundation stone of energy management - a short, authoritative statement of an organisation's commitment to managing energy. Before drafting one, it is essential to understand what a policy is for, and what it is not.

## What an energy policy is

An **energy policy** is a brief, high-level document in which an organisation formally states its commitment to energy management and sets the direction for everything that follows. It declares intent, establishes commitment at the top, and provides the framework within which objectives, targets, and actions are set.

A good policy is typically a single page. Its power lies not in length but in authority - it carries the visible backing of top management, signalling that energy matters to the organisation.

## What a policy is not

A policy is often confused with other things it is not:
- It is **not a strategy** - it states commitment and direction, not the detailed plan to deliver it
- It is **not an action plan** - it does not list specific projects or tactics
- It is **not a technical document** - it sets intent, not engineering detail
- It is **not a one-off** - it is a living commitment, reviewed and renewed

The policy sits at the top of a hierarchy: policy (commitment and direction) → strategy (the plan) → objectives and targets (measurable goals) → action plans (specific projects). Confusing these levels produces either a bloated, unusable policy or a vague, toothless one.

## Why a policy matters

A formal energy policy serves several vital purposes:
- **Mandate:** it gives the energy manager and team the authority to act, backed by top management
- **Direction:** it sets the organisation's energy priorities and commitments
- **Signal:** it tells everyone - staff, stakeholders, suppliers - that energy is taken seriously
- **Framework:** it provides the basis for setting objectives and targets (the ISO 50001 link)
- **Accountability:** it commits the organisation publicly, creating accountability

Without a policy, energy management depends on individual initiative and is easily marginalised. With one - genuinely backed at the top - it has a mandate that endures.

## Scope: what the policy covers

A key early decision is the policy's **scope** - what it commits the organisation to:
- **Operational energy:** the energy used running the organisation's buildings and operations (the minimum scope)
- **Supply chain:** the energy and carbon embedded in what the organisation buys (a wider scope - the link to scope 3 emissions)
- **Products and services:** the energy impact of what the organisation produces or sells (the widest scope)

A growing number of organisations extend scope beyond their own operations to their value chain, reflecting the reality that most of their energy and carbon impact often lies there. The right scope depends on the organisation's ambition, influence, and stakeholder expectations.

## The mark of a good policy

A good energy policy is:
- **Brief** - short enough that people actually read it
- **Clear** - unambiguous about what the organisation commits to
- **Authoritative** - visibly backed and signed by top management
- **Ambitious yet credible** - stretching but believable
- **Actionable** - providing a real framework for objectives, not empty words
- **Aligned** - connected to the organisation's broader strategy and values

But it all rests on understanding, first, what an energy policy is fundamentally for: a clear, authoritative declaration of commitment that gives energy management its mandate and direction.
