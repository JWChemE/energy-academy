A policy commits the organisation, but organisations act through people. Defining clear roles and responsibilities - who does what, who decides what, who answers for what - is what turns a policy's commitments into action by real individuals.

## Why roles must be explicit

Energy management spans the whole organisation, which creates a danger: when everyone is vaguely responsible, no one is actually accountable. Explicit roles and responsibilities prevent this by assigning clear ownership:
- Who leads energy management?
- Who is accountable for performance?
- Who approves investment?
- Who implements measures?
- Who is responsible at each site and for each significant energy use?

Without this clarity, energy management falls into the gaps between functions - everyone assuming someone else is handling it.

## Top management responsibility

Responsibility starts at the top. ISO 50001 and good practice require top management to:
- Demonstrate **leadership and commitment** (the leadership link)
- Establish and support the policy
- Provide **resources** for energy management
- Assign roles and responsibilities
- Ensure targets are set and reviewed
- Hold the organisation accountable

A board-level sponsor or senior executive with explicit energy responsibility anchors the whole structure (the governance link). Without genuine top-management ownership, everything below it is undermined.

## The energy manager / management representative

A designated **energy manager** (or, in ISO 50001 terms, a management representative) leads the day-to-day work:
- Coordinating energy management activities
- Developing and tracking targets and action plans
- Reporting performance to management
- Driving the management system and continual improvement
- Engaging and supporting others across the organisation

This role needs both the competence to do the work and the authority (granted by top management) to act across functional boundaries (the influence and cross-functional links).

## Distributed responsibility

Energy management cannot rest on one person. Effective policies distribute responsibility:
- **Site and building managers** accountable for their facilities' energy performance
- **Process and equipment owners** responsible for their significant energy uses
- **Department heads** responsible for their areas' energy use and behaviour
- **Procurement** responsible for buying energy and efficient equipment (the procurement link)
- **Finance** responsible for funding and evaluating investments (the investment-case link)
- **All staff** responsible for their everyday energy behaviour (the culture link)

This distribution embeds energy management throughout the organisation rather than isolating it in one office.

## Embedding in job roles

Responsibilities have force when built into people's actual jobs:
- Energy objectives in managers' performance goals and appraisals
- Energy responsibilities in job descriptions
- Energy performance reflected in how people are assessed and rewarded

When a manager's appraisal includes their area's energy performance, energy becomes part of the day job, not an optional extra (the governance and incentives links). This is one of the most powerful ways to make distributed responsibility real.

## Energy teams and champions

Beyond individual roles, organisations often establish:
- A **cross-functional energy team or committee** - bringing together operations, finance, procurement, facilities to coordinate and drive energy management (the cross-functional link)
- A network of **energy champions** - enthusiasts across departments who promote energy awareness and action at grassroots level (the organisational-change link)

These structures spread ownership and engagement, turning energy management from a central function into a distributed movement.

## The RACI clarity

For major energy activities, it helps to be explicit about who is:
- **Responsible** - does the work
- **Accountable** - answers for the result
- **Consulted** - gives input
- **Informed** - kept aware

This clarity (a RACI approach) prevents the confusion and gaps that arise when roles are assumed rather than defined - particularly for cross-functional activities where several parties are involved.

## From responsibility to result

Clear roles and responsibilities are what connect the policy's commitments to actual human action. The policy commits the organisation; targets quantify the commitment; and defined responsibilities ensure that named people, with the authority and accountability to act, actually deliver. Combined with the supply-chain dimension covered next, and the adoption and embedding covered in the final module, clear responsibilities turn a policy from a corporate statement into a working allocation of who will make it happen.
