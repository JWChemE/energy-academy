A policy approved by the board but unknown to the organisation achieves nothing. Launching the policy and cascading it through every level - so that people understand it, own it, and act on it - is what turns an approved document into a living commitment.

## The gap between approval and action

Many energy policies are approved, filed, and forgotten. The board signs, the document goes on the intranet, and nothing changes - because the people whose daily decisions determine energy use never engaged with it. Bridging this gap requires deliberate launch and communication, turning a corporate statement into something the whole organisation knows and acts upon.

## Launching the policy

A policy deserves a genuine launch, not a quiet posting:
- **Visible leadership:** senior leaders announce and champion it, signalling its importance (the leadership link)
- **Clear communication:** explaining what the policy means and why it matters
- **Compelling framing:** connecting it to things people care about - cost, jobs, climate, pride (the communication link)
- **Multiple channels:** meetings, intranet, email, posters, briefings - reaching everyone

The launch sets the tone. A high-profile, leadership-backed launch signals that energy matters; a quiet upload signals that it does not.

## Cascading through the organisation

The policy must reach every level, translated into what it means for each:
- **Senior managers:** their accountability for performance in their areas
- **Middle managers:** their role in delivering targets and engaging their teams
- **Operational staff:** what it means for how they run equipment and processes
- **All employees:** how their everyday behaviour contributes

Cascading is not just broadcasting the same message - it is translating the policy into relevant, specific meaning for each level and role. A board-level commitment to net zero becomes, for a machine operator, specific actions about switching off and reporting waste.

## Making it relevant

People engage with what affects them. Effective cascading connects the policy to each person's world:
- What does this mean for *my* job?
- What am *I* being asked to do?
- Why does it matter to *me*?
- How will it affect *my* area?

Generic communication washes over people; relevant, specific messaging engages them. This requires tailoring the message for different audiences (the stakeholder and communication links) rather than issuing one corporate statement to all.

## Building understanding and capability

Awareness is not enough - people need to understand and be able to act:
- **Training and briefing** on what the policy means and what is expected
- **Practical guidance** on the specific actions people can take
- **Skills** where needed to act effectively

A policy that asks people to save energy without showing them how sets them up to fail. Building capability alongside awareness is what enables action (the organisational-change link).

## Embedding in everyday practice

The ultimate goal is to embed the policy in how the organisation actually operates:
- Energy considerations built into routine decisions and procedures
- Targets cascaded into team and individual objectives (the governance link)
- Energy on the agenda of regular meetings
- The policy reflected in purchasing, design, and operation

Embedded this way, the policy stops being a separate document and becomes part of how things are done - the deepest and most durable form of adoption.

## Engagement, not just communication

The most effective launches go beyond telling people - they involve them:
- Inviting ideas and input
- Engaging staff in setting local targets and finding savings
- Creating energy champions to carry the message (the organisational-change link)
- Two-way dialogue, not one-way broadcast

People support what they help create. Engagement turns passive recipients into active participants who own the policy rather than merely complying with it (the culture link).

## Sustaining attention

A launch creates initial awareness, but attention fades. Sustaining the policy in people's minds requires ongoing communication:
- Regular updates on progress against targets
- Celebrating successes and recognising contributions
- Keeping energy visible through continued campaigns
- Refreshing the message so it does not go stale

The launch begins the journey of adoption; sustained communication and engagement keep it alive. Combined with the review and renewal covered next, this ongoing effort is what ensures the policy genuinely shapes the organisation's behaviour over the long term, rather than fading into the forgotten archive of well-meant documents.
