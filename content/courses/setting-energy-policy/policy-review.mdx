A policy is not a monument but a living commitment. Regular review and renewal keep it relevant, ambitious, and effective as the organisation, technology, and external context evolve. A policy never reviewed slowly drifts into irrelevance.

## Why policies need review

An energy policy can become outdated in many ways:
- **Targets achieved** (or proven unrealistic) and needing replacement
- **Technology advanced** - new options (cheaper renewables, heat pumps, storage) that the policy should embrace
- **Regulation changed** - new legal requirements or carbon commitments
- **Business changed** - growth, restructuring, new operations, shifting priorities
- **Ambition raised** - external expectations (net zero) that the policy must catch up with
- **Drift** - the policy quietly forgotten and needing renewal

Regular review catches these, keeping the policy current and credible rather than letting it slowly date.

## The review cycle

Good practice reviews the policy on a regular cycle:
- **Annual review** of progress against targets and the policy's continued relevance
- **Periodic renewal** (every few years) of the policy itself - refreshing commitments, targets, and ambition
- **Triggered review** when significant change occurs (major regulation, restructuring, missed or met targets)

This cycle aligns naturally with the management-system reviews of ISO 50001 (the management review) and with annual reporting - building policy review into the organisation's regular rhythm rather than leaving it to chance.

## What review examines

A policy review asks:
- **Progress:** are we meeting our targets? Why or why not? (the M&V link)
- **Relevance:** does the policy still fit the organisation, technology, and context?
- **Ambition:** is the policy ambitious enough given what peers, regulation, and stakeholders now expect?
- **Effectiveness:** is the policy actually driving change, or has it become a dead letter?
- **Gaps:** are there new areas (scope 3, new commitments) the policy should address?

Honest answers - including admitting where the policy has underperformed - are what make review valuable. A review that simply re-approves the status quo achieves nothing.

## Raising ambition over time

A recurring outcome of review is raising ambition. As the organisation progresses, technology improves, and external expectations rise, policies typically tighten over time:
- Efficiency targets that stretch further
- Carbon commitments that accelerate toward net zero
- Scope extended to the supply chain
- Renewable commitments increased

This ratcheting of ambition reflects the journey of energy transformation (the leadership link) - each review building on the last, raising the bar as the organisation matures. The net-zero course explores how science-based targets give this ambition external rigour.

## Learning from experience

Review is also a chance to learn:
- What worked and what did not?
- Which targets were realistic and which were not?
- What obstacles emerged, and how can the policy address them?
- What can be learned from peers and best practice?

This learning improves not just the policy but the whole approach to energy management - feeding the continual improvement that defines a mature programme (the ISO 50001 link).

## Keeping it alive

Beyond formal review, keeping a policy alive requires ongoing attention:
- Regular communication of progress (the launch-and-cascade link)
- Visible leadership engagement (the governance link)
- Celebrating achievements and acknowledging shortfalls
- Continually connecting the policy to current priorities

A policy that is regularly reviewed, reported on, and refreshed stays present in the organisation's consciousness; one that is set and forgotten fades, however good its words.

## The policy lifecycle

Taken together, this course describes a policy lifecycle: understanding what a policy is for, structuring it around sound pillars, expressing it in clear language, giving it teeth through targets and accountability, defining who is responsible, extending it to the supply chain, securing genuine board commitment, launching and cascading it through the organisation, and finally reviewing and renewing it over time. Run as a continuous cycle - not a one-off drafting exercise - this lifecycle produces a policy that is genuinely authoritative, comprehensive, and effective: a living commitment that gives energy management its mandate and direction, and keeps doing so as the organisation and its world evolve.
