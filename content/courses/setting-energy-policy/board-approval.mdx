A policy gains its authority from the top. Securing genuine board or executive approval - not just a signature, but real commitment - is what gives an energy policy its mandate. Winning that approval is a task of framing, evidence, and persuasion.

## Why board approval matters

An energy policy backed by the board carries weight that one issued by the facilities department never can:
- It signals that energy matters to the whole organisation
- It grants the energy manager authority to act across functions (the influence link)
- It commits resources and accountability at the highest level
- It embeds energy in governance, surviving personnel changes (the governance link)

The difference between a policy the board genuinely owns and one it merely rubber-stamps is the difference between a mandate and a piece of paper. Securing real commitment is therefore worth serious effort.

## Speaking the board's language

Boards do not respond to technical enthusiasm; they respond to business value and risk. To win approval, frame energy in terms the board cares about (the communication link):
- **Cost:** energy as a significant, controllable operating cost, and the savings available
- **Risk:** exposure to energy price volatility, supply disruption, and tightening regulation
- **Reputation:** stakeholder, customer, and investor expectations on carbon and sustainability
- **Compliance:** legal obligations and the cost of non-compliance
- **Opportunity:** competitive advantage, resilience, and future-proofing
- **Strategy:** how energy supports the organisation's broader goals

Presenting energy as a strategic business issue - not a technical or moral one - is what gets a board's attention and assent.

## Building the case for the policy

Approval is more likely when the proposal is backed by evidence:
- **The current position:** what the organisation spends on energy, its carbon footprint, its risks
- **The opportunity:** the savings, risk reduction, and reputational benefit available
- **The ask:** what the policy commits to, and what resources it needs
- **The benchmark:** what peers and competitors are doing (boards dislike being behind)
- **The risk of inaction:** what happens if the organisation does nothing

A concise, compelling business case - quantified where possible - turns the policy from an abstract good intention into a justified business decision (the investment-case link).

## Addressing board concerns

Anticipate and address the board's likely concerns:
- **Cost:** what will this commit us to spending? (frame as investment with return)
- **Risk:** are the targets achievable, or are we over-committing? (credible, evidence-based targets)
- **Flexibility:** does this tie our hands? (commitment with sensible review mechanisms)
- **Accountability:** who will be responsible? (clear ownership)

Addressing concerns proactively, with honest answers, builds the confidence a board needs to approve. Over-promising to win approval backfires when reality bites (the credibility link).

## Genuine commitment, not just a signature

The goal is not merely a signed document but genuine board commitment:
- The board understands and believes in the policy
- It is prepared to provide resources and back difficult decisions
- It will hold the organisation (and itself) accountable
- It will champion energy in its own communications and decisions

A signature without commitment produces a hollow policy that withers in practice. Real commitment - won through a compelling case and genuine engagement - produces a policy with lasting force. It is worth taking the time to build true buy-in rather than settling for token approval.

## Securing a champion

Ideally, board approval includes securing a **board-level champion** - a senior individual who takes personal ownership of energy and carbon (the governance link). This champion:
- Keeps energy on the board's agenda
- Advocates for resources and decisions
- Lends seniority and authority to the energy agenda

A committed champion at the top is one of the strongest predictors of energy management success - turning board approval from a one-off event into ongoing senior sponsorship.

## From approval to action

Board approval is a milestone, not the finish line. With the mandate secured, the work turns to launching the policy, cascading it through the organisation, and embedding it in practice - the subjects of the next lessons. But none of that succeeds without the genuine top-level commitment that board approval, properly won, provides. It is the foundation on which an effective, authoritative energy policy is built.
