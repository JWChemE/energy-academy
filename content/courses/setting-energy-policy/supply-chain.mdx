For many organisations, the largest energy and carbon impact lies not in their own operations but in their supply chain - the goods and services they buy. A policy that addresses only direct operations may miss most of the picture. Extending it to the supply chain is increasingly essential.

## The scope 3 reality

An organisation's emissions fall into three scopes (detailed in the net-zero course):
- **Scope 1:** direct emissions from owned sources (fuel burnt on site)
- **Scope 2:** indirect emissions from purchased energy (electricity)
- **Scope 3:** all other indirect emissions - the supply chain, purchased goods and services, transport, the use of sold products

For many organisations - especially retailers, service firms, and manufacturers of assembled products - **scope 3 dominates**, often representing 70-90% of total emissions. A policy confined to scopes 1 and 2 therefore addresses only a fraction of the real impact. Extending the policy to the supply chain is what makes it comprehensive.

## Why address the supply chain

Beyond completeness, there are strong reasons to extend the policy to suppliers:
- **Impact:** the biggest reductions often lie in the supply chain
- **Stakeholder expectations:** investors, customers, and regulators increasingly demand scope 3 action
- **Risk:** supply-chain energy and carbon exposure is a business risk (cost, regulation, reputation)
- **Influence:** large organisations can drive significant change by influencing their suppliers
- **Credibility:** net-zero claims that ignore scope 3 are increasingly seen as incomplete (the credibility link)

## Supplier expectations

A supply-chain energy policy sets expectations of suppliers:
- Requiring suppliers to measure and report their energy and carbon
- Setting standards or targets suppliers must meet
- Favouring suppliers with strong energy and carbon performance
- Requiring efficient, low-carbon products and services
- Collaborating with key suppliers on reduction

These expectations turn the organisation's buying power into leverage for change beyond its own boundaries.

## Sustainable procurement

The policy connects directly to procurement practice (the procurement course):
- **Whole-life cost** rather than purchase price - so efficient equipment that costs more upfront but less to run is favoured (countering the split-incentive trap)
- **Energy and carbon criteria** in tendering and supplier selection
- **Efficiency standards** for purchased equipment
- **Low-carbon energy** procurement (renewable tariffs, PPAs)

Embedding energy and carbon into procurement decisions is one of the most powerful ways to act on the supply chain - and requires aligning the policy with how the organisation actually buys.

## Influence and collaboration

How an organisation engages its supply chain depends on its leverage:
- **Large buyers** can set requirements and drive supplier change
- **Smaller buyers** may collaborate, encourage, and choose better suppliers rather than dictate
- **Key strategic suppliers** warrant partnership and joint reduction efforts
- **Many small suppliers** may be engaged through standards and selection criteria

The policy should reflect the organisation's realistic influence - ambitious where it has leverage, collaborative where it does not.

## Products and services

The widest scope extends to the energy impact of what the organisation itself produces or sells:
- The energy efficiency of products in use (often the largest impact for manufacturers)
- The energy intensity of services delivered
- Designing products and services for low energy and carbon

For a manufacturer of energy-using products, the in-use energy of those products can dwarf all operational energy - making product energy efficiency the single most impactful commitment the policy could make.

## Balancing ambition and practicality

Extending a policy to the supply chain is challenging - scope 3 data is harder to obtain, influence is limited, and progress is slower. The policy should be ambitious about direction while realistic about pace:
- Start by measuring and understanding the supply-chain impact
- Engage the most significant suppliers first
- Set expectations and build them over time
- Collaborate where direct control is impossible

A credible supply-chain commitment acknowledges the difficulty while committing to genuine, progressive action - avoiding both the timidity of ignoring scope 3 and the false promise of claiming to control what cannot yet be measured. Done well, it extends the policy's reach to where much of the real impact lies - and signals to suppliers, customers, and stakeholders that the organisation's commitment is comprehensive and serious.
