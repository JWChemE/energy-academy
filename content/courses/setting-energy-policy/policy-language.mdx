A policy lives or dies by its language. The same commitments expressed in vague, hedged words achieve nothing; expressed clearly and confidently, they drive action. Getting the language right is what turns a policy from wallpaper into a working document.

## Clarity above all

The first rule of policy language is clarity. A policy must be understood by everyone - from the board to the shop floor - so it must be:
- **Plain** - free of jargon and technical detail
- **Direct** - saying what it means without hedging
- **Concrete** - specific commitments, not woolly aspirations
- **Concise** - short enough to be read and remembered

Compare "the organisation will endeavour, where reasonably practicable and subject to operational considerations, to give appropriate consideration to energy matters" - which commits to nothing - with "we will reduce our energy use year on year" - which commits clearly. The second drives action; the first is wallpaper.

## Active and committed

Policy language should be active and committed, not passive and tentative:
- **"We will"** - not "we will try to" or "we aim to where possible"
- **Active voice** - "we will reduce" not "reductions will be sought"
- **First person** - "we" creates ownership; "the organisation" distances

The difference between "we will" and "we will endeavour to" is the difference between a commitment and an excuse. Confident, committed language signals that the organisation means it.

## Ambitious yet credible

Policy language must balance ambition with credibility:
- **Too timid** - and the policy inspires no one and achieves little
- **Too grandiose** - and it loses credibility when reality falls short

The art is language that stretches the organisation while remaining believable. "We will be carbon neutral by next year" may be neither credible nor achievable; "we will halve our carbon emissions by 2030 and reach net zero by 2040" is ambitious yet credible. Stretch goals motivate; impossible ones breed cynicism (the credibility theme that runs through net-zero claims).

## Linking to strategy and values

Effective policy language connects energy to the organisation's wider strategy and values:
- To business goals (cost, risk, resilience, growth)
- To values (responsibility, stewardship, innovation)
- To stakeholder commitments (customers, community, investors)

This framing makes the policy part of who the organisation is, not a standalone technical statement. Language that says "as part of our commitment to responsible business, we will..." roots energy in the organisation's identity (the leadership and governance links).

## Avoiding the traps

Common language traps that weaken policies:
- **Weasel words** - "where practicable," "subject to," "endeavour," "consider" - that provide escape routes from every commitment
- **Vagueness** - "improve," "reduce," "promote" without specifics
- **Jargon** - technical language that excludes most readers
- **Over-promising** - commitments the organisation cannot keep
- **Length** - so much text that nobody reads it

Each of these drains the policy of force. Ruthless editing - cutting hedges, jargon, and padding - sharpens it.

## Tone

The tone should be confident, positive, and purposeful:
- **Confident** - the organisation knows what it is doing
- **Positive** - framing energy as opportunity, not just obligation
- **Purposeful** - conveying genuine intent to act

A policy written with conviction reads differently from one written to tick a box - and people sense the difference.

## The test of good language

A simple test: would a reader, having read the policy, know clearly what the organisation commits to and be able to hold it to account? If the language is so hedged that no one could ever say the organisation failed to meet its policy, the policy commits to nothing. Good policy language makes clear, accountable commitments in plain, confident words - the foundation of a policy with teeth.
