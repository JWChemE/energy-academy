A policy without targets is a wish. Targets, timelines, and accountability are what give a policy teeth - turning a statement of intent into measurable commitments that someone owns and the organisation can be held to.

## Why targets give a policy teeth

A commitment to "improve energy performance" can never be failed - or achieved. It commits to nothing measurable. **Targets** transform the policy's commitments into concrete, measurable goals:
- They make progress visible and accountable
- They focus effort and resources
- They allow success or failure to be judged
- They drive action in a way that vague aspirations never can

Targets are what separate a policy that changes things from a policy that decorates a wall.

## SMART targets

Effective targets follow the SMART discipline (introduced in the strategy course):
- **Specific** - exactly what, and which energy or carbon
- **Measurable** - quantified, so progress is tracked (the M&V and EnPI links)
- **Achievable** - stretching but realistic
- **Relevant** - aligned with the policy and the business
- **Time-bound** - with clear deadlines

"Reduce energy" is not a target. "Reduce energy use per square metre by 15% by the end of 2028, against a 2024 baseline" is - specific, measurable, time-bound, and accountable.

## Types of target

Energy policies typically include several kinds of target:
- **Energy efficiency targets** - reducing energy use or intensity (kWh/m2, kWh/unit)
- **Carbon targets** - reducing emissions (often the headline: net zero by a date)
- **Renewable targets** - a proportion of energy from renewable sources
- **Process targets** - milestones in building the management system

Carbon and net-zero targets increasingly dominate, but should be underpinned by efficiency and renewable targets that show *how* the carbon goal will be reached (the net-zero roadmap link).

## Timelines and milestones

Targets need timelines, and long-term targets need interim milestones:
- A distant goal ("net zero by 2040") is too far off to drive present action alone
- **Interim milestones** ("30% reduction by 2027, 60% by 2032") create near-term accountability and keep momentum
- A trajectory of milestones turns a distant destination into a series of checkable steps

Without interim milestones, long-term targets invite procrastination - the hard work deferred until it is too late. Milestones force steady progress and reveal slippage early.

## Accountability and ownership

Targets achieve nothing without ownership. The policy and its supporting structure must establish:
- **Who owns** each target - a named role or person accountable for delivery
- **Who reports** progress, to whom, and how often
- **What happens** if targets are missed - the accountability that gives them force

A target everyone is responsible for is one no one is responsible for. Clear, individual accountability - ideally embedded in managers' objectives and appraisals (the governance link) - is what makes targets bite.

## Baselines and measurement

Every target needs a baseline and a measurement method:
- A **baseline** - the reference point against which progress is measured (the M&V link)
- A **measurement method** - how progress will be tracked, normalised for the factors that affect energy use
- **Regular reporting** of progress against the target

Without a sound baseline and honest measurement, target progress is meaningless - and easily manipulated (the M&V pitfalls). The measurement disciplines underpin credible targets.

## Balancing ambition and realism

Setting the level of a target is a judgement:
- **Too easy** - and it fails to stretch the organisation or drive real change
- **Too hard** - and it demoralises, or invites gaming and excuses

Good targets are ambitious enough to require genuine effort and change, yet realistic enough to be credibly achievable. They often align with external reference points - science-based targets for carbon, sector benchmarks for efficiency - which lend both ambition and credibility (the net-zero credibility link).

## The accountability chain

Ultimately, targets, timelines, and accountability form a chain that gives the policy force: the policy commits, targets quantify the commitment, timelines and milestones schedule it, baselines and measurement track it, and accountability ensures someone delivers it. This chain is what transforms a policy from words into results - and it leads directly to the next questions of who, exactly, is responsible, which the following lesson addresses.
