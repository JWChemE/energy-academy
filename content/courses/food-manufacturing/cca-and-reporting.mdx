Food manufacturing is energy-intensive enough that the UK tax system treats it specially. Through a Climate Change Agreement, a food factory can claim a substantial discount on the Climate Change Levy it pays on every unit of gas and electricity, in exchange for committing to energy-efficiency targets. The scheme was renewed in the mid-2020s and now runs deep into the next decade, so this is not legacy policy: for most factories the CCA is worth real money every year, and the target it carries turns everything else in this course into a compliance asset. This lesson covers the scheme as it stands, plus how ESOS and SECR land on a food business.

## The CCA deal, as it stands

The [Climate Change Levy](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/climate-change-levy) is charged on business energy; a **Climate Change Agreement** buys it down substantially (the discount has been around nine-tenths on electricity and closer to nine-tenths on gas in recent settings; the current rates are on GOV.UK) in exchange for meeting sector-negotiated efficiency targets. The machinery, as at mid-2026:

- A **new scheme** was legislated with its first target period starting **1 January 2026** and targets running to the end of 2030, with reduced CCL rates for compliant participants continuing until **31 March 2033**.
- Eligibility is sector-based. Food manufacturing's umbrella agreement is negotiated by the **Food and Drink Federation**, with individual sites holding underlying agreements; other food subsectors (dairy, meat, bakers) have their own sector bodies and agreements.
- Targets are typically expressed as improvements in energy per unit of throughput, measured against a baseline: exactly the **specific energy consumption** discipline from the [benchmarking lesson](/courses/food-manufacturing/kwh-per-tonne). Miss the target and the shortfall is settled with a buy-out payment per tonne of CO₂e.
- **New entrants** can join through annual application windows (historically 1 January to 31 August each year), so a factory that never joined, or a new site, should check eligibility rather than assume the door is shut.

  <Given>
    - A factory using 2,462,000 kWh of electricity and 1,200,000 kWh of gas a year
    - Illustrative main CCL rates of roughly £0.0078/kWh (electricity) and £0.0069/kWh (gas), with CCA discounts of 92% and 89% respectively (check current rates on GOV.UK; they change at Budgets)
  </Given>
  <Find>The approximate annual value of holding a CCA.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Full levy: 2,462,000 × 0.0078 + 1,200,000 × 0.0069 ≈ £19,204 + £8,280 ≈ **£27,484 a year**.

    With the discounts: electricity levy falls by ~£17,668 and gas by ~£7,369, so the CCA is
    worth roughly **£25,000 a year** to this site, every year, for meeting targets the rest of
    this course would pursue anyway. Against administration costs of a few thousand pounds, the
    case makes itself; the real work is the measurement system behind the target reporting.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## ESOS and SECR for a food business

The reporting schemes from the [Level 1 regulation course](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/esos) apply with no special food exemptions. **ESOS** catches large undertakings (broadly 250+ employees, or the turnover/balance-sheet tests) for four-yearly audits, with Phase 4 assessments due by December 2027; a multi-site food group audits all its sites' energy, and the CCA's measurement infrastructure does most of the data work for free. **SECR** puts energy, emissions and an intensity ratio into large companies' annual reports; for a food manufacturer the natural intensity metric is again kWh (or tCO₂e) per tonne, and consistency between the SECR figure, the CCA reporting and the retailers' carbon questionnaires saves everyone a painful reconciliation. The practical rule: build one measurement system, feed every scheme from it.

<Callout variant="key" title="One target, three audiences">
The CCA target, the SECR disclosure and the retail customer's carbon questionnaire all ultimately ask the same question: energy per tonne, trending which way? A food business that maintains one normalised, production-adjusted dataset answers all three from a single source, claims its levy discount, and turns every efficiency project in this course into simultaneously a cost saving, a compliance credit and a commercial answer. Run them as three separate spreadsheets and each audit season starts from zero.
</Callout>

## The auditor's angle

For an energy auditor, a site's CCA status is intelligence: it means throughput and energy data exist in reportable quality, it names the person who owns the numbers, and it puts a price on underperformance (the buy-out) that strengthens every recommendation's business case.
