Everything a food factory washes away, it pays for three times: once to buy the water, once to heat it, and once to send it down the drain, and that third payment is where food manufacturing gets uniquely expensive. Trade effluent from a food site carries fats, starches, sugars and product losses that are costly to treat, and the water company charges by strength as well as volume. That pricing turns effluent from a facilities afterthought into an energy manager's ally, because most of what reduces effluent charges (less water, less heat, less product in the drain) reduces the energy bill in the same motion.

## How effluent is charged

UK water companies price trade effluent using strength-based formulas (the Mogden formula and its variants), whose practical logic the [brewery course covers in depth](/courses/breweries/trade-effluent-and-water): you pay per cubic metre, scaled up by the effluent's **COD** (chemical oxygen demand, the load of organic material to be treated) and suspended solids relative to the works' averages. Food effluent is strong: fats and oils, starches from vegetable prep and rinses, sugars and proteins from product residues, and the detergents from [washdown and CIP](/courses/food-manufacturing/hygiene-and-washdown). Strong effluent at high volume produces charges that surprise sites into their first proper water audit, and a **fat, oil and grease (FOG)** management regime is usually a consent condition, not an option: interceptors and fat traps maintained on schedule, because fat in the sewer is both a charge multiplier and an offence.

## Product down the drain is the expensive kind

Here is the compounding arithmetic that makes effluent an energy topic. A kilogram of product rinsed into the drain was bought as raw material, processed with gas and electricity through every stage it reached, and then becomes COD you pay the water company to digest. Yield losses and effluent strength are the same problem seen from two invoices.

<WorkedExample title="one percent of product, three invoices">
  <Given>
    - A 12,000 t/yr factory loses 1% of throughput (120 t) to drain as washings, spills and line-clearance waste
    - Raw material and processing value: conservatively £800/t; the site's process energy: ~305 kWh/t already spent on that product
    - Effluent surcharge from the added COD: roughly £150 per tonne of product-equivalent for a typical strong food effluent (site-specific; from the water company's Mogden terms)
  </Given>
  <Find>The scale of the annual cost, and where it shows up.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Materials and processing: 120 × £800 ≈ **£96,000** of bought-and-made product to the sewer.
    Embedded energy: 120 × 305 ≈ 36,600 kWh, about **£5,000 of gas and electricity** at typical
    prices. Effluent surcharge: 120 × £150 ≈ **£18,000** in strength charges.

    Round £120,000 a year, and only the last slice is labelled "effluent" on any invoice, which
    is why dry clean-up (scrape, sweep and capture before the hose comes out) is one of the
    highest-value habits in the sector: it recovers yield, cuts COD, and shrinks the hot-water
    bill in one move.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## The water-energy nexus, factory edition

Because so much of a food site's water is *hot* water, every cubic metre saved carries an energy dividend: at the washdown conditions from the [hygiene lesson](/courses/food-manufacturing/hygiene-and-washdown) (10 to 60 °C lift, 85% calorifier), each m³ avoided saves about 58 kWh of heat, roughly 68 kWh of gas, £4 of fuel, on top of the water purchase and the effluent volume charge. Water-efficiency measures therefore rank by temperature: a saved cubic metre of hot washdown water is worth several of cold yard water. Heat also leaves in the drain directly: hot CIP dumps and warm effluent streams are recoverable (heat exchangers on steady warm outfalls, recovery tanks on batch dumps) where the flows justify it, one of the pairings the [heat-recovery lesson](/courses/food-manufacturing/heat-recovery-in-food) ranks.

<Callout variant="key" title="Read the effluent bill as an energy document">
Volume tells you how much water (much of it heated) the site really discharges; COD tells you how much bought, processed, energy-soaked product went with it. A rising effluent charge is frequently the first visible symptom of a process problem: a leaking valve on a product line, washdown discipline slipping, a CIP recipe dumping hot caustic. The energy manager who reviews effluent invoices alongside gas and electricity finds faults months before they surface anywhere else.
</Callout>

## Consents and the compliance frame

Discharging trade effluent requires a **consent** from the water company (volume, strength, temperature, FOG conditions), with sampling and charges under it; exceeding consent risks enforcement, and temperature limits matter to heat-recovery designs. Water scarcity is pushing companies toward tighter consents and higher strength charges, so the direction of travel rewards sites that treat water, energy and yield as one system.
