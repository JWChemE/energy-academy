## How the stages work

This capstone follows the same staged format as the [Energy Audits course's own capstone](/courses/energy-audits/field-audit-capstone), because a food factory audit *is* an energy audit: this course has given you everything sector-specific on top of that general method:

1. **Plan & Scope**: choose the audit level, draw the boundary correctly (does the haulier's diesel count?), and plan hygiene inductions, the ammonia plant room and a washdown-shift visit before you walk in.
2. **On-Site**: the observations that matter on a cook-chill site, plus twelve months of production and utility data with one figure missing for you to derive.
3. **Analyse & Normalise**: build the two-variable models (production coefficients, base loads and the cooling-degree-day term on the electricity meter), then compute the per-tonne figures a retailer can track.
4. **Opportunities**: quantify the desuperheater and the defrost conversion from this course's diagnostics, then decide what belongs in the recommendations and what would cross a food-safety floor.
5. **Report & M&V**: what the report owes its audiences, including the retail customer reading the carbon plan built on it, and how savings get verified rather than assumed when production moves year to year.

<Callout variant="key" title="The base load is the plot twist">
Stage 3 makes you compute that 40% of Ashdown's gas is production-independent: washdown, CIP and space heating that happen whether the lines ran hard or not. That single number reframes the whole audit, because it says nearly half the gas bill lives outside the cooking process everyone assumes dominates, in exactly the hygiene loads this course taught you to quantify, recover into, and never confuse with the per-tonne efficiency a retailer's questionnaire asks about.
</Callout>

## What "audit-ready" looks like here

A strong run through this capstone means every stage's answers hold up on their own: the boundary defensible, the hazards genuinely food-specific (hygiene regimes and ammonia, not generic hard hats), the model exact to the meter, the opportunities correctly separated from the HACCP floors, and a report whose savings claims survive a production-adjusted comparison. That's the same bar the [Energy Audits capstone](/courses/energy-audits/field-audit-capstone) sets, applied to the cook-chill-clean world this course has taught you to navigate.
