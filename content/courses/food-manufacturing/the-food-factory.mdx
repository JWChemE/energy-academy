Food manufacturing is the UK's largest manufacturing sector, and its energy story is unlike any other industry on this platform. A food factory does three energetic things, over and over, on a rhythm set by food safety rather than convenience: it heats food to cook it, it cools food to keep it safe, and it heats water to clean everything the food touched. Heat, cold and hot water. Follow a product through the building once and you will know where every significant kilowatt-hour on site is going, and why the biggest gas load of the day often starts after production stops.

## From intake to despatch

The details vary enormously between a bakery, a dairy and a ready-meals plant, but the archetype holds:

1. **Intake and chilled storage.** Raw materials arrive and most go straight into chilled or frozen storage: a refrigeration load that runs around the clock, production or not.
2. **Preparation.** Washing, cutting, mixing, forming. Mechanically light, but the prep areas themselves are often chilled to 10 to 12 °C for food safety, so the *room* is a refrigeration load even when the process isn't.
3. **Cooking.** Kettles, ovens, fryers and steamers take the product through its thermal kill step. This is the factory's big gas load: steam, direct-fired ovens or thermal oil, covered properly in the next lesson.
4. **Chilling or freezing.** Cooked product must come down through the danger zone quickly: blast chillers take it to 5 °C or below, spiral freezers to −18 °C. The [refrigeration lesson](/courses/food-manufacturing/chilling-and-freezing) quantifies why the freezing route costs several times the chilling route.
5. **Packaging.** Flow wrappers, tray sealers, vacuum packing, labelling and case packing, plus the compressed air behind the pneumatics: the electrical hum of the factory's back half.
6. **Cold despatch.** Finished goods wait in chilled or frozen warehousing until the hauliers arrive. Like intake storage, it never switches off.

Then, when the last product leaves the line, the factory's other shift begins: **washdown and CIP**. Every surface, vessel and line that touched food is cleaned to a verified hygiene standard, most of it with hot water. On many food sites a third or more of the annual gas bill is burned after production has finished for the day.

## The three energy centres

Roll that journey up and the site's energy concentrates in three places. **Process heat** (gas, mostly): cooking, baking, frying, plus the hot water and steam for cleaning. **Refrigeration** (electricity): chilled prep rooms, blast chilling, freezing, and storage at both ends of the process, usually the largest electrical load on site and, on bigger sites, often an ammonia plant. **Everything else** (electricity): packaging lines, compressed air, ventilation, lighting: individually modest, collectively worth auditing, covered in the [utilities lesson](/courses/food-manufacturing/utilities-and-packaging).

A food factory heats product, cools product, and heats water to clean up afterwards, and the three loads are physically adjacent all day long. That adjacency is the sector's defining opportunity: the refrigeration plant rejects heat continuously a few metres from a washdown system buying the same heat from the gas main. Most of the savings this course finds, and both of its diagnostic cases, live in the space between these three loads.

## Why the schedule is half the story

Like the [brewery course](/courses/breweries/the-brewing-process), a food factory's load follows its production plan, not the weather. But there is a food-specific twist: chunks of the load are **fixed by hygiene and safety, not by output**. The washdown happens whether the line ran at full rate or half rate. The cold stores hold temperature through weekends and shutdowns. Prep rooms are chilled all shift regardless of throughput. This fixed base is why a quiet month barely dents the bills, why per-tonne energy gets *worse* at low production, and why the [benchmarking lesson](/courses/food-manufacturing/kwh-per-tonne) insists on separating base load from production-driven load before judging anything.
