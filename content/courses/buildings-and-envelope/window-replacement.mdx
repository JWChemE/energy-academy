Replacing windows is the most visible fabric upgrade and often the one occupants ask for first. On pure energy grounds, though, it usually has the longest payback of any fabric measure, because windows are a small fraction of the total fabric area. Knowing when replacement genuinely makes sense, and when the money is better spent elsewhere first, avoids both disappointment and wasted capital. This lesson works the energy case honestly, then sets it alongside the comfort, condensation and noise benefits that usually justify the decision.

## The energy case, honestly

Upgrading from single glazing (around 5.0 W/m²K) to modern double glazing (around 1.4 W/m²K) cuts that element's heat loss by roughly 70%. But because windows are a small share of the fabric, the whole-building saving is modest.

<WorkedExample title="the window-replacement payback">
  <Given>
    - 15 m² of single glazing (U ≈ 5.0) replaced with good double glazing (U ≈ 1.4)
    - UK heating degree-days ≈ 2,100; gas heating at £0.06/kWh; a full glazing job ≈ £8,000
  </Given>
  <Find>The annual energy saving and the payback on energy alone.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Annual saving ≈ (5.0 − 1.4) × 15 × 2,100 × 24 ÷ 1,000 ≈ 2,720 kWh, worth about
    **£163 a year**. Payback on energy alone ≈ 8,000 ÷ 163 ≈ **49 years**, far beyond the
    windows' own life. This is why glazing sits late in the [fabric-first sequence](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/retrofit-approach):
    on energy alone it rarely justifies itself. The case for replacement almost always
    rests on the other benefits below, and on the fact that windows often need replacing
    anyway.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## The wider case

Energy is rarely the only driver, and it is usually not the decisive one:

- **Comfort**: modern glazing eliminates the cold-radiation and downdraught effect of single panes, which is what actually makes a room near a window feel unpleasant.
- **Condensation**: warm inner surfaces stop the streaming condensation common on single glazing, protecting frames and finishes.
- **Noise**: double, and especially acoustic laminated, glazing cuts external noise dramatically, often the real reason occupants want it.
- **Security and maintenance**: new units bring better locks and end the upkeep of rotting frames.

When these are valued alongside energy, replacement often makes sense, particularly when the windows need replacing anyway.

## Whole-window and installation matter

Specify by whole-window U-value (Uw), not just the glass, and insist on argon fill with a soft-coat low-E, a warm-edge spacer, an insulated thermally broken frame, and good installation with an insulated, airtight perimeter seal. A superb unit fitted with a gappy, foam-only perimeter leaks air around its edge and disappoints, undoing much of what you paid for.

<Callout variant="key" title="Bundle glazing with work you are doing anyway">
The economics of window replacement transform when it is done as part of wider works: a refurbishment, an extension, or [external wall insulation](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/insulation-retrofit), where new windows can be moved into the insulation plane to cut thermal bridging. Replacing windows during works already under way captures the comfort, condensation and noise benefits while sharing the access and disruption costs, and it avoids the fifty-year payback of doing it purely for energy. The rule: rarely replace windows for energy alone, but rarely miss the chance to upgrade them when you have the walls open.
</Callout>

Where original windows must be kept for heritage reasons, [secondary glazing](/courses/insulation-systems/windows-glazing) roughly halves the heat loss at far lower cost and disruption, and the insulation-systems course covers that route. The final lessons of this course turn to the largest fabric prize: insulating the walls.
