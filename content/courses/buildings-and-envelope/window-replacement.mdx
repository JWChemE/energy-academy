Replacing windows is the most visible fabric upgrade and often the one occupants ask for first - but on pure energy grounds it usually has the longest payback. Knowing when it makes sense avoids disappointment.

## The energy case

Upgrading from single glazing (around 5.0 W/m2K) to modern double glazing (around 1.4 W/m2K) cuts that element's heat loss by roughly 70%. But windows are a small fraction of total fabric area, so the whole-building saving is modest.

A typical house spending 1,200 GBP/year on heating might save 80-120 GBP/year from a full glazing upgrade costing 6,000-10,000 GBP - a payback of 50 years or more on energy alone. This is why glazing sits late in the fabric-first sequence.

## The wider case

Energy is rarely the only driver:
- **Comfort:** modern glazing eliminates the cold-radiation and downdraught effect of single panes near seating.
- **Condensation:** warm inner surfaces stop the streaming condensation common on single glazing.
- **Noise:** double and especially acoustic laminated glazing cut external noise dramatically.
- **Security and maintenance:** new units bring better locks and end the upkeep of rotting frames.

When these are valued alongside energy, replacement often makes sense - particularly when windows need replacing anyway.

## Whole-window matters

Specify by whole-window U-value (Uw), not just the glass. Insist on:
- Argon fill and a soft-coat low-E
- A warm-edge spacer
- An insulated, thermally broken frame
- Good installation with insulated, airtight perimeter sealing

A superb unit fitted with a gappy, foam-only perimeter leaks air around its edge and disappoints.

## Bundle for value

The economics transform when glazing is replaced as part of wider work - a refurbishment, an extension, or external wall insulation (where new windows can be moved into the insulation plane to cut thermal bridging). Replacing windows during works you are doing anyway captures the comfort and condensation benefits while sharing the access and disruption costs.
