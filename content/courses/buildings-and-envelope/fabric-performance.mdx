The building fabric, its walls, roof, floor and windows, decides how much heat escapes in winter and enters in summer, and therefore how large the heating and cooling systems have to be to compensate. Controlling losses at the fabric is almost always cheaper over a building's life than oversizing the plant to fight them, which is why "fabric first" is the founding principle of low-energy building. This lesson introduces the U-value, the fabric-first logic, and where a building's heat actually goes.

## The U-value

A **U-value** measures how readily heat passes through a building element, in watts per square metre per kelvin (W/m²K). Lower is better. It is the single most useful number in fabric work, and the [insulation-systems course](/courses/insulation-systems/heat-loss-calculation) covers how it is calculated from the layers of a construction.

- A solid Victorian brick wall: around 2.0 W/m²K
- An uninsulated cavity wall: around 1.5 W/m²K
- A modern insulated wall: 0.18 to 0.28 W/m²K
- Best-practice (Passivhaus) wall: below 0.15 W/m²K

Heat loss through an element is simply U × area × temperature difference, so the U-value scales the loss directly.

<WorkedExample title="what the U-value is worth on one wall">
  <Given>
    - A 50 m² wall; indoor-outdoor temperature difference 20 °C
    - Before insulation U = 1.5 W/m²K; after, U = 0.3 W/m²K
  </Given>
  <Find>The heat-loss rate before and after.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Before: 1.5 × 50 × 20 = **1,500 W**. After: 0.3 × 50 × 20 = **300 W**, an **80%
    reduction** in the heat leaking through that wall, hour after hour, all winter. Every
    fabric decision reduces to this arithmetic: lower the U-value, and the loss falls in
    exact proportion. The [retrofit lessons](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/insulation-retrofit)
    turn this rate into annual pounds.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Fabric first

The "fabric first" principle says: reduce demand through the building envelope before investing in efficient systems or renewables. A well-insulated building needs a smaller boiler, smaller radiators, less pipework, and less renewable generation to reach net zero, and it holds its temperature through a power cut or a price spike. The savings compound, and low fabric heat loss is also what makes low-temperature [heat pumps](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/heating-modes) viable, since a leaky building needs hot radiators that wreck a heat pump's efficiency.

## Where heat is lost

In a typical uninsulated house, heat escapes roughly as:

- 35% through walls
- 25% through the roof
- 15% through draughts and ventilation
- 15% through floors
- 10% through windows

The proportions shift as you insulate: once walls and roof are dealt with, draughts and windows come to dominate. This is why a retrofit follows a [sequence](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/retrofit-approach) rather than tackling everything at once, and why the biggest single element (walls) usually leads.

<Callout variant="key" title="Diminishing returns set the economic limit">
Each added layer of insulation saves less than the last. Cutting a wall from U = 2.0 to U = 0.5 removes 1.5 units of loss; going further to 0.3 removes only 0.2 more. There is an economic optimum where more insulation no longer justifies its cost, the subject of [economic thickness](/courses/insulation-systems/economic-thickness). Fabric-first does not mean insulate without limit; it means insulate to the point where the next pound is better spent elsewhere, then move on.
</Callout>
