The building fabric - walls, roof, floor, and windows - determines how much heat escapes in winter and enters in summer. Controlling losses at the fabric is almost always cheaper than oversizing the heating and cooling plant to compensate.

## The U-value

A **U-value** measures how readily heat passes through a building element, in watts per square metre per kelvin (W/m2K). Lower is better.

- A solid Victorian brick wall: around 2.0 W/m2K
- An uninsulated cavity wall: around 1.5 W/m2K
- A modern insulated wall: 0.18-0.28 W/m2K
- Best-practice (Passivhaus) wall: below 0.15 W/m2K

Heat loss through an element is simply U x Area x temperature difference. A 100 m2 wall at 2.0 W/m2K loses four times as much as the same wall at 0.5 W/m2K for the same indoor-outdoor temperature gap.

## Fabric first

The "fabric first" principle says: reduce demand through the building envelope before investing in efficient systems or renewables. A well-insulated building needs a smaller boiler, smaller radiators, less pipework, and less renewable generation to reach net zero. The savings compound.

## Where heat is lost

In a typical uninsulated house, heat escapes roughly as:
- 35% through walls
- 25% through the roof
- 15% through draughts and ventilation
- 15% through floors
- 10% through windows

The proportions shift as you insulate - once walls and roof are dealt with, draughts and windows dominate. This is why a retrofit follows a sequence rather than tackling everything at once.

## Diminishing returns

Each added layer of insulation saves less than the last. Going from U=2.0 to U=0.5 saves 1.5 units of loss; going from 0.5 to 0.3 saves only 0.2. There is an economic optimum where the cost of more insulation no longer justifies the marginal saving - the subject of economic thickness, covered later.
