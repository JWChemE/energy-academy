Modern glass is engineered with microscopically thin coatings that transform its thermal behaviour without you being able to see them. The right coating can halve a window's heat loss or block unwanted solar heat while keeping the view clear, and coatings are the single biggest reason a modern window outperforms an old one of the same thickness. This lesson covers the two coating families, how to match them to climate and orientation, and how they compare with shading.

## Low-emissivity (low-E) coatings

A **low-E coating** is a transparent metallic-oxide layer that reflects long-wave (heat) radiation while letting visible light through. In winter it bounces indoor heat back into the room instead of letting it radiate out through the glass, cutting the loss dramatically.

- **Soft-coat (sputtered) low-E**: highest performance, used on a protected surface inside a sealed unit, bringing centre-of-glass U-values down to 1.0 to 1.1 W/m²K.
- **Hard-coat (pyrolytic) low-E**: more durable, slightly lower performance, and can be used on exposed surfaces.

Low-E is now standard in virtually all new double and triple glazing, and it is the main reason modern windows beat old ones so decisively.

## Solar-control coatings

Where overheating is the concern, **solar-control coatings** reflect a portion of the near-infrared solar spectrum (the heat) while keeping good visible-light transmission (the daylight). This is the key to controlling cooling loads in glazed offices without resorting to dark tinted glass that forces the lights on. The trade-off is described by the **light-to-solar-gain ratio**: the best solar-control glass admits plenty of daylight while rejecting much of the solar heat, so you keep the view and the daylight but lose the overheating.

## Matching coating to climate and orientation

The correct specification depends on what dominates, and, as the [solar-gain lesson](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/solar-gain) showed, that varies by elevation:

- **Heating-dominated building, north facade**: maximise low-E, accept or even welcome solar gain.
- **South facade with overheating risk**: combine low-E with solar control, or rely on external shading.
- **Cooling-dominated office, west facade**: a strong solar-control coating to block the low afternoon sun.

A single building often needs different glazing specifications on different elevations. Specifying one product everywhere either wastes money on faces that do not need it or leaves a facade overheating.

## Coatings versus shading

Coatings and [shading](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/solar-gain) solve overlapping problems differently, and the best designs use both. Coatings work all year round and cannot be adjusted, so a solar-control coating that tames a summer west facade also rejects welcome heat on a sunny winter afternoon. External shading, by contrast, can be tuned to sun geometry (a fixed overhang) or adjusted (louvres, blinds) to admit winter sun and block summer sun. The complementary strategy is a sensible coating plus shading matched to each orientation, rather than relying on either alone.

<Callout variant="key" title="Coatings are permanent; shading is selective">
The key distinction when choosing between them: a coating applies its effect to every hour of every season equally, which is ideal for the year-round benefit of low-E but a blunt instrument for solar control, where you often *want* winter sun and only reject it in summer. Where the requirement is seasonal, adjustable external shading beats a fixed coating, because it can tell the seasons apart. Use low-E everywhere for the heat-loss benefit, then decide solar control facade by facade, favouring external shading where winter gain is worth keeping.
</Callout>
