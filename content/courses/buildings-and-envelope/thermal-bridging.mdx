A thermal bridge is a localised path where heat bypasses the insulation - through a more conductive material or a geometric weak point. Even a well-insulated building can lose significant heat, and suffer condensation, through neglected bridges.

## Two kinds of bridge

**Repeating bridges** occur regularly across an element: timber or steel studs in a framed wall, mortar joints, joist ends. Their effect is averaged into the element U-value.

**Non-repeating (linear) bridges** occur at junctions: where a wall meets a floor, around window reveals, at balconies, lintels, and wall-roof junctions. These are quantified by a **psi-value** (linear thermal transmittance, W/mK) and can add 10-30% to the calculated fabric heat loss if ignored.

## Why bridges matter beyond energy

Where a bridge chills the inner surface, two problems follow:
- **Condensation and mould.** If the inner surface drops below the dew point, moisture condenses. Cold corners behind furniture are classic mould sites.
- **Comfort.** Cold inner surfaces radiate away body heat, making a room feel colder than the air temperature suggests.

The risk is captured by the **temperature factor (fRsi)** - the ratio describing how warm the inner surface stays. Building standards require fRsi above about 0.75 to avoid mould.

## Designing them out

- Maintain a continuous insulation layer - draw it as an unbroken line around the section without lifting the pen.
- Use insulated lintels, cavity closers, and thermal breaks at balconies and parapets.
- Specify accredited construction details with known psi-values.
- In framed walls, add a continuous layer of external or internal insulation to interrupt the repeating stud bridge.

## A worked sense of scale

A typical house with poorly detailed junctions can lose the equivalent of an extra 0.1-0.2 W/m2K averaged over its whole envelope through linear bridges alone - comparable to leaving a small portion of the walls uninsulated. Good detailing is cheap at design stage and almost impossible to fix later.
