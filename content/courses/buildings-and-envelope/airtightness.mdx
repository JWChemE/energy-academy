Insulation slows the heat that conducts through the fabric, but heat also escapes a second way: bodily, carried out in warm air leaking through gaps in the construction and replaced by cold air that must be heated from scratch. A beautifully insulated but leaky building still feels draughty and still costs a fortune to heat. Airtightness is the discipline of controlling that leakage, and it sits alongside insulation as one of the two halves of fabric performance. This lesson covers why leakage matters, how it is measured, and the "build tight, ventilate right" principle that stops airtightness becoming a health problem.

## Why air leakage matters

Every cubic metre of warm air that escapes through a gap is replaced by cold outside air that must be heated to room temperature. In a leaky building, this uncontrolled **infiltration** can account for 15 to 25% of total heat loss, and it does not respond to a thicker layer of insulation, only to sealing the gaps. It also undermines comfort directly: cold draughts at ankle level make occupants turn the thermostat up, compounding the loss.

## Measuring it: the blower-door test

A blower-door test seals a fan into an external doorway and pressurises (or depressurises) the building to 50 pascals. The airflow needed to hold that pressure reveals the leakage rate, expressed as **air changes per hour (ACH) at 50 Pa** or as **m³/h per m²** of envelope area. Typical results span a wide range:

- Old, leaky building: 10 to 15 ACH50
- UK new-build minimum: around 5 to 8 m³/h/m²
- Best practice: 1 to 3 ACH50
- Passivhaus standard: 0.6 ACH50 or lower

During the test, a smoke pencil or a [thermal imaging camera](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-installation) pinpoints the exact leakage paths while the building is under pressure.

## Where leaks hide

The usual culprits are predictable, and few of them are the obvious ones:

- Service penetrations: pipes, cables, extractor ducts
- Gaps around window and door frames
- Loft hatches and recessed downlights
- Junctions between floors and walls
- Unsealed skirting and floorboards

## Ventilation: tight but not sealed

A common worry is that an airtight building cannot breathe. The principle that resolves it is **"build tight, ventilate right"**: seal the uncontrolled leaks, then provide deliberate, controlled ventilation you can actually manage. Uncontrolled leakage ventilates a building at random, heavily on windy days, barely on still ones, and never where you want it. Controlled ventilation delivers fresh air on purpose, and in high-performance buildings it does so through mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (MVHR), which supplies fresh air while recovering 85 to 90% of the heat from the outgoing stale air, the [air-to-air recovery](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/air-to-air-recovery) covered in the waste-heat course.

<Callout variant="key" title="Seal the leaks you don't want, then ventilate on purpose">
Airtightness is not about sealing a building shut; it is about replacing random, uncontrollable leakage with deliberate, heat-recovering ventilation. Uncontrolled infiltration wastes heat *and* fails to guarantee air quality, the worst of both. A tight envelope plus controlled ventilation (MVHR in high-performance buildings, well-managed mechanical or trickle ventilation elsewhere) gives good air quality without the heat penalty of draughts. Sealing without adding ventilation, though, traps moisture and is a genuine hazard, which is why the two must be designed together.
</Callout>

Airtightness and insulation together control conductive and convective loss through the opaque fabric. The next lessons turn to the transparent parts, where the trade-offs are sharpest: solar gain and glazing.
