Insulation slows conductive heat loss, but air leakage - uncontrolled draughts through gaps in the fabric - can waste just as much energy. A beautifully insulated but leaky building still feels cold and costs a fortune to heat.

## Why air leakage matters

Every cubic metre of warm air that escapes through a gap is replaced by cold outside air that must be heated from scratch. In a leaky building, infiltration can account for 15-25% of total heat loss. It also undermines comfort: cold draughts at ankle level make occupants turn the thermostat up.

## Measuring it: the blower-door test

A blower-door test seals a fan into an external doorway and pressurises (or depressurises) the building to 50 pascals. The air flow needed to hold that pressure reveals the leakage rate, expressed as:
- **Air changes per hour (ACH) at 50 Pa**, or
- **m3/h per m2** of envelope area

Typical results:
- Old, leaky building: 10-15 ACH50
- UK new-build minimum: around 5-8 m3/h/m2
- Best practice: 1-3 ACH50
- Passivhaus standard: 0.6 ACH50 or lower

## Where leaks hide

Common leakage paths include:
- Service penetrations (pipes, cables, extractor ducts)
- Gaps around window and door frames
- Loft hatches and recessed downlights
- Junctions between floors and walls
- Unsealed skirting and floorboards

Smoke pencils and thermal imaging during a blower-door test pinpoint the exact paths.

## Ventilation: tight but not sealed

A common worry is that an airtight building cannot breathe. The principle is "build tight, ventilate right" - seal the uncontrolled leaks, then provide deliberate, controlled ventilation. In high-performance buildings this means mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (MVHR), which supplies fresh air while recovering 85-90% of the heat from the outgoing stale air. You get good air quality without the heat penalty of draughts.
