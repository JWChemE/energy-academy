Sunlight entering through glazing is free heat in winter and an unwanted load in summer. Managing solar gain is about capturing it when useful and rejecting it when not.

## The g-value

The **g-value** (or solar heat gain coefficient, SHGC) is the fraction of solar energy striking the glass that ends up inside, from 0 to 1.

- Clear double glazing: g around 0.7 (admits 70% of solar energy)
- Solar-control glass: g of 0.3-0.4
- Heavily tinted/reflective: g below 0.25

A high g-value is an asset on a sunny south facade in a heating climate and a liability on a west facade prone to overheating.

## Orientation changes everything

- **South** (northern hemisphere): high sun angle in summer, low in winter. A modest horizontal overhang blocks high summer sun while admitting low winter sun - the classic passive-solar move.
- **East and west:** low sun morning and evening, striking glass almost head-on. Overhangs barely help; these elevations need vertical shading, solar-control glass, or internal blinds.
- **North:** little direct sun, mostly diffuse light. Prioritise low U-value over solar control.

## Shading strategies

**Fixed external shading** - overhangs, fins, brise-soleil - is maintenance-free and tuned to sun geometry. Most effective on south facades.

**Adjustable shading** - external louvres, internal blinds - adapts to conditions and occupant preference. External is far more effective than internal, because it stops solar energy before it passes the glass.

**Landscape and self-shading** - deciduous trees shade in summer and admit sun in winter once leaves fall; building form can shade its own glazing.

## Balancing daylight, glare, and heat

The goal is not to eliminate solar gain but to balance three things: useful daylight (reducing lighting energy), glare control (occupant comfort), and heat (heating benefit versus cooling penalty). Over-shading forces lights on at midday; under-shading bakes the occupants. Good design tunes each facade to its orientation and the building's heating-versus-cooling balance.
