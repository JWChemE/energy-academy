Sunlight entering through glazing is free heat in winter and an unwanted load in summer, and the same window can be an asset or a liability depending on which season and which elevation you are looking at. Managing solar gain is about capturing it when it helps and rejecting it when it does not, and it is one of the few fabric decisions where the right answer genuinely differs from one face of the building to the next. This lesson covers the g-value, why orientation changes everything, and the shading strategies that tune each facade.

## The g-value

The **g-value** (or solar heat gain coefficient, SHGC) is the fraction of solar energy striking the glass that ends up inside, from 0 to 1.

- Clear double glazing: g around 0.7 (admits 70% of solar energy)
- Solar-control glass: g of 0.3 to 0.4
- Heavily tinted or reflective: g below 0.25

A high g-value is an asset on a sunny south facade in a heating-dominated climate and a liability on a west facade prone to overheating. There is no universally "good" g-value; there is only the right one for a given orientation and the building's heating-versus-cooling balance.

## Orientation changes everything

- **South** (northern hemisphere): the sun is high in summer and low in winter. A modest horizontal overhang blocks the high summer sun while admitting the low winter sun, the classic passive-solar move that costs nothing to run.
- **East and west**: the sun is low morning and evening, striking glass almost head-on. Overhangs barely help; these elevations need vertical shading, solar-control glass, or internal blinds.
- **North**: little direct sun, mostly diffuse light. Prioritise a low U-value here over solar control, because there is little gain to manage.

This is why a single glazing specification applied to every elevation either wastes money or leaves a facade overheating: each face has a different sun geometry and wants a different answer.

## Shading strategies

**Fixed external shading** (overhangs, fins, brise-soleil) is maintenance-free and tuned to the sun's geometry, most effective on south facades where the summer-winter sun-angle difference is largest.

**Adjustable shading** (external louvres, internal blinds) adapts to conditions and occupant preference. External shading is far more effective than internal, because it stops solar energy *before* it passes the glass; once the sun is through the glass, an internal blind is largely reradiating heat that is already inside.

**Landscape and self-shading**: deciduous trees shade in summer and admit sun in winter once the leaves fall, and building form can shade its own glazing.

<Callout variant="key" title="Balance daylight, glare and heat; do not just block the sun">
The goal is not to eliminate solar gain but to balance three things at once: useful daylight (which cuts [lighting energy](/courses/lighting/daylight-harvesting)), glare control (occupant comfort), and heat (a winter benefit but a summer penalty). Over-shading forces the lights on at midday, turning a cooling saving into a lighting cost; under-shading bakes the occupants and drives up cooling. Good design tunes each facade to its orientation and to whether the building is heating- or cooling-dominated, rather than applying one rule everywhere.
</Callout>
