Insulating the walls of an existing building is the highest-impact fabric measure - and the one most likely to cause problems if done badly. There are two routes: internal and external.

## External wall insulation (EWI)

A layer of insulation is fixed to the outside of the wall and finished with render or cladding.

**Advantages:**
- No loss of internal floor area
- Existing wall becomes part of the warm side, adding thermal mass and reducing overheating swings
- Treats thermal bridges continuously
- Can be done while the building stays occupied
- Refreshes the external appearance

**Disadvantages:**
- Changes the external look - often impossible on protected or terraced frontages
- Detailing around eaves, windows, and the ground line is critical and skilled
- Higher cost (typically 100-200 GBP/m2)

EWI is usually the better technical choice where appearance and planning allow.

## Internal wall insulation (IWI)

Insulation is added to the inside face of external walls.

**Advantages:**
- No change to external appearance - suits conservation areas and flats
- Can be done room by room
- Lower cost per room

**Disadvantages:**
- Loses internal floor area (50-100 mm per wall)
- Hard to treat thermal bridges at floor and party-wall junctions
- Disruptive internally - radiators, sockets, skirting must be removed
- Higher condensation risk inside the wall build-up

## The moisture question

The biggest IWI risk is **interstitial condensation**. By insulating internally, the original wall stays cold; if warm, moist indoor air reaches the cold wall, it condenses inside the construction. Mitigation includes vapour-control layers, breathable insulation systems for older solid walls, and careful detailing. A condensation-risk assessment (Glaser method or hygrothermal modelling) should precede any IWI on a solid wall.

## Cavity walls

Where a wall has a clear, suitable cavity, blown insulation (mineral fibre or bead) fills it at a fraction of solid-wall cost - typically 15-25 GBP/m2 with paybacks under 5 years. It is unsuitable for exposed, damp, or already-partially-filled cavities, so a survey comes first.
