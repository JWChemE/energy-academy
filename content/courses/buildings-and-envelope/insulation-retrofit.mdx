Insulating the walls is the highest-impact fabric measure on most existing buildings, walls being the single largest heat-loss path, and it is also the one most likely to cause problems if done badly. There are two routes for solid walls, internal and external, plus the much cheaper option of filling a suitable cavity. Choosing between them, and handling the moisture risk each carries, is what separates a transformative retrofit from an expensive one that grows mould. This lesson covers all three and the condensation question that governs them.

## External wall insulation (EWI)

A layer of insulation is fixed to the outside of the wall and finished with render or cladding. Its advantages are substantial: no loss of internal floor area, the existing wall becomes part of the warm side (adding useful thermal mass and reducing overheating swings), [thermal bridges](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/thermal-bridging) are treated continuously, the building can stay occupied, and the external appearance is refreshed. The drawbacks are that it changes the external look (often impossible on protected or terraced frontages), the detailing around eaves, windows and the ground line is critical and skilled, and it costs more, typically £100 to £200/m².

EWI is usually the better technical choice where appearance and planning allow, because it keeps the wall warm and dry and treats bridges continuously.

## Internal wall insulation (IWI)

Insulation added to the inside face suits conservation areas and flats where the exterior cannot change, can be done room by room, and costs less per room. But it loses internal floor area (50 to 100 mm per wall), makes [thermal bridges](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/thermal-bridging) at floor and party-wall junctions hard to treat, is internally disruptive (radiators, sockets and skirting must come off), and carries a higher condensation risk.

## The moisture question

The biggest IWI risk is **interstitial condensation**. By insulating internally, the original wall is left cold; if warm, moist indoor air reaches that cold wall, it condenses *inside* the construction, where it can rot timbers and decay the fabric unseen. Mitigation includes vapour-control layers, breathable insulation systems for older solid walls, and careful detailing, and a condensation-risk assessment (Glaser method or hygrothermal modelling) should precede any IWI on a solid wall. This is the [unintended consequence](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/retrofit-approach) that makes retrofit a matter of assessment, not just product installation.

## Cavity walls: the cheap win

Where a wall has a clear, suitable cavity, blown insulation (mineral fibre or bead) fills it at a fraction of solid-wall cost, typically £15 to £25/m² with paybacks under five years. On the same 90 m² wall, filling a cavity from U ≈ 1.5 to 0.5 saves around £270 a year for perhaps £1,800, a payback near six years rather than thirty. It is unsuitable for exposed, damp, or already partially filled cavities, so a survey comes first.

<Callout variant="key" title="Cavity fill is a bargain; solid-wall insulation is an investment">
The economics of wall insulation split sharply on construction. A suitable clear cavity is one of the best-value measures in the whole building, paying back in a few years, and should almost always be done. Solid-wall insulation (internal or external) rarely pays on energy alone at current prices; it is justified by comfort, carbon, condensation cure, and bundling with other works, and it demands a moisture assessment to avoid doing harm. Survey the wall first: the right measure, and its economics, depend entirely on what the wall actually is.
</Callout>

Choosing measures is one thing; sequencing them safely across a whole building is another, which is the final lesson.
