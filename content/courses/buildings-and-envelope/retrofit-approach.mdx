Retrofitting an existing building is harder than designing a new one: you work around what is already there, often while the building stays occupied, and every measure interacts with the others. Done in the wrong order, or without thinking about the building as a system, retrofit wastes money and can actively cause harm, rotting walls and growing mould where there was none before. A structured approach avoids both. This closing lesson covers the fabric-first sequence, whole-house thinking, and the unintended consequences that make assessment essential.

## Fabric first, in sequence

Tackle measures in order of cost-effectiveness and logic:

1. **Draught-proofing and loft insulation**: cheapest, fastest payback (often under two years).
2. **Cavity wall insulation** where cavities exist and are suitable: another strong payback.
3. **Heating controls and upgrades**: once demand is reduced, [right-size the system](/courses/economic-analysis/total-cost-of-ownership).
4. **Solid wall insulation** (internal or external): expensive but transformative.
5. **Glazing upgrades**: usually last on energy grounds, though comfort or condensation may bring them forward.
6. **Renewables and low-carbon heat**: only after demand is minimised.

Doing this out of order wastes money. Install a big new boiler or [heat pump](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/heating-modes) first, and you size it for the leaky building you are about to stop having, ending up with oversized, inefficient plant. Reduce the demand, then meet it.

## Whole-house thinking

The **PAS 2035** framework formalises "whole-house" retrofit: assess the building as a system, produce a medium-term improvement plan, and sequence measures so they work together rather than clash. The clash that matters most is between airtightness and moisture: sealing a building tight without adding [controlled ventilation](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/airtightness) traps moisture that used to escape through the draughts, and the result is condensation and mould. Whole-house planning catches these interactions before they become damage, and it is now the expected standard for funded domestic retrofit in the UK.

## The danger of unintended consequences

Retrofit changes how moisture and heat move through the fabric, and getting it wrong causes real harm:

- **Interstitial condensation** inside walls (especially from [internal wall insulation](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/insulation-retrofit)), leading to rot and decay.
- **Surface mould** from new [thermal bridges](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/thermal-bridging) or from reduced ventilation after sealing.
- **Overheating** if insulation and airtightness are added without considering summer [solar gain](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/solar-gain) and ventilation.

This is precisely why retrofit needs assessment, not just product installation: the same measure that saves energy can damage the building if its moisture and comfort consequences are not thought through.

<Callout variant="key" title="Reduce demand first, and treat the building as one system">
Two rules carry most of retrofit: sequence measures fabric-first so you never size heat plant for a building you are about to improve, and assess the whole building as a system so that sealing, insulating and ventilating are designed together rather than one at a time. Piecemeal retrofit, a bit of insulation here, some sealing there, no ventilation plan, is how well-meaning efficiency work ends up causing condensation and mould. The assessment costs a fraction of the works and is what makes the difference between a healthier, cheaper building and an expensive mistake.
</Callout>

Major fabric measures are disruptive and capital-intensive, so the pragmatic approach is to align them with other works: insulate externally when the render needs replacing, upgrade glazing during a refurbishment, add loft insulation before re-roofing. Bundling cuts both cost and disruption and, as the [economic analysis course](/courses/economic-analysis/portfolio-approach) shows, transforms the economics that fabric measures struggle with in isolation.

That completes the course: the fabric as a system, its weak points, and the strategy to improve it safely. The [diagnostics capstone](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/diagnostics-capstone) puts a real building's fabric in front of you to assess and prioritise.
