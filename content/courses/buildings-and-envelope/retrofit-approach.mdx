Retrofitting an existing building is harder than designing a new one - you work around what is already there, often while the building stays occupied. A structured approach avoids wasted money and the dangerous unintended consequences that careless retrofit can cause.

## Fabric first, in sequence

Tackle measures in order of cost-effectiveness and logic:

1. **Draught-proofing and loft insulation** - cheapest, fastest payback (often under 2 years).
2. **Cavity wall insulation** where cavities exist and are suitable.
3. **Heating controls and upgrades** - once demand is reduced, right-size the system.
4. **Solid wall insulation** (internal or external) - expensive but transformative.
5. **Glazing upgrades** - usually last on energy grounds, though comfort or condensation may bring them forward.
6. **Renewables and low-carbon heat** - only after demand is minimised.

Doing this out of order wastes money: install a big new boiler first and you will oversize it relative to the insulated building you end up with.

## Whole-house thinking

The PAS 2035 framework formalises "whole-house" retrofit: assess the building as a system, produce a medium-term improvement plan, and sequence measures so they work together. Piecemeal measures can clash - for example, sealing a building airtight without adding ventilation traps moisture.

## The danger of unintended consequences

Retrofit changes how moisture and heat move through the fabric. Get it wrong and you cause:
- **Interstitial condensation** inside walls, leading to rot and decay.
- **Surface mould** from new thermal bridges or reduced ventilation.
- **Overheating** if insulation is added without considering summer comfort.

This is why retrofit needs assessment, not just product installation.

## Funding and disruption

Major fabric measures are disruptive and capital-intensive. The pragmatic approach is to align them with other works - insulate externally when the render needs replacing, upgrade glazing during a refurbishment, add loft insulation before re-roofing. Bundling cuts both cost and disruption, and dramatically improves the economics covered in the next lessons.
