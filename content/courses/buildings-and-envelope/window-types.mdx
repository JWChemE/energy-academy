Windows are the weakest thermal link in most facades. A wall might achieve 0.2 W/m²K while the glazing beside it struggles to reach 1.0, so a large area of glass can dominate a building's fabric loss even when the walls are excellent. Understanding what makes one window perform far better than another, and why the frame matters as much as the glass, is essential to controlling both heat loss and solar gain. This lesson covers the glazing generations, the whole-window U-value, and the frame and spacer choices that decide real performance.

## Glazing generations

**Single glazing** runs at around 5.0 W/m²K, effectively a sheet of cold radiating surface. Rare in new work but common in older stock and a major source of both heat loss and discomfort.

**Double glazing** uses two panes with a sealed cavity. A basic air-filled unit reaches around 2.8 W/m²K; a modern argon-filled, [low-emissivity](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/glazing-coatings) unit reaches 1.2 to 1.6 W/m²K, roughly halving the loss of the basic version.

**Triple glazing** uses three panes and two cavities, achieving whole-window U-values of 0.8 to 1.0 W/m²K, approaching wall performance. Standard in cold climates and Passivhaus work, and increasingly specified in the UK for low-energy buildings.

## The whole-window U-value

A window's rating combines three parts, and the headline figure blends them:

- **Ug**: the centre-of-glass value (the best-performing part)
- **Uf**: the frame value
- **Psi-g**: the edge-of-glass thermal bridge at the spacer

The number that matters is **Uw**, the area-weighted whole-window value. A superb glass unit in a poor frame still performs badly, which is why the frame matters as much as the glass, and why specifying by glass U-value alone (a common sales trick) flatters a window's real performance.

## Frame materials

- **uPVC**: good insulation, low cost; multi-chambered profiles reach 1.2 to 1.4 W/m²K.
- **Timber**: naturally insulating and attractive, but needs maintenance.
- **Aluminium**: strong and slim but highly conductive, so it must have a polyamide **thermal break**, or the frame becomes a giant [thermal bridge](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/thermal-bridging) around every window.
- **Composite (timber-aluminium)**: insulating timber inside, weatherproof aluminium outside, a premium option combining the strengths of both.

## Spacers and gas fills

The **warm-edge spacer** that separates the panes used to be aluminium, a conductive bridge running around the entire glass perimeter. Modern insulating spacers (stainless steel or polymer) cut edge losses and reduce condensation on the inner glass edge. The cavity is filled with **argon** (or occasionally krypton in slim triple units) instead of air, which reduces convective heat transfer across the gap by around 30% for a negligible cost.

<Callout variant="key" title="Specify the whole window, not just the glass">
The commonest way to be disappointed by new windows is to specify the glass and ignore the frame, spacer and installation. A great sealed unit in a conductive frame, on an aluminium spacer, fitted with a gappy perimeter, performs far worse than its glass number suggests. Insist on the whole-window Uw, an insulated (thermally broken) frame, a warm-edge spacer, argon fill and low-E glass, and an airtight, insulated installation. The glass is only one of five things that decide how the window actually performs.
</Callout>

For a heating-dominated UK climate, prioritise a low Uw with useful solar gain. For a cooling-dominated or heavily glazed building, the [coatings and solar control](/courses/buildings-and-envelope/glazing-coatings) covered next become the deciding factor.
