You've learned how the fabric loses heat, why air leakage matters as much as insulation, how thermal bridges chill surfaces and grow mould, and how glazing and solar gain trade off across the seasons. This capstone walks you round an ageing building as the retrofit assessor, finding the waste, putting numbers on it, and prescribing the right measure in the right order.

The thread through every case is **fabric first, in sequence**: the cheapest, highest-impact measures before the expensive ones, and never a fix that causes the moisture or overheating it doesn't pay for. Some call-outs you'll crack with a quick heat-loss sum; others you reason out from the symptoms.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate**: read the brief and the panel: U-values, areas, air-change rates, surface temperatures, g-values.
2. **Calculate**: work out the figure that decides it: the heat-loss reduction (ΔU × area), the annual energy and cost via degree-days, infiltration losses, thermal-bridge heat loss, payback, or summer solar gain. The **reference data is on hand**: U-values, the annual loss factor, airtightness and glazing constants, but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose**: pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe**: build an ordered work plan (cheap fabric measures first, then capital).
5. **Verify**: score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="The fabric-first sequence">
Tackle measures in order of cost-effectiveness: **draught-proofing and loft insulation** first (paybacks under two years), then **cavity fill** where suitable, then heating controls, then **solid-wall insulation**, with **glazing last** on energy grounds, unless comfort or condensation bring it forward. Doing this out of order wastes money (a big new boiler oversized for the building you end up with), and sealing without ventilating traps moisture. Assess the whole building as a system, not a shopping list of products.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **The heat-loss chain**: ΔU × area → annual kWh via degree-days → cost → payback, applied to loft, cavity, solid wall and glazing.
- **Airtightness**: turning a blower-door result into an infiltration loss, and the "build tight, ventilate right" rule.
- **Thermal bridging**: reading the mould-in-the-corner pattern and the temperature factor, and treating junctions rather than adding more insulation.
- **The right diagnosis**: a solid wall is not a cavity; a summer gain problem is not a winter loss one.
- **Sequencing**: why glazing pays back in decades and belongs late, and how bundling transforms the economics.

## Differential thinking

The same symptom rarely has one cause. Mould in a corner might be a thermal bridge, penetrating damp, or a building sealed without ventilation, and the fix for each is different. A cold, expensive building might need loft insulation, cavity fill, or draught-proofing first, depending on where the heat actually goes. An overheating room needs solar control or shading, not the low-E coating that helps in winter. The decisive evidence is what you measure: the U-value and area, the blower-door reading, the surface temperature, the g-value and orientation.

## After you finish

- Which **measurement** decided each case: the U-value, the ACH50, the fRsi, the g-value?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, where does it sit in the fabric-first sequence: a sub-two-year quick win, a capital measure to bundle with other works, or a measure whose real justification is comfort and condensation rather than energy?
