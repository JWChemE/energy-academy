A few metres below the surface, UK ground holds a steady 8 to 12 °C all year: warmer than winter air exactly when heating demand peaks, cooler than summer air exactly when cooling demand peaks. A ground-source heat pump (GSHP) swaps the air-source machine's fickle outdoor coil for that stability, buying a consistently smaller temperature lift at the price of serious groundworks. This lesson covers how the ground loop works, what the stability is worth, and the honest economics of when the digging pays.

## The ground loop

A GSHP collects heat through a closed loop of plastic pipe circulating a water-antifreeze mix. Three formats dominate. **Vertical boreholes** (typically 100 to 200 m deep, roughly one per 5 to 8 kW of capacity) suit constrained sites and give the steadiest temperatures; drilling is most of the project's cost. **Horizontal loops** buried 1 to 2 m deep need significant land (several hundred square metres for a house, proportionally more for larger buildings) but replace the drilling rig with an excavator. **Open-loop** systems pump groundwater directly through a heat exchanger and return it: the best temperatures of all where an aquifer allows, with abstraction licensing from the Environment Agency as part of the design.

The loop's job is simply to present the heat pump with a source around 0 to 10 °C in winter, instead of air that may be −5 °C, wet, and demanding [defrost cycles](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/reverse-cycle), which ground-source machines never need.

## What the steadier source is worth

The smaller, steadier lift shows up directly in seasonal efficiency: well-designed GSHPs deliver SCOPs around **4.0 and above**, against roughly 3.0 for a good air-source installation on the same building.

<WorkedExample title="ground vs air on a 100 MWh heating load">
  <Given>
    - A building needs 100 MWh of heat a year
    - Air-source SCOP 3.0; ground-source SCOP 4.0
    - Electricity £0.20/kWh
  </Given>
  <Find>The running-cost difference, and what it says about payback.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Air-source: 100 ÷ 3.0 ≈ 33.3 MWh of electricity ≈ £6,670/yr.
    Ground-source: 100 ÷ 4.0 = 25 MWh ≈ £5,000/yr.

    Saving ≈ **£1,670 a year**. Against a ground-loop premium that can run £20,000 and
    upward at this scale, the *efficiency alone* is a long payback. The honest GSHP case
    is built on more than the SCOP gap: longer plant life (the loop outlives several
    machines), no outdoor units (noise, space, planning), no defrost, and, where the
    building also needs cooling, the ground doubling as a superb heat sink, sometimes
    providing "free" passive cooling through the loop alone.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

The scale economics matter: larger and longer-running loads amortise drilling far better, which is why GSHP shines on estates, heat networks and buildings with year-round balanced heating and cooling, where the same boreholes are charged in summer and discharged in winter.

## Design realities

Three factors decide whether a GSHP delivers its promise. **Ground thermal design**: loops sized too small cool the ground year on year, dragging the SCOP down; proper design uses thermal conductivity data (or a test borehole on larger schemes). **Balanced use**: buildings that only ever extract heat benefit from summer recharge (cooling rejection or solar thermal); balanced schemes are the technology at its best. And the same [flow temperature discipline](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/heating-modes) as any heat pump: the ground fixes the bottom of the lift, but a 65 °C heating circuit squanders the advantage at the top.

<Callout variant="key" title="GSHP buys certainty, not just efficiency">
The air-source machine's SCOP is a bet on the weather; the ground-source machine's is close to a design parameter. For an energy manager, that predictability, capacity that does not sag in a cold snap, no defrost, performance that can be guaranteed in a contract, is often worth as much as the efficiency margin. Evaluate GSHP where its fixed costs spread over scale, long life or dual heating-and-cooling duty; evaluate air-source everywhere else, and let the [whole-life economics](/courses/economic-analysis) decide rather than enthusiasm for either.
</Callout>
