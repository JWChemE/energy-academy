**Ground-source heat pumps (GSHP)** use the stable subsurface temperature as a heat source/sink instead of fluctuating outdoor air.

## How it works

Underground temperature is stable year-round: ~10–15°C UK (varies by latitude and depth).

A closed-loop pipe filled with fluid circulates through the ground, absorbing (winter) or rejecting (summer) heat. The heat pump uses this fluid as the heat source.

Advantages over air-source:
- Stable source temperature = consistent high COP (3.5–4.5) regardless of season
- Works in any climate (cold, hot, variable)
- Quieter (underground unit, no outdoor fan noise)

## Installation types

**Closed-loop borehole:** Drill 50–300 m deep holes, insert plastic pipe loops filled with glycol/water. Most common.

Cost: £2,000–5,000 per borehole. Multiple boreholes for large buildings.

**Horizontal loop:** Bury pipe 1–2 m deep across large land area (10–20m² per kW typical). Cheaper if land available, but large area needed.

**Open-loop groundwater:** Pump groundwater, run through heat pump, return to aquifer. Very efficient but regulatory approval needed, risk of aquifer contamination.

## Economics

High installation cost (£20,000–100,000+ for a building).

Annual operating savings: 40–50% vs conventional boiler (high COP).

Payback: 8–15 years typical.

**Good if:**
- Long-term ownership (owner plans 15+ year horizon)
- High operating hours (continuous or frequent heating/cooling)
- Land available (for borehole or horizontal loop)
- Electricity price low relative to fuel (makes COP 3.5 attractive)

**Not good if:**
- Short-term tenant (won't see the payback)
- Shallow bedrock (borehole impossible)
- High-cost electricity (reduces cost advantage)

## Performance monitoring

Monitor COP and source temp to verify system is working:
- COP should be 3.5–4.5 year-round
- Source temp should stay 8–15°C (if it drifts much, thermal short-circuit or loop problem)

Annual maintenance: filter fluid, check for leaks.
