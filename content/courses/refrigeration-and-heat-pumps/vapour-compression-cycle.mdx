Every fridge, freezer, chiller, air-conditioner and heat pump you will ever audit runs on the same trick: the **vapour-compression cycle**, a closed loop that pumps heat *uphill* — from somewhere cold to somewhere hot — by exploiting how a refrigerant's boiling point changes with pressure. Understand this one loop and you understand the machine behind most of a building's cooling bill and, increasingly, its heating too.

The essential insight: a refrigerant boils at a temperature set by its pressure. Hold it at low pressure and it boils below 0 °C, greedily absorbing heat from anything warmer — that's the evaporator. Squeeze it to high pressure and its condensing temperature jumps above the outside air's — so the same heat pours back out at the condenser. The compressor is what buys that pressure difference, and it's the only place in the loop where you pay for energy.

## The energy balance

The cycle obeys the same [conservation law](/courses/mass-and-energy-balances/conservation-laws) as everything else on this platform: what goes in must come out. Heat absorbed in the evaporator plus the compressor's work must all leave through the condenser.

<WorkedExample title="chiller energy balance">
  <Given>
    - A water chiller absorbs **100 kW** from the chilled-water loop (the cooling duty)
    - Its compressor draws **30 kW** of electricity
  </Given>
  <Find>The heat rejected at the condenser, and the cycle's COP.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Heat rejected = heat absorbed + work in = 100 + 30 = **130 kW** — this is why condenser
    (heat-rejection) plant is always bigger than the cooling duty it serves.

    COP = useful cooling ÷ electricity = 100 ÷ 30 ≈ **3.3** — every 1 kWh of electricity moves
    3.3 kWh of heat.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## A refrigerator, concretely

Your kitchen fridge runs exactly this loop at small scale. The evaporator (the cold plate inside) holds refrigerant boiling at around −20 °C, cold enough to pull heat out of 4 °C food. The compressor (the humming lump at the back) squeezes the vapour until it condenses at around 40–60 °C in the black grille — warmer than your kitchen, so the heat leaves. Net effect: heat moved from a 4 °C box into a 20 °C room, in defiance of where heat "wants" to flow, at the cost of the compressor's electricity.

<Callout variant="key" title="The temperatures set the cost">
The compressor's job — and its power draw — depends on how far apart the evaporator and condenser pressures are, which is set by the temperatures you ask for. Ask for a colder evaporator or a hotter condenser, and the compressor works harder for every kWh moved. This one fact drives most refrigeration efficiency work: raise the cold-side setpoint, lower the heat-rejection temperature, and the same machine delivers the same cooling for less electricity. The [COP lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/cop-and-performance) makes this quantitative.
</Callout>
