One number summarises everything that matters about a refrigeration system or heat pump: how many kilowatt-hours of heat it moves for each kilowatt-hour of electricity it consumes. That ratio, the **coefficient of performance (COP)**, is what you compare machines with, what you track to catch deterioration, and what every improvement in this course ultimately raises. This lesson defines the family of performance metrics properly, shows what physics limits them, and prices what a COP point is worth.

## COP, and its seasonal cousins

For cooling, COP is useful cooling delivered divided by electrical input: the chiller from the [vapour-compression lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/vapour-compression-cycle) absorbing 100 kW with 30 kW of compressor work has a COP of 3.33. For heating, COP is heat delivered over electricity in, and it is always higher for the same machine and conditions, because the compressor's work also ends up in the delivered heat.

A machine's COP is not one number; it moves constantly with operating conditions, and mostly with one variable: the **temperature lift**, the gap between where heat is collected and where it is rejected. Because of that, single-point COPs flatter or slander machines depending on the test conditions, and the honest metrics average across a year: **SCOP** (seasonal COP, for heat pumps), **SEER/ESEER** (seasonal efficiency ratios for cooling). When comparing equipment, seasonal figures at conditions resembling yours are the only fair basis.

## The physics: Carnot sets the ceiling

Thermodynamics caps what any machine can achieve between two temperatures. For cooling, the ideal (Carnot) COP is the cold absolute temperature divided by the lift. Between a 0 °C evaporator (273 K) and a 40 °C condenser, the ceiling is 273 ÷ 40 ≈ **6.8**; real machines achieve perhaps 40 to 60% of their ceiling, hence real-world COPs around 3 for those conditions.

The ceiling formula explains the single most important design fact in this field: **the smaller the lift, the higher the possible COP**. Every degree you avoid lifting is efficiency you keep, which is why so many lessons in this course reduce to "collect heat warmer, reject it cooler": raising a chilled-water setpoint, cleaning a condenser, using [water instead of air for heat rejection](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/water-cooled-systems), choosing the [ground over winter air](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/ground-source-heat-pumps) as a source.

## Measuring the real thing

Nameplate COP describes a test rig; your machine's actual performance needs two measurements: electrical input (a [logged power reading](/courses/energy-audits/measurement-and-logging)) and thermal output (flow and temperature difference on the chilled or heated water circuit, or a reasonable proxy). Sites with both can trend COP monthly on the [M&T report](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/exception-reporting), and a drifting COP is the earliest warning of the fouling, charge and control faults the [maintenance lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/maintenance-and-faults) covers. Sites with neither are running their most complex plant blind.

<Callout variant="key" title="COP is a lift meter wearing an efficiency badge">
When a COP disappoints, resist blaming the machine first. COP mostly reports the temperature lift you asked for: setpoints too cold, heat rejection too hot, fouling adding artificial lift on both sides. The machine converts conditions into performance; the conditions are usually where the money is. That principle, lift first, machine second, organises every practical lesson that follows.
</Callout>
