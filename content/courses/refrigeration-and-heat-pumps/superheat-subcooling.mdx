**Superheat** and **subcooling** are the two key parameters for commissioning and optimising refrigeration systems.

## Superheat (at evaporator outlet)

Superheat = actual temperature - saturation temperature at that pressure.

Example: At 5 bar, saturation temp is -5°C. If actual temp is +5°C, superheat = 10°C.

**Why it matters:** Ensures all liquid has evaporated before reaching the compressor (prevents liquid slugging, which damages the compressor).

**Typical target:** 5–10°C. If superheat is too low (say, 2°C), risk of liquid reaching compressor. If too high (say, 20°C), not all evaporator is being utilised.

**Field adjustment:** Adjust expansion device (thermostatic valve or electronic expansion valve) to achieve target superheat.

## Subcooling (at condenser exit)

Subcooling = saturation temperature - actual temperature at that pressure.

Example: At 20 bar, saturation temp is 46°C. If actual temp is 40°C, subcooling = 6°C.

**Why it matters:** Ensures the liquid won't flash to vapour (flash = lost capacity). Also improves expansion device performance.

**Typical target:** 5–15°C. Higher subcooling (cooling the liquid further) is good but requires more condenser area.

## System tuning

Proper superheat and subcooling settings can improve efficiency by 5–15%:
- Superheat too high: Evaporator is partly unused (wasted area, poor cooling)
- Superheat too low: Risk of slugging and compressor damage
- Subcooling too low: Flash losses in expansion
- Subcooling too high: Extra cooling needed (more work)

Commissioning includes measuring and adjusting superheat and subcooling.
