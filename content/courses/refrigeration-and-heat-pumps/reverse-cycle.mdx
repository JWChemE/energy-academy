A heat pump is not a different machine from the chillers this course has covered; it is the same vapour-compression cycle with its purpose reversed. Where a chiller's product is the cold side and the rejected heat is waste, a heat pump's product is the hot side and the cold is collected outdoors for free. Understanding this identity, and the reversing valve that lets one machine do both jobs, demystifies the technology now central to UK heat decarbonisation.

## The same loop, pointed the other way

Run the [vapour-compression cycle](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/vapour-compression-cycle) with the evaporator outside and the condenser inside, and heat flows from cold outdoors to warm indoors. The evaporator's trick is pressure: even at 0 °C outside, refrigerant held at low pressure boils at perhaps −10 °C, so heat flows from the "cold" air into the colder refrigerant willingly. The compressor lifts it to a pressure whose saturation temperature exceeds the heating circuit's, and the condenser delivers it. Outdoor air at 0 °C contains enormous thermal energy (absolute zero is 273 degrees further down); a heat pump is simply a machine for concentrating it.

The energy balance from the cooling lessons pays out here as a bonus: delivered heat equals collected heat *plus* compressor work, so the heating COP always exceeds the same machine's cooling COP at the same conditions. The [economics of that multiplier](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/heating-modes) get their own lesson next.

## The reversing valve

A **reversible** heat pump adds one component: a four-way valve at the compressor that swaps which coil receives hot discharge gas and which feeds suction. Flip it, and the indoor coil becomes the condenser (heating mode) or the evaporator (cooling mode); the outdoor coil takes the opposite role. One machine, both seasons: this is how almost every air-conditioning split sold today also heats, and why "air conditioning" installations are quietly one of the largest heat pump fleets in the country.

## Defrost: the honest cost of cold, damp air

An outdoor coil running below roughly 0 to 5 °C in humid UK weather grows frost, which insulates the coil and throttles airflow. The machine's answer is periodic **defrost**: the reversing valve flips briefly, hot gas melts the frost from inside, and heating pauses for a few minutes. It is normal, automatic, and visible as steam rising from the outdoor unit; the seasonal energy penalty is typically **3 to 10%**, worst in damp climates hovering around 0 to 5 °C, which describes a British winter well. Modern demand-based defrost (initiating on measured coil conditions rather than a fixed timer) trims the penalty, and is worth asking about when comparing units.

Very cold snaps raise a second issue: capacity falls just as demand peaks (colder source = bigger lift = less output, per the [COP lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/cop-and-performance)). Systems handle it with a supplementary electric element, or in **hybrid/dual-fuel** arrangements, an existing boiler that takes over below a changeover temperature. A few design responses, generous heat emitter sizing and lower flow temperatures, matter far more to real-world performance, and they belong to the [heating modes lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/heating-modes).

<Callout variant="key" title="Judge heat pumps by seasonal figures, never cold-snap anecdotes">
The recurring public argument ("do they work when it's freezing?") confuses capacity on the coldest hour with performance across a year. A UK heating season is overwhelmingly mild-and-damp hours, exactly where an air-source machine's COP is strong; design handles the rare extreme. The honest comparison metric is SCOP over the real climate, including defrost, and on that metric well-installed systems deliver three-plus units of heat per unit of electricity, which no fuel-burning appliance can approach.
</Callout>
