A **heat pump** reverses the refrigeration cycle to provide heating instead of cooling.

## Heat pump heating mode

In cooling mode, the evaporator is inside (absorbs heat) and condenser is outside (rejects heat).

In heating mode, this reverses:
- **Evaporator** is now outside (absorbs heat from outdoor air, ground, or water)
- **Condenser** is now inside (rejects heat to space or hot water)

Compressor still does the same job: raise pressure and temperature of the low-pressure gas.

## Example: air-source heat pump in winter

- **Outdoor unit (now evaporator):** Even at 0°C, refrigerant boils at -10°C (due to low pressure). Absorbs heat from cold outdoor air.
- **Compressor:** Raises pressure/temp to 50°C.
- **Indoor unit (now condenser):** Rejects heat to space or hot water. Refrigerant condenses at 50°C.
- **Expansion device:** Throttles back to low pressure.
- Cycle repeats.

Net result: Heat moved from outside (cold) to inside (warm), using compressor work.

## Coefficient of performance (COP) in heating

Heat delivered / compressor work = COP

Example: 10 kW heating delivered with 3 kW compressor work = COP 3.33

At mild weather (outdoor 10°C), COP might be 4–5.
At very cold (outdoor -15°C), COP might drop to 2–2.5 because the temperature lift is larger.

COP > 1 is always good (moving heat is more efficient than generating heat electrically).

## Challenges

- **Defrost cycle:** In winter, evaporator (outdoor unit) accumulates frost. Periodically reverse cycle briefly to melt frost. Uses heat but necessary.
- **Electric backup:** On very cold days, heat pump alone may not keep up; electric immersion heater provides supplementary heating.
- **Part-load:** Heat demand is less than peak on mild days; COP is excellent at part-load (opposite of fossil boilers).
