A **pressure-enthalpy (P-h) diagram** is the refrigeration engineer's map of the cycle.

## How to read it

- **Y-axis:** Pressure (log scale, bar)
- **X-axis:** Enthalpy (kJ/kg) — the energy content of the refrigerant
- **Saturation curve:** The boundary where liquid and vapour coexist. Left side = liquid, right side = vapour.
- **Isotherms:** Lines of constant temperature. In the saturation region, all states at the same pressure have the same temperature.

## The cycle on a P-h diagram

**Point 1 (compressor inlet):** Low pressure, high enthalpy (dry gas). Example: 5 bar, 350 kJ/kg.

**Point 2 (compressor outlet):** High pressure, even higher enthalpy (compressed hot gas). Example: 20 bar, 420 kJ/kg.

**Point 3 (condenser exit):** High pressure, lower enthalpy (liquid). Example: 20 bar, 250 kJ/kg. Heat was rejected in the condenser: 420 - 250 = 170 kJ/kg per kg of refrigerant.

**Point 4 (after expansion):** Low pressure, low enthalpy (cold liquid-vapour mix). Example: 5 bar, 250 kJ/kg.

**Back to point 1:** Low-pressure vapour boils in evaporator, heat absorbed: 350 - 250 = 100 kJ/kg per kg.

## Refrigerant types

Common: R410A (high pressure), R134a (medium), R22 (older, being phased out), R290/R600a (natural, flammable).

Each has different P-h diagram (saturation curve shape, pressures for given temps).

Reading P-h diagrams: Plot your four cycle points, read enthalpy values, calculate cooling/heating capacity.
