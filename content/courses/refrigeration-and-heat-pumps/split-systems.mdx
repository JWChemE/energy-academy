**Split units** are the most common air-source heat pump configuration: outdoor unit and one or more indoor units connected by refrigerant piping.

## Components

**Outdoor unit:** Compressor, condenser/evaporator (switches between modes), fan.

**Indoor unit(s):** Air coil (evaporator in cooling, condenser in heating), fan, controls.

**Refrigerant lines:** Copper tubes carrying liquid and vapour refrigerant. Must be insulated (suction line) and may be charged on-site.

## Installation

Refrigerant piping: Distance from outdoor to indoor should be under 30 m typical. Longer = pressure drop losses, efficiency penalty.

**Critical:** Proper charge (amount of refrigerant). Too little = low capacity, high superheat. Too much = high pressure, poor efficiency, risk of liquid slugging.

Installation is skilled work; poor charging ruins performance.

## Single vs multi-split

**Single split:** One indoor unit per outdoor unit. Simpler, lower cost.

**Multi-split:** One outdoor unit feeds 2–5 indoor units. More efficient if loads are balanced; can serve multiple rooms.

## Advantages

- Compact (no ducts needed)
- Efficient (no transmission loss)
- Individual room control (each indoor unit can have own setpoint)
- Quick installation (outdoor can be mounted on wall or ground)

## Disadvantages

- Outdoor unit visible/audible (noise ~50 dB at 1 m)
- Refrigerant lines exposed (aesthetic concern)
- Requires skilled commissioning (refrigerant charge)
