Walk past any UK office, shop or surgery and count the outdoor units on the wall: split systems are how most commercial buildings actually meet their cooling, and increasingly their heating. They are cheap to install, efficient when done well, and chronically mismanaged in practice. This lesson covers the configurations (single splits to VRF), what decides their real-world efficiency, and the operating habits that cost multi-split estates a fortune.

## From single split to VRF

A **single split** pairs one outdoor unit (compressor and coil) with one indoor unit, connected by refrigerant pipework: the [vapour-compression loop](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/vapour-compression-cycle) stretched across a wall. A **multi-split** runs several indoor units from one outdoor unit, saving outdoor space and capital.

**VRF (variable refrigerant flow)** scales the idea to whole buildings: one large outdoor system serves tens of indoor units, with inverter compressors modulating refrigerant flow to match the summed demand. **Heat-recovery VRF** adds the clever trick: when some zones call for cooling while others heat (a south-facing meeting room versus a north-facing office in spring), the system moves heat *between them*, and the reclaimed heat is close to free. Buildings with simultaneous heating and cooling demands are where VRF earns its premium; buildings without them mostly pay it.

## What decides real efficiency

Modern splits carry impressive seasonal ratings, but three installation factors decide whether a building sees them.

**Pipework length and quality.** Every metre of refrigerant pipe between units costs a little capacity and efficiency; long, uninsulated or poorly-brazed runs cost a lot. Manufacturers publish limits; good installers stay well inside them.

**Correct charge.** Splits are charged on site, and the [superheat and subcooling lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/superheat-subcooling) is the whole story: field studies repeatedly find a large share of installed splits mischarged, quietly running 5 to 15% below rated efficiency. Commissioning records showing the measured values are the evidence to ask for.

**Sizing.** An oversized split short-cycles: bursts of full output, then off, repeatedly. Cycling wrecks efficiency and comfort alike, where a right-sized inverter unit would modulate smoothly at part load, the regime [the part-load lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/partload-efficiency) shows is actually a split's sweet spot.

## The estate problem: settings and fighting

Splits multiply across buildings one meeting room at a time, usually with no central control, and the waste patterns are predictable. Units heating and cooling *the same air* in adjacent zones because their setpoints crossed. Remote controls set to 18 °C in summer meetings and never reset. Units running around the clock because nobody owns the off switch. The [walk-through lesson's](/courses/energy-audits/building-walkthrough) after-hours survey finds these in any multi-split estate, and the fixes are governance more than engineering: centralised control or simple lockable setpoint ranges, dead-bands so heating and cooling cannot overlap (19 to 24 °C works for most spaces), and schedules with local override rather than local sovereignty.

<Callout variant="key" title="A dead-band policy is the cheapest efficiency measure splits have">
Where individual room controls allow any setpoint, adjacent zones end up in thermal combat, each unit's waste counted as the other's load. Locking all units to heat only below 19 °C and cool only above 24 °C costs nothing, offends nobody's genuine comfort, and routinely cuts a multi-split estate's consumption by double-digit percentages. It is the single highest-return intervention this lesson can offer, and it needs a policy, not a purchase order.
</Callout>

One regulatory note: split and VRF systems hold F-gas refrigerants, so the leak-check duties in the [maintenance lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/maintenance-and-faults) apply estate-wide, and air-conditioning systems above 12 kW require periodic [TM44 inspections](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/epc-and-building-standards), whose reports are a free source of audit findings.
