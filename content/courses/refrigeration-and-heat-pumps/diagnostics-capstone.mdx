You've learned the vapour-compression cycle, how COP measures performance, how to read superheat and subcooling, and the levers that lift seasonal efficiency. This capstone puts you on site as the engineer, working chillers and heat pumps that look like they're running fine but are quietly bleeding energy.

The unifying metric is **COP**: a system's electrical input is its useful output divided by its COP. Almost every fault here is a COP that has collapsed — through fouling, undercharge, an unnecessarily large temperature lift, or bad part-load control — or heat made the expensive way, with resistance heaters or an over-used backup.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate** — read the brief and the panel: cooling duty, COP, pressures, superheat/subcooling, flow temperatures.
2. **Calculate** — work out the figure that decides it: the compressor power from output and COP, a setpoint or free-cooling saving, the superheat and subcooling, a heat-pump business case. The **reference data is on hand** — the COP relationship, tuning targets, setpoint rules and prices — but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose** — pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe** — build an ordered work plan (no-cost first, then capital).
5. **Verify** — score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="Protect the COP">
A heat pump or chiller is only as good as its COP, and the COP is governed by the temperature lift and the state of the kit. Keep the lift small (warmer chilled water, cooler condenser water, lower flow temperature), keep the heat exchangers clean and the charge right, control the part-load properly, and grab free cooling when the weather offers it. And never make heat with a COP-1 resistance heater when a heat pump can move it for a fraction of the energy.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **COP economics** — turning output and COP into compressor power, cost, and savings.
- **Reading the gauges** — superheat and subcooling, and what high-and-low together mean.
- **Maintenance faults** — a fouled condenser, an undercharged system (and why you don't just top it up).
- **Optimisation** — chilled-water setpoint, heat-pump flow temperature, and free cooling.
- **Part-load** — why hot-gas bypass wrecks efficiency and a VFD restores it.
- **Heat-pump strategy** — resistance versus heat pump, and a backup that won't switch off.

## Differential thinking

The same complaint — "it's using more energy than it should" — can be a dirty condenser, a low charge, a setpoint set too cold, or bad part-load control, and the fix for each is different. Read the evidence: high head pressure with normal charge points at the condenser; high superheat with low subcooling points at undercharge; near-full power at part load points at the controls. A heat-pump system running up big bills might have a healthy heat pump and a backup heater that simply won't switch off.

## After you finish

- Which **reading** decided each case — the COP, the head pressure, the superheat/subcooling, the part-load power?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each fix, is it a no-cost change (raise the setpoint, recalibrate the controls), a maintenance job (clean the condenser, repair the leak), or a capital project (VFD, economiser, heat pump) that has to earn its payback?
