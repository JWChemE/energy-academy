Heat pumps can operate in several modes depending on building need.

## Heating-only

Heat pump heating only, no cooling. Used where cooling is rarely needed (cold climates).

Pros: Simpler, cheaper.
Cons: No summer cooling option (must add separately if later needed).

## Heating + cooling (reversible)

Reversible heat pump switches between heating (winter) and cooling (summer) as needed.

Most common in modern systems. Single outdoor unit, can heat or cool.

Pros: Year-round comfort, efficient.
Cons: More complex (requires reversing valve), slightly more expensive.

## Defrost cycle (air-source)

In winter, outdoor evaporator collects frost/ice, reducing efficiency. Periodic defrost cycle:
1. Briefly reverse to heating (outdoor unit becomes condenser, melts frost)
2. Duration: 5–15 minutes every 30–60 minutes of heating
3. Energy cost: Lost heating for that period (3–10% seasonal penalty typical)

Ground-source heat pumps don't need defrost (underground is above freezing).

## Electric backup (auxiliary heat)

On very cold days, heat pump alone may not meet demand. Backup electric heater (immersion or strip heater) supplements.

Thermostat logic: Use heat pump up to, say, -5°C, then electric backup below that.

Tradeoff: Backup is inefficient but necessary on extreme cold days. Overall seasonal COP still favours heat pump.

## Dual-fuel systems

Heat pump for mild weather (efficient), fossil boiler for cold (reliable, familiar).

Logic: Heat pump when COP > 2 (typically -5°C and above), boiler when colder.

Pros: Best of both (efficiency + reliability).
Cons: Two systems to maintain, more complex control.
