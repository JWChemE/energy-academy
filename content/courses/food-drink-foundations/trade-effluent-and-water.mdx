Everything a food or drink site washes away, it pays for three times: once to buy the water, once to heat it, and once to send it down the drain, and that third payment is where this sector gets uniquely expensive. Food and drink effluent carries sugars, fats, yeast, starches and product losses that are costly to treat, and the water company charges by strength as well as volume. That pricing turns effluent from a facilities afterthought into an energy manager's ally, because most of what reduces effluent charges (less water, less heat, less product in the drain) reduces the energy bill in the same motion.

## Consents and the Mogden logic

Any UK business discharging non-domestic wastewater to sewer needs a **trade effluent consent** from its water and sewerage company, under the Water Industry Act 1991, setting limits on flow, strength (BOD/COD and suspended solids), temperature and pH. Food and drink effluent tests every one of those limits: it is high in organic load (sugars, yeast, fats, product residues), sometimes warm (hot CIP rinses and pasteuriser discharge), and swings in pH as caustic and acid cleaning stages discharge.

The charge itself follows strength-based formulas (the **Mogden formula** and its variants): you pay per cubic metre, scaled up by how concentrated the organic load is relative to the treatment works' average. The same volume at twice the strength costs significantly more, not because you used more water, but because you made the water company's job harder. Volume and strength are therefore two largely independent levers, worth attacking separately.

## Product down the drain is the expensive kind

A kilogram of product rinsed into the drain was bought as raw material, processed with gas and electricity through every stage it reached, and then becomes COD you pay the water company to digest. Yield losses and effluent strength are the same problem seen from two invoices.

<WorkedExample title="one percent of product, three invoices">
  <Given>
    - A 12,000 t/yr factory loses 1% of throughput (120 t) to drain as washings, spills and line-clearance waste
    - Raw material and processing value: conservatively £800/t; the site's process energy: ~305 kWh/t already spent on that product
    - Effluent surcharge from the added COD: roughly £150 per tonne of product-equivalent for a typical strong food effluent (site-specific; from the water company's Mogden terms)
  </Given>
  <Find>The scale of the annual cost, and where it shows up.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Materials and processing: 120 × £800 ≈ **£96,000** of bought-and-made product to the sewer.
    Embedded energy: 120 × 305 ≈ 36,600 kWh, about **£5,000 of gas and electricity**. Effluent
    surcharge: 120 × £150 ≈ **£18,000** in strength charges.

    Round £120,000 a year, and only the last slice is labelled "effluent" on any invoice, which
    is why dry clean-up (scrape, sweep and capture before the hose comes out) is one of the
    highest-value habits in the sector: it recovers yield, cuts COD, and shrinks the hot-water
    bill in one move.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

## Strength management: segregate, recover, neutralise

The strength lever rewards knowing what is actually in the drain. **Segregating strong streams** (spent yeast and trub in a brewery, fats and product slurries in a factory, strong first-rinse CIP liquors anywhere) for separate disposal, or better, sale as by-product or feedstock, can cut the strength charge dramatically. **Fat, oil and grease (FOG)** management is usually a consent condition, not an option: interceptors maintained on schedule, because fat in the sewer is both a charge multiplier and an offence. And **pH and temperature** limits are met most cheaply by blending and by recovering heat from hot discharges before they reach the connection, which satisfies the consent and captures value in the same step.

## The water-energy nexus

Because so much of the sector's water is *hot* water, every cubic metre saved carries an energy dividend: at typical washdown conditions (10 to 60 °C lift, 85% calorifier), each m³ avoided saves about 58 kWh of heat, roughly 68 kWh of gas, £4 of fuel, on top of the water purchase and the volume charge. Water-efficiency measures therefore rank by temperature: a saved cubic metre of hot washdown water is worth several of cold yard water. The same logic runs in reverse for recovery: a brewery that reuses its wort-cooling water as the next brew's liquor cuts the gas bill and the volume charge with one loop, on the order of 3,600 m³ a year at the scale of the [brewery course's heat-recovery case](/courses/breweries/heat-recovery-in-the-brewhouse).

<Callout variant="key" title="Read the effluent bill as an energy document">
Volume tells you how much water (much of it heated) the site really discharges; strength tells you how much bought, processed, energy-soaked product went with it. A rising effluent charge is frequently the first visible symptom of a process problem: a leaking valve on a product line, washdown discipline slipping, a CIP recipe dumping hot caustic. The energy manager who reviews effluent invoices alongside gas and electricity finds faults months before they surface anywhere else.
</Callout>
