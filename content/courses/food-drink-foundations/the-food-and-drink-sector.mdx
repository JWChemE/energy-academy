Food and drink is the UK's largest manufacturing sector, and from an energy manager's chair its thousands of sites (breweries, bakeries, dairies, ready-meal factories, soft-drinks plants) are variations on one underlying machine. Every one of them heats product to transform or make it safe, cools product to keep it safe, and heats water to clean everything the product touched. Cook, chill, clean. The processes differ enormously; the rules, the utilities and the recurring opportunities do not, which is why this platform teaches the sector in two layers: this shared foundations course, and process courses that build on it.

## One sector, one shape

Walk any food or drink site and the same three energy centres appear:

- **Process heat**, usually gas: cooking, boiling, baking, pasteurising, sterilising, and the hot water and steam behind cleaning. The technology varies (a brewery's copper, a bakery's oven, a dairy's steriliser) but the [steam](/courses/steam-and-condensate/steam-basics) and [combustion](/courses/boilers-and-fired-systems/excess-air) engineering underneath is common property.
- **Refrigeration**, electrical: chilled storage and prep, fermentation control, blast chilling, freezing, cold despatch. Almost always the largest electrical load, running through nights and shutdowns, and governed by the same operating levers everywhere, which is why [running the cold plant](/courses/food-drink-foundations/running-the-cold-plant) lives in this course rather than being retaught three times.
- **Hygiene**, the sector's signature: a nightly second shift of washdown and CIP that consumes hot water at production-shift rates after production has stopped. No other industry has it, every food and drink site does, and it gets [its own lesson here](/courses/food-drink-foundations/cip-and-washdown).

Around those three run the shared constraints: **HACCP-based food safety** setting floors no energy measure may cross, **trade effluent** charging by strength for whatever reaches the drain, and a compliance landscape (sector **Climate Change Agreements**, ESOS, SECR, packaging producer responsibility) that applies across the sector with only the trade body changing.

## Why a shared course, then process courses

The platform's rule is [cross-link, don't re-teach](/courses/intro-to-energy-management/the-energy-management-cycle), and this sector is where it earns its keep. HACCP works identically in a brewery and a dairy; teaching it twice would waste your time and dilute both courses. But the *processes* could hardly be more different: a brewery's energy follows a batch through mash, boil and fermentation; a ready-meals factory's follows cook-chill lines and spiral freezers; a dairy's follows milk through pasteurisers, separators and (on powder sites) the most energy-hungry unit operation in the sector. So the split is:

1. **This course** owns what is shared: the safety floors, the hygiene utility, effluent and compliance, the cold plant's operating levers, and the heat-recovery matching patterns that recur everywhere.
2. **The process courses** ([Breweries](/courses/breweries), [Food Manufacturing](/courses/food-manufacturing), and [Dairies](/courses/dairies)) own their process's loads, benchmarks, signature opportunities, diagnostics and a full audit capstone each, assuming everything taught here.

Take this course first. It is short, and every process course leans on it the way all sector courses lean on [Levels 1 and 2](/levels/level-1).

<Callout variant="key" title="Learn the sector once, the processes many times">
The knowledge that transfers between food and drink sites is exactly the knowledge that never appears on a process flow diagram: where food safety genuinely constrains energy, what the nightly clean costs, how effluent is charged, which schemes apply at which company size, and the handful of heat-recovery pairings that recur from brewhouse to dairy. Master this shared layer and every site in the sector becomes a variation you can read; skip it and every site looks like a new industry.
</Callout>

## Where the money usually is

Across the sector the audit findings rhyme, which is this course's other justification. The biggest gas surprise is usually the clean, not the cook. The biggest electrical opportunity is usually the cold plant's operating habits, not its hardware. The most reliable capital project is usually a heat-recovery pairing between refrigeration rejection and hot-water demand. And the most dangerous recommendation is always the one that mistakes a validated food-safety limit for an inherited habit, or the reverse. The next lesson starts exactly there: the floors.
