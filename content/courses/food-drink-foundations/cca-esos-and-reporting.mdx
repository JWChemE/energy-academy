Food and drink is energy-intensive enough that the UK tax system treats it specially: through the sector's Climate Change Agreements, a qualifying site can claim a substantial discount on the Climate Change Levy in exchange for committing to efficiency targets. But the compliance landscape is also where audits most often embarrass themselves, by prescribing schemes to businesses that aren't in scope for them. This lesson covers the CCA machinery as it stands, who actually falls under ESOS and SECR at food-and-drink company sizes, and the packaging levy that increasingly sits alongside the energy bills.

## The CCA deal, as it stands

The [Climate Change Levy](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/climate-change-levy) is charged on business energy; a **Climate Change Agreement** buys it down substantially in exchange for meeting sector-negotiated efficiency targets, with buy-out payments for shortfalls. The machinery, as at mid-2026:

- A **renewed scheme** runs with its first target period from **1 January 2026** and targets to the end of 2030, with reduced CCL rates for compliant participants continuing until **31 March 2033**.
- Eligibility is sector-based, through umbrella agreements held by trade bodies: the **Food and Drink Federation** for general food manufacture, with brewing and malting, dairy processing and other subsectors holding their own. Individual sites hold underlying agreements beneath the umbrella.
- Targets are typically **intensity-based** (energy per unit of throughput) against a baseline, which is exactly the specific-energy discipline every process course on this platform teaches; the measurement system that runs the site's benchmarking runs its CCA reporting too.
- **New entrants** can join through annual application windows, so a site that never joined, or a new site, should check current eligibility rather than assume the door is shut.

## ESOS and SECR: check the size before writing the section

Both [ESOS Phase 4](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/esos) (four-yearly audits, compliance by 5 December 2027) and [SECR](/courses/uk-energy-regulation/secr) (energy and carbon in annual reports) apply only to **large undertakings**: broadly 250+ employees, or the turnover and balance-sheet thresholds. That single fact divides the sector cleanly. The large groups (national brewers, major dairies, the big food manufacturers) are squarely in scope for both, and their multi-site data obligations make the shared measurement system all the more valuable. But the overwhelming majority of UK food and drink businesses (the whole craft-brewing boom, most regional producers) are SMEs, comfortably exempt, and want an audit because it saves money, not because a regulator requires it. Two nuances catch people: **ownership** (a small site owned by a large group is swept into the group's obligations), and the **customer cascade** (retailers' carbon questionnaires impose SECR-like reporting on suppliers of every size, contractually rather than legally).

## Packaging producer responsibility

Not an energy scheme, but a live cost line on the same desk: UK **Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)** for packaging places a growing share of the cost of collecting and recycling packaging onto the businesses that put it on the market: bottles, cans, trays, cartons and multipacks are all in scope. It pushes the sector toward lighter and better-chosen materials, which reduces embodied energy and carbon alongside the fee itself, and it belongs on the scoping checklist of any food-and-drink audit even though no kilowatt-hour flows through it.

<Callout variant="key" title="One dataset, every scheme, and check scope first">
The CCA target, the SECR disclosure and the retail customer's questionnaire all ultimately ask the same question: energy per unit of product, trending which way? One normalised, production-adjusted dataset answers all three, claims the levy discount, and turns every efficiency project into a compliance credit as well as a saving. And before writing any compliance section, confirm what actually applies: size, ownership, CCA status, packaging exposure. Prescribing ESOS rigour to an exempt craft brewery wastes the client's money as surely as missing a large group's obligations risks theirs.
</Callout>

## The auditor's angle

A site's CCA status is intelligence: it means throughput and energy data exist in reportable quality, it names the person who owns the numbers, and it puts a price on underperformance that strengthens every recommendation's business case.
