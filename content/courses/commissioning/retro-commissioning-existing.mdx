Most of the building stock that exists today was never properly commissioned, and almost none of it still performs as designed. Occupancy has changed, controls have drifted, plant has been patched, and setpoints have been nudged for a decade by whoever last complained. Retro-commissioning is the process of applying commissioning discipline to an existing, occupied building to recover the performance it has lost, and because the faults it finds are usually cheap to fix, it is one of the highest-return activities in energy management. This lesson covers what retro-commissioning is, how it is structured, and its monitoring-based successor.

## Retro-commissioning, defined

Retro-commissioning (RCx) is the systematic investigation and tuning of an existing building's systems to restore, or exceed, their intended performance. Unlike new-build commissioning it has no design handover to test against, so it starts by establishing what the building *should* do, then finds where it falls short. The faults are predictable, because they are the accumulated drift the [what-is-commissioning lesson](/courses/commissioning/what-is-commissioning) described: sensors out of calibration, control sequences incomplete or overridden, plant oversized for a load that shrank years ago, [simultaneous heating and cooling](/courses/control-systems-and-bms/multi-loop-control), schedules that never changed when the building's use did. RCx is owner-driven, aimed squarely at cutting operating cost, and typically saves 10 to 30% of energy with paybacks of one to three years, because so many of its fixes are tuning rather than capital.

## How an RCx project is structured

A retro-commissioning project runs in four phases:

- **Assessment.** An [energy audit](/courses/energy-audits/why-audit) and benchmarking to size the prize, a walkthrough of plant condition, and a review of controls, sensors and [BMS trends](/courses/control-systems-and-bms/bms-functions) to locate the faults. This phase decides where effort pays.
- **Quick wins.** The cheap, high-impact fixes first: controls tuning, sensor calibration, schedule corrections, setpoint resets, dead bands. These typically repay in under a year and fund the momentum for what follows.
- **Capital measures.** The bigger interventions the assessment justifies: plant replacement, [heat recovery](/courses/waste-heat-recovery/economics-and-matching), fabric or renewables, on longer paybacks.
- **Ongoing commissioning.** Monitoring and periodic re-checking so the recovered performance persists rather than drifting away again, which the [re-commissioning lesson](/courses/commissioning/recommissioning) develops.

The sequence is deliberate: quick wins build cash and confidence, exactly as the [portfolio approach](/courses/economic-analysis/portfolio-approach) recommends for any programme.

## Monitoring-based commissioning

The modern evolution replaces periodic manual investigation with continuous analysis of the building's own data. **Monitoring-based commissioning (MBCx)** uses BMS and meter trends, increasingly with automated fault-detection software, to surface faults as they emerge rather than at the next audit: a valve that stops closing, a schedule someone overrode, a chiller that started running at night. It costs more to set up but catches problems in days instead of years, and it directly addresses the persistence problem that defeats one-off exercises. MBCx is the point where commissioning and [monitoring and targeting](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/why-mt) become the same activity.

<Callout variant="key" title="Existing buildings hide larger, cheaper savings than most retrofits">
The instinct on an underperforming building is to reach for capital: new plant, new fabric, new kit. Retro-commissioning usually finds a bigger, faster saving first, in tuning what is already installed, because a decade of drift has left low-hanging faults everywhere. Assess and tune before you replace: the sensor recalibration, the corrected schedule and the dead band often deliver more, sooner, than the boiler you were about to specify, and they make the capital case for whatever genuinely does need replacing far stronger.
</Callout>
