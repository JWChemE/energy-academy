You've learned what commissioning is, how to test performance, balance flows, verify sensors and sequences, document the handover, and re-commission to fight drift. This capstone puts you on site as the commissioning agent, running the acceptance tests, finding the gaps between design and reality, and deciding what to fix before you sign anything off.

The thread through every case: **commissioning is the bridge between design intent and actual performance.** Fifteen to thirty per cent of new buildings have a significant performance gap, and the cardinal sin is accepting a handover that doesn't meet design, because fixing a defect after handover costs ten times what it costs now.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate**: read the brief and the panel: measured flows, capacities, sensor readings, setpoints, pressures.
2. **Calculate**: work out the figure that decides it: actual capacity (flow × Cp × ΔT), the deviation from design, the cost of a defect or of drift. The **reference data is on hand**: acceptance criteria, performance tests, sensor verification, drift, but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose**: pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe**: decide the action: balance, calibrate, set, fix and retest, re-commission, document, or, the trap, accept the handover anyway.
5. **Verify**: score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="'It runs' is not 'it meets design'">
Commissioning is the discipline of proving performance, not just operation. A chiller that runs but delivers 84% of its rating fails; a sequence that runs but heats while it cools fails; a sensor that's installed but uncalibrated fails. Test against the acceptance criteria, find the cause, fix it and re-test, and never sign off a defect to be 'snagged later', because later is ten times the cost and runs up the bills in between.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **Performance testing**: capacity from flow and temperature, against the ±10% acceptance band.
- **Flow balancing**: finding starved and over-served zones and setting them to design.
- **Sequences & sensors**: catching logic defects and uncalibrated sensors before handover.
- **Setpoints & pressures**: design values, not factory defaults; verified duct static.
- **Persistence**: re-commissioning to recover drift, and the handover documentation that makes performance last.

## Differential thinking

A building "using too much energy" at handover might be unbalanced flows, an under-performing chiller, a sequence defect, an uncalibrated sensor, or default setpoints: each caught by a different test, each with a different fix. And the recurring decision is the same: does it meet design? If not, the answer is never "accept and sign off." The decisive evidence is the measurement against the acceptance criterion.

## After you finish

- Which **test** decided each case: the flow, the capacity, the sequence walk, the sensor check, the pressure?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each defect, is the fix a setting (setpoint, static, balance), a calibration, a logic correction and re-test, or documentation and training, and would you have signed it off?
