Commissioning can find every fault, tune every loop and prove every rating, and still fail, if the knowledge it generated leaves the site in the commissioning agent's head. Handover documentation is what turns a commissioned building into an operable one: the record of how the systems are set up, how to run them, and how to keep them performing. Poor handover is why so many well-commissioned buildings drift back to waste within a year, because the operators who inherit them never learn how they were meant to work. This lesson covers what a good handover package contains and why each part matters.

## What the package must contain

A complete handover leaves the operator five things:

- **The commissioning report**: an executive summary (findings, defects fixed, the performance baseline), the detailed test results, and the [as-built information](/courses/commissioning/performance-testing), including the setpoints and configurations actually left in place. This is the record that the building was proven, and the reference for detecting later drift.
- **Operation manuals**, one per major system: how to start, stop and adjust it, its normal operating modes across the seasons, emergency procedures, and who to call. Written for the person who will run the building at 6 am, not for the engineer who designed it.
- **A maintenance schedule**: the annual tasks (filter changes, [sensor calibration](/courses/control-systems-and-bms/temperature-sensors), cleaning) and longer-cycle servicing, with responsibilities named.
- **Control documentation**: how to use the BMS, the control sequences in plain form (so the next engineer can understand the logic without reverse-engineering it), and what each alarm means.
- **Training sign-off**: a record that the operators were actually trained, on what, and confirmed they understood it.

## Why each part earns its place

Every gap in the package becomes a running cost. Without clear operation manuals, operators run systems by guesswork, override strategies they do not understand, and waste energy while chasing comfort complaints. Without a maintenance schedule, service is deferred or done wrong, and the plant [degrades](/courses/commissioning/drift-and-degradation). Without documented control sequences, the next controls contractor cannot safely change anything, so improvements stall and faults linger. And without recorded setpoints and a baseline, no one can ever tell whether performance has drifted, because there is nothing to compare against. The documentation is not the tail-end formality it is often treated as; it is the mechanism by which everything commissioning achieved actually persists.

## Training: the part most often skipped

Documentation only works if the people inheriting the building can use it, which is why the training sign-off matters as much as the manuals. A handover meeting that walks the operators through the systems, the strategies, the alarms and the emergency procedures, and confirms they followed it, is what connects the paperwork to practice. It is also the moment to hand over the [monitoring and targeting](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/why-mt) baseline, so the operators start tracking performance against the commissioned numbers from day one.

<Callout variant="key" title="Handover is where commissioning either persists or evaporates">
The performance a building leaves commissioning with is the best it will ever have, and how long it holds that performance depends almost entirely on handover. A thorough package (recorded setpoints and baseline, plain-language sequences, a real maintenance schedule, trained operators) lets the building be run and maintained as intended for decades. A thin one (a box of manufacturer manuals and no training) guarantees drift, because nobody who runs the building knows how it was meant to work. Invest in the documentation and the training: it is the cheapest insurance on the whole commissioning spend.
</Callout>
