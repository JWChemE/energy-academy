**Commissioning** is the process of verifying that building systems are installed correctly, configured properly, and deliver the **intended performance**.

It's not testing (does it run?), and it's not handover (does the occupant know how to use it?). Commissioning is the bridge between design intent and actual performance.

## Why it matters

Studies show 15–30% of new buildings have significant performance gaps between design and reality:
- Control sequences incorrect or incomplete
- Sensors installed but not calibrated
- Equipment oversized or undersized
- Dampers, valves stuck or miscalibrated
- Occupants don't know how to operate systems

Commissioning catches these before handover. Retrofitting a failed system costs 10× more than fixing it during commissioning.

## Scope

Commissioning typically covers:
- **HVAC:** Boilers, chillers, pumps, fans, dampers, controls
- **Lighting:** Daylighting controls, occupancy sensors, dimming
- **Water systems:** Pumps, pressure, temperature
- **Controls:** Sensors, actuators, BMS logic, setpoints
- **Renewable:** Solar, heat pumps, batteries
- **Optional:** Envelope (air-tightness), electrical demand management, water conservation

## New vs existing building

**New construction:** Commissioning is part of the project (contractor responsibility, architect oversight).

**Existing buildings (retro-commissioning or ETICS):** Usually owner-driven to improve performance and reduce operating costs. Payback is often 1–3 years.

## Cost

Commissioning typically costs 0.5–2% of project cost (or 1–5% for retrofit). Return on investment is usually > 200% (savings exceed cost within 1–3 years).
