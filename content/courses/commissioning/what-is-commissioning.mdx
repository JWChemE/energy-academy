A building can be designed well, specified well and built to drawing, and still waste a fifth of its energy from the day it opens, because nobody checked that the systems actually do what the design intended. Commissioning is that check: the structured process of verifying that plant is installed correctly, configured properly, and delivering its intended performance in the real building. It is the bridge between design intent and reality, and skipping it is one of the most expensive false economies in the whole industry. This lesson establishes what commissioning is, why the performance gap it closes is so large, and what it costs against what it saves.

## What commissioning is, and is not

Commissioning is not the same as testing ("does it switch on?") or handover ("does the operator have the manual?"). It asks the harder question: does this system deliver the performance the design promised, under the conditions it will actually meet? A boiler that fires is not commissioned; a boiler proven to hit its rated efficiency at typical load, with its controls sequencing correctly and its sensors reading true, is.

The scope spans everything that affects energy and comfort: HVAC plant and its [controls](/courses/commissioning/controls-commissioning), lighting and its occupancy and daylight controls, hot water systems, metering, and increasingly renewables and heat pumps. On larger projects it can extend to the building fabric's airtightness.

## Why the gap is so large

Study after study finds that 15 to 30% of new buildings carry a significant gap between designed and actual performance, and the causes are mundane and repetitive: control sequences programmed wrongly or left incomplete, sensors installed but never calibrated, dampers and valves stuck or reversed, plant oversized and cycling, and setpoints left at factory defaults. None of these announces itself. The building runs, occupants are roughly comfortable, and the extra energy leaks away invisibly for years. Commissioning exists to catch these before handover, when they are cheap to fix; the same fault found after the contractors have left can cost ten times as much to put right.

## The economics

Commissioning typically costs 0.5 to 2% of project cost for new construction (higher, 1 to 5%, for a [retrofit exercise on an existing building](/courses/commissioning/retro-commissioning-existing)), and it routinely returns several times that within one to three years through energy the building would otherwise have wasted. On a project spending, say, £250,000 a year on energy, closing even a 15% performance gap is worth around £37,500 a year, against a commissioning cost that is usually a fraction of the first year's saving. Few line items in a construction budget have a stronger business case, which is exactly why cutting commissioning to save money so reliably backfires.

<Callout variant="key" title="An uncommissioned building bakes its waste in for its whole life">
The performance gap is not a teething problem that settles down; without commissioning it persists for the building's twenty or thirty-year life, quietly overspending every year. Because the systems work well enough that nobody complains, the waste is never investigated. Commissioning is the one point in a building's life where these faults are cheap to find and cheap to fix, which makes it less an optional quality step than the moment the building's whole operating cost is set. Treat it as infrastructure, not paperwork.
</Callout>
