Balanced flows and correct control sequences prove a system is set up right; performance testing proves the plant itself delivers the capacity and efficiency it was specified for. A chiller that should make 100 kW of cooling but manages only 85 is a fault the sequence tests would never reveal, because the controls are behaving perfectly, calling for cooling the plant cannot supply. This lesson covers how capacity and efficiency are measured on the two workhorses, chillers and boilers, and why the results become the building's performance baseline.

## Measuring what plant actually delivers

The method is the same for any water-side plant: measure the flow and the temperature change, and the heat transferred follows from a conservation balance, the same arithmetic as the [mass and energy balances course](/courses/mass-and-energy-balances/conservation-laws). For water, power (kW) ≈ flow (kg/s) × 4.18 × temperature difference (°C).

<WorkedExample title="testing a chiller against its rating">
  <Given>
    - A chiller is rated to deliver 100 kW of cooling, chilled water off at 6 °C, back at 12 °C (a 6 °C rise across the load)
    - Water specific heat 4.18 kJ/kg·°C
  </Given>
  <Find>The design flow, and how a fault would show.</Find>
  <Solution>
    Rearranging, design flow = 100 ÷ (4.18 × 6) ≈ **4.0 kg/s** (about 4 litres/second).

    In the test you run the chiller at full load and measure the actual flow and the actual
    6 °C-versus-12 °C temperatures. If the numbers multiply out to only 85 kW, the chiller
    is delivering **15% below rating**, and the shortfall names its own likely causes: a
    fouled condenser or evaporator, low refrigerant charge, or a tired compressor, exactly
    the faults the [refrigeration course](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/maintenance-and-faults)
    diagnoses. Acceptance is typically within ±10% of design; 15% low is a defect to
    resolve before handover, not after.
  </Solution>
</WorkedExample>

Boilers are tested the same way for output (flow × 4.18 × temperature rise) and additionally for **efficiency**, by measuring fuel input against useful heat out, ideally with a [flue gas analysis](/courses/steam-and-condensate/efficiency-assessment) to read combustion quality directly. Efficiency well below nameplate points to a boiler that is fouled, badly tuned (excess air), or degraded, all correctable, all cheaper to fix now than later.

## Test across the load range, not just at full output

Plant behaves differently at part load, and since most plant spends most of its life there (the [part-load lesson](/courses/refrigeration-and-heat-pumps/partload-efficiency) made this the central fact of chiller economics), testing only at full output misses how the building will actually run. Good performance testing samples multiple conditions: peak load on a design day, and part load in milder weather, where oversized plant tends to cycle inefficiently. Seasonal testing is more work but it verifies the behaviour that dominates the annual bill.

<Callout variant="key" title="Performance tests become the baseline you defend the building against">
The numbers from performance testing are not just a pass/fail gate; they are the reference the building's future is measured against. A chiller proven at 100 kW and a boiler proven at its rated efficiency, recorded with their test conditions, are the baseline that [re-commissioning](/courses/commissioning/drift-and-degradation) and [monitoring and targeting](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/establishing-baseline) later compare against to catch drift. Skip the testing and there is no baseline, so no one can ever say whether the plant has deteriorated or was simply never any good. Measure it once, properly, at handover, and every later investigation has a firm number to stand on.
</Callout>

Testing produces numbers; those numbers, and everything else commissioning found, are only useful if they reach the people who run the building, which is the handover lesson next.
