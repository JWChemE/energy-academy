The most efficient maintenance happens exactly when equipment needs it - no sooner, no later. Condition monitoring and predictive maintenance make this possible by watching equipment health and acting on the evidence.

## From calendar to condition

Preventive maintenance services on a fixed schedule, which inevitably means sometimes servicing equipment that did not need it, and sometimes missing a fault that develops between visits. **Condition-based (predictive) maintenance** instead monitors the actual state of the equipment and intervenes only when the data shows deterioration - more efficient with both maintenance resources and energy.

## What to monitor

Several measurable indicators reveal developing problems before failure:

**Vibration analysis.** The richest source of early warning for rotating equipment. Changing vibration signatures reveal bearing wear, imbalance, misalignment, and looseness - often weeks or months before failure. The cornerstone of predictive maintenance on motors, pumps, fans, and compressors.

**Temperature / thermography.** Infrared cameras spot hot spots - failing bearings, electrical connections, overloaded motors, and (for energy) missing insulation and fouled heat exchangers. A thermal survey of a plant room reveals both maintenance and energy issues at once.

**Oil analysis.** For engines, compressors, and gearboxes, analysing lubricant reveals wear metals, contamination, and degradation - an early indicator of internal wear.

**Efficiency / performance trending.** Tracking energy performance itself (next lesson) detects gradual degradation - a chiller's COP slipping, a boiler's flue temperature rising, a compressor's specific energy climbing.

**Acoustic / ultrasonic.** Detects compressed-air and vacuum leaks, electrical discharge, and early bearing faults.

## How predictive maintenance works

1. **Establish a baseline** of normal readings for healthy equipment
2. **Monitor** periodically or continuously
3. **Detect deviation** as readings trend away from baseline
4. **Diagnose** the developing fault
5. **Plan intervention** at a convenient time, before failure

This catches problems early (avoiding consequential damage and inefficient running) while avoiding unnecessary servicing.

## The energy dimension

Condition monitoring serves energy efficiency directly, because the same degradation that leads to failure also wastes energy along the way:
- Worn bearings drag (vibration rises, energy rises)
- Fouled heat exchangers lose effectiveness (thermography and efficiency trends reveal it)
- Leaks waste energy (ultrasonic finds them)

By catching these early, predictive maintenance prevents the long, slow, energy-wasting decline that precedes a breakdown - not just the breakdown itself.

## Continuous monitoring and the IoT

Increasingly, sensors feed continuous data to monitoring systems and the BMS, with software flagging anomalies automatically. This "always watching" approach catches faults the moment they begin and integrates maintenance with energy management - the building's data revealing both when to service and where energy is being wasted.

## Matching the method to the asset

Condition monitoring costs money - instruments, sensors, analysis - so it is targeted at equipment where it pays:
- **Critical** plant whose failure causes major disruption
- **Expensive** equipment where consequential damage is costly
- **Energy-intensive** plant where efficiency loss is expensive

For minor, cheap, non-critical items, simple preventive or reactive maintenance remains sensible. The art is directing predictive effort where the combined reliability and energy payoff justifies it.
