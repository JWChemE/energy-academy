The most efficient maintenance happens exactly when equipment needs it, no sooner and no later. Condition monitoring and predictive maintenance make this possible by watching equipment health and acting on the evidence, rather than on a fixed calendar that inevitably services some things too often and misses faults in others. This lesson covers the move from calendar to condition, what to monitor, how predictive maintenance works, its energy dimension, and how to target it.

## From calendar to condition

[Preventive maintenance](/courses/maintenance/planned-reactive) services on a fixed schedule, which inevitably means sometimes servicing equipment that did not need it and sometimes missing a fault that develops between visits. **Condition-based (predictive) maintenance** instead monitors the actual state of the equipment and intervenes only when the data shows deterioration, using both maintenance resources and energy more efficiently.

## What to monitor

Several measurable indicators reveal developing problems before failure:

- **Vibration analysis**: the richest source of early warning for rotating equipment, revealing bearing wear, imbalance, misalignment and looseness, often weeks or months before failure. The cornerstone of predictive maintenance on [motors, pumps, fans and compressors](/courses/maintenance/motor-pump).
- **Temperature / thermography**: infrared cameras spot failing bearings, electrical connections, overloaded motors and, for energy, missing [insulation](/courses/insulation-systems/pipe-installation) and fouled heat exchangers, so a thermal survey reveals maintenance and energy issues at once.
- **Oil analysis**: for engines, compressors and gearboxes, analysing lubricant reveals wear metals, contamination and degradation, an early indicator of internal wear.
- **Efficiency / performance trending**: tracking [energy performance](/courses/maintenance/performance-tracking) itself detects gradual degradation, a chiller's COP slipping, a boiler's flue temperature rising, a compressor's specific energy climbing.
- **Acoustic / ultrasonic**: detects compressed-air and vacuum leaks, electrical discharge and early bearing faults.

## How predictive maintenance works

The method is a loop: establish a **baseline** of normal readings for healthy equipment, monitor periodically or continuously, detect deviation as readings trend away from baseline, diagnose the developing fault, and plan intervention at a convenient time before failure. This catches problems early (avoiding consequential damage and inefficient running) while avoiding unnecessary servicing.

<Callout variant="key" title="The same degradation that ends in breakdown wastes energy all the way there">
Condition monitoring is usually sold on reliability, but its energy value is just as real, because the deterioration that leads to failure also wastes energy along the way: worn bearings drag (vibration rises, energy rises), fouled heat exchangers lose effectiveness (thermography and efficiency trends reveal it), and leaks waste energy continuously (ultrasonic finds them). By catching these early, predictive maintenance prevents the long, slow, energy-wasting decline that *precedes* a breakdown, not just the breakdown itself. Target it where it pays, on critical, expensive or energy-intensive plant, and leave simple preventive or reactive maintenance for minor items, exactly the [criticality-and-energy prioritisation](/courses/maintenance/maintenance-plan) the plan sets out. Increasingly, continuous sensors feed the [BMS](/courses/control-systems-and-bms/bms-functions) and flag anomalies automatically, merging maintenance with energy monitoring.
</Callout>
