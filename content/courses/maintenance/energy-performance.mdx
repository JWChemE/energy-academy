Equipment does not lose efficiency all at once - it drifts downward gradually as it fouls, wears, and falls out of adjustment. Understanding these mechanisms shows why maintenance is an energy measure.

## How equipment loses efficiency

Several universal mechanisms erode performance over time:

**Fouling and deposits.** Heat-transfer surfaces accumulate scale, soot, dust, and dirt that insulate against the very heat transfer they are meant to enable:
- Boiler tubes fur up with scale and soot, raising flue temperature
- Chiller and condenser coils clog with dust, raising condensing temperature
- Heat exchangers scale up, reducing effectiveness

Even thin deposits have a large effect - a millimetre of scale on a boiler tube can add several percent to fuel use.

**Wear and friction.** Moving parts wear, increasing the energy needed to do the same work:
- Bearings wear and drag
- Belts slip and lose transmission efficiency
- Seals leak, reducing pump and compressor output

**Leaks.** Compressed-air leaks, steam-trap failures, refrigerant loss, and duct leakage all waste energy continuously and silently.

**Control drift.** Sensors lose calibration, setpoints creep, and dampers and valves stick - so systems no longer operate at their efficient design points.

## The cumulative effect

Individually small, these effects compound. A poorly maintained building can drift 10-30% above its optimal energy use through the accumulation of fouled surfaces, worn parts, leaks, and drifted controls - usually without any single dramatic failure to draw attention.

## Maintenance as efficiency recovery

Each maintenance action reverses one of these mechanisms:
- Cleaning restores heat transfer
- Lubrication and belt adjustment cut friction losses
- Leak repair stops continuous waste
- Recalibration returns controls to efficient operation

So maintenance is not just about avoiding breakdowns - it actively recovers lost efficiency. A boiler clean, a coil wash, a belt tension, a sensor recalibration each return measurable energy savings, usually for far less than the energy they recover.

## Maintenance intervals and efficiency

The right interval balances the cost of maintenance against the rate of efficiency loss. Equipment that fouls quickly (a boiler in a dirty environment, a coil in a dusty space) needs frequent attention; slow-degrading equipment less. Monitoring the efficiency trend (next-level topic) reveals the optimal interval - service often enough to keep equipment near design efficiency, but no more often than that requires.

## The hidden cost of deferral

Deferring maintenance to save money is usually a false economy from an energy standpoint. The equipment keeps running, but progressively less efficiently, quietly burning extra fuel or power every hour until the deferred work is finally done. The energy cost of deferral often dwarfs the maintenance cost saved - the central reason energy managers should champion maintenance budgets.
