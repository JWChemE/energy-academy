Equipment does not lose efficiency all at once; it drifts downward gradually as it fouls, wears and falls out of adjustment. Understanding these mechanisms is what shows why maintenance is an energy measure, not just a reliability one, and why deferring it is usually a false economy. This lesson covers how equipment loses efficiency, the cumulative effect, maintenance as efficiency recovery, and the hidden cost of deferral, echoing the [commissioning drift](/courses/commissioning/drift-and-degradation) the commissioning course describes.

## How equipment loses efficiency

Several universal mechanisms erode performance over time:

- **Fouling and deposits**: heat-transfer surfaces accumulate scale, soot, dust and dirt that insulate against the very heat transfer they are meant to enable. Boiler tubes fur up (raising flue temperature), condenser coils clog (raising condensing temperature), heat exchangers scale (reducing effectiveness). Even thin deposits have a large effect: a millimetre of scale on a boiler tube can add several percent to fuel use.
- **Wear and friction**: moving parts wear, increasing the energy needed to do the same work. Bearings drag, belts slip, seals leak.
- **Leaks**: compressed-air leaks, [steam-trap failures](/courses/steam-and-condensate/trap-failure), refrigerant loss and duct leakage all waste energy continuously and silently.
- **Control drift**: [sensors lose calibration](/courses/control-systems-and-bms/temperature-sensors), setpoints creep, and dampers and valves stick, so systems no longer operate at their efficient design points.

## The cumulative effect

Individually small, these effects compound. A poorly maintained building can drift **10 to 30% above its optimal energy use** through the accumulation of fouled surfaces, worn parts, leaks and drifted controls, usually without any single dramatic failure to draw attention. This is the same silent, gradual erosion that [re-commissioning](/courses/commissioning/recommissioning) exists to reverse, seen from the maintenance side.

## Maintenance as efficiency recovery

Each maintenance action reverses one of these mechanisms: cleaning restores heat transfer, lubrication and belt adjustment cut friction losses, leak repair stops continuous waste, and recalibration returns controls to efficient operation. So maintenance is not just about avoiding breakdowns; it actively *recovers* lost efficiency. A boiler clean, a coil wash, a belt tension, a sensor recalibration each return measurable energy savings, usually for far less than the energy they recover.

<Callout variant="key" title="Deferring maintenance is a false economy paid in fuel, hour after hour">
The tempting saving in a tight year is to defer maintenance, but from an energy standpoint it usually costs more than it saves. The equipment keeps running, just progressively less efficiently, quietly burning extra fuel or power every hour until the deferred work is finally done, and the energy cost of that deferral often dwarfs the maintenance cost avoided. This is the central reason energy managers should champion maintenance budgets: a deferred boiler clean or coil wash is not a saving banked, it is a bill accumulating invisibly. The [right interval](/courses/maintenance/performance-tracking) balances the cost of maintenance against the rate of efficiency loss, service often enough to hold equipment near design efficiency, but no more often than that requires, which is a question only measurement can answer.
</Callout>

Making these invisible losses visible is the job of performance tracking, the final lesson.
