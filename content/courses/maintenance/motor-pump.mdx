Motors drive the pumps, fans and compressors that account for a huge share of industrial and commercial electricity use. Their maintenance is often overlooked precisely because they usually "just run", until inefficiency or failure sets in, and because they run for thousands of hours, small inefficiencies add up to large energy costs over a year. This lesson covers why motor maintenance matters, bearings, cooling, alignment and belts, then pumps and fans specifically, complementing the [motors and drives course](/courses/motors-and-drives/motor-basics).

## Why motor maintenance matters

A motor that keeps spinning can still be wasting energy and heading for failure: worn bearings increase friction and drag, misalignment stresses shafts and wastes energy, dirt and blocked cooling cause overheating (which cuts efficiency and shortens life), and slipping belts lose transmission efficiency. None of these stops the motor, so none draws attention until it becomes a fault, yet each raises the electricity bill continuously.

## Bearings, cooling, alignment and belts

**Bearings** are the commonest motor maintenance item: lubricate to schedule (but not over-grease, which causes overheating), monitor for wear via noise, temperature and ideally [vibration analysis](/courses/maintenance/condition-monitoring), and remember that bearing failure is the leading cause of motor breakdown and develops silently, so by the time it is audible the damage is done. **Cooling**: motors are cooled by airflow over their casing fins, so keep fins and vents clear of dust (a clogged motor runs hot, and insulation life roughly halves for each 10 °C rise above design). **Alignment and belts**: misalignment wastes energy and wears bearings and seals, loose belts slip (losing energy) while over-tight belts overload bearings, and worn or glazed belts should be replaced, ideally with efficient cogged or synchronous types.

## Pumps and fans specifically

For **pumps**, check seals (a leaking seal wastes fluid and energy and signals wear), inspect impellers for fouling and damage, keep strainers clear, and verify the pump runs near its best-efficiency point rather than throttled hard against a closed valve, a sign it is oversized and a candidate for a [variable-speed drive or impeller trim](/courses/motors-and-drives/system-optimization). For **fans**, maintain bearings and belts as for motors, keep impeller blades clean (fouling loses efficiency and balance), keep filters and dampers clear, and correct any imbalance (an unbalanced fan vibrates, wearing bearings and wasting energy). Where **variable-speed drives** are fitted, ensure they are actually modulating to match demand rather than running flat out, because a [misconfigured VSD](/courses/motors-and-drives/vfd-installation) delivers none of its savings.

<Callout variant="key" title="Motors run for thousands of hours, so small losses compound into large bills">
Because a motor's energy cost over a year dwarfs its purchase price (the [six-week fact](/courses/motors-and-drives/motor-basics) from the motors course), every small inefficiency, a dragging bearing, a slipping belt, a hot casing, a throttled pump, is expensive when multiplied by the running hours. Well-maintained motors and driven equipment run at design efficiency, last longer and fail less, and combined with predictive [vibration monitoring](/courses/maintenance/condition-monitoring) on critical units, motor maintenance protects a large slice of a facility's electricity use while avoiding the far greater cost of unplanned failure and the inefficient running that precedes it. Catch the wear early and you save energy twice: on the fault itself and on the slow decline before it.
</Callout>
