Motors drive the pumps, fans, and compressors that account for a huge share of industrial and commercial electricity use. Their maintenance is often overlooked precisely because they usually "just run" - until inefficiency or failure sets in.

## Why motor maintenance matters

A motor that keeps spinning can still be wasting energy and heading for failure:
- Worn bearings increase friction and drag
- Misalignment stresses shafts and wastes energy
- Dirt and blocked cooling cause overheating, which cuts efficiency and shortens life
- Slipping belts lose transmission efficiency

Because motors run for thousands of hours, small inefficiencies add up to large energy costs over a year.

## Bearings and lubrication

Bearings are the commonest motor maintenance item:
- **Lubricate** to schedule - but not over-grease, which causes overheating
- **Monitor** for wear via noise, temperature, and (ideally) vibration analysis
- Bearing failure is the leading cause of motor breakdown, and develops silently - by the time it is audible, damage is done

Predictive vibration monitoring (covered next) catches bearing wear early.

## Cooling and cleanliness

Motors are cooled by airflow over their casing fins:
- Keep fins and vents **clear of dust and dirt** - a clogged motor runs hot
- A motor running 10 C hotter than design loses efficiency and life (insulation life roughly halves for each 10 C rise)
- Check the cooling fan and airflow path

## Alignment and belts

For belt- and coupling-driven loads:
- **Shaft alignment:** misalignment wastes energy and wears bearings and seals - check and correct it
- **Belt tension:** loose belts slip (losing transmission efficiency and energy); over-tight belts overload bearings
- **Belt condition:** replace worn or glazed belts; consider efficient cogged or synchronous belts

## Pumps specifically

- **Seals:** check for leaks (a leaking seal wastes fluid and energy, and signals wear)
- **Impellers:** inspect for fouling, wear, and damage that reduce efficiency
- **Strainers and filters:** keep clear to maintain flow without excess energy
- **Operating point:** verify the pump runs near its best-efficiency point, not throttled hard against a closed valve (a sign it is oversized - a candidate for a variable-speed drive or trimming)

## Fans specifically

- **Bearings and belts:** as for motors
- **Impeller cleanliness:** fouled fan blades lose efficiency and balance
- **Filters and dampers:** clogged filters and stuck dampers raise fan energy
- **Balance:** an unbalanced fan vibrates, wearing bearings and wasting energy

## Drives and controls

Where **variable-speed drives (VSDs)** are fitted, ensure they are actually modulating speed to match demand rather than running flat-out - a misconfigured VSD delivers none of its savings. Keep drive cooling clean and check parameter settings.

## The maintenance payoff

Well-maintained motors and driven equipment run at their design efficiency, last longer, and fail less. Combined with predictive monitoring on critical units, motor maintenance protects a large slice of a facility's electricity use - and, by catching wear early, avoids the far greater cost of unplanned failure and the inefficient running that precedes it.
