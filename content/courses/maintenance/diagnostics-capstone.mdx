You've learned that maintenance and energy efficiency are inseparable — that equipment at its design condition uses the least energy, and that fouling, wear, leaks and control drift quietly push it up. This capstone puts you in the energy manager's chair, deciding what to maintain, when, how, and where to spend a limited budget for the most energy and reliability.

The thread through every case: **maintenance is an energy measure, not just a breakdown-avoidance one.** The skill isn't fixing a specific fault — the system capstones do that — it's the strategy: spotting the false economy of deferral, matching the maintenance approach to each asset, optimising intervals, reading the KPI trends, and directing effort where it protects the most energy.

## How the cases work

Each is the same five-step loop as your other deep-dive capstones:

1. **Investigate** — read the brief and the panel: KPIs, efficiency drift, criticality, energy significance, costs.
2. **Calculate** — work out the figure that decides it: the cost of deferral or drift, a strategy comparison, the value of a percentage point. The **reference data is on hand** — strategy, degradation mechanisms, condition monitoring — but the *method* is yours. **"I'm stuck"** gives the formula then a nudge, but using it blocks a clean pass until you retry unaided.
3. **Diagnose** — pick the root cause from the evidence and your numbers.
4. **Prescribe** — choose the strategy: do it now, change the interval, switch to preventive/predictive, prioritise the significant users.
5. **Verify** — score across Calculation, Diagnosis, Prescription, Sequence and Capture, then read the debrief.

<Callout variant="key" title="Maintain where it protects the most energy">
Every maintenance pound is an investment, so spend it where it pays. Never defer maintenance whose energy cost dwarfs its price. Match the strategy to the asset — reactive for cheap non-critical items, preventive for the bulk of plant, predictive for critical, energy-intensive equipment. Set intervals by how fast each asset actually fouls, track the efficiency KPIs to catch drift early, and put the effort on the significant energy users, not evenly across everything.
</Callout>

## What you'll practise

- **The cost of deferral** — why putting maintenance off to "save money" usually loses it.
- **Performance tracking** — reading flue-temp and COP trends to catch degradation early.
- **Strategy** — reactive vs preventive vs predictive, matched to criticality and energy.
- **Condition monitoring** — turning a rising vibration trend into a planned, cheap intervention.
- **Intervals & prioritisation** — servicing fast-fouling assets often enough, and the big users first.

## Differential thinking

A rising bill with no failure is the maintenance signature — accumulated fouling, wear, leaks and drift, none dramatic enough to notice. The decision is rarely "which fault" but "which strategy": is this asset critical and energy-intensive enough for predictive monitoring, or cheap enough to run to failure? Is the interval matched to how fast it degrades? Is the effort on the chiller or the trivial fan? The decisive evidence is the energy-and-criticality numbers, not the mechanism.

## After you finish

- Which **number** decided each case — the deferral cost, the KPI trend, the value of a percentage point?
- Could you reproduce each **calculation** on site, without the hints?
- For each asset, what's the right strategy — reactive, preventive or predictive — and what interval, and where does it sit in the priority list?
