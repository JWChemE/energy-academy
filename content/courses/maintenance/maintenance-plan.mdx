Good maintenance does not happen by accident. It needs a structured plan: knowing what equipment you have, what each item needs, when, and at what cost. Without that structure, maintenance slips whenever people are busy, and the efficiency losses the [previous lesson](/courses/maintenance/planned-reactive) described creep back in. This lesson covers building the plan: the asset register, prioritisation, tasks and intervals, scheduling, budgeting, and the CMMS that holds it all together.

## Start with an asset register

Everything begins with an **asset register**, a complete inventory of the equipment to be maintained: what it is (type, make, model, capacity), where it is, its condition and age, its criticality (how much its failure would matter), and its energy significance (how much energy it uses). Without knowing what you have, you cannot maintain it systematically, so the register is the foundation of the whole plan.

## Prioritise by criticality and energy

Not all equipment deserves equal attention. Rank assets by **criticality** (the consequences of failure for safety, production, comfort) and **energy significance** (how much energy the asset uses, the significant energy users). This focuses maintenance effort, and [predictive monitoring](/courses/maintenance/condition-monitoring), where it protects the most reliability and energy: a critical, energy-intensive chiller warrants far more attention than a minor non-critical fan.

## Define tasks, intervals, schedule and budget

For each asset, specify what tasks are needed (the [energy-focused checklist](/courses/maintenance/maintenance-checklist) tasks: cleaning, tuning, lubrication, calibration), how often (the interval matched to the asset's rate of degradation and energy impact), who does it, and what to record (readings that feed [performance trending](/courses/maintenance/performance-tracking)). Manufacturer recommendations set a starting point, refined by experience and condition data. Turn the tasks into a **schedule** that spreads work through the year, times seasonal maintenance appropriately (boilers before winter, chillers before summer), and coordinates with operations to minimise disruption (bundling with other works, such as insulation while plant is open). And give it a **budget** that recognises maintenance as an investment, not just a cost: routine labour and materials, contractor agreements, spares, condition-monitoring instruments, and major periodic overhauls.

## Use a CMMS, and keep the plan alive

A **Computerised Maintenance Management System (CMMS)** turns the plan into a working tool: it holds the asset register and history, schedules and issues work orders, records what was done and found, tracks costs and performance trends, and flags overdue tasks. It makes the plan repeatable and auditable, captures the data for performance trending, and stops maintenance slipping when people are busy.

<Callout variant="key" title="A maintenance plan is a living loop, not a fixed schedule">
The plan is not set once and forgotten; it is reviewed against evidence. Adjust intervals where condition data shows equipment degrades faster or slower than assumed, add [predictive monitoring](/courses/maintenance/condition-monitoring) where failures or energy losses justify it, and update the register as equipment changes. Tied to [performance tracking](/courses/maintenance/performance-tracking), the plan becomes a continuous-improvement loop, the same [Plan-Do-Check-Act cycle](/courses/intro-to-energy-management/the-energy-management-cycle) that underpins all energy management, keeping equipment near its design efficiency throughout its life at the lowest sensible cost. Framing the spend against the energy, reliability and capital life it protects is how you justify the budget to finance.
</Callout>
