Good maintenance does not happen by accident. It needs a structured plan - knowing what equipment you have, what each item needs, when, and at what cost. This lesson covers building that plan.

## Start with an asset register

Everything begins with an **asset register** - a complete inventory of the equipment to be maintained:
- What it is (type, make, model, capacity)
- Where it is
- Its condition and age
- Its criticality (how much its failure would matter)
- Its energy significance (how much energy it uses)

Without knowing what you have, you cannot maintain it systematically. The register is the foundation of the whole plan.

## Prioritise by criticality and energy

Not all equipment deserves equal attention. Rank assets by:
- **Criticality:** the consequences of failure (safety, production, comfort)
- **Energy significance:** how much energy the asset uses (the significant energy users)

This focuses maintenance effort - and predictive monitoring - where it protects the most reliability and energy. A critical, energy-intensive chiller warrants far more attention than a minor non-critical fan.

## Define tasks and intervals

For each asset, specify:
- **What tasks** are needed (the energy-focused checklist tasks - cleaning, tuning, lubrication, calibration)
- **How often** - the interval matched to the asset's rate of degradation and energy impact
- **Who** does it (in-house or contractor) and what skills/parts are needed
- **What to record** (readings that feed performance trending)

Manufacturer recommendations set a starting point, refined by experience and condition data.

## Schedule and resource

Turn the tasks into a **schedule**:
- Spread work through the year to balance the workload
- Time seasonal maintenance appropriately (boilers before winter, chillers before summer)
- Plan for spares, tools, and access
- Coordinate with operations to minimise disruption (and bundle with other works - e.g. insulation while plant is open)

## Budget

A maintenance plan needs a **budget** that recognises maintenance as an investment, not just a cost:
- Routine labour and materials
- Contractor agreements
- Spares and consumables
- Condition-monitoring instruments and analysis
- Major periodic overhauls

Framing maintenance spend against the energy, reliability, and capital-life it protects helps justify the budget - the link to the investment-case and leadership courses.

## Use a CMMS

A **Computerised Maintenance Management System (CMMS)** turns the plan into a working tool:
- Holds the asset register and maintenance history
- Schedules and issues work orders
- Records what was done and what was found
- Tracks costs, spares, and performance trends
- Flags overdue tasks

A CMMS makes the plan repeatable and auditable, captures the data for performance trending, and stops maintenance slipping when people are busy.

## A living plan

The maintenance plan is not fixed. Review it using the evidence:
- Adjust intervals where condition data shows equipment degrades faster or slower than assumed
- Add predictive monitoring where failures or energy losses justify it
- Update the register as equipment changes

Tied to performance tracking (next lesson), the plan becomes a continuous-improvement loop - maintaining equipment near its design efficiency throughout its life, at the lowest sensible cost.
