Maintenance protects energy efficiency, but only if you can see whether it is working. Performance tracking - monitoring how equipment efficiency changes over time - closes the loop, turning maintenance into a measurable, improvable process.

## Why track performance

Equipment efficiency degrades gradually and invisibly. Without measurement, you cannot tell whether a boiler is still efficient, whether a chiller's COP has slipped, or whether last month's maintenance actually helped. **Performance tracking** makes the invisible visible - revealing degradation early and verifying that maintenance restores efficiency.

## Key performance indicators

Track KPIs that reflect each system's efficiency:

- **Boilers:** flue-gas temperature, combustion efficiency, fuel use per unit of heat output
- **Chillers:** COP (cooling delivered per unit of electricity), kW per tonne of cooling
- **Motors/pumps/fans:** power draw, current, specific energy (energy per unit of work)
- **Compressed air:** specific energy (kWh per m3 of air), pressure
- **Whole systems:** energy use normalised for output, occupancy, or weather

The principle is to measure efficiency, not just consumption - a metric that should hold steady if equipment stays healthy, and that drifts when it does not.

## Trending and baselines

The power is in the **trend**, not the single reading:
1. Establish a **baseline** of efficiency when equipment is known to be in good condition
2. Track the KPI over time
3. Watch for **drift** away from baseline, signalling developing problems
4. Verify that maintenance returns the KPI toward baseline

A chiller's COP slowly declining over months points to a fouling condenser; a boiler's flue temperature creeping up points to fouling or excess air. The trend reveals what a snapshot cannot.

## Sources of data

- **BMS and sub-meters:** continuous data on temperatures, flows, and energy
- **Maintenance logs:** the readings recorded at each service visit
- **Spot measurements:** combustion tests, power readings, lux levels
- **Condition-monitoring data:** vibration, thermography, oil analysis

Recording readings at every maintenance visit (built into the plan) creates the data series that trending needs.

## Linking maintenance to results

Performance tracking lets you judge maintenance effectiveness:
- Did cleaning the condenser restore the chiller's COP? The trend shows it
- Is the boiler holding its efficiency between services, or degrading too fast (suggesting a shorter interval)?
- Are some assets degrading faster than expected, needing attention?

This evidence refines the maintenance plan - adjusting intervals, targeting effort, and justifying spend with hard numbers.

## Connecting to energy management

Performance tracking is where maintenance meets the wider disciplines of monitoring and targeting and measurement and verification. The same data that tells you a pump needs servicing also feeds the energy management system, and verifying maintenance savings uses the same baseline-and-trend logic as verifying any energy project.

## The continuous-improvement loop

Together, the maintenance plan and performance tracking form a loop:
1. Maintain equipment to plan
2. Track its efficiency
3. Detect drift and diagnose causes
4. Act - service, adjust intervals, investigate
5. Verify the KPI recovers
6. Refine the plan

Run continuously, this loop keeps the whole estate near its design efficiency, catches problems early, proves the value of maintenance in energy and cost terms, and steadily improves performance over time - the essence of maintenance as an energy-management discipline.
