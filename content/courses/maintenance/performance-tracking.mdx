Maintenance protects energy efficiency, but only if you can see whether it is working. Performance tracking, monitoring how equipment efficiency changes over time, closes the loop, turning maintenance from a hopeful routine into a measurable, improvable process. This closing lesson covers why to track, the key performance indicators, trending and baselines, data sources, and how tracking connects maintenance to the wider energy-management disciplines.

## Why track performance

Equipment efficiency degrades gradually and invisibly, as the [previous lesson](/courses/maintenance/energy-performance) showed. Without measurement, you cannot tell whether a boiler is still efficient, whether a chiller's COP has slipped, or whether last month's maintenance actually helped. **Performance tracking** makes the invisible visible, revealing degradation early and verifying that maintenance restores efficiency.

## Key performance indicators

Track KPIs that reflect each system's efficiency, measuring efficiency rather than just consumption, because an efficiency metric should hold steady if equipment stays healthy and drift when it does not:

- **Boilers**: flue-gas temperature, combustion efficiency, fuel per unit of heat output
- **Chillers**: COP, kW per tonne of cooling
- **Motors, pumps, fans**: power draw, current, specific energy (energy per unit of work)
- **Compressed air**: [specific energy](/courses/compressed-air/pressure-flow) (kWh per m³ of air), pressure
- **Whole systems**: energy [normalised](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/normalisation) for output, occupancy or weather

## Trending and baselines

The power is in the **trend**, not the single reading: establish a baseline of efficiency when equipment is known to be in good condition, track the KPI over time, watch for drift away from baseline (signalling developing problems), and verify that maintenance returns the KPI toward baseline. A chiller's COP slowly declining over months points to a [fouling condenser](/courses/maintenance/chiller-cooling); a boiler's flue temperature creeping up points to [fouling or excess air](/courses/maintenance/boiler-maintenance). The trend reveals what a snapshot cannot. The data comes from the [BMS and sub-meters](/courses/control-systems-and-bms/bms-functions), the readings recorded at each service visit (built into the [checklist](/courses/maintenance/maintenance-checklist)), spot measurements, and condition-monitoring data.

## Connecting to energy management

Performance tracking is where maintenance meets the wider disciplines of [monitoring and targeting](/courses/monitoring-and-targeting/why-mt) and [measurement and verification](/courses/measurement-and-verification): the same data that tells you a pump needs servicing also feeds the energy management system, and verifying maintenance savings uses the same baseline-and-trend logic as verifying any energy project.

<Callout variant="key" title="Maintenance and tracking form one continuous-improvement loop">
Together, the [maintenance plan](/courses/maintenance/maintenance-plan) and performance tracking form a loop that mirrors the whole [energy-management cycle](/courses/intro-to-energy-management/the-energy-management-cycle): maintain to plan, track efficiency, detect drift and diagnose causes, act (service, adjust intervals, investigate), verify the KPI recovers, and refine the plan. Run continuously, this loop keeps the whole estate near design efficiency, catches problems early, proves the value of maintenance in hard energy and cost terms (which is how you defend the budget), and steadily improves performance over time. That is the essence of maintenance as an energy-management discipline rather than a reactive chore, and it is where this course has been heading throughout.
</Callout>
