How a facility maintains its equipment has a direct and often underestimated effect on its energy use, because equipment at its design condition uses the least energy, and every kind of neglect makes it use more. The first choice is strategic: do you maintain before things break, or after? This lesson covers the three maintenance strategies, the energy argument that runs through the whole course, and how to match strategy to each asset.

## Reactive maintenance

**Reactive (breakdown) maintenance** means running equipment until it fails, then fixing it. It looks cheap (no spend until something breaks) but carries hidden costs: equipment runs inefficiently for a long time before outright failure (a fouled boiler, a slipping belt, a fouled coil each waste energy for months), failures happen at the worst time with emergency-repair premiums and downtime, and consequential damage (a failed bearing wrecks a shaft) costs far more than prevention. From an energy view, reactive maintenance lets efficiency drift downward unchecked between failures.

## Planned preventive maintenance (PPM)

**Planned preventive maintenance** services equipment on a schedule, before failure, to keep it running efficiently and reliably: cleaning, adjustment, lubrication and replacement at set intervals. It catches efficiency-robbing problems (fouling, clogged filters, drifting controls) early, reduces unplanned downtime and emergency costs, and keeps equipment near its design efficiency throughout its life. PPM costs more in routine attention but saves far more in avoided energy waste, downtime and major repairs.

## Predictive (condition-based) maintenance

A more advanced approach, **predictive maintenance**, services equipment based on its actual condition rather than a fixed calendar: it monitors indicators (vibration, temperature, efficiency, oil condition) and intervenes only when the data shows developing deterioration, avoiding both unnecessary servicing and unexpected failure. This is the [condition-monitoring lesson](/courses/maintenance/condition-monitoring) later in the course, and it represents the most efficient use of maintenance resources.

## The energy argument, and choosing a strategy

The core message of this course is that maintenance and energy efficiency are inseparable. Equipment at its design condition uses the least energy; as it fouls, wears and drifts, energy use creeps up, often invisibly. A boiler losing 5% to fouling, a motor losing output to worn bearings, a chiller losing COP to a dirty condenser: each wastes energy continuously, and each is prevented by routine maintenance costing a fraction of the energy it saves. In practice, facilities blend the three strategies.

<Callout variant="key" title="Match the strategy to the asset's criticality and energy impact">
No single strategy fits everything. Use **reactive** for non-critical, cheap-to-replace items where failure has little consequence; **preventive** for the bulk of important plant; and **predictive** for critical, expensive, or energy-intensive equipment where continuous monitoring pays for itself. The skill is directing maintenance effort where it protects the most energy, reliability and cost, which means knowing which of your assets are the [significant energy users](/courses/maintenance/maintenance-plan). Spending predictive-monitoring money on a minor fan while running the main chiller to failure is exactly the wrong allocation, and the [asset register](/courses/maintenance/maintenance-plan) in the next lesson is what prevents it.
</Callout>

Choosing strategies per asset requires knowing what you have, which is the maintenance plan, next.
