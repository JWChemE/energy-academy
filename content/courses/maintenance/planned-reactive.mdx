How a facility maintains its equipment has a direct and often underestimated effect on its energy use. The first choice is strategic: do you maintain before things break, or after?

## Reactive maintenance

**Reactive (breakdown) maintenance** means running equipment until it fails, then fixing it. It seems cheap - no spend until something breaks - but it carries hidden costs:
- Equipment runs inefficiently for a long time before outright failure (a fouled boiler, a slipping belt, a fouled coil all waste energy for months)
- Failures happen at the worst time, causing downtime and emergency-repair premiums
- Consequential damage - a failed bearing wrecks a shaft - costs far more than prevention

From an energy view, reactive maintenance lets efficiency drift downward unchecked between failures.

## Planned preventive maintenance (PPM)

**Planned preventive maintenance** services equipment on a schedule - before failure - to keep it running efficiently and reliably:
- Cleaning, adjustment, lubrication, and replacement at set intervals
- Catches efficiency-robbing problems (fouling, fouled filters, drifting controls) early
- Reduces unplanned downtime and emergency costs
- Keeps equipment near its design efficiency throughout its life

PPM costs more in routine attention but saves far more in avoided energy waste, downtime, and major repairs.

## Predictive (condition-based) maintenance

A more advanced approach, **predictive maintenance**, services equipment based on its actual condition rather than a fixed calendar:
- Monitors indicators (vibration, temperature, efficiency, oil condition)
- Intervenes only when the data shows developing deterioration
- Avoids both unnecessary servicing and unexpected failure

This is covered in detail later in the course - it represents the most efficient use of maintenance resources.

## The energy argument for prevention

The core message of this course is that maintenance and energy efficiency are inseparable. Equipment at its design condition uses the least energy; as it fouls, wears, and drifts, energy use creeps up - often invisibly. Preventive and predictive maintenance hold equipment near design efficiency, while reactive maintenance lets it slide.

A boiler losing 5% efficiency to fouling, a motor losing output to worn bearings, a chiller losing COP to a dirty condenser - each wastes energy continuously, and each is prevented by routine maintenance costing a fraction of the energy it saves.

## Choosing a strategy

In practice, facilities blend approaches:
- **Reactive** for non-critical, cheap-to-replace items where failure has little consequence
- **Preventive** for the bulk of important plant
- **Predictive** for critical, expensive, or energy-intensive equipment where monitoring pays

The skill is matching the strategy to each asset's criticality and energy impact - investing maintenance effort where it protects the most energy, reliability, and cost.
