Every investment carries uncertainty, and energy projects are no exception. Finance directors fund projects whose risks are understood and managed, not hidden. Assessing and presenting risk honestly is essential to a credible investment case - and often to winning approval.

## Why risk matters to the case

A project's value depends on assumptions - energy prices, savings, costs, lifespan - that may not hold. Finance scrutinises these assumptions because the return depends on them. An investment case that presents a single confident number, ignoring the uncertainty behind it, invites distrust. One that openly assesses the risks, and shows they are manageable, builds confidence. Honest risk assessment is therefore not a weakness in the case - it is a strength.

## Sources of risk in energy projects

Energy projects face several characteristic uncertainties:
- **Energy price risk:** future prices are uncertain, and they drive the savings. Higher prices help the case; lower prices hurt it.
- **Performance risk:** will the project deliver the predicted savings? (technical performance, the M&V link)
- **Behavioural risk:** will occupants undermine savings (the rebound effect)?
- **Cost risk:** might the project cost more than budgeted?
- **Operational risk:** could it disrupt operations?
- **Lifespan risk:** will equipment last as long as assumed?
- **Regulatory risk:** could policy or incentives change?

Identifying the relevant risks for a given project is the first step to assessing and managing them.

## Sensitivity analysis

The key tool for assessing risk is **sensitivity analysis** - testing how the project's value (NPV, IRR) changes as assumptions vary:
- What if energy prices are 20% lower than expected?
- What if savings are only 80% of predicted?
- What if capital costs 15% more?
- What if the equipment lasts 12 years instead of 15?

Sensitivity analysis reveals which assumptions the result is most sensitive to - and whether the project still makes sense under pessimistic assumptions. A project that remains positive even under adverse scenarios is robust; one that only works under optimistic assumptions is risky.

## Scenario analysis

Beyond varying one assumption at a time, **scenario analysis** examines coherent combinations:
- **Best case:** favourable prices, full savings, on-budget
- **Expected case:** the central, realistic assumptions
- **Worst case:** adverse prices, shortfall savings, cost overrun

Presenting the range - rather than a single point estimate - gives decision-makers an honest picture of the possible outcomes and the downside risk. It answers the board's natural question: "what if it goes wrong?"

## Managing and mitigating risk

Identifying risks leads to managing them. The case is strengthened by showing how risks are mitigated:
- **Energy price risk:** often actually a hedge - efficiency reduces exposure to price rises (a benefit, not just a risk)
- **Performance risk:** robust design, proven technology, M&V to verify and protect savings, performance guarantees (the EPC link)
- **Cost risk:** firm quotes, contingency, phased commitment
- **Behavioural risk:** engagement and controls to secure savings (the organisational-change link)

Showing that risks are understood and managed transforms them from reasons to reject into manageable features of a sound investment.

## Energy efficiency as risk reduction

A powerful and often overlooked point: energy efficiency itself reduces risk. By cutting energy use, efficiency projects:
- Reduce exposure to energy price volatility and rises
- Improve resilience to supply disruption
- Reduce exposure to tightening carbon regulation and pricing

This means an energy project is not just a return-generating investment but a **risk-reducing** one - a hedge against an uncertain energy future. Framing efficiency as risk management (alongside its return) appeals strongly to a board concerned with managing business risk, and is a distinctive strength of the energy investment case (the co-benefits link).

## Presenting risk honestly

The investment case should present risk transparently:
- The key assumptions and their basis
- Sensitivity and scenario analysis showing the range of outcomes
- The risks identified and how they are managed
- An honest view of the downside, and why the project is still worth doing

This honesty builds the credibility that wins funding. Boards trust a case that openly addresses risk far more than one that pretends there is none - and a project shown to be robust under adverse assumptions is a far easier decision to approve.
