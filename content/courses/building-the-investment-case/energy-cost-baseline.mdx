Every investment case rests on a foundation of fact: what the organisation currently spends on energy, and what it would spend in future without the project. Establishing this baseline accurately is the starting point for credibly quantifying any saving.

## Why the baseline matters

A saving is always measured against a reference - what would have happened without the project. If the baseline is wrong, the saving is wrong, and the whole case is undermined. Establishing a sound energy cost baseline:
- Quantifies the current cost (and carbon) - the problem the project addresses
- Provides the reference against which savings are calculated
- Grounds the case in verifiable fact rather than assumption
- Forms the basis for later verification (the M&V link)

This is the same baseline discipline as the Measurement and Verification course, applied here as the foundation of the investment case.

## Establishing current energy cost

The starting point is a clear picture of current energy use and cost:
- **Consumption** by fuel (electricity, gas, oil) from bills and meters
- **Cost** by fuel, including all components (unit rates, standing charges, demand charges, taxes)
- **Patterns** - when and where energy is used (the monitoring link)
- **Drivers** - what affects energy use (weather, production, occupancy)

Sub-metering and monitoring data (from the M&T course) give the granular picture needed to identify where a specific project's savings will come from.

## Forecasting future costs

A critical and often-neglected point: the baseline is not static. Energy costs change over time, and the saving must be measured against future costs, not just today's:
- **Energy price escalation** - prices generally rise over time (though volatile). A common assumption is 2-4% real escalation per year, but this is uncertain (the risk link)
- **Consumption growth or decline** - changing operations, occupancy, or production
- **Carbon costs** - rising carbon prices and taxes that add to fuel costs

Because savings accrue over the project's whole life (often 15-20 years), the future cost trajectory matters enormously. A project saving energy worth more each year as prices rise is worth far more than a static calculation suggests.

## The rising baseline effect

Future price escalation has a powerful effect on the case: as energy prices rise, the value of each unit saved rises too. A project saving a fixed quantity of energy saves *more money* each year as prices climb. Over a 15-year life with 3% annual price rises, the cumulative value of savings is substantially higher than today's prices imply. Modelling this rising baseline - rather than assuming flat prices - often strengthens the case considerably (while being honest about the uncertainty).

## Avoiding baseline errors

Common baseline mistakes that distort the case:
- **Using today's prices flat** - understating the value of long-lived savings
- **Ignoring the counterfactual** - not accounting for what costs would have done anyway (rising prices, growing demand)
- **Poor data** - inaccurate consumption or cost figures
- **Ignoring all cost components** - missing demand charges, taxes, or carbon costs
- **Boundary confusion** - unclear what the baseline includes (the M&V link)

A rigorous baseline avoids these, grounding the case in accurate current cost and a credible future trajectory.

## Normalising for fair comparison

As in M&V, the baseline must account for the factors that affect energy use, so the project's saving is not confused with changes in weather, output, or occupancy. A baseline normalised for these drivers allows the saving to be isolated and credibly attributed to the project - essential both for the case and for later verification.

## From baseline to savings

The energy cost baseline is the foundation on which the project's savings are quantified (the next lesson). With a clear picture of current cost, a credible forecast of future cost, and proper normalisation, the saving can be calculated as the difference between the baseline trajectory and the projected cost with the project in place. This grounds the investment case in fact - the essential first step before quantifying savings, structuring the finance, and presenting the case to those who control the capital.
