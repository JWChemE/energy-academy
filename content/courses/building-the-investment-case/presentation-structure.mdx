A sound analysis poorly presented wins no funding. The way an investment case is structured and presented to financial decision-makers can matter as much as its underlying merit. Knowing how to present to finance is a decisive skill.

## Presenting to a financial audience

Finance directors and investment committees see many proposals competing for limited capital. They decide quickly, on a few key factors, and they speak a particular language (the communication link). An energy investment case must be presented in their terms - concise, financially framed, and answering their questions - not in the technical language natural to an engineer.

## Lead with the decision

Financial decision-makers want the conclusion first, not a journey to it:
- **What is being asked for?** (the investment and the decision needed)
- **What is the return?** (NPV, IRR, payback against the hurdle rate)
- **What is the risk?** (briefly - and that it is managed)
- **Why does it matter?** (strategic fit, co-benefits, risk reduction)

Lead with these. The detailed analysis supports the conclusion; it does not precede it. An executive summary that states the ask, the return, and the rationale up front respects the decision-maker's time and frames everything that follows.

## The structure of a strong case

A well-structured investment case typically follows this order:

1. **Executive summary** - the ask, the return, the recommendation, in a few lines
2. **The opportunity** - the problem and the opportunity (current cost, the saving available)
3. **The proposal** - what the project is, concisely
4. **The financial case** - NPV, IRR, payback, the full value including co-benefits
5. **The risks** - key risks and how they are managed (the risk link)
6. **The funding** - how it will be paid for and the structure (the financial-structure link)
7. **The strategic fit** - how it supports broader goals
8. **The recommendation and ask** - the clear decision requested

This structure leads with the conclusion, provides the supporting detail in logical order, and ends with a clear ask.

## Financial framing

Throughout, frame in financial terms:
- **Return metrics** the audience uses (NPV, IRR against the hurdle rate)
- **The full value** - energy savings, co-benefits, risk reduction, carbon (the co-benefits link)
- **Risk** presented honestly and as managed (the risk link)
- **Comparison** to the alternatives, including doing nothing

Avoid drowning the case in technical detail - the engineering belongs in an appendix, not the main argument. Finance cares about the return, the risk, and the strategic value, not the kilowatt-by-kilowatt analysis.

## Clarity and concision

Financial decision-makers value brevity and clarity:
- **Concise** - a short main case, with detail available but not imposed
- **Clear** - plain language, key numbers prominent, one message per slide or page
- **Visual** - charts that make the point instantly, not dense tables
- **Confident** - a well-evidenced recommendation, not a hedged maybe

A case that respects the decision-maker's time and presents the essentials clearly is far more persuasive than an exhaustive technical document.

## Anticipating questions

Strong presentation anticipates the questions a finance audience will ask:
- "How confident are you in these savings?" (the verification commitment - the M&V link)
- "What if energy prices fall?" (sensitivity analysis - the risk link)
- "Why this rather than other uses of the capital?" (the competition for capital - the portfolio link)
- "What happens if we do nothing?" (the cost and risk of inaction)
- "How will we know it worked?" (the M&V commitment)

Having clear, evidence-based answers ready - and pre-empting the key ones in the case itself - builds confidence and avoids being caught out.

## The strategic dimension

Beyond the numbers, connect the project to the organisation's strategy and priorities (the leadership and governance links):
- Cost and risk management
- Carbon and sustainability commitments (net zero)
- Reputation and stakeholder expectations
- Resilience and future-proofing

A project that clearly advances the organisation's strategic goals - not just its energy bill - carries weight beyond its financial return, and aligns with what the board is trying to achieve.

## From presentation to portfolio

A single well-presented case wins a single project. But energy managers typically have many potential projects competing for limited capital - which raises the question of how to prioritise and present them as a portfolio. That is the subject of the next lesson. The skill of presentation, though, applies throughout: leading with the decision, framing in financial terms, presenting honestly and concisely, and connecting to strategy - turning sound analysis into approved investment.
