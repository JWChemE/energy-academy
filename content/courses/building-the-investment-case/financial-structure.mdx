How an energy project is funded shapes its appeal as much as the savings it delivers. The financial structure - whether it sits on the balance sheet, is funded from operating budgets, or is financed externally - determines who bears the cost and risk, and often whether the project happens at all.

## Why structure matters

Two projects with identical savings can have very different chances of approval depending on how they are funded. The financial structure affects:
- **Whether capital is available** - many good projects stall not because they fail the return test but because capital is constrained
- **Who bears the risk** - the organisation, or a third party
- **The balance-sheet impact** - capital expenditure versus operating expense
- **The cash flow** - upfront cost versus spread-out payments

Understanding the structuring options lets the energy manager present a project in the way most likely to win approval, and overcome the capital constraints that block many sound projects.

## CAPEX versus OPEX

A fundamental distinction:
- **Capital expenditure (CAPEX):** the organisation buys the project outright, as a capital asset. It owns the savings but bears the upfront cost and the capital is tied up.
- **Operating expense (OPEX):** the project is funded through ongoing payments (leasing, energy services, financing) rather than upfront capital. No large capital outlay, but ongoing cost.

The choice matters because organisations often have limited capital budgets but more room in operating budgets. A project that cannot get scarce CAPEX may proceed easily as OPEX through financing - the same project, structured differently to fit the available budget (the financing course explores this in depth).

## On-balance-sheet versus off-balance-sheet

Related to CAPEX/OPEX is whether the project appears on the balance sheet:
- **On-balance-sheet:** the organisation owns the asset and any associated debt - affecting its financial ratios and borrowing capacity
- **Off-balance-sheet:** a third party owns the asset (e.g. an energy services company), and the organisation simply pays for the service or the savings - keeping the asset and debt off its books

Off-balance-sheet structures (such as energy performance contracts and energy-as-a-service - the financing course) can make projects possible where balance-sheet capacity or capital is constrained, though accounting rules on what qualifies have tightened.

## Funding routes

Energy projects can be funded through various routes (detailed in the financing course):
- **Internal capital** - the organisation's own funds (simplest, but competes for scarce CAPEX)
- **Debt** - borrowing to fund the project (corporate loans, green loans)
- **Leasing** - paying for equipment over time
- **Energy performance contracts (EPCs)** - a provider funds and delivers the project, paid from the savings
- **Energy-as-a-service** - paying for the energy outcome rather than owning the equipment
- **Grants and incentives** - reducing the net cost

Each route has different implications for cost, risk, ownership, and balance sheet - and the best choice depends on the project, the organisation's finances, and its priorities.

## The cost of capital

Whatever the route, capital has a cost - the return that must be earned to justify using it (the hurdle rate / NPV link):
- **Internal capital** carries an opportunity cost (what else it could earn)
- **Debt** carries an interest cost (though green finance may offer preferential rates - the financing course)
- **Third-party finance** carries the provider's required return

The cost of capital feeds directly into the project's appraisal (the discount rate in NPV). A lower cost of capital - through cheap green finance, for instance - improves the project's economics. Structuring to minimise the cost of capital strengthens the case.

## Matching structure to circumstance

There is no single best structure - the right choice depends on:
- **Capital availability** - constrained capital favours OPEX/financed routes
- **Risk appetite** - transferring risk favours EPCs and energy-as-a-service
- **Cost of capital** - cheap internal funds may favour CAPEX; expensive capital favours external finance
- **Strategic factors** - ownership, control, balance-sheet considerations

The energy manager's skill is matching the structure to the organisation's circumstances - presenting the project in the form most likely to be funded. A project rejected as CAPEX may sail through as a performance contract; one that fails on constrained internal funds may succeed on cheap green finance.

## From structure to presentation

The financial structure is a key part of the investment case - it answers not just "is this project worth doing?" but "how will we pay for it, and who bears the risk?" Presenting a well-chosen structure, matched to the organisation's financial reality, removes a major obstacle to approval. The financing course explores the structures in detail; here, the lesson is that how a project is funded is as important to winning approval as the savings it delivers - and the next module turns to presenting the complete, well-structured case to those who decide.
