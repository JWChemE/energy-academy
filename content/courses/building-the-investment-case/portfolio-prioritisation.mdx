Energy managers rarely have just one project - they have a pipeline of opportunities competing for limited capital and attention. Prioritising them as a portfolio, and presenting them strategically, is how to maximise the energy and value delivered from a constrained budget.

## From single project to portfolio

A mature energy programme generates many potential projects - quick wins, equipment upgrades, controls, renewables, deep retrofits. They cannot all be done at once; capital, time, and organisational capacity are limited. The energy manager must therefore think as a portfolio manager: which projects, in which order, deliver the most value within the constraints? This portfolio view is more sophisticated - and more powerful - than pursuing projects one at a time.

## Ranking projects

Projects are prioritised against multiple criteria:
- **Financial return** - NPV, IRR, payback (the appraisal link)
- **Cost** - the capital required (and its availability)
- **Risk** - certainty of the savings and ease of delivery
- **Strategic fit** - alignment with carbon targets and broader goals
- **Co-benefits** - the wider value beyond energy (the co-benefits link)
- **Dependencies** - whether some projects enable or require others

No single criterion suffices. A project with a high return but high risk, or one with a modest return but vital strategic fit, must be weighed in the round. Ranking projects across these dimensions reveals the priorities.

## The marginal approach

A useful lens is the marginal one (which the net-zero course develops as the MAC curve): rank projects by their cost-effectiveness - the value (or carbon saved) per pound invested. This reveals:
- The **cheapest, highest-return projects** to do first
- The point at which further projects cost more than they return
- How far the budget stretches across the pipeline

Doing the most cost-effective projects first maximises the value (and savings) extracted from each pound of capital - the logic of marginal abatement.

## Balancing the portfolio

A good portfolio is balanced, not just a list ranked by return:
- **Quick wins** - cheap, fast-payback projects that build credibility, momentum, and savings to fund later work (the leadership link)
- **Strategic projects** - larger, longer-payback investments essential to the bigger goals (net zero)
- **Risk balance** - a mix of certain and more ambitious projects
- **Dependencies** - sequencing so that enabling projects come first (e.g. efficiency before renewables - the net-zero sequencing link)

Starting with quick wins generates savings and credibility that unlock funding and support for the bigger, harder projects - a deliberate sequencing that the leadership and net-zero courses both emphasise.

## Bundling projects

Individual projects can be strengthened by bundling:
- **Combining quick paybacks with longer ones** - so the bundle's average return clears the hurdle, carrying a strategically important but slower project on the back of fast ones
- **Sharing costs** - access, disruption, and overheads spread across several measures done together (the maintenance and retrofit links)
- **Presenting a programme** rather than a series of separate asks - often more compelling to a board than piecemeal requests

Bundling can make viable a worthwhile project that would fail to clear the hurdle alone, and can be more efficient to deliver - a key portfolio technique.

## Presenting the portfolio

Presenting the pipeline as a strategic portfolio, rather than a stream of individual requests, is powerful:
- It shows a coherent, prioritised programme aligned with the organisation's goals
- It lets the board commit to a multi-year investment plan rather than reacting case by case
- It demonstrates strategic thinking, building the energy function's credibility
- It connects individual projects to the bigger picture (net zero, cost, risk)

A board presented with a well-prioritised, strategically framed portfolio - "here is our roadmap, here is what it delivers, here is the phasing" - is more likely to commit sustained funding than one asked to approve disconnected projects one at a time (the net-zero roadmap link).

## Matching to the budget

Finally, the portfolio is matched to the available budget and capacity:
- Prioritising within the capital available
- Phasing projects over time as budget and capacity allow
- Using the savings from early projects to fund later ones (a revolving fund approach)
- Seeking external finance for projects beyond the internal budget (the financing link)

This turns a long list of opportunities into a deliverable, funded programme - maximising the energy and value delivered within the organisation's real constraints. The investment case, mastered at the project level and applied across the portfolio, is what secures the sustained investment that an ambitious energy programme - and ultimately net zero - requires. The final lesson turns to securing approval for that programme.
