Energy projects compete with every other call on an organisation's capital. To win funding, they must be appraised in the language finance directors use - net present value, internal rate of return, and the hurdle rates against which all investments are judged.

## Beyond simple payback

The Economic Analysis course introduced simple payback - capital cost divided by annual saving. It is useful for quick screening but has a fatal flaw for serious investment decisions: it ignores the **time value of money** and everything that happens after the payback point. A project paying back in 4 years but lasting 20 looks identical, on payback, to one lasting 5 - yet they are worth vastly different amounts.

Serious investment appraisal therefore uses discounted cash flow methods: NPV and IRR.

## The time value of money

A pound today is worth more than a pound next year - it could be invested, and it carries no risk of not arriving. **Discounting** captures this by reducing future cash flows to their present value using a discount rate:

Present value = future value / (1 + discount rate)^years

The further off a cash flow, the more it is discounted. This is the foundation of NPV and IRR.

## Net present value (NPV)

**NPV** sums the present values of all a project's cash flows - the initial investment (negative) and the future savings (positive, discounted) - over its life:

NPV = sum of discounted cash flows - initial investment

- **NPV positive:** the project creates value (returns more than the discount rate) - worth doing
- **NPV negative:** it destroys value - reject
- **Higher NPV:** more value created

NPV is the gold standard because it captures the full life of the project and the time value of money, expressing the result as a single pound figure of value created.

## Internal rate of return (IRR)

**IRR** is the discount rate at which a project's NPV equals zero - effectively the percentage return the project earns:
- An IRR of 15% means the project returns 15% per year on the capital invested
- Compare the IRR to the organisation's required return (hurdle rate)
- **IRR above the hurdle rate:** worth doing; **below:** reject

IRR is intuitive (a percentage return that finance understands instantly) and allows projects of different sizes to be compared, which is why finance directors love it - though it has quirks (it can mislead for unusual cash-flow patterns, where NPV is more reliable).

## The hurdle rate

The **hurdle rate** (or required rate of return) is the minimum return an investment must earn to be worth doing. It reflects:
- The organisation's **cost of capital** (what it costs to raise money)
- The **risk** of the project (riskier projects need higher returns)
- **Opportunity cost** (other uses of the capital)

Energy projects often face hurdle rates of 10-20%. Crucially, energy projects compete against all other investments for the same capital - a new production line, an acquisition, a marketing campaign - so they must clear the same hurdle. Understanding this competition is key to framing energy projects to win (the portfolio link).

## Applying it to energy projects

For an energy project, the cash flows are:
- **Initial investment** (capital cost) - negative, up front
- **Annual energy savings** - positive, each year
- **Maintenance savings or costs** - as applicable
- **Residual value** or replacement costs over the life

Discounting these over the project's life and comparing the NPV and IRR to the hurdle rate gives a rigorous, finance-credible answer to whether the project is worth doing - far more robust than payback alone.

## The energy advantage and challenge

Energy projects often have a particular profile: meaningful upfront cost, then steady savings over a long life. This profile usually looks good on NPV and IRR over the full life - but can look unremarkable on simple payback, which ignores the long tail of savings. Presenting energy projects on NPV and IRR, over their full life, therefore often reveals value that payback hides - a key reason to master these methods.
