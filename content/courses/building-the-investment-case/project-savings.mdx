The heart of an energy investment case is the saving it claims. Quantifying that saving credibly - neither overstating to win approval nor understating through excessive caution - is a discipline in itself, and the integrity of the whole case depends on it.

## The central importance of credible savings

The saving is what justifies the investment. Get it wrong and the consequences are serious:
- **Overstate it**, and the project disappoints, the credibility of the energy function is damaged, and future cases are distrusted (the M&V link)
- **Understate it**, and good projects are rejected as not worth doing

Credible, defensible savings estimates are therefore the foundation of both individual project approval and the long-term credibility that makes future investment possible. This is where the rigour of the whole curriculum - technical knowledge, measurement, and honesty - comes together.

## Calculating the saving

A project's energy saving is the difference between the baseline (what would be used without the project) and the projected use with the project:

Saving = baseline energy cost - projected energy cost with project

This draws on:
- The **energy cost baseline** (the previous lesson) - the reference and its future trajectory
- The **engineering estimate** of the project's effect - how much energy it will save, based on the technical analysis (the technical courses)
- The **future energy prices** that value the saved energy

The result is the annual saving, projected over the project's life.

## Engineering estimates and their limits

The technical saving comes from engineering analysis - calculating how much less energy the efficient equipment or measure will use. But engineering estimates have well-known tendencies:
- They often assume ideal conditions and perfect operation
- They may not fully account for behaviour (the rebound effect)
- They can overlook interactions between systems
- They are predictions, not measurements

Good practice applies sensible caution to engineering estimates, and commits to verifying the actual saving once the project is in place (the M&V link). Presenting an engineering estimate as a guaranteed outcome invites disappointment.

## Conservative and honest estimation

The integrity of the case rests on honest estimation:
- **Be realistic, not optimistic** - resist the temptation to inflate savings to win approval
- **Account for real-world factors** - behaviour, part-load, interactions, degradation
- **Apply sensible caution** - it is better to under-promise and over-deliver than the reverse
- **Be transparent** about assumptions and uncertainties (the risk link)

A conservative, honest estimate that the project then beats builds credibility; an optimistic estimate that the project misses destroys it. Over a career, the energy manager's credibility - built on savings that materialise - is their most valuable asset (the self-leadership link).

## Including all the value

While being conservative on energy savings, a complete case includes all the value the project delivers:
- **Energy cost savings** (the core, conservatively estimated)
- **Maintenance savings** (efficient new equipment often costs less to maintain)
- **Co-benefits** (productivity, comfort, reliability, asset value - the co-benefits lesson)
- **Risk reduction** (reduced exposure to price and regulation - the risk lesson)
- **Carbon value** (avoided carbon cost, increasingly significant)

Conservative on the core energy saving, but complete in capturing the full value, produces a case that is both credible and compelling.

## Verification commitment

A powerful element of a credible savings case is the commitment to verify:
- Stating how the actual savings will be measured (the M&V plan)
- Committing to report actual against predicted
- Building in the measurement infrastructure to do so

This commitment signals confidence and integrity. It also protects the savings over time (catching drift and underperformance) and builds the track record of verified results that makes future cases easier to fund. Boards trust an energy manager who says "here is my estimate, and here is how I will prove what we actually achieved."

## From savings to the full case

Credible savings, valued over the project's life against a sound baseline, and combined with the full value (co-benefits, risk reduction, carbon), produce the financial heart of the investment case. The next questions are how to fund the project (the financial-structure lesson) and how to present the whole case persuasively to those who control the capital (the presentation module). But it all rests on this: a saving estimate the organisation can believe, and the energy manager can stand behind - the foundation of a case with integrity.
